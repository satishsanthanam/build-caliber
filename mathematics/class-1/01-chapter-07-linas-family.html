Class 1 — Chapter 7: Lina’s Family

7.1 Height and Distance Comparison

This section introduces the concepts of comparing heights and relative distances using real-world examples from Lina's family and her surroundings.

7.1.1 Tallest and Shortest

We can compare the heights of different people or objects to determine who or what is the tallest or shortest.

  • Tallest: The person or object with the greatest height. For example, in Lina's family, we compare the heights of her grandparents, parents, and siblings to find the tallest member.
  • Shortest: The person or object with the least height.
  • Standing on an elevated platform (like a stool) makes a person appear taller, but does not change their actual height.

7.1.2 Nearest and Farthest

Distance can be described relatively using the terms nearest to and farthest from:

  • Nearest to: The shortest distance between two points or objects.
  • Farthest from: The longest distance between two points or objects.
  • Example: Analyzing a map to determine if Lina's house is nearest to the shop or farthest from the school.

7.1.3 Amazing Facts: The Statue of Unity

The statue of Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, also known as the Statue of Unity, is the tallest statue in the world. It is located in Gujarat, India.

7.2 Length, Thickness, and Non-Standard Measurement

Understanding how to compare physical dimensions of objects and measure them using informal, non-standard units of measurement.

7.2.1 Comparing Dimensions

Objects can be compared based on different physical attributes:

  • Length: Comparing objects to find which is the longest or shortest (e.g., pencils, braids, or ropes).
  • Thickness: Comparing objects to find which is the thickest or thinnest (e.g., books, tree trunks, or candles).

7.2.2 Non-Standard Units of Measurement

Before using standard tools like rulers, we can estimate and measure lengths using parts of our body:

  • Handspan: The distance from the tip of the thumb to the tip of the little finger on an outstretched hand. Used for medium-sized objects like tables, bags, books, and sweaters.
  • Fingers: Used to measure smaller objects (e.g., erasers, keys, or pencils) where a handspan is too large.
  • Footspan: Measuring distance by walking heel-to-toe. Useful for measuring floor distances, such as the distance between a seat and the classroom door, or between two walls.
7.3 Weight and Capacity

Introduction to comparing how heavy objects are and how much liquid containers can hold.

7.3.1 Heavier and Lighter

Weight is a measure of how heavy an object is. We compare objects to identify which is heavier and which is lighter:

  • A bucket full of water is heavier than an empty mug.
  • A watermelon is heavier than an orange.
  • A small packet of salt is lighter than a large sack of rice.

7.3.2 Capacity (Volume)

Capacity is the amount of liquid a container can hold.

  • Larger containers (like buckets or pots) have a higher capacity than smaller containers (like glasses, bowls, or cups).
  • We can measure capacity by seeing how many smaller units (e.g., cups or jugs of water) are needed to completely fill a larger container.

7.3.3 Water Conservation and Usage

Water is a precious resource, and different activities require different amounts of water:

  • Brushing teeth: Requires a small amount (a mug full of water).
  • Bathing: Requires a larger amount (a bucket full of water instead of a continuous shower to avoid waste).
  • Watering plants: Can be done efficiently using a watering can or mug.
  • Conservation Tip: Always close the tap when not in use, reuse leftover drinking water for plants, and avoid running tap water unnecessarily.
