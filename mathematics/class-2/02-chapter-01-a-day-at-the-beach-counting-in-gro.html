Class 2 — Chapter 1: A Day at the Beach

1.1 Introduction: Counting in Groups

Learning to count objects efficiently by grouping them rather than counting one by one.

Counting at the Beach

Observing a beach scene to count various items in groups or bunches:

  • Coconuts: Counted on trees or in groups.
  • Boats: Counted individually or in clusters along the shore.
  • Children: Playing in groups.
  • Oranges: Arranged in baskets or groups.

Counting in groups or bunches helps in counting larger numbers quickly and reduces errors compared to counting one by one.

Let us Do: Grouping Items

Practicing grouping with different objects:

  • Shell Necklaces: Necklaces made of shells, where each necklace contains exactly $10$ shells.
  • Balloons: Grouped into bunches with loose balloons remaining.
  • Bananas: Grouped into bunches of a specific quantity with some loose bananas.
  • Seashells: Grouping collected seashells to make bracelets and necklaces of equal groups.
1.2 Tens and Ones: Packs of Ten

Understanding the base-10 number system by grouping objects into tens and ones.

Tasty Chikoos!

Manoj helps his father sell chikoos by arranging them in trays of $10$. This demonstrates how two-digit numbers are composed of tens and ones:

  • $25 ext{ chikoos} = 2 ext{ trays of ten} + 5 ext{ loose chikoos}$ (represented as $20 + 5$)
  • $43 ext{ chikoos} = 4 ext{ trays of ten} + 3 ext{ loose chikoos}$ (represented as $40 + 3$)
  • $35 ext{ chikoos} = 3 ext{ trays of ten} + 5 ext{ loose chikoos}$ (represented as $30 + 5$)
  • $58 ext{ chikoos} = 5 ext{ trays of ten} + 8 ext{ loose chikoos}$ (represented as $50 + 8$)

Fun with Blocks and Strips

Using concrete representations for tens and ones:

  • Block Stick: A single stick made of $10$ individual blocks. $$1 ext{ block stick} = 10 ext{ blocks}$$
  • Ten Strip: A strip divided into $10$ units. $$1 ext{ ten strip} = 10 ext{ units}$$

Place Value Table

Representing numbers using Ten Strips and Units:

  • 24: $2$ Ten Strips, $4$ Units
  • 36: $3$ Ten Strips, $6$ Units
  • 72: $7$ Ten Strips, $2$ Units
  • 69: $6$ Ten Strips, $9$ Units
  • 46: $4$ Ten Strips, $6$ Units
1.3 Making 100 and Counting with Cards

Extending the grouping concept to represent larger numbers up to 100.

Let us Make 100!

Joining $10$ ten-strips together creates a grid of $100$ units. Different combinations of yellow and red strips can make $100$:

  • $70 ext{ yellow units} + 30 ext{ red units} = 100 ext{ units}$ ($7 ext{ yellow ten-strips} + 3 ext{ red ten-strips} = 10 ext{ ten-strips}$)
  • $50 ext{ yellow units} + 50 ext{ red units} = 100 ext{ units}$ ($5 ext{ yellow ten-strips} + 5 ext{ red ten-strips} = 10 ext{ ten-strips}$)
  • $60 ext{ yellow units} + 40 ext{ red units} = 100 ext{ units}$ ($6 ext{ yellow ten-strips} + 4 ext{ red ten-strips} = 10 ext{ ten-strips}$)

Counting through Cards

Using shape cards to represent place value:

  • A triangle card ($ riangle$) represents a group of $10$ (Tens).
  • A circle card ($ullet$) represents a single unit (Ones).

Examples:

  • $3 ext{ tens} + 1 ext{ one} = 30 + 1 = 31$
  • $2 ext{ tens} + 8 ext{ ones} = 20 + 8 = 28$
  • $7 ext{ tens} + 5 ext{ ones} = 70 + 5 = 75$
1.4 Comparing and Making Numbers

Comparing two-digit numbers and understanding their relative sizes.

Comparing Tens and Ones

To compare two-digit numbers, look at the tens place first:

  • Comparing 43 and 34: $43$ has $4$ tens, while $34$ has only $3$ tens. Since $4 ext{ tens} > 3 ext{ tens}$, $43$ is greater than $34$ ($43 > 34$).
  • Comparing numbers with the same tens digit (e.g., 43 and 47): Both have $4$ tens. Compare the ones place: $43$ has $3$ ones, and $47$ has $7$ ones. Since $7 > 3$, $47$ is greater than $43$ ($47 > 43$).

Play with Numbers: Who am I?

Riddles to test understanding of two-digit numbers:

  • Largest two-digit number: $99$
  • Largest two-digit number with no repeated digits: $98$
  • Smallest two-digit number: $10$
  • Smallest two-digit number with repeated digits: $11$
  • Smallest two-digit number with 3 at the tens place: $30$
  • Largest two-digit number with 2 at the ones place: $92$
1.5 Ordinal Numbers and Calendars

Understanding order, position, and reading dates on a calendar.

Vallam Kali (Snake Boat Race)

Using the context of the famous snake boat race in Kerala to learn ordinal numbers (first, second, third, etc.) to describe positions of boats approaching the finish line.

Reading a Calendar

Using a calendar (e.g., August 2023) to identify specific days and dates:

  • Independence Day: August 15th
  • Onam: August 29th
  • Raksha Bandhan: August 30th

Numbers as Labels

Understanding that numbers are also used as labels or names around us, such as jersey numbers, house numbers, bus route numbers, and train numbers.
