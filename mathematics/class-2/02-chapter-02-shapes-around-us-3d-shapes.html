Chapter 2: Shapes Around Us

2.1 Introduction to Shapes Around Us

We observe various shapes in our daily lives. During festivals, celebrations, and daily activities, different objects around us exhibit distinct geometric characteristics.

Identifying Shapes in Musical Instruments

In a musical gathering, we can identify shapes based on common instruments:

  • Cylinder: Similar in shape to a drum (dholak or tabla).
  • Sphere: Similar in shape to a matka (clay pot) or round sweets like laddu.
  • Cone: Similar in shape to a shehnai or a party hat.
  • Cuboid: Similar in shape to a harmonium or a gift box.
2.2 Solid Shapes and Their Properties

Solid objects have three-dimensional structures characterized by faces, edges, and corners.

Faces, Edges, and Corners

Using a standard rectangular gift box as an example, we can define these core components:

  • Faces: The flat surfaces of the solid object. A standard box or cube has $6$ faces.
  • Edges (Sides): The line segments where two faces meet. A standard box or cube has $12$ edges.
  • Corners (Vertices): The points where three or more edges meet. A standard box or cube has $8$ corners.

Properties of Common 3D Shapes

Different solid shapes have varying numbers of faces, edges, and corners:

  • Cube (e.g., playing dice): $6$ flat faces, $12$ straight edges, $8$ corners.
  • Cuboid (e.g., eraser, brick, crayon box): $6$ flat faces, $12$ straight edges, $8$ corners.
  • Cone (e.g., ice cream cone, carrot): $2$ faces ($1$ flat circular face and $1$ curved face), $1$ curved edge, $1$ corner (apex).
  • Cylinder (e.g., pipe, water bottle, marker): $3$ faces ($2$ flat circular faces and $1$ curved face), $2$ curved edges, $0$ corners.
  • Sphere (e.g., ball, globe, orange): $1$ curved face, $0$ edges, $0$ corners.
2.3 Interactive Activities and Games

Practical exercises help in understanding the tactile and visual properties of shapes.

Touch and Tell Game

A game where a blindfolded child picks an object from a collection and identifies it based on descriptions of its faces, edges, and corners (e.g., "Find an object with no corners" or "Find an object with only one corner").

Classifying Objects by Corners

  • No Corners: Spherical and cylindrical objects like balls, marbles, pipes, and bottles.
  • One Corner: Conical objects like funnels, conical tents, and ice cream cones.
  • Multiple Corners: Cubes and cuboids like books, wooden blocks, and boxes.

Project Work

Try creating physical models using cardboard sheets, clay, or dough:

  • Make an object with four faces (such as a triangular pyramid).
  • Make an object with only one face (such as a sphere).
