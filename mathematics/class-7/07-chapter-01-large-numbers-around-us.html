Chapter 1: LARGE NUMBERS AROUND US

1.1 A Lakh Varieties!

Eshwarappa, a farmer in Chintamani, Karnataka, visits the market for rice seeds. He overhears a conversation about the decline in rice varieties. Ramanna states that earlier there were about one lakh varieties of rice, but now only a handful remain. Lakshmamma mentions a seed bank near her house with about a hundred indigenous varieties.

The concept of a lakh is introduced. One lakh is $1,00,000$.

Estu, Eshwarappa's son, is surprised by the number of rice varieties, wondering if one could taste all of them in a lifetime.

Understanding Lakh

The chapter explores the magnitude of a lakh by observing number patterns:

  • The largest 3-digit number is $999$. Adding $1$ gives the smallest 4-digit number, $1000$.
  • The largest 4-digit number is $9999$. Adding $1$ gives the smallest 5-digit number, $10000$.
  • The largest 5-digit number is $99999$. Adding $1$ gives the smallest 6-digit number, $1,00,000$.

Roxie and Estu discuss how many rice varieties could be tasted in a lifetime. If one variety is tasted per day, it's far from a lakh in 100 years ($365 imes 100 = 36,500$). They consider if tasting 2 or 3 varieties a day would make a difference.

Getting a Feel of Large Numbers

Large numbers are often better understood by comparison to familiar objects or quantities.

  • The Statue of Unity in Gujarat is about $180$ metres tall.
  • The Kunchikal waterfall in Karnataka drops from about $450$ metres.

An example is given with Somu, who is $1$ metre tall. If each floor of a building is about four times his height ($4$ metres), a $10$-floor building would be $40$ metres tall.

Questions are posed to compare the heights of the Statue of Unity and the Kunchikal waterfall to Somu's building.

Is One Lakh a Very Large Number?

Roxie and Estu have different perspectives on whether a lakh is a large number:

  • Roxie's view (large):
    • One lakh varieties of rice is a lot.
    • Living one lakh days means living for $274$ years.
    • One lakh people standing shoulder to shoulder could stretch $38$ kilometres.
  • Estu's view (not that big):
    • The cricket stadium in Ahmedabad has a seating capacity of more than one lakh.
    • Most humans have $80,000$ to $1,20,000$ hairs, so one lakh hairs fit in a tiny space.
    • Some female fish can lay almost one lakh eggs at once.
Reading and Writing Numbers

The Indian place value system uses commas to help read large numbers. Commas are placed after the thousands place and then after every two digits (e.g., $1,00,000$ for one lakh).

  • The number $12,78,830$ is read as twelve lakh seventy eight thousand eight hundred thirty.
  • The number $15,75,000$ is read as fifteen lakh seventy five thousand.

Exercises are provided to write numbers in words.

1.2 Land of Tens

This section introduces special calculators with specific buttons to understand place values and large numbers.

The Thoughtful Thousands

This calculator has only a $+1000$ button. Exercises involve determining how many times the button must be pressed to reach numbers like $3000$, $10,000$, $53,000$, $90,000$, and One Lakh.

The Tedious Tens

This calculator has only a $+10$ button. Exercises involve determining how many times the button must be pressed to reach numbers like $500$, $780$, $1000$, $3700$, $10,000$, and One Lakh.

The Handy Hundreds

This calculator has only a $+100$ button. Exercises involve determining how many times the button must be pressed to reach numbers like $400$, $3700$, $10,000$, $53,000$, $90,000$, $97,600$, and One Lakh.

Questions also explore how many hundreds are needed to make ten thousand or one lakh.

Creative Chitti

This calculator has multiple buttons: $+1, +10, +100, +1000, +10000, +100000, +1000000$. It can achieve numbers in multiple ways. For example, to get $321$, it can press $+10$ thirty-two times and $+1$ once, or $+100$ two times, $+10$ twelve times, and $+1$ once.

Exercises involve finding different ways to obtain numbers like $5072$, $8300$, $40629$, $56354$, $66666$, and $367813$ using button clicks and writing expressions for them.

Systematic Sippy

This calculator aims to use buttons as minimally as possible. It has buttons: $+1, +10, +100, +1000, +10000, +100000$.

Exercises involve finding the minimum number of button clicks to obtain numbers like $5072$ and $8300$. The smallest number of button clicks for a number corresponds to the sum of its digits in its Indian place value notation (e.g., $5072 = 5 imes 1000 + 0 imes 100 + 7 imes 10 + 2 imes 1$, so $5+0+7+2 = 14$ clicks).

1.3 Of Crores and Crores!

The chapter introduces even larger numbers like crore and arab.

  • $1$ crore is $100$ lakhs, written as $1,00,00,000$ ($1$ followed by seven zeroes).

A table compares the Indian System and the American System (also called the International System) for naming numerals and placing commas:

  • Indian System: Commas are placed in a $3-2-2-2...$ pattern from right to left (thousands, lakhs, crores, arabs).
  • American System: Commas are placed in a $3-3-3-3...$ pattern from right to left (thousands, millions, billions).

The Indian system is followed in several South Asian countries. The words lakh and crore originate from Sanskrit words lakṣha (लक्ष) and koṭi (कोटि).

Key relationships:

  • $1$ lakh = $1$ followed by $5$ zeroes ($1,00,000$).
  • $1$ crore = $1$ followed by $7$ zeroes ($1,00,00,000$).
  • $1$ million = $1$ followed by $6$ zeroes ($1,000,000$), which is ten lakhs.
  • $1$ arab = $1$ followed by $9$ zeroes ($1,000,000,000$), which is $100$ crores or $1$ billion.

Exercises involve reading and writing numbers in both systems and comparing them.

1.4 Exact and Approximate Values

Often, exact numbers are not required, and an approximation is sufficient to give an idea of quantity.

  • For example, the population of Chintamani town was $76,068$ in $2011$, but saying it's about $75,000$ is often enough.

There are situations where it's better to round up (approximated number is more than actual), like ordering $750$ sweets for $732$ people.

There are situations where it's better to round down (approximated number is less than actual), like saying an item costing $₹470$ is around $₹450$.

Students are encouraged to think about situations where rounding up, rounding down, either is okay, or exact numbers are needed.

Nearest Neighbours

For large numbers, it's useful to know their nearest thousand, lakh, or crore. This involves rounding numbers to different place values.

Example: The nearest neighbours of $6,72,85,183$ are:

  • Nearest thousand: $6,72,85,000$
  • Nearest ten thousand: $6,72,90,000$
  • Nearest lakh: $6,73,00,000$
  • Nearest ten lakh: $6,70,00,000$
  • Nearest crore: $7,00,00,000$

Exercises involve finding nearest neighbours for given numbers and estimating sums and differences, then evaluating the accuracy of these estimates.

Populations of Cities

A table provides the populations of various Indian cities in $2011$ and $2001$. Questions based on this data require students to make observations and calculations by approximation, such as population increase and cities whose populations have almost doubled.

1.5 Patterns in Products

This section explores multiplication shortcuts and patterns.

A Multiplication Shortcut

Roxie's method for $116 imes 5$: $116 imes 5 = 116 imes rac{10}{2} = 58 imes 10 = 580$.

Estu's method for $824 imes 25$: $824 imes 25 = 824 imes rac{100}{4} = 206 imes 100 = 20600$.

The concept is to multiply by powers of $10$ and then divide, simplifying calculations.

Exercises involve finding quick ways to calculate products like $2 imes 1768 imes 50$ and $72 imes 125$.

How Long is the Product?

This sub-section presents multiplication problems that produce interesting patterns, such as $11 imes 11 = 121$, $111 imes 111 = 12321$, etc. Students are asked to evaluate these and extend the patterns.

Questions also explore the number of digits in products of numbers with a certain number of digits (e.g., two 2-digit numbers can result in a 3- or 4-digit number).

Fascinating Facts about Large Numbers

This section presents interesting facts involving large numbers, requiring students to perform calculations (multiplication or division) to uncover the facts and then read the numbers in both Indian and American naming systems.

  • The product of $1250 imes 380$ reveals the number of kīrtanas composed by Purandaradāsa.
  • The product of $2100 imes 70,000$ gives the approximate distance in kilometers between the Earth and the Sun.
  • The product of $6400 imes 62,500$ gives the average number of litres of water the Amazon river discharges into the Atlantic Ocean every second.
  • The quotient of $13,95,000 imes 150$ gives the distance (in kms) of the longest single-train journey in the world (Moscow to Vladivostok).
  • The quotient of $10,50,00,000 imes 700$ gives the weight of an adult blue whale in kilograms.
  • The quotient of $52,00,00,00,000 imes 130$ gives the weight, in tonnes, of global plastic waste generated in $2021$.
  • A large number fact about bacteria and fungi in a single gram of healthy soil is also provided.
1.6 Did You Ever Wonder...?

This section encourages students to think critically about large numbers and make estimations based on real-world scenarios.

  • Mumbai Population in Buses: Estu wonders if the entire population of Mumbai could fit into one lakh buses. Assuming a bus accommodates $50$ people, one lakh buses can accommodate $1,00,000 imes 50 = 50,00,000$ ($50$ lakh) people. Since Mumbai's population is more than $1$ crore $24$ lakhs, it cannot fit.
  • RMS Titanic Capacity: Can Mumbai's population fit into $5000$ RMS Titanic ships (each carrying about $2500$ passengers)?
  • Travel to Moon/Sun: Roxie wonders if she could reach the Moon ($3,84,400$ km) in $10$ years by traveling $100$ km/day. Students are asked to calculate this and also if one could reach the Sun by traveling $1000$ km/day.
  • Reasonable Assumptions: Questions involve making assumptions to answer:
    • Lifting one lakh sheets of paper (each $5$ grams).
    • Whether a million babies are born in a day if $250$ babies are born every minute.
    • Counting one million coins in a day (assuming $1$ coin per second).
  • Figure it Out: Exercises include creating a $10$-digit number using digits $0-9$ exactly once, finding a $7$-digit number name with maximum letters, and other number puzzles.
SUMMARY
  • The chapter covered large numbers like lakhs, crores, and arabs, and their international equivalents (millions, billions).
  • It explained how to read and write these numbers in both the Indian and American/International naming systems.
    • $1$ lakh is $1$ followed by $5$ zeroes ($1,00,000$).
    • $1$ crore is $1$ followed by $7$ zeroes ($1,00,00,000$).
    • $1$ million is $1$ followed by $6$ zeroes ($1,000,000$), which is also ten lakhs.
    • $1$ arab is $1$ followed by $9$ zeroes ($1,000,000,000$), which is also $100$ crores or $1$ billion.
  • The importance of rounding up or rounding down large numbers for approximation was discussed.
  • Methods to get a sense of large numbers by comparing them to more familiar quantities were explored.
  • Techniques for factorising numbers and regrouping to simplify multiplications were introduced.
  • Various thought experiments were conducted to apply understanding of large numbers to real-world scenarios.
