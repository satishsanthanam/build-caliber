Chapter 4: Another Peek Beyond the Point

4.1 A Quick Recap of Decimals

Decimals are the natural extension of the place value system to represent decimal fractions like $\frac{1}{10}$, $\frac{1}{100}$, $\frac{1}{1000}$, and so on, and their sums.

For example, the number $27.53$ refers to a quantity that has:

  • 2 Tens ($2 \times 10$)
  • 7 Units/Ones ($7 \times 1$)
  • 5 Tenths ($\frac{5}{10}$)
  • 3 Hundredths ($\frac{3}{100}$)

Rule for Dividing by Powers of 10

To divide any number by a number of the form 1 followed by zeroes (10, 100, 1000, etc.):

  • Step 1: Write the dividend as it is and place a decimal point at the end.
  • Step 2: Count the number of zeroes in the divisor.
  • Step 3: Move the decimal point to the left by the same number of places as the count of zeroes. Add zeroes in front if needed.

Examples: $24 \div 100 = 0.24$, $12 \div 1000 = 0.012$, $12345 \div 1000 = 12.345$.

4.2 Decimal Multiplication

Multiplication of decimals is conceptually the same as the multiplication of their corresponding fractions.

Rule for Multiplying Two Decimals

  • Multiply the two numbers as if they were counting numbers (by removing the decimal points).
  • Count the total number of decimal places (digits after the decimal point) in both the multiplier and the multiplicand.
  • Place the decimal point in the product so that it has the same number of decimal places as the sum calculated above.

Example: To evaluate $5.96 \times 24.8$:

  • Multiply $596 \times 248 = 147808$.
  • $5.96$ has 2 decimal places, and $24.8$ has 1 decimal place. Total = 3 decimal places.
  • The product is $147.808$.

Relationship Between Product and Multiplied Numbers

  • Situation 1: Both numbers are greater than 1 (e.g., $3.4 \times 6.5$). The product ($22.1$) is greater than both numbers.
  • Situation 2: Both numbers are between 0 and 1 (e.g., $0.75 \times 0.4$). The product ($0.3$) is less than both numbers.
  • Situation 3: One number is between 0 and 1 and the other is greater than 1 (e.g., $0.75 \times 5$). The product ($3.75$) is less than the larger number and greater than the smaller number.
4.3 Decimal Division

Division by Powers of 10

When we divide a decimal by 10, 100, 1000, etc., we move the decimal point to the left by as many places as there are zeroes in the divisor.

Example: $18.7 \div 100 = 0.187$, $0.13 \div 10 = 0.013$.

Division Using Place Value (Long Division)

The procedure of division using place value can be extended to find quotients with decimal values by regrouping remainders into smaller place values (Ones to Tenths, Tenths to Hundredths, etc.).

Important Rule: When we regroup Ones into Tenths, we must place a decimal point in the quotient.

Example: $237 \div 8 = 29.625$

Division with a Decimal Divisor

When the divisor is a decimal, we can convert it into a counting number by multiplying both the dividend and the divisor by a suitable power of 10 (10, 100, 1000, etc.).

Example: $$\frac{4.68}{0.13} = \frac{4.68 \times 100}{0.13 \times 100} = \frac{468}{13} = 36$$

Infinite and Recurring Decimals

Some decimal divisions never end because a non-zero remainder keeps repeating in a cycle.

  • $10 \div 3 = 3.333...$
  • $1 \div 7 = 0.142857142857...$ (The digits $142857$ repeat in a cycle).
4.4 Look Before You Leap! (The Math of Leap Years)

The Earth takes approximately $365.2422$ days to complete one revolution around the Sun.

The Leap Year Rules

  • Rule 1: Every 4th year is a leap year (adds 1 extra day, making 366 days) to compensate for the $0.2422$ extra days per year.
  • Rule 2: To avoid overcompensation, century years (divisible by 100) are NOT leap years, unless they are also divisible by 400.

This calendar system keeps our calendar year aligned with the astronomical year with an error of only about 0.2 days every 1000 years.
