Chapter 7: Fractions

Introduction to Fractions

When a whole number of things are shared equally among a certain number of people, fractions tell us how much each share is.

  • Sharing 1 roti between 2 children: Each gets half, written as $\frac{1}{2}$ (read as "one half" or "one upon two").
  • Sharing 1 roti among 4 children: Each gets a quarter, written as $\frac{1}{4}$.
  • Since more children share the same roti in the second case, each gets a smaller share: $\frac{1}{2} > \frac{1}{4}$.
7.1 Fractional Units and Equal Shares

When one unit is divided into several equal parts, each part is called a fractional unit or unit fraction. Examples include $\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{5}, \dots$

Comparing Unit Fractions

As the denominator increases, the value of the unit fraction decreases because the same whole is shared among more parts:

  • $\frac{1}{5} > \frac{1}{9}$
  • $\frac{1}{100} > \frac{1}{200}$
7.2 Fractional Units as Parts of a Whole

We can measure parts of a whole using fractional units. For example, if a whole chikki is divided into 4 equal parts, each part is $\frac{1}{4}$. A piece consisting of 3 such parts is measured as $\frac{3}{4}$ of the chikki.

7.3 Measuring Using Fractional Units

By folding paper strips, we can visualize and collect fractional units to measure quantities:

  • $2 \text{ times } \frac{1}{4} = \frac{2}{4}$
  • $3 \text{ times } \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}$
  • $4 \text{ times } \frac{1}{4} = \frac{4}{4} = 1$

In any fraction $\frac{a}{b}$:

  • The top number $a$ is the numerator (representing how many fractional units are collected).
  • The bottom number $b$ is the denominator (representing the fractional unit $\frac{1}{b}$).
7.4 Marking Fraction Lengths on the Number Line

Fractions can be represented as points on a number line by dividing the unit distance between integers into equal parts.

  • To mark $\frac{1}{3}$, divide the interval from $0$ to $1$ into 3 equal parts. The first mark represents $\frac{1}{3}$, the second represents $\frac{2}{3}$.
  • There are an uncountable number of fractions lying between $0$ and $1$.
7.5 Mixed Fractions

Fractions can be classified based on their relationship to 1 unit:

  • Fractions less than 1: The numerator is smaller than the denominator (e.g., $\frac{1}{2}, \frac{2}{3}$).
  • Fractions greater than 1: The numerator is larger than the denominator (e.g., $\frac{3}{2}, \frac{5}{2}$).

Mixed Numbers

A mixed number (or mixed fraction) contains a whole number part and a fractional part less than 1.

  • Example: $\frac{8}{3} = 2 + \frac{2}{3} = 2\frac{2}{3}$ (read as "two and two thirds").
  • Converting mixed numbers to regular fractions: $3\frac{1}{4} = 3 + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{12}{4} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{15}{4}$.
7.6 Equivalent Fractions

Fractions that represent the same value, length, or share are called equivalent fractions.

  • Example: $\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{4}{8}$

Simplest Form (Lowest Terms)

A fraction is in its lowest terms or simplest form if its numerator and denominator have no common factor other than 1.

  • To simplify a fraction, divide both the numerator and denominator by their highest common factor (HCF).
  • Example: $\frac{16}{20} = \frac{16 \div 4}{20 \div 4} = \frac{4}{5}$.
7.7 Comparing Fractions

To compare fractions with different denominators, convert them into equivalent fractions with a common denominator:

  1. Find a common multiple of the denominators.
  2. Convert each fraction to an equivalent fraction with this common denominator.
  3. Compare the numerators.

Example: To compare $\frac{4}{5}$ and $\frac{7}{9}$, use the common denominator $45$:

  • $\frac{4}{5} = \frac{4 \times 9}{5 \times 9} = \frac{36}{45}$
  • $\frac{7}{9} = \frac{7 \times 5}{9 \times 5} = \frac{35}{45}$
  • Since $36 > 35$, we have $\frac{4}{5} > \frac{7}{9}$.
7.8 Addition and Subtraction of Fractions

With the Same Denominator

Just add or subtract the numerators while keeping the denominator the same:

  • $\frac{2}{5} + \frac{1}{5} = \frac{2+1}{5} = \frac{3}{5}$
  • $\frac{6}{7} - \frac{4}{7} = \frac{6-4}{7} = \frac{2}{7}$

With Different Denominators (Brahmagupta's Method)

To add or subtract fractions with different denominators:

  1. Find equivalent fractions with a common denominator.
  2. Add or subtract the numerators.
  3. Simplify the result to lowest terms if needed.

Example: $\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{3}{12} + \frac{4}{12} = \frac{7}{12}$.

7.9 A Pinch of History

Fractions have a rich history originating in ancient India:

  • In Sanskrit, a fraction was called bhinna (meaning 'broken'), bhaga, or ansha (meaning 'part' or 'piece').
  • The modern notation of writing fractions (numerator over denominator) originated in Indian texts like the Bakshali manuscript (c. 300 CE).
  • The formal rules for adding and subtracting fractions were codified by Brahmagupta in his treatise Brahmasphuṭasiddhānta (628 CE).
Fraction Puzzles

Egyptian Fractions: Ancient Egyptians expressed general fractions as sums of unique unit fractions.

  • Writing 1 as the sum of three different unit fractions: $\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{6} = 1$.
