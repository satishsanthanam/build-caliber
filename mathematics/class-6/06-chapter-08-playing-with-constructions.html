Class 6 — Chapter 8: Playing with Constructions

8.1 Artwork

Observe and draw figures freehand, then explore drawing them precisely using a ruler and a compass.

Understanding Curves and Circles

A curve is any shape that can be drawn on paper with a pencil, including straight lines, circles, and other freeform figures.

To draw a circle of a specific radius (e.g., $4\text{ cm}$) around a point $P$:

  • Mark a point $P$ as the centre.
  • Use a ruler to open the compass to a distance of $4\text{ cm}$ between the tip and the pencil.
  • Keep the tip fixed at $P$ and rotate the pencil to draw the full curve.
  • The distance from the centre $P$ to any point on the circle is the radius.

Creative Constructions

  • A Person: A figure composed of a circle (head) and a body with curved lines. The challenge lies in finding where to place the compass tip and what radius to use for the curved neck.
  • Wavy Wave: Constructing waves on a central line $AB = 8\text{ cm}$ using half circles. For a wave of length $8\text{ cm}$, the radius of each half-circle wave is $2\text{ cm}$, and the segment length $AX = 4\text{ cm}$.
  • Eyes: Constructing symmetrical eye shapes using intersecting arcs.
8.2 Squares and Rectangles

Basic geometric figures with straight lines in their boundaries.

Properties of Rectangles and Squares

For a rectangle (e.g., $ABCD$):

  • R1: The opposite sides are equal in length ($AB = CD$ and $AD = BC$).
  • R2: All angles are right angles ($90^\circ$).

For a square:

  • S1: All four sides are equal in length ($AB = BC = CD = DA$).
  • S2: All angles are right angles ($90^\circ$).

Naming Conventions

In a valid name for a square or rectangle, the corners must be listed in the order of travel around the boundary, starting from any corner (e.g., $ABCD$, $BCDA$, $ADCB$). Non-consecutive naming like $ABDC$ or $ACBD$ is invalid.

Rotated Squares and Rectangles

Rotating a square or rectangle does not change its side lengths or angles. A rotated square is still a square, and a rotated rectangle is still a rectangle.

8.3 Constructing Squares and Rectangles

Step-by-step construction of a square $PQRS$ with side length $6\text{ cm}$:

  • Step 1: Draw a line segment $PQ = 6\text{ cm}$ using a ruler.
  • Step 2: Use a protractor to mark a perpendicular ($90^\circ$) line through $P$.
  • Step 3: Mark point $S$ on this perpendicular such that $PS = 6\text{ cm}$ (using a ruler or compass).
  • Step 4: Draw a perpendicular to $PQ$ through $Q$.
  • Step 5: Mark point $R$ on this perpendicular such that $QR = 6\text{ cm}$.
  • Step 6: Join $R$ and $S$ to complete the square $PQRS$.
8.4 An Exploration in Rectangles

Moving Points on a Rectangle

Consider a rectangle $ABCD$ with $AB = 7\text{ cm}$ and $BC = 4\text{ cm}$. Let $X$ be a point on $AD$ and $Y$ be a point on $BC$. By varying the positions of $X$ and $Y$, we can measure the changing length of $XY$:

  • When $X$ is at $A$ and $Y$ is at $B$, $XY = AB = 7\text{ cm}$ (minimum distance).
  • When $X$ and $Y$ are at equal distances from $A$ and $B$ respectively, $ABYX$ forms a rectangle, and $XY = AB = 7\text{ cm}$.
  • The maximum distance between $X$ and $Y$ occurs when they are at opposite corners (diagonals $AC$ or $BD$).

Breaking Rectangles

Constructing a rectangle that can be divided into identical squares:

  • To divide a rectangle into $2$ identical squares of side $a$, the dimensions of the rectangle must be $a \times 2a$.
  • To divide a rectangle into $3$ identical squares of side $a$, the dimensions must be $a \times 3a$.
  • A compass can be used to transfer the length of one side multiple times along a perpendicular line to mark out identical squares without a ruler.
8.5 Exploring Diagonals of Rectangles and Squares

The lines joining opposite corners of a rectangle or square are called diagonals (e.g., $PR$ and $QS$ in rectangle $PQRS$).

Properties of Diagonals

  • The diagonals of a rectangle are equal in length ($PR = QS$).
  • A diagonal divides each opposite angle into two smaller angles (e.g., $\angle P$ is divided into $c$ and $d$; $\angle R$ is divided into $g$ and $h$). In a general rectangle, $c \neq d$ and $g \neq h$.
  • In a square, the diagonal divides the opposite angles into equal parts ($45^\circ$ each).

Construction with Diagonals and Angles

Example 1: Construct a rectangle where a diagonal divides the opposite angles into $60^\circ$ and $30^\circ$:

  • Draw base $AB$ of arbitrary length.
  • Draw a perpendicular at $B$.
  • Draw a line from $A$ at an angle of $60^\circ$ to $AB$. The intersection of this line with the perpendicular from $B$ gives point $C$.
  • Draw a perpendicular to $BC$ at $C$ (or use a compass with radius $AD = BC$ from $A$) to locate point $D$. Join $CD$ to complete the rectangle $ABCD$.

Example 2: Construct a rectangle with one side $5\text{ cm}$ and diagonal $7\text{ cm}$:

  • Draw base $CD = 5\text{ cm}$.
  • Draw a perpendicular line $l$ to $CD$ at point $C$.
  • Set the compass to a radius of $7\text{ cm}$. With $D$ as the centre, draw an arc intersecting line $l$ at point $B$.
  • Construct perpendiculars to $DC$ and $BC$ through $D$ and $B$ respectively to find the intersection point $A$, completing rectangle $ABCD$.
8.6 Points Equidistant from Two Given Points

The House Construction Problem

To construct a house shape where all border lines are of length $5\text{ cm}$:

  • Step 1: Draw the base $DE = 5\text{ cm}$, and perpendicular sides $DB = 5\text{ cm}$ and $EC = 5\text{ cm}$.
  • Step 2: To locate the roof peak $A$ which must be exactly $5\text{ cm}$ from both $B$ and $C$:
    • Set the compass to a radius of $5\text{ cm}$.
    • With $B$ as the centre, draw an arc.
    • With $C$ as the centre, draw another arc.
    • The intersection of these two arcs is the point $A$.
  • Step 3: Join $AB$ and $AC$ with straight lines.
  • Step 4: Draw the curved ceiling arc touching $B$ and $C$ by placing the compass tip at $A$ with a radius of $5\text{ cm}$.
Chapter Summary
  • All points on a circle are equidistant from its centre; this distance is the radius.
  • A compass is used to construct circles, arcs, and transfer lengths.
  • A rough diagram is essential for planning geometric constructions.
  • A rectangle can be uniquely constructed if the lengths of its sides, or one side and a diagonal, are given.
