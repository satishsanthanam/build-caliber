Class 6 — Chapter 2: Lines and Angles

2.1 Point

A point determines a precise location. It is the most basic building block of geometry.

  • It has no length, breadth, or height.
  • It is represented by a tiny dot on paper.
  • It is denoted by a single capital letter, such as $P$, $Z$, or $T$.
2.2 Line Segment

A line segment corresponds to the shortest path connecting two distinct points.

  • It has a fixed length and two distinct endpoints.
  • The line segment joining points $A$ and $B$ is denoted by $\overline{AB}$ or $\overline{BA}$.
  • The points $A$ and $B$ are called the end points of the segment.
2.3 Line

A line is obtained when a line segment is extended indefinitely in both directions.

  • It has no endpoints and extends forever.
  • A line passing through two points $A$ and $B$ is written as $\overleftrightarrow{AB}$.
  • It can also be denoted by a single lowercase letter, such as $l$ or $m$.
  • Key Property: Any two distinct points determine a unique line that passes through them.
2.4 Ray

A ray is a portion of a line that starts at one point and goes on endlessly in a single direction.

  • The starting point is called the initial point or starting point.
  • A ray starting at point $A$ and passing through point $P$ is denoted by $\overrightarrow{AP}$.
  • Real-world models include a beam of light from a lighthouse, a ray of light from a torch, or sun rays.
2.5 Angle

An angle is formed by two rays that share a common starting point.

Components of an Angle
  • Vertex: The common starting point of the two rays (e.g., point $B$ in Fig. 2.8).
  • Arms: The two rays that form the angle (e.g., rays $\overrightarrow{BD}$ and $\overrightarrow{BE}$).

Naming and Notation

An angle formed by rays $\overrightarrow{BD}$ and $\overrightarrow{BE}$ can be named:

  • Using the vertex: $\text{Angle } B$
  • Using three points: $\angle DBE$ or $\angle EBD$ (the vertex letter must always be in the middle).

Size of an Angle

The size of an angle is the amount of rotation or turn needed about the vertex to move the first ray (initial position) to the second ray (final position).

2.6 Comparing Angles

Angles can be compared using different methods to determine which one is larger (has a greater amount of rotation).

  • Superimposition: Placing one angle over another so that their vertices overlap and one arm of each angle lies along the same line. This reveals which angle is smaller or larger.
  • Equal Angles: Two angles are equal in size if their vertices match and both pairs of arms overlap perfectly when superimposed. This indicates an equal amount of rotation.
  • Comparison using a Circle: Placing a transparent circle over an angle with its center at the vertex, marking the points where the arms intersect the circle, and comparing the arc length to another angle.
2.7 & 2.8 Special Types of Angles

Angles are classified based on their rotation and relationship to straight lines.

  • Straight Angle: An angle formed by a half turn of a ray, where the two arms lie in a straight line. It measures $180^\circ$.
  • Right Angle: An angle that is exactly half of a straight angle (a quarter turn). It measures $90^\circ$ and resembles the shape of an 'L'.
  • Perpendicular Lines: Two lines that meet or intersect at right angles ($90^\circ$).
  • Angle Bisector: A line or ray that divides a given angle into two equal halves. The process of dividing an angle equally is called bisecting.
2.9 Measuring Angles

To assign precise numerical values to angles, mathematicians divide a full turn into $360$ equal parts.

  • Each unit part is called 1 degree, written as $1^\circ$.
  • Full Turn: Measures $360^\circ$.
  • Straight Angle: Measures $180^\circ$.
  • Right Angle: Measures $90^\circ$.
  • Protractor: A geometric tool used to measure angles in degrees, typically divided into $180$ or $360$ equal parts. Labelled protractors feature two scales: one increasing from right to left, and the other from left to right.
2.10 Drawing Angles

To draw an angle of a specific measure (e.g., $\angle TIN = 30^\circ$) using a protractor:

  1. Draw a base ray, such as $\overrightarrow{IN}$.
  2. Place the center point of the protractor on the vertex $I$ and align the ray $\overrightarrow{IN}$ with the $0^\circ$ line.
  3. Starting from $0^\circ$, count the degrees up to the desired measure ($30^\circ$) on the scale and mark a point $T$.
  4. Remove the protractor and use a ruler to join point $I$ to point $T$. The resulting angle is $\angle TIN = 30^\circ$.
2.11 Classification of Angles by Measure

Angles are classified into distinct categories based on their degree measures:

  • Acute Angle: An angle that is greater than $0^\circ$ and less than $90^\circ$ (smaller than a right angle).
  • Right Angle: An angle that measures exactly $90^\circ$.
  • Obtuse Angle: An angle that is greater than $90^\circ$ and less than $180^\circ$ (between a right angle and a straight angle).
  • Straight Angle: An angle that measures exactly $180^\circ$.
  • Reflex Angle: An angle that is greater than $180^\circ$ and less than $360^\circ$ (greater than a straight angle but less than a full turn).
