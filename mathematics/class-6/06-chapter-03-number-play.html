Class 6 — Chapter 3: Number Play

Numbers are used in different contexts and in many different ways to organise our lives. In this chapter, we play with numbers, notice patterns, and learn to use numbers and operations in new ways.

3.1 Numbers can Tell us Things

Numbers can represent specific arrangements or relationships. In a line of children of different heights, each child says a number based on their neighbors' heights:

  • A child says 1 if there is only one taller child standing next to them.
  • A child says 2 if both children standing next to them are taller.
  • A child says 0 if neither of the children standing next to them are taller.

This represents the number of taller neighbours each person has.

3.2 Supercells

A cell in a grid or table is called a supercell if the number in it is larger than its adjacent cells (left, right, top, and bottom where applicable).

  • In a single row, a cell is a supercell if it is larger than its immediate left and right neighbors. End cells only have one neighbor to compare with.
  • In a 2D grid, a cell is a supercell if it is greater than all its immediate horizontal and vertical neighbors.
3.3 Patterns of Numbers on the Number Line

Placing numbers on a number line helps visualize their relative sizes and intervals. By identifying known points (e.g., $2010$ and $2020$), we can determine the scale of the number line and label other positions accurately.

3.4 Playing with Digits

Digit Sums of Numbers

The digit sum of a number is the sum obtained by adding all its individual digits together.

For example, the digit sum of $68$ is $6 + 8 = 14$, which is the same as the digit sum of $176$ ($1 + 7 + 6 = 14$) and $545$ ($5 + 4 + 5 = 14$).

Digit Detectives

Analyzing how many times a specific digit appears within a range of numbers:

  • Between $1$ and $100$, the digit $7$ occurs $20$ times.
  • Between $1$ and $1000$, the digit $7$ occurs $300$ times.
3.5 Pretty Palindromic Patterns

A palindrome or palindromic number is a number that reads the same from left to right as it does from right to left (e.g., $66$, $848$, $121$, $1111$).

Reverse-and-Add Method

A process to generate palindromes from non-palindromic numbers:

  1. Start with any 2-digit number (e.g., $48$).
  2. Reverse its digits ($84$).
  3. Add the two numbers ($48 + 84 = 132$).
  4. If the sum is not a palindrome, repeat the process ($132 + 231 = 363$, which is a palindrome).
3.6 The Magic Number of Kaprekar

Discovered by mathematician D.R. Kaprekar, the number 6174 is known as the Kaprekar constant.

Kaprekar's Routine for 4-digit numbers:

  1. Choose any four-digit number with at least two different digits (e.g., $6382$).
  2. Arrange the digits in descending order to get the largest number, $A$ ($8632$).
  3. Arrange the digits in ascending order to get the smallest number, $B$ ($2368$).
  4. Subtract $B$ from $A$ to get $C = A - B$ ($8632 - 2368 = 6264$).
  5. Repeat the process with the digits of $C$. Eventually, you will always reach the constant $6174$, which repeats indefinitely ($7641 - 1467 = 6174$).

For 3-digit numbers, the routine always leads to the repeating number 495.

3.7 Clock and Calendar Numbers

Numbers on clocks and calendars exhibit unique patterns:

  • Clock Palindromes: Times like $10:10$, $12:21$, or $10:01$ read the same forward and backward.
  • Calendar Repetitions: A calendar year matches exactly with another year after a certain number of years (usually repeating every $6$ or $11$ years, or $5$ years depending on leap years).
3.8 Mental Math

Developing strategies for quick mental calculations using addition, subtraction, and digit operations to form target numbers under specific constraints.

3.9 Playing with Number Patterns

Finding quick ways to sum structured grids of numbers by identifying symmetry, grouping, or multiplication patterns instead of adding them one by one.

3.10 An Unsolved Mystery—the Collatz Conjecture!

The Collatz Conjecture is a famous unsolved mathematical problem proposed by Lothar Collatz in 1937. The rules are:

  • Start with any positive integer $n$.
  • If $n$ is even, divide it by $2$: $n \rightarrow \frac{n}{2}$.
  • If $n$ is odd, multiply it by $3$ and add $1$: $n \rightarrow 3n + 1$.
  • Repeat the process.

The conjecture states that no matter what number you start with, the sequence will always eventually reach $1$.

3.11 Simple Estimation

Estimation is the process of finding an approximate value that is close enough to the actual value for practical purposes, without performing exact counts or calculations.

3.12 Games and Winning Strategies

Mathematical games often have a deterministic winning strategy where a player can guarantee a win by reaching specific key numbers during their turn.

  • Game 21: Two players take turns adding $1$, $2$, or $3$ to a running total starting from $0$. The player who reaches $21$ wins.
  • Game 99: Players take turns adding any number from $1$ to $10$. The first to reach $99$ wins.
Summary
  • Numbers serve multiple purposes: conveying information, creating patterns, estimating magnitudes, and forming the basis of games and puzzles.
  • Formulating systematic procedures to solve number puzzles builds computational thinking.
  • Some simple-to-state number problems remain unsolved for decades, such as the Collatz Conjecture.
