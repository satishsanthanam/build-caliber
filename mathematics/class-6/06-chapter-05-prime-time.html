Chapter 5: Prime Time

5.1 Common Multiples and Common Factors

Idli-Vada Game & Common Multiples

The Idli-Vada Game is a circle game of numbers where players say:

  • 'idli' for multiples of 3 ($3, 6, 9, 12, \dots$)
  • 'vada' for multiples of 5 ($5, 10, 15, 20, \dots$)
  • 'idli-vada' for numbers that are multiples of both 3 and 5 ($15, 30, 45, \dots$)

Numbers that are multiples of both 3 and 5 are called common multiples. The first common multiple of 3 and 5 is $15$.

Jump Jackpot & Factors

In the Jump Jackpot game, jumping on a grid helps identify factors. For example, jump sizes of 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, and 24 will land exactly on 24. These numbers are called factors or divisors of 24.

The jump sizes that can reach two different numbers are their common factors. For example, the common factors of 14 and 36 are 1 and 2.

A perfect number is a number for which the sum of all its factors is equal to twice the number. For example, 28 is a perfect number because its factors are 1, 2, 4, 7, 14, and 28, and their sum is $56 = 2 \times 28$. The only perfect number between 1 and 10 is 6.

5.2 Prime Numbers

Numbers can be classified based on their factors:

  • Prime Numbers: Numbers that have only two factors—1 and the number itself. Examples include $2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, \dots$
  • Composite Numbers: Numbers that have more than two factors. Examples include $4, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 14, 15, \dots$
  • The number 1 has only one factor and is neither prime nor composite.

Sieve of Eratosthenes

The Sieve of Eratosthenes is a systematic method developed by the Greek mathematician Eratosthenes to find all prime numbers up to 100 by crossing out multiples of prime numbers starting from 2.

Twin Primes

Twin primes are pairs of prime numbers that have a difference of 2. Examples include $(3, 5)$, $(5, 7)$, $(11, 13)$, $(17, 19)$, $(29, 31)$, $(41, 43)$, $(59, 61)$, and $(71, 73)$.

5.3 Co-prime Numbers

Two numbers are said to be co-prime to each other if they have no common factor other than 1. For example, 4 and 9 are co-prime because their only common factor is 1, whereas 15 and 39 are not co-prime because they share a common factor of 3.

5.4 Prime Factorisation

Every composite number greater than 1 can be expressed uniquely as a product of prime numbers. This is called prime factorisation.

Methods and Uniqueness

For any number, there is only one unique prime factorisation, regardless of the order of the factors. For example, the prime factorisation of 36 is always $2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3$, which can be written as $2^2 \times 3^2$.

Applications of Prime Factorisation

  • Checking Co-primality: Two numbers are co-prime if they share no common prime factors in their prime factorisations. For example, $40 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 5$ and $231 = 3 \times 7 \times 11$ share no common prime factors, so they are co-prime.
  • Checking Divisibility: A number is divisible by another if the prime factorisation of the second number is completely included in the prime factorisation of the first number. For example, 168 ($2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 7$) is divisible by 12 ($2 \times 2 \times 3$).
5.5 Divisibility Tests

Divisibility tests allow us to check if a large number is divisible by another without performing long division:

  • Divisibility by 10: A number is divisible by 10 if its last digit is 0.
  • Divisibility by 5: A number is divisible by 5 if its last digit is either 0 or 5.
  • Divisibility by 2: A number is divisible by 2 if its last digit is even ($0, 2, 4, 6, 8$).
  • Divisibility by 4: A number is divisible by 4 if the number formed by its last two digits is divisible by 4.
  • Divisibility by 8: A number is divisible by 8 if the number formed by its last three digits is divisible by 8.
5.6 Fun with Numbers & Summary

Special Numbers & Prime Puzzles

Numbers can have unique properties (e.g., 9 is the only single-digit square in a set, 43 is the only prime). Prime puzzles involve filling grids with prime numbers such that the product of each row and column matches the given target numbers.

Chapter Summary

  • A number is a factor of another if it divides it exactly.
  • Prime numbers have exactly two factors; composite numbers have more than two.
  • Co-prime numbers share no common factors other than 1.
  • Prime factorisation represents a number uniquely as a product of primes.
