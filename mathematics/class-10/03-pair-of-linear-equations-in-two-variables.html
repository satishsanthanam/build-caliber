Chapter 3: Pair of Linear Equations in Two Variables

3.1 Introduction
  • A linear equation in two variables is of the form ax + by + c = 0, where a, b, and c are real numbers, and a and b are not both zero.
  • A pair of such equations is called a pair of linear equations in two variables.
3.2 Graphical Method of Solution
  • Consistent Pair: A pair of linear equations that has at least one solution.
  • Inconsistent Pair: A pair of linear equations that has no solution.
  • Dependent Pair: A pair of linear equations that are equivalent and have infinitely many solutions (always consistent).

Algebraic and Graphical Relationships

For a pair of linear equations: a₁x + b₁y + c₁ = 0 and a₂x + b₂y + c₂ = 0:

  • Intersecting Lines: If a₁/a₂ ≠ b₁/b₂, the lines intersect at a single point. There is exactly one unique solution (Consistent).
  • Coincident Lines: If a₁/a₂ = b₁/b₂ = c₁/c₂, the lines coincide. There are infinitely many solutions (Dependent/Consistent).
  • Parallel Lines: If a₁/a₂ = b₁/b₂ ≠ c₁/c₂, the lines are parallel. There is no solution (Inconsistent).
3.3 Algebraic Methods of Solving a Pair of Linear Equations

3.3.1 Substitution Method

  • Step 1: Express one variable (say y) in terms of the other variable (say x) from any one of the equations.
  • Step 2: Substitute this value of y in the other equation to reduce it to a linear equation in one variable x, and solve it.
  • Step 3: Substitute the value of x obtained in Step 2 into the equation from Step 1 to get the value of y.

3.3.2 Elimination Method

  • Step 1: Multiply both equations by suitable non-zero constants to make the coefficients of one variable (either x or y) numerically equal.
  • Step 2: Add or subtract one equation from the other so that one variable gets eliminated, leaving an equation in one variable.
  • Step 3: Solve this equation to find the value of the remaining variable.
  • Step 4: Substitute this value in either of the original equations to find the value of the other variable.
3.4 Summary
  • A pair of linear equations can be solved graphically or algebraically.
  • Algebraic methods include the Substitution Method and the Elimination Method.
  • The ratio comparison of coefficients determines the nature of solutions directly without plotting.
