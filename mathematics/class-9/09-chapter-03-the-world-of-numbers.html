Chapter 3: The World of Numbers

3.1 The Dawn of Mathematics: The Human Need to Count

3.1.1 A History Written in Bone

  • Early humans used tally marks carved into bone to keep count.
  • The Lebombo Bone (approx. 35,000 years old) from Africa features 29 notches, believed to be a lunar phase counter or menstrual calendar.

3.1.2 The Indian Context: Trade and Astronomy

  • The Ishango bone (approx. 20,000 BCE) from Congo shows groupings of prime numbers (11, 13, 17, 19) and multiplication by 2.
  • Ancient Indian civilizations (Indus Valley) used standardized weights and measures for trade.
  • Vedic texts named powers of 10 up to 1012 (parārdha) and 1053 (tallakshaņa).
  • The use of powers of 10 in the Rigveda set the stage for the modern number system.
  • This development paved the way for the concept of zero.
3.2 The Revolution of Śhūnya: When Nothing Became Something

3.2.1 From Philosophy to Mathematics: The Concept of Śhūnyatā

  • The philosophical concept of Śhūnyatā (emptiness or nothingness) in Indian traditions (Upanishads, Buddhist literature) laid the groundwork for zero.
  • The word shūnya means zero.

3.2.2 The Bakhśhālī Manuscript and Brahmagupta's Rules

  • The Bakhshālī Manuscript (early CE) used a bold dot (bindu) to represent zero.
  • Brahmagupta (628 CE) formally defined zero in his Brāhmasphuțasiddhānta as the result of subtracting a number from itself (a - a = 0).
  • Brahmagupta's Rules for Zero:
    • When zero is added to a number, the number remains unchanged: a + 0 = a.
    • When zero is subtracted from a number, the number remains unchanged: a - 0 = a.
    • When any number is multiplied by zero, the result is zero: a × 0 = 0.
3.3 Integers: Expanding the Horizon
  • Brahmagupta introduced the concept of Negative Numbers to address subtractions where a larger number is taken from a smaller one (e.g., 3 - 5).
  • He categorized numbers as Fortunes (Dhana) for positive numbers (assets) and Debts (Riņa) for negative numbers (liabilities).
  • The set of Integers (Z) includes positive natural numbers, their negative counterparts, and zero.

3.3.1 The Arithmetic of Integers

  • Brahmagupta's rules for integers:
    • A fortune plus a fortune is a fortune: 5 + 4 = 9.
    • A debt plus a debt is a debt: (-5) + (-4) = -9.
    • A fortune minus zero is a fortune, a debt minus zero is a debt: 7 - 0 = 7, and -6 - 0 = -6.
    • The product of a debt and a fortune is a debt: (-3) × 4 = -12.
    • The product of two debts is a fortune: (-3) × (-4) = 12.
3.4 Filling the Spaces: Fractions and Rational Numbers
  • Fractions represent parts of a whole.
  • The set of Rational Numbers (Q) combines all integers and fractions (positive and negative).
  • A rational number is defined as any number that can be expressed in the form p/q, where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0.
  • Observations about Rational Numbers:
    • They include natural numbers, whole numbers, and integers.
    • They do not have a unique representation (e.g., 1/3 = 2/6).
    • For unique representation, p and q are assumed to have no common factors other than 1.
  • Laws of Arithmetic for Rational Numbers:
    • Equality: a/b = c/d if ad = bc.
    • Addition/Subtraction: a/b + c/b = (a+c)/b; a/b - c/b = (a-c)/b.
    • Multiplication: (a/b) × (c/d) = (ac)/(bd).
    • Division: (a/b) ÷ (c/d) = (a/b) × (d/c) = (ad)/(bc).
    • They are commutative for addition and multiplication.
    • They follow the distributive law: p(q + r) = pq + pr.
    • They are closed under addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division (except by zero).

3.4.1 Representation of Rational Numbers on the Number Line

  • Rational numbers can be represented on a number line by dividing the unit interval into 'q' equal parts and moving 'p' parts.

3.4.2 The Density of Rational Numbers

  • Rational numbers are dense, meaning an infinite number of rational numbers exist between any two given rational numbers.
  • The average of two rational numbers (a+b)/2 is always a rational number between them.
3.5 Irrational Numbers
  • Some lengths, like the diagonal of a unit square (√2), cannot be expressed as a ratio of two integers.
  • Irrational Numbers are values that cannot be written as fractions.

3.5.1 The Proof of Irrationality of √2

  • First proven by Hippasus (c. 400 BCE) using Proof by Contradiction.
  • Steps:
    • Assume √2 is rational (p/q in simplest form, p, q co-prime integers, q ≠ 0).
    • Square both sides: 2 = p²/q² → 2q² = p².
    • This implies p² is even, so p must be even (let p = 2k).
    • Substitute p = 2k: 2q² = (2k)² → 2q² = 4k² → q² = 2k².
    • This implies q² is even, so q must be even.
    • Contradiction: Both p and q are even, meaning they share a common factor of 2, which contradicts the initial assumption that p/q was in simplest form.
    • Therefore, √2 is irrational.

3.5.2 Construction of Length √n

  • Lengths like √2 can be constructed on the number line using the Pythagorean theorem (e.g., for √2, a right triangle with legs of 1 unit).

3.5.3 The Story of Pi (π) and Madhava's Infinite Series

  • Pi (π) is another famous irrational number.
  • Aryabhata (499 CE) gave an approximation (3927/1250 ≈ 3.1416) but noted it was an asanna (approximation).
  • Lambert (1761) formally proved π is irrational.
  • Mādhava of Sangamagrama (14th century) discovered an infinite series for π: π = 4 × (1 - 1/3 + 1/5 - 1/7 + ...).
3.6 Real Numbers: Decimals and Cyclic Patterns
  • The union of Rational and Irrational Numbers forms the continuous line of Real Numbers (R).
  • Decimal expansions distinguish rational from irrational numbers.

3.6.1 Rational Decimals: Terminating and Repeating

  • Rational numbers have two types of decimal expansions:
    • Terminating decimals: The division eventually leaves a remainder of 0 (e.g., 3/8 = 0.375). This occurs when the prime factors of the denominator (in lowest terms) are only 2s and/or 5s.
    • Repeating decimals: The division never reaches a remainder of 0, and the sequence of digits loops infinitely (e.g., 5/11 = 0.4545...).
  • Converting repeating decimals to p/q form:
    • Pure repeating (e.g., 0.6): Let x = 0.6, multiply by 10 (for 1 repeating digit), subtract original equation: 10x - x = 6.6 - 0.6 → 9x = 6 → x = 2/3.
    • General repeating (e.g., 0.16): Shift non-repeating digits past decimal (10x = 1.6), then shift repeating block (100x = 16.6), subtract: 100x - 10x = 16.6 - 1.6 → 90x = 15 → x = 1/6.

3.6.2 The Magic of Cyclic Numbers

  • A cyclic number is a repeating block of digits (e.g., 142857 from 1/7) where multiplying it by integers 1 through 6 results in cyclic shifts of the same digits.

3.6.3 Irrational Decimals: Chaos and Infinity

  • Irrational numbers have decimal expansions that never end and never repeat (e.g., √2 ≈ 1.414..., π ≈ 3.141...).
3.7 Conclusion: The Never-Ending Journey
  • The journey of numbers evolved from simple counting to complex systems.
  • The Real Number Line is continuous, encompassing rational and irrational numbers.
  • The concept of Imaginary Numbers (e.g., √-1), denoted by 'i', extends the number system beyond the real number line, essential for advanced mathematics and physics.
