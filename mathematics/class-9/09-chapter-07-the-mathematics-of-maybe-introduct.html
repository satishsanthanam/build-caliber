Class 9 — Chapter 7: The Mathematics of Maybe: Introduction to Probability

7.1 What is Probability?

Probability is a measurement used to quantify the likelihood of events or how certain we are that a particular event will occur.

  • Subjective Probability: Estimating likelihood based on personal judgment, intuition, or interpretation of current conditions (e.g., predicting rain based on feeling hot).

7.1.1 What is Randomness?
  • Randomness: A situation or action where you cannot predict exactly what will happen, even if all possible outcomes are known.
  • Random Experiment (or Trial): An action that can be repeated where the result might be different each time, and the exact outcome cannot be known in advance (e.g., tossing a coin, rolling a die).

7.1.2 The Probability Scale

Probability is measured on a scale from 0 to 1 (or 0% to 100%):

  • 0 (Impossible): The event cannot happen.
  • 0.25 (Less likely): Low chance of occurrence.
  • 0.5 (Equally likely / Even chance): 50/50 chance (e.g., flipping a fair coin).
  • 0.75 (More likely): High chance of occurrence.
  • 1 (Certain): The event is guaranteed to happen.
7.2 Measuring Probability Objectively

There are two primary objective methods to estimate probability:

7.2.1 Experimental Probability
  • Based on actual data collected from trials or experiments.
  • Formula:
    Experimental Probability = (Number of times the event occurred) / (Total number of trials)
  • Relative Frequency: The ratio of the number of times an event occurs to the total number of trials.

7.2.2 Theoretical Probability
  • Calculated under ideal, perfectly fair situations where all outcomes are assumed to be equally likely. No physical experiment is required.
  • Formula:
    Theoretical Probability P(E) = (Number of favourable outcomes) / (Total number of possible outcomes)
  • Law of Large Numbers: As the number of trials in an experiment increases, the experimental probability tends to get closer to the theoretical probability.
  • Gambler's Fallacy: The mistaken belief that past random trials affect future independent trials (e.g., thinking tails is "due" after rolling heads 6 times). Coins and dice have no memory.
  • Fair and Unbiased: An object (like a coin or die) is unbiased if it is symmetrical and has an equal chance of landing on any of its faces.

7.2.3 Analysing Statistical Data Using Probability
  • Population: The entire group under study (e.g., all 1500 students in a school).
  • Sample: A smaller, representative subset of the population used to gather evidence (e.g., 50 students from one class).
  • Sampling: The process of selecting a representative group to make predictions about the larger population.
7.3 Elements of Probability: Sample Spaces and Events

7.3.1 Sample Space
  • Sample Space (S): The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment.
    • Must include every possible outcome.
    • No outcome should be listed more than once.
  • Sample Size (n(S)): The total number of elements in the sample space.
  • Examples:
    • Tossing a coin: S = {H, T}, n(S) = 2
    • Rolling a die: S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}, n(S) = 6
    • Tossing two coins: S = {HH, HT, TH, TT}, n(S) = 4

7.3.2 Events
  • Event (E): Any single possible result or combination of results; a subset of the sample space.
  • Examples:
    • Rolling a die and getting a number > 4: E = {5, 6}
    • Tossing two coins and getting at least one head: E = {HH, HT, TH}
7.4 Tree Diagrams
  • Tree Diagram: A visual representation used to list all possible outcomes of a multi-step experiment (an experiment involving a series of independent trials).
  • Each branch represents a possible outcome, and branches split to show subsequent events.
  • Useful for visualising multi-step experiments and listing all outcomes of a sample space.
🎯 High-Yield Practice & Concepts
  • Calculating Probability of Even Numbers on a Die:
    • Sample Space S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}, n(S) = 6
    • Favourable Event (Even numbers) E = {2, 4, 6}, n(E) = 3
    • P(Even) = 3/6 = 0.5
  • Two-Coin Toss Probability (At least one Head):
    • S = {HH, HT, TH, TT}, n(S) = 4
    • E = {HH, HT, TH}, n(E) = 3
    • P(at least one Head) = 3/4 = 0.75
