Chapter 1: Orienting Yourself: The Use of Coordinates

1.1 Introduction & Historical Context

Historical Evolution of Coordinates
  • Coordinate System: A structured framework (like grid lines) enabling numbers to describe exact physical locations.
  • Sindhu-Sarasvatī Civilisation: Earliest systematic use of grids on an urban scale with streets planned North-South and East-West.
  • Baudhāyana (c. 800 BCE): Used geometric grid lines, laying foundations for coordinate geometry and the Baudhāyana-Pythagoras Theorem.
  • Ujjayinī: Historically regarded as the central longitude meridian (prime meridian) in ancient Indian astronomy.
  • Āryabhaṭa (c. 499 CE): Introduced sines to replace Greek chords, mapping the sky using celestial coordinates.
  • Brahmagupta (c. 628 CE): Formalized zero and negative numbers, enabling the modern four-quadrant system.
  • René Descartes (1637 CE): Formalized the 2-D plane where any point is defined by two numbers representing distances from perpendicular axes.
1.2 The 2-D Cartesian Coordinate System

System Components
  • x-axis: The horizontal coordinate line.
  • y-axis: The vertical coordinate line perpendicular to the x-axis.
  • Origin (O): The point of intersection of the axes, represented as (0, 0).
  • Coordinates: An ordered pair (x, y) representing a point's perpendicular distances from the y-axis and x-axis respectively.
  • Axes Points: Points on the x-axis have coordinates (x, 0); points on the y-axis have coordinates (0, y).

Quadrants of the Cartesian Plane

The axes divide the Cartesian plane (or xy-plane) into four regions called quadrants:

  • Quadrant I: Both coordinates are positive (+, +).
  • Quadrant II: Negative x, positive y (-, +).
  • Quadrant III: Both coordinates are negative (-, -).
  • Quadrant IV: Positive x, negative y (+, -).
1.3 Distance Between Two Points

The Distance Formula
  • Horizontal Distance: Between (x₁, y) and (x₂, y) is given by |x₂ - x₁|.
  • Vertical Distance: Between (x, y₁) and (x, y₂) is given by |y₂ - y₁|.
  • General Distance Formula: Derived using the Baudhāyana-Pythagoras Theorem. The distance d between any two points P(x₁, y₁) and Q(x₂, y₂) is:

    d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]
  • Reflection Invariance: Reflecting points across axes preserves the distances between them.
