8.1 Introduction to Sequences

We see patterns around us everywhere in nature, art, music, and finance. In mathematics, a sequence is defined as an ordered list of numbers where each number is a term of the sequence.

  • Notation: We use $t_1$ to represent the first term, $t_2$ for the second term, and $t_n$ for the $n^{\text{th}}$ term.
  • Finite vs. Infinite: Sequences can be finite (having a limited number of terms, e.g., $6, 12, 24, 48, 96$) or infinite (continuing indefinitely, indicated by three dots $\dots$).

Common Sequences

  • Natural Numbers: $1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, \dots$ where each term is $1$ more than the previous.
  • Odd Numbers: $1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, \dots$ with a difference of $2$ between consecutive terms.
  • Triangular Numbers: $1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21, \dots$ where each term is the sum of natural numbers up to that term (e.g., $t_5 = 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 = 15$).
  • Square Numbers: $1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, \dots$ where each term is the sum of consecutive odd numbers (e.g., $t_4 = 1 + 3 + 5 + 7 = 16$).
8.2 Explicit Rule for a Sequence

An explicit formula (or explicit rule) uses the term's position number, $n$, to calculate its value directly without needing to know the previous terms.

  • Example: For the sequence of odd numbers, the explicit rule is $u_n = 2n - 1$. Substituting $n = 1, 2, 3$ gives $u_1 = 1, u_2 = 3, u_3 = 5$.
  • Finding Positions: We can use the explicit rule to check if a number is a term in a sequence. For example, to check if $137$ is in the sequence $u_n = 2n - 1$, we solve $2n - 1 = 137 \implies n = 69$. Since $69$ is a natural number, $137$ is the $69^{\text{th}}$ term.
8.3 Recursive Rule for a Sequence

A recursive rule (or recursive formula) defines a term of a sequence by relating it to its preceding term(s). To use a recursive rule, the initial term(s) must be specified.

  • Example: The sequence $1, 4, 7, 10, 13, \dots$ can be defined recursively as: $t_1 = 1, t_n = t_{n-1} + 3$ for $n \ge 2$.

Virahānka–Fibonacci Sequence

A recursive rule can also involve multiple previous terms. The most famous example is the Virahānka–Fibonacci sequence:

$$V_1 = 1, V_2 = 2, \text{ and } V_n = V_{n-1} + V_{n-2} \text{ for } n \ge 3$$

This generates the sequence: $1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34, \dots$ where each term is the sum of the previous two terms.

Historical Note: This sequence was first written down explicitly by the Indian scholar Virahānka in his work Vᤷttajātisamuchaya (7th century CE) in the context of poetic meters. It was later studied by Gopāla (c. 1135 CE), Hemachandra (c. 1150 CE), and subsequently by the Italian mathematician Fibonacci (c. 1200 CE).

8.4 Arithmetic Progressions

An arithmetic progression (AP) is a sequence in which the difference between any two consecutive terms is a constant value. This constant difference is called the common difference ($d$).

  • General Form of an AP: $$a, a + d, a + 2d, a + 3d, \dots, a + (n - 1)d$$ where $a$ is the first term and $d$ is the common difference.
  • $n^{\text{th}}$ Term Formula: The explicit rule for the $n^{\text{th}}$ term of an AP is: $$t_n = a + (n - 1)d$$
  • Recursive Rule: $$t_1 = a, t_n = t_{n-1} + d \text{ for } n \ge 2$$

8.4.1 Visualising an AP

When the ordered pairs $(n, t_n)$ representing the terms of an arithmetic progression are plotted on a coordinate plane, they always lie on a straight line, representing a linear pattern.

8.5 Sum of the First $n$ Natural Numbers

The sum of the first $n$ natural numbers, denoted by $S_n = 1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + n$, is given by the formula:

$$S_n = \frac{n(n + 1)}{2}$$

  • Derivation: By writing the sum in forward and reverse order and adding them:
    $S_n = 1 + 2 + \dots + n$
    $S_n = n + (n-1) + \dots + 1$
    Adding these gives: $2S_n = n(n + 1) \implies S_n = \frac{n(n + 1)}{2}$.
  • Historical Note: The first known written mention of this result is found in Āryabhaᥩa’s Āryabhaᥩĩya (Chapter 2, Verse 19).
8.6 Geometric Progressions

A geometric progression (GP) is a sequence in which each term after the first is obtained by multiplying the preceding term by a non-zero constant multiplier. This constant multiplier is called the common ratio ($r$).

  • General Form of a GP: $$a, ar, ar^2, ar^3, \dots, ar^{n-1}$$ where $a$ is the first term and $r$ is the common ratio.
  • $n^{\text{th}}$ Term Formula: The explicit rule for the $n^{\text{th}}$ term of a GP is: $$t_n = a r^{n-1}$$
  • Recursive Rule: $$t_1 = a, t_n = r \cdot t_{n-1} \text{ for } n \ge 2$$

8.6.1 Fun with Fractals

Fractals are complex geometric shapes or patterns that repeat themselves at different scales (self-similarity). Many fractal attributes lead to geometric progressions.

  • Sierpiński Triangle: Starting with an equilateral triangle (Stage 0), repeatedly removing the middle triangular sub-stage creates a fractal.
  • Number of Black Triangles: Generates the GP $1, 3, 9, 27, \dots$ with explicit formula $t_n = 3^n$ (for stage $n$).
  • Shaded Area: If the initial area is $1$, the area at stage $n$ is given by the GP $1, \frac{3}{4}, \left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^2, \dots$ with explicit formula $s_n = \left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^n$.

8.6.2 Visualising a GP

When the ordered pairs $(n, t_n)$ of a geometric progression are plotted on a coordinate plane, they do not lie on a straight line. Instead, they form a rapidly rising or falling curve (exponential pattern).
