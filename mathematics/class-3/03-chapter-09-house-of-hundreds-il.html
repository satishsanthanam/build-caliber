Class 3 — Chapter 9: House of Hundreds - II

9.1 Introduction: Akbar and Birbal's Crow Counting

A classic tale of Akbar and Birbal introduces the concept of counting large numbers. When Akbar asks how many crows are in the city, Birbal confidently states there are exactly Nine Hundred and Sixty Three ($963$) crows. He explains any variation by suggesting some are on holiday or others are visiting, demonstrating a playful approach to estimation and exact numbers.

9.2 Representing and Locating Numbers

9.2.1 Representing Numbers with Tiles

Numbers can be represented visually using tiles of different values:

  • Large square tiles represent Hundreds ($100$).
  • Rectangular tiles represent Tens ($10$).
  • Small square tiles represent Ones ($1$).

For example, the number $832$ is represented by $8$ hundred-tiles, $3$ ten-tiles, and $2$ one-tiles.

9.2.2 Locating Numbers on a Number Line

Number lines help visualize the relative positions and order of numbers. Students practice locating numbers such as:

  • $530, 540, 628, 696, 590$ on a number line ranging from $500$ to $700$.
  • $703, 721, 759, 810, 855, 887$ on a number line ranging from $695$ to $895$.
9.3 Neighbouring Numbers and Steps

9.3.1 Neighbouring Hundreds, Fifties, and Tens

Finding the boundaries of a number helps in rounding and estimation. For any given number, we can identify its:

  • Neighbouring Hundreds: The multiples of $100$ between which the number lies.
  • Neighbouring Fifties: The multiples of $50$ between which the number lies.
  • Neighbouring Tens: The multiples of $10$ between which the number lies.

Example for $468$:

  • Neighbouring Hundreds: $400$ and $500$
  • Neighbouring Fifties: $450$ and $500$
  • Neighbouring Tens: $460$ and $470$

9.3.2 Steps to Reach Target Numbers

Using a number line, we can calculate the distance (steps) to the nearest tens or hundreds:

  • To reach $650$ from $667$: $667 - 17 = 650$ (Length of steps: $17$)
  • To reach $700$ from $667$: $667 + 33 = 700$ (Length of steps: $33$)
9.4 Number Games and Puzzles

9.4.1 Tambola and Number Clues

Tambola involves filling a grid with numbers between $570$ and $630$ and striking them out based on mathematical clues, such as:

  • A number with $4$ as a digit.
  • Numbers between $595$ and $605$.
  • A number with $1$ as the tens digit.
  • Two more than $610$ ($612$).
  • $5$ less than $625$ ($620$).

9.4.2 Skip and Solve (Patterns)

Completing number patterns using skip counting rules:

  • Skip counting by $+10$: $720 \rightarrow 730 \rightarrow 740 \rightarrow 750$
  • Skip counting by $+100$: $450 \rightarrow 550 \rightarrow 650 \rightarrow 750$
  • Skip counting by $+25$: $850 \rightarrow 875 \rightarrow 900 \rightarrow 925$
  • Skip counting by $+15$: $830 \rightarrow 845 \rightarrow 860 \rightarrow 875$

9.4.3 Number Sentences and Puzzles

Expressing a central number in multiple ways using addition or subtraction sentences:

  • For $400$: $290 + 110$, $300 + 100$, $500 - 100$
  • For $775$: $750 + 25$, $700 + 75$, $800 - 25$
9.5 Matching Quantities and Conditions

9.5.1 Estimating Quantities

Matching real-world quantities to appropriate numerical ranges:

  • Number of children in your school: $501 - 1000$
  • Number of books in your classroom: $51 - 100$
  • Number of pages in your mathematics book: $101 - 200$
  • Number of stars in the sky: More than $1000$

9.5.2 Number Riddles

Solving riddles based on place value and conditions:

  • "I have 2 zeroes as digits and am very close to 99" $\rightarrow 100$
  • "I have 3 hundreds, 6 tens and 7 ones" $\rightarrow 367$
  • "I am century + half century" $\rightarrow 150$
  • "I come between 400 and 450, and I have 5 as a digit" $\rightarrow 425$
9.6 The Number Detective

9.6.1 Special Number Patterns

Exploring unique properties of numbers:

  • Consecutive ascending digits: $123, 345, 789$
  • Consecutive descending digits: $321, 876$
  • Twin digits: $11, 22, 33$
  • Triplet digits: $111, 222, 333$
  • Palindromes (same left-to-right and right-to-left): $353, 868$
  • Numbers with zeroes: $210, 404, 800$

9.6.2 Making Numbers with Slips and Digits

Forming numbers using limited resources:

  • Using six paper slips of values $100$, $10$, or $1$ to form numbers like $231$ ($2$ of $100$, $3$ of $10$, $1$ of $1$).
  • Creating 2-digit and 3-digit numbers using only the digits $3$ and $8$ (e.g., $38, 83, 338, 388, 883$).
9.7 Bhutasankhya (Word Numerals)

Bhutasankhya is an ancient Indian system of associating numbers with words or concepts from nature, literature, and tradition:

  • Moon: Represents $1$ (since there is only one Moon).
  • Eyes: Represents $2$ (since humans have two eyes).
  • Tri kala: Represents $3$ (Past, Present, Future).
  • Directions: Represents $4$ (North, South, East, West).
  • Palm / Sense organs: Represents $5$ (five fingers on a palm, five senses).
  • Hexagon: Represents $6$ (six sides).
  • Sapta svara: Represents $7$ (seven musical notes: Sa, Re, Ga, Ma, Pa, Dha, Ni).
