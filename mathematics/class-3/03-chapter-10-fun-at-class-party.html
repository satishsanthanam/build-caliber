Class 3 — Chapter 10: Fun at Class Party!

10.1 Introduction to Measurement and Informal Tools

Children of Class 3 use informal methods to measure lengths while preparing for a class celebration. These informal tools help in understanding the concept of length before moving to standard units.

10.1.1 Informal Measurement Activities

Based on classroom observations, children can measure and compare lengths using various body parts and objects:

  • Hand span: Used to measure the length of tables or desks. Note that different children may have different hand span sizes, leading to different measurements for the same object.
  • Footstep: Used to measure larger distances, such as the distance between two walls of a classroom.
  • Paper strings: Used to measure heights (like windows or doors) and decorate boards.

10.1.2 Comparing and Ordering Lengths

Comparing lengths directly helps identify which object is longer or shorter:

  • Shortest and Longest: Identifying the shortest paper string (to be colored red) and the longest paper string (to be colored green) by visual comparison or direct alignment.
  • Estimating Quantity: Determining how many paper strings or decorative patterns are needed to cover a specific boundary or border.
10.2 Indirect Comparison and Problem Solving

Sometimes, direct comparison of two lengths is not possible because the objects cannot be brought next to each other. In such cases, we use an intermediate tool for measurement.

10.2.1 Measuring with a Thread

To find out if a heavy table can pass through a classroom door without lifting it:

  • Use a piece of thread to measure the length and breadth of the table.
  • Compare the length of this thread with the width of the door.
  • If the thread (representing the table's width or breadth) is shorter than the door's width, the table can pass through.

This is an example of indirect measurement using a common unit (the thread).

10.2.2 Let us Play: Bridge Challenge

Build a bridge using boxes or bags and guess which classroom objects can pass through the bridge. This helps develop spatial awareness and estimation skills.

10.3 Body Parts as Measuring Units

Exploring whether body proportions are consistent across different individuals.

10.3.1 Body Proportion Hypotheses

Students can test the following common statements to see if they are true for everyone:

  • Is your head roundness equal to $3$ handspans?
  • Is the length of your forearm equal to the length of your foot?
  • Is your height equal to the length of your arms wide open?

10.3.2 Scaling Up Informal Units

When measuring large objects like a saree or a wall, small units like pencils take too long. We can create longer informal units:

  • A paper strip that is double the size of a pencil.
  • Sticking two paper strips together to double the length.
  • Using a paper strip that is exactly one arm long.
10.4 Standard Unit of Length: The Metre

To ensure everyone gets the same measurement, we use standard units. The standard unit of length introduced here is the metre ($m$).

10.4.1 Making a Metre Rope

Steps to create a standard measuring tool:

  1. Take a standard metre rod or an inch tape, along with a rope or thread.
  2. Make a knot at one end of the rope.
  3. Align the metre rod along the rope starting from the first knot.
  4. Mark the point where the rod ends and tie a second knot there.
  5. The distance between the two knots is exactly one metre ($1\text{ m}$).

10.4.2 Estimating and Categorizing with Metre

Using the metre rope, objects and heights can be classified into three categories:

  • More than one metre: Heights of taller classmates, length of the blackboard, height of the classroom door.
  • Less than one metre: Length of a notebook, height of a school bag, length of a pencil box.
  • Equal to one metre: Specific objects or heights that align perfectly with the metre rope.

10.4.3 Fractions of a Metre

By folding the standard metre rope, we can derive smaller standard measurements:

  • Half metre: Obtained by folding the one-metre rope exactly in half ($1/2\text{ m}$).
  • Quarter metre: Obtained by folding the half-metre rope in half again ($1/4\text{ m}$).
