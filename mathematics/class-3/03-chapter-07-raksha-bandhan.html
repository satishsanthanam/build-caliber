Class 3 — Chapter 7: Raksha Bandhan

7.1 Introduction & Making Rakhis

The chapter begins with the festive preparation for Raksha Bandhan. Gopal and Dhara are decorating their house and preparing to make Rakhis with their mother.

7.1.1 Counting and Grouping Objects

To prepare for the festival, children observe and count various decorative items in the house:

  • Leaves used in garlands
  • Glasses of water arranged on the table
  • Pomegranates in baskets
  • Flowers for decoration

7.1.2 Multiplication as Repeated Addition

Making a single Rakhi requires a specific set of materials: 1 flower, 2 threads, and 4 beads. To find the total materials needed for making multiple Rakhis, we use repeated addition, which leads to multiplication.

For 5 Rakhis:

  • Flowers: $$1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 5$$

    This can be written as: $5 \text{ times } 1$ or $5 \times 1 = 5$ flowers.

  • Threads: $$2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 10$$

    This can be written as: $5 \text{ times } 2$ or $5 \times 2 = 10$ threads.

  • Beads: $$4 + 4 + 4 + 4 + 4 = 20$$

    This can be written as: $5 \text{ times } 4$ or $5 \times 4 = 20$ beads.

7.1.3 Try It Out!
  • To make $10$ such Rakhis, we need: $10 \times 1 = 10$ flowers, $10 \times 2 = 20$ threads, and $10 \times 4 = 40$ beads.
  • If you have $30$ flowers, $30$ threads, and $30$ beads, the maximum number of complete Rakhis you can make is limited by the threads. Since each Rakhi needs $2$ threads, $30$ threads can make $30 \div 2 = 15$ Rakhis. However, each Rakhi also needs $4$ beads, so $30$ beads can only make $30 \div 4 = 7$ complete Rakhis (with $2$ beads left over). Thus, the maximum number of Rakhis possible is $7$.
7.2 Grouping and Equal Sharing (Division)

This section introduces the concept of equal sharing and division through sweets and other items.

7.2.1 Counting Laddoos in Boxes

At the sweet shop, laddoos are arranged in boxes to make counting easier:

  • One box contains $3$ rows of $3$ laddoos: $$3 + 3 + 3 = 9 \text{ or } 3 \times 3 = 9 \text{ laddoos}$$
  • For $2$ boxes of laddoos: $$9 + 9 = 18 \text{ or } 2 \times 9 = 18 \text{ laddoos}$$
  • This can also be grouped as $6$ columns of $3$ laddoos: $$6 \times 3 = 18 \text{ laddoos}$$

7.2.2 Equal Sharing (Division)

When a total quantity is distributed equally among a group, we perform division.

  • Sharing Laddoos: Distributing $18$ laddoos equally among $9$ people: $$18 \div 9 = 2 \text{ laddoos each}$$
  • Sharing Kaju Katlis (Repeated Subtraction): Distributing $20$ kaju katlis equally among $5$ people:
    • Step 1: Give $1$ to each ($5$ used) $\rightarrow 20 - 5 = 15$ left.
    • Step 2: Give $1$ more to each ($5$ used) $\rightarrow 15 - 5 = 10$ left.
    • Step 3: Give $1$ more to each ($5$ used) $\rightarrow 10 - 5 = 5$ left.
    • Step 4: Give $1$ more to each ($5$ used) $\rightarrow 5 - 5 = 0$ left.
    • Each person gets $1 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 4$ kaju katlis.
    • Mathematically: $$20 \div 5 = 4$$
  • Sharing Pedas: Distributing $15$ pedas equally among $5$ plates: $$15 \div 5 = 3 \text{ pedas per plate}$$
7.3 Arrays and Commutative Property

Observing arrangements in grids helps us understand that the order of multiplication does not change the product.

7.3.1 Plants in the Garden

Dhara and Gopal look at a flower bed and count the plants in different ways:

  • Dhara's Way (Rows): $8$ rows of $6$ plants each: $$6 + 6 + 6 + 6 + 6 + 6 + 6 + 6 = 48 \text{ or } 8 \times 6 = 48$$
  • Gopal's Way (Columns): $6$ columns of $8$ plants each: $$8 + 8 + 8 + 8 + 8 + 8 = 48 \text{ or } 6 \times 8 = 48$$

This demonstrates the commutative property of multiplication: $$8 \times 6 = 6 \times 8 = 48$$

7.4 Skip Jumping and Number Lines

Skip counting is another visual way to understand multiplication tables and division steps.

7.4.1 Skip Jumping by 3

Starting from $0$, jumping $3$ steps forward each time:

  • $1 \text{ jump} \rightarrow 3$
  • $2 \text{ jumps} \rightarrow 3 + 3 = 6 = 2 \times 3$
  • $3 \text{ jumps} \rightarrow 3 + 3 + 3 = 9 = 3 \times 3$
  • $10 \text{ jumps} \rightarrow 30 = 10 \times 3$

7.4.2 Skip Jumping by 4 and 6
  • Skip jumping by 4: $4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 28, 32, 36, 40, \dots$
  • Skip jumping by 6: $6, 12, 18, 24, 30, 36, 42, 48, 54, 60, \dots$
7.5 Fun Ways of Writing Tables

Multiplication tables can be constructed using physical models like intersecting sticks.

7.5.1 Mithu's Stick Method

By placing horizontal and vertical sticks, the number of intersection points represents the product of the two numbers:

  • To find $3 \times 5$:
    • Arrange $3$ horizontal sticks.
    • Lay $5$ vertical sticks across them.
    • Count the red dots at the intersections: $15$ dots.
    • Therefore, $3 \times 5 = 15$.

7.5.2 Relations Between Tables

Tables can be added together to form new tables. For example, the table of $2$ and the table of $3$ add up to the table of $5$:

  • $(1 \times 2) + (1 \times 3) = 2 + 3 = 5 \rightarrow 1 \times 5 = 5$
  • $(2 \times 2) + (2 \times 3) = 4 + 6 = 10 \rightarrow 2 \times 5 = 10$
  • $(3 \times 2) + (3 \times 3) = 6 + 9 = 15 \rightarrow 3 \times 5 = 15$
7.6 Word Problems & Applications

Applying multiplication and division concepts to real-world scenarios.

7.6.1 Real-Life Examples
  • Spiders: A spider has $8$ legs. How many legs do $5$ spiders have? $$5 \times 8 = 40 \text{ legs}$$
  • Auto Rickshaws: An auto rickshaw has $3$ wheels. How many wheels are on $18$ auto rickshaws? $$18 \times 3 = 54 \text{ wheels}$$
  • Ants: An ant has $6$ legs. What is the total number of legs for $55$ ants? $$55 \times 6 = 330 \text{ legs}$$
  • Sea-Shells: Dhruv has $112$ sea-shells and wants to make necklaces of $28$ shells each. How many necklaces can he make?
    • $112 - 28 = 84$ (1st necklace)
    • $84 - 28 = 56$ (2nd necklace)
    • $56 - 28 = 28$ (3rd necklace)
    • $28 - 28 = 0$ (4th necklace)
    • He can make exactly $4$ necklaces.
