Class 3 — Chapter 1: What's in a Name?

1.1 Keeping Track Without Counting

Before learning formal counting, people used creative methods to keep track of quantities. This story introduces the concept of one-to-one correspondence.

The Story of Deba and Deep

Deba and Deep were cowherds who did not know how to count. To ensure they did not lose any cows, they used a simple marking system:

  • Leaving for grazing: For each cow that left the gate, they made a vertical mark ($|$) on the wall.
  • Returning home: As each cow re-entered, they struck out one mark from the wall.
  • Result: If all marks were struck out, all cows had returned safely. If any marks remained unstruck, it indicated missing cows.

Tracking Large Numbers

As their herd grew, they had many more marks on the wall. When two marks were left unstruck, they knew exactly two cows were missing and quickly found them in a nearby field.

Activity: Help Hemant keep track of his $36$ cows and $23$ sheep by making similar tracking marks.

1.2 Letter Counts in Names (Data Handling)

Analyzing the length of names by counting their letters is an introductory step toward data collection and comparison.

The Animal Game: Finding Captains

Some animals decided to form two teams based on the length of their names:

  • First Team Captain (Longest Name): Elephant ($8$ letters)
  • Second Team Captain (Shortest Name): Ox ($2$ letters)

Other animal name lengths for comparison:

  • $3$ letters: Rat, Dog
  • $4$ letters: Deer, Goat, Lion
  • $5$ letters: Tiger, Snake
  • $6$ letters: Monkey

Analyzing Friends' Names

Students can collect names of their friends and analyze them using the following criteria:

  • Identifying the longest and shortest names.
  • Counting names that start with the same letter.
  • Finding the most common starting and ending letters.
  • Identifying letters of the alphabet that are not used as starting letters in any friend's name.
1.3 Number Names and Word Puzzles

Numbers can be represented as words, and we can analyze these number names by their letter counts.

Letter Counts of Number Names

  • Forty-Three: $10$ letters ($5$ in "Forty" + $5$ in "Three")
  • Seventeen: $9$ letters
  • Fifty-Six: $8$ letters ($5$ in "Fifty" + $3$ in "Six")
  • Forty-Two: $8$ letters ($5$ in "Forty" + $3$ in "Two")

Number Name Puzzles

  • Puzzle 1: "My number name has two words. The first word has $6$ letters, the second word has $5$ letters. I am very near to $100$. Who am I?"
    Answer: 93 (Ninety-three: "Ninety" has $6$ letters, "three" has $5$ letters).
  • Puzzle 2: "My friends and I are the numbers from $63$ to $78$. My number name is the smallest (fewest letters) among all my friends. Who am I?"
    Answer: 70 (Seventy: $7$ letters).

Fun Facts: Longest and Shortest Place Names

  • Longest Place Name in India: Venkatanarasimharajuvaripeta (located in Andhra Pradesh, near the Tamil Nadu border).
  • Shortest Place Names in India: Ib (in Odisha) and Od (in Gujarat).
1.4 Classification and Grouping

Objects can be sorted and grouped in multiple ways based on different common characteristics.

Methods of Grouping

Teji and Jojo grouped the same set of picture cards differently:

  • Teji's Grouping: "Those that eat food" (living things like birds, insects, mice, fish) vs "Those that don't eat food" (non-living things like kites, airplanes, phones, drones).
  • Jojo's Grouping: Grouped by other attributes (e.g., things that fly vs things that do not fly, or electronic devices vs non-electronic items).

Household Objects Classification

Sorting household items based on their power source:

  • Things that need electricity: TV, Lamp, Fan, Clock.
  • Things that don't need electricity: Chair, Bed, Pillow, Blanket.

Data Collection: Hair Styles

An activity to observe, classify, and record the number of children in a class with different hairstyles (e.g., one ponytail, two ponytails, short hair, curly hair).
