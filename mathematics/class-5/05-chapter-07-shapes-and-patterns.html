Class 5 — Chapter 7: Shapes and Patterns

7.1 Weaving Mats

Weaving is a process of combining strips or threads to form patterns. We can explore patterns by weaving paper mats using strips of different colors.

7.1.1 Making a Paper Mat

To make a basic paper mat, you need a base colored paper ($30\text{ cm} \times 20\text{ cm}$) and eight paper strips of two different colors ($3\text{ cm}$ wide and longer than $20\text{ cm}$).

  • Fold the base paper in half along the longer side.
  • Cut vertical slits from the closed end, leaving a gap of $3\text{ cm}$ at the top.
  • Weave the strips through the slits in alternating patterns.

7.1.2 Describing Weave Patterns

Weave patterns can be described mathematically by the sequence of going over and under the slits:

  • Pattern 1:
    • Row 1: 1 under, 1 over, 1 under, 1 over, ... (repeat)
    • Row 2: 1 over, 1 under, 1 over, 1 under, ... (repeat)
  • Pattern 2:
    • Row 1: 2 over, 1 under, 2 over, 1 under, ... (repeat)
    • Row 2: 2 under, 1 over, 2 under, 1 over, ... (repeat)
  • Pattern 3:
    • Row 1: 2 over, 1 under, 2 over, 1 under, ... (repeat)
    • Row 2: 1 under (do not repeat), 3 over, 3 under, 3 over, 3 under, ... (repeat)
    • Row 3: 2 under, 1 over, 2 under, ... (repeat)
    • Row 4: 1 over (do not repeat), 3 under, 3 over, ... (repeat)
7.2 Tiling and Tessellation

A tessellation or tiling is created when a shape or combination of shapes covers a flat surface (region) without any gaps or overlaps.

7.2.1 Regular Shapes and Tessellation

A regular shape is a geometric figure where all sides are equal in length and all angles are equal.

  • Regular Pentagon (5-sided): Does not tessellate. When placed around a point, they leave gaps.
  • Equilateral Triangle (3-sided): Tessellates. Six equilateral triangles fit perfectly around a point without gaps or overlaps ($6 \times 60^\circ = 360^\circ$).
  • Square (4-sided): Tessellates. Four squares fit perfectly around a point ($4 \times 90^\circ = 360^\circ$).
  • Regular Hexagon (6-sided): Tessellates. Three regular hexagons fit perfectly around a point ($3 \times 120^\circ = 360^\circ$).
  • Regular Octagon (8-sided): Does not tessellate on its own because its interior angle is $135^\circ$, which does not divide $360^\circ$ evenly.

7.2.2 Tessellation with Multiple Shapes

Tessellations can also be formed by combining different shapes:

  • Octagons and Squares: Regular octagons can tile a plane if the gaps are filled with small squares.
  • Squares and Triangles: A combination of squares and equilateral triangles can form a stable tiling pattern.
7.3 Properties of Triangles and Quadrilaterals

By dissecting and combining geometric shapes, we can discover their fundamental properties.

7.3.1 Types of Triangles

Triangles can be classified based on the equality of their sides and angles:

  • Isosceles Triangle: Has at least 2 equal sides and 2 equal angles.
  • Equilateral Triangle: Has all 3 sides equal and all 3 angles equal (each angle is $60^\circ$).
  • Scalene Triangle: Has no equal sides and no equal angles.

7.3.2 Types of Quadrilaterals

A quadrilateral is a 4-sided polygon. Key types include:

  • Rhombus: A quadrilateral with all four sides of equal length. It can be divided into four congruent right-angled triangles.
  • Kite: A quadrilateral with two pairs of equal-length adjacent sides (Side 1 = Side 2, and Side 3 = Side 4).
  • Parallelogram: A quadrilateral whose opposite sides are equal and parallel. Opposite angles are also equal.
  • Rectangle: A special type of parallelogram where all four angles are equal and are right angles ($90^\circ$).

7.3.3 Tangrams

A tangram is a traditional puzzle consisting of flat shapes (called tans) that are put together to form various designs. Analyzing tangram pieces helps in understanding spatial relationships, angles, and side lengths.

7.4 Which Shape Am I?

We can identify geometric shapes by matching their defining properties:

  • Square: All sides are equal, and all angles are right angles.
  • Rectangle: Opposite sides are equal, and all angles are right angles.
  • Rhombus: All sides are equal, but angles are not necessarily right angles (opposite angles are equal).
  • Parallelogram: Opposite sides are equal, and opposite angles are equal.
7.5 Play with Circles and Circle Designs

Circles possess unique geometric properties that can be used to construct other shapes and beautiful symmetric designs.

7.5.1 Inscribing Shapes in Circles

If you draw two perpendicular diameters of a circle and join their four endpoints in order, you form a square (or a rectangle if the diameters are not perpendicular).

7.5.2 Circle Designs

By dividing a circle's circumference into equal parts (e.g., 24 points) and connecting them using specific rules (such as connecting point $n$ to point $n+10$), intricate geometric patterns and star polygons can be created.

7.6 Cube Connections

Exploring 3D spatial visualization through cubes, nets, and puzzles.

7.6.1 Cube Nets and Views

A net is a 2D pattern that can be folded to form a 3D solid. A cube has 11 distinct nets.

7.6.2 Painted Cube Problem

When a large solid cube made of $27$ small unit cubes ($3 \times 3 \times 3$) is painted red on the outside, the distribution of painted faces on the individual unit cubes is as follows:

  • Three faces painted red: $8$ cubes (located at the corners).
  • Two faces painted red: $12$ cubes (located along the edges, excluding corners).
  • One face painted red: $6$ cubes (located at the center of each of the 6 faces).
  • No faces painted red: $1$ cube (located at the very center/core of the large cube).
7.7 Icosahedron and Dodecahedron

These are two of the five regular Platonic solids, characterized by having congruent regular polygonal faces meeting at each vertex in the same way.

  • Icosahedron: Has $20$ faces, all of which are equilateral triangles. It has $12$ vertices and $30$ edges. At each vertex, $5$ faces meet.
  • Dodecahedron: Has $12$ faces, all of which are regular pentagons. It has $20$ vertices and $30$ edges. At each vertex, $3$ faces meet.
