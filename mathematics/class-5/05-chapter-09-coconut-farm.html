Class 5 — Chapter 9: Coconut Farm

9.1 Introduction to Division and Multiplication Facts

Division and multiplication are closely related operations. Every multiplication fact can lead to related division facts.

The Relationship Between Multiplication and Division

Consider an array of $35$ coconuts arranged in $5$ rows and $7$ columns:

  • Multiplication Fact: $5 \times 7 = 35$
  • Division Fact 1: $35 \div 7 = 5$ ($35$ split into $7$ groups has $5$ in each group)
  • Division Fact 2: $35 \div 5 = 7$ ($35$ split into $5$ groups has $7$ in each group)

The core components of a division statement are defined as:

  • Dividend (N): The number to be divided.
  • Divisor (D): The number by which the dividend is divided.
  • Quotient (Q): The result obtained from division.

This relationship is expressed as:

$$\text{Dividend (N)} = \text{Divisor (D)} \times \text{Quotient (Q)}$$

9.2 Patterns in Division and Place Value

Observing patterns when dividing by multiples of $10$, $100$, or when the dividend and divisor share common factors simplifies calculations.

Division Patterns with Multiples of 10

Observe the following patterns in division:

  • $150 \div 3 = 50$ (since $50 \times 3 = 150$)
  • $1000 \div 10 = 100$ (since $10 \times 100 = 1000$)
  • $2000 \div 20 = 100$
  • $440 \div 44 = 10$ (since $44 \times 10 = 440$)

Place Value Chart in Division

When dividing numbers by $10$ or its multiples, the digits shift place values:

  • $40 \div 10 = 4$ (Ones place)
  • $400 \div 10 = 40$ (Tens place)
  • $4000 \div 10 = 400$ (Hundreds place)
9.3 Mental Strategies for Division

Different mental strategies can be used to perform division quickly without standard long division.

Strategy 1: Splitting the Number into Convenient Parts

A large dividend can be split into parts that are easily divisible by the divisor.

Example: Solve $1248 \div 6$

  • Split $1248$ into $1200 + 48$.
  • We know $1200 \div 6 = 200$.
  • And $48 \div 6 = 8$.
  • So, $1248 \div 6 = 200 + 8 = 208$.

Example (Subtraction): Solve $1992 \div 4$

  • Express $1992$ as $2000 - 8$.
  • We know $2000 \div 4 = 500$.
  • And $8 \div 4 = 2$.
  • So, $1992 \div 4 = 500 - 2 = 498$.

Strategy 2: Repeated Halving

To divide a number by $4$, you can halve the number twice. To divide by $8$, you can halve it three times.

Example: Solve $128 \div 4$

  • First half: Half of $128$ is $64$.
  • Second half: Half of $64$ is $32$.
  • Therefore, $128 \div 4 = 32$.
9.4 Division with Remainders

When a divisor does not completely divide the dividend, it leaves a leftover value called the remainder.

The General Division Formula

For any division, the relationship between the components is given by:

$$\text{Dividend (N)} = \text{Divisor (D)} \times \text{Quotient (Q)} + \text{Remainder (R)}$$

Where the Remainder (R) must always be less than the Divisor (D).

Example: $726 \div 4$

  • $726 = 4 \times 181 + 2$
  • Here, Dividend $N = 726$, Divisor $D = 4$, Quotient $Q = 181$, and Remainder $R = 2$.

Partial Quotients Method

This method involves taking away easy multiples of the divisor from the dividend step-by-step.

Example: Solve $535 \div 25$

  • Take away $20$ groups of $25$: $535 - 500 = 35$.
  • Take away $1$ group of $25$: $35 - 25 = 10$.
  • Total Quotient = $20 + 1 = 21$.
  • Remainder = $10$.
9.5 Division Using Place Value Columns

Standard long division can be systematically performed by dividing and regrouping through place value columns: Thousands (Th), Hundreds (H), Tens (T), and Ones (O).

Regrouping Example

Example: Solve $324 \div 3$

  • Hundreds: $3\text{ Hundreds} \div 3 = 1\text{ Hundred}$.
  • Tens: $2\text{ Tens} \div 3$ is not possible without regrouping. Write $0$ in the Tens place of the quotient.
  • Regrouping: Regroup $2\text{ Tens}$ into $20\text{ Ones}$. Total ones = $20 + 4 = 24\text{ Ones}$.
  • Ones: $24\text{ Ones} \div 3 = 8\text{ Ones}$.
  • Final Quotient: $108$.
9.6 Mathematical Statements and Logic

Analyzing mathematical statements helps build logical reasoning about operations and number properties.

True or False Statements

A mathematical statement is true if both sides of the '=' sign have the same value.

  • $8 \times 9 = 70 + 2$ is True ($72 = 72$).
  • $20 - 6 = 7 \times 3$ is False ($14 \neq 21$).
  • $48 \div 3 = 4 \times 4$ is True ($16 = 16$).

General Rules of Numbers
  • Odd Numbers: When two odd numbers are added, the sum is always even (e.g., $3 + 5 = 8$).
  • Halving: Halving an even number can sometimes lead to an odd number (e.g., half of $10$ is $5$) and sometimes to an even number (e.g., half of $12$ is $6$).
  • Multiplication by 5: Multiplying an even number by $5$ always leads to a number with '0' in the Ones place (e.g., $4 \times 5 = 20$).
