Class 5 — Chapter 3: Angles as Turns

3.1 Introduction to Turns

Turns are a fundamental way to understand angles in our daily lives. We observe turns in many real-life situations, such as playing games, watching the hands of a clock, or riding a giant wheel.

  • Full Turn: A complete rotation where an object turns all the way around and returns to its starting position. For example, the minute hand of a clock makes a full turn when it comes back to its initial position.
  • Half Turn: A rotation that is half of a complete circle (e.g., making a half-moon shape).
  • Quarter Turn: A rotation that is one-fourth of a complete circle.

3.1.1 Everyday Examples of Turns

Many everyday objects involve turns. Examples include:

  • Taps
  • Doors with hinges
  • Scissors
  • Clothes clips
  • Tongs
  • File covers
3.2 Classifying Angles by Turns

Angles arise in situations that involve a turn. We can classify angles based on the fraction of a full turn they represent:

  • Right Angle: Formed by a quarter ($\frac{1}{4}$) of a full turn.
  • Acute Angle: Formed by less than a quarter ($\frac{1}{4}$) turn.
  • Obtuse Angle: Formed by more than a quarter ($\frac{1}{4}$) turn but less than a half ($\frac{1}{2}$) turn.
  • Straight Angle: Formed by two quarter turns, which is equal to a half ($\frac{1}{2}$) turn.
3.3 Angle Measuring Tools

We can create simple tools to measure turns and identify angles by folding circular paper into equal parts.

3.3.1 The 8-Part Measuring Tool

By folding a circle into 8 equal parts, we can measure turns in eighths:

  • $\frac{1}{8}$ turn: Half of a quarter turn.
  • $\frac{2}{8}$ turn: Equivalent to a $\frac{1}{4}$ turn (Right Angle).
  • $\frac{4}{8}$ turn: Equivalent to a $\frac{1}{2}$ turn (Straight Angle).

3.3.2 The 12-Part Measuring Tool

By folding a circle into 12 equal parts, we can relate turns to the minutes on a clock face:

  • $\frac{1}{12}$ of a full turn is equivalent to $5$ minutes of movement by the minute hand.
  • $\frac{1}{6}$ of a full turn ($\frac{2}{12}$) is equivalent to $10$ minutes.
  • $\frac{1}{4}$ of a full turn ($\frac{3}{12}$) is equivalent to $15$ minutes.
  • $\frac{1}{2}$ of a full turn ($\frac{6}{12}$) is equivalent to $30$ minutes.
  • A full turn ($\frac{12}{12}$) is equivalent to $60$ minutes.
3.4 Direction and Rotation

Turns can be made in two opposite directions:

  • Clockwise: The direction in which the hands of a clock move.
  • Anti-clockwise: The direction opposite to the movement of the hands of a clock.

3.4.1 Turns and Cardinal Directions

Using the cardinal directions (North, South, East, West), we can determine the ending direction after making specific turns:

  • Starting North, making 2 right angles clockwise leads to South.
  • Starting South, making 2 right angles anti-clockwise leads to North.
  • Starting East, making 4 right angles anti-clockwise leads back to East.
  • Starting North, making 5 right angles clockwise leads to East (since 4 right angles make a full turn, and the 5th right angle is a quarter turn clockwise).
