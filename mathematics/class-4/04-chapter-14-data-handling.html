Class 4 — Chapter 14: Data Handling

14.1 Collecting and Recording Data

Data handling begins with asking the right questions to collect information systematically. When we want to find out preferences (like the 'most liked subject'), the question must be clear and specific.

14.1.1 Asking the Right Question

Consider the questions asked by Rohan and Anjali to find the most liked subject in their class:

  • Rohan's Question: "Which subjects do you like?" (This can lead to multiple answers per child, making it harder to find a single 'most liked' subject).
  • Anjali's Question: "Which subject do you like the most?" (This limits each child to one favorite choice, which is more appropriate for finding the 'most liked' subject).

14.1.2 Recording Responses

To record responses efficiently, we can use short codes or abbreviations for each category:

  • M for Mathematics
  • L for Languages
  • T for The World Around Us
  • A for Arts
  • P.E. for Physical Education

Example of recorded raw data for $45$ children:

T, A, P.E., A, M, P.E., L, L, P.E., A, T, M, A, P.E., P.E., T, P.E., M, L, P.E., P.E., A, M, A, L, P.E., M, T, A, T, M, M, P.E., A, L, M, L, P.E., A, T, L, M, M, T, L

14.2 Organizing and Displaying Data

Raw data can be difficult to read and interpret. Organizing it into tables makes it much easier to understand and analyze.

14.2.1 Frequency Tables

By counting the occurrences of each abbreviation in our recorded data, we can fill out a structured table to find the number of children who prefer each subject:

  • Mathematics (M)
  • Languages (L)
  • The World Around Us (T)
  • Physical Education (P.E.)
  • Arts (A)

14.2.2 Comparing Methods of Display

There are two main ways to display this information:

  1. Raw List: Listing every individual response sequentially.
  2. Organized Table: Grouping the responses by category and showing the total count (frequency) for each.

An organized table is much easier to understand because it allows us to quickly identify the most common and least common favorites without counting the entire list manually.

14.3 Pictorial Representation (Pictographs)

A Pictograph is a way of representing data using pictures or symbols to represent quantities.

14.3.1 Colourful Golas Example

Rohan and Anjali recorded the ice golas eaten by different children during lunch break to find the most popular color:

  • Rohan's Method: Used stick figures next to each gola color to represent the number of children.
  • Anjali's Method: Drew the corresponding number of colored golas directly in a row.

Both methods help us easily visualize which color gola is eaten the most and which is eaten the least.

14.3.2 Bal Mela (Analyzing Sales over Days)

Anjali and Rohan recorded the sales of Fruit Chaats and Sandwiches over three days using pictographs:

  • Fruit Chaat Sales: Represented by drawings of fruit chaat bowls for Day 1, Day 2, and Day 3.
  • Sandwich Sales: Represented by drawings of sandwich slices for Day 1, Day 2, and Day 3.

By counting the symbols, we can answer questions such as which day had the highest sales and which item was more popular.

14.4 Two-Way Classification Tables

Sometimes we need to organize data based on more than one category at the same time (e.g., gender and game preference).

14.4.1 Chess or Cricket Activity

To find out how many boys and girls play chess, cricket, both, or neither, we can organize the data into a two-way table with columns for both girls and boys across different game categories.
