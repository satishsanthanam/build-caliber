2.1 Where Are You Hiding?

This section introduces spatial perspective and how objects look different depending on where you are standing or looking from.

2.1.1 Understanding Perspectives

When playing games like hide and seek, our ability to see others depends on our line of sight and position:

  • Line of Sight: An imaginary straight line along which an observer can see.
  • If a person is facing a certain direction, they can only see objects and people within their field of view in that direction.
2.2 Views of Objects

Objects look different when viewed from different directions. The three primary views are:

  • Top View: How an object looks when viewed from directly above.
  • Front View: How an object looks when viewed from the front.
  • Side View: How an object looks when viewed from the side.

2.2.1 Examples of Different Views

  • Tree: From the front, it shows the trunk and the canopy. From the top, it looks like a circular green shape.
  • Brick:
    • Top View: Shows the top face (often with a stamp or pattern, e.g., '7777').
    • Front View: Shows the long side face.
    • Side View: Shows the shorter end face.

2.2.2 Identifying Views of Everyday Objects

Practice identifying the object and its specific view:

  • Television: Front view shows the screen.
  • Sofa: Front view shows the seating area and cushions.
  • Slipper: Top view shows the footbed and straps.
  • Cup and Saucer: Top view shows concentric circles.
  • School Bus: Back view shows the rear door and 'STOP' sign.
  • Chair: Side view shows the profile of the legs and backrest.
  • Nut (Hardware): Top view shows a hexagon with a central circle.
  • Pizza Cutter: Top/Side view shows the wheel and handle.
  • Helmet: Side view shows the profile and visor.
2.3 Boxy Buildings

Using 3D blocks or matchboxes, we can construct buildings and analyze their structures from different perspectives.

  • Front View: Shows the arrangement of boxes visible from the front.
  • Back View: Shows the arrangement of boxes visible from behind.
  • Top View: Shows the footprint or layout of the boxes from above.
2.4 Grid Positioning and Navigation

Grids help us locate positions and navigate paths using rows, columns, and step-by-step directions.

2.4.1 Classroom Grid (Rows and Desks)

Positions can be described using rows (horizontal lines) and desks/columns (vertical lines):

  • Example: 'Third desk in the first row' or 'Middle desk of the second row'.

2.4.2 Grid Game (Coordinates)

A Grid is a network of uniformly spaced horizontal and vertical lines. Positions are defined by intersecting rows and columns:

  • Row 1, Column 1: Bottom-left corner.
  • Row 3, Column 3: Top-right corner.

2.4.3 Treasure Hunt (Pathfinding)

Navigating a grid involves moving in cardinal directions (up, down, left, right) by a specific number of steps:

  • Diagonal movements are not allowed.
  • Distance can be measured in the number of grid steps required to reach a destination.
2.5 Maps and Aerial Views

Maps are simplified, top-down representations of real-world spaces used for navigation and planning.

2.5.1 Drone View

A drone captures a Top-Down Perspective (aerial view) of a large area, such as a school campus, showing the relative positions of buildings, fields, and boundaries.

2.5.2 Sight Maps

A Sight Map is a visual layout of an area (like a school) showing key landmarks:

  • Key Landmarks: Playground, Classrooms, Principal's Office, Library, Sports Room, Medical Room, Entrance.
  • Pathfinding on a Map: Finding the shortest route between two points (e.g., from Grade 4 classroom to the stage) by tracing available pathways.
