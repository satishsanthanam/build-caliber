Class 4 — Chapter 12: Ticking Clocks and Turning Calendar

12.1 Turning Calendar

This section explores how we measure days, months, and years using calendars, with a special focus on leap years and date formats.

12.1.1 Leap Years

A Leap Year is a year that has one additional day, making it 366 days instead of the usual 365 days. This extra day is added to the end of February, making it 29 days long instead of 28.

  • Leap years occur once every four years.
  • Example: The year 2016 was a leap year, which means 2020 and 2024 are also leap years.
  • February 2024 has 29 days (Leap Year).
  • February 2025 has 28 days (Non-Leap Year).

12.1.2 Date Formats

Dates can be written in different formats, most commonly the DD/MM/YYYY format (Day/Month/Year).

  • Example: 04/07/2024 represents the 4th of July 2024.
  • This can also be written as July 4, 2024.

12.1.3 Alternative Calendars

Different communities use different traditional calendars alongside the standard solar (English/Gregorian) calendar, such as:

  • Hindu Calendar
  • Islamic Calendar
  • Sikh Calendar

These calendars often track lunar cycles, marking important phases like the New Moon and Full Moon to determine the start of months and festival dates.

12.2 How Many Hours in a Day?

Understanding the 24-hour cycle of a day and how daily activities are distributed across time.

12.2.1 The 24-Hour Cycle

A single day consists of exactly 24 hours, which represents the time from one morning (e.g., 6 o'clock morning) to the next morning.

12.2.2 AM and PM

To distinguish between morning and night times on a standard 12-hour clock, we use AM and PM:

  • AM (Ante Meridiem): Used for times from midnight to just before noon (e.g., 8:00 AM is morning).
  • PM (Post Meridiem): Used for times from noon to just before midnight (e.g., 8:00 PM is night).

12.2.3 12-Hour vs. 24-Hour Digital Format

Digital clocks often display time in a 24-hour format to avoid confusion between AM and PM:

  • 12:00 AM = 00:00 hours
  • 1:00 AM = 01:00 hours
  • 12:00 PM = 12:00 hours
  • 1:00 PM = 13:00 hours
  • 8:00 PM = 20:00 hours
  • 11:45 PM = 23:45 hours
12.3 Hours and Minutes

This section covers measuring smaller intervals of time and understanding fractions of an hour.

12.3.1 Calculating Time Intervals

To find the duration of an activity, calculate the difference between the start time and the end time.

  • Example: If Raghav leaves home at 8:20 AM and returns at 8:35 AM, the time taken is $35 - 20 = 15$ minutes.

12.3.2 Fractions of an Hour

An hour can be divided into smaller fractional parts:

  • One Hour: $1\text{ hour} = 60\text{ minutes}$
  • Half Hour: $\frac{1}{2}\text{ hour} = 30\text{ minutes}$
  • Quarter Hour: $\frac{1}{4}\text{ hour} = 15\text{ minutes}$
  • Three Quarters of an Hour: $\frac{3}{4}\text{ hour} = 45\text{ minutes}$

12.3.3 Reading the Clock Correctly

When reading an analog clock, pay close attention to both the hour hand (shorter) and the minute hand (longer):

  • The numbers 1 to 12 on the clock represent hours, but for minutes, each gap represents 5 minutes.
  • Example: If the hour hand is past 12 and the minute hand points to 4, the time is 12:20 (not 12:04).
