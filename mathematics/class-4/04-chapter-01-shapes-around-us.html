Class 4 — Chapter 1: Shapes Around Us

1.1 Introduction to Shapes and Building Models

Exploring shapes through real-world monuments and building models using blocks.

  • Monuments of Delhi: India Gate, Qutub Minar, Safdarjung Tomb, Akshardham, National Museum, Jantar Mantar, and Sansad Bhawan.
  • Building Components: Analyzing parts of a building such as the roof, pillars, and base, and identifying which 3D shapes represent them best.

Qutub Minar Facts

The Qutub Minar is a UNESCO World Heritage site and the tallest building made of bricks. It features:

  • Storeys: 5 storeys
  • Stairs: 379 stairs
  • Building Materials: Historically made with clay bricks, stone blocks, or wood. Modern construction also uses concrete blocks and hollow blocks.
1.2 Prisms, Pyramids, and Nets

Understanding 3D shapes by unfolding them into 2D layouts called nets.

  • Net of a Box: A folded open and flattened box. Folding along the dotted lines of a net reconstructs the 3D box.

Prisms

A Prism is a 3D solid with identical polygon ends (bases) and flat rectangular sides.

  • Triangular Prism: Has triangular bases and rectangular lateral faces.
  • Square Prism (Cube): All faces are squares.
  • Hexagonal Prism: Has hexagonal bases and rectangular lateral faces.

Pyramids

A Pyramid is a 3D solid with a polygon base and triangular lateral faces that meet at a single point called the apex.

  • Triangular Pyramid (Tetrahedron): Base and all faces are triangles.
  • Square Pyramid: Square base with four triangular faces.
  • Pentagonal Pyramid: Pentagonal base with five triangular faces.
1.3 Properties of 3D Shapes

Analyzing 3D shapes based on their faces, corners (vertices), and edges.

  • Face (F): A flat or curved surface of a 3D shape.
  • Corner / Vertex (V): A point where two or more edges meet.
  • Edge (E): A line segment where two faces meet.

Shape Properties Table

Properties of common 3D shapes:

  • Cube / Square Prism: $F = 6$, $V = 8$, $E = 12$
  • Cuboid / Rectangular Prism: $F = 6$, $V = 8$, $E = 12$
  • Triangular Pyramid: $F = 4$, $V = 4$, $E = 6$
  • Square Pyramid: $F = 5$, $V = 5$, $E = 8$
  • Triangular Prism: $F = 5$, $V = 6$, $E = 9$

Euler's Formula Relationship: For these polyhedra, the relationship between faces, vertices, and edges is given by: $$F + V - E = 2$$

1.4 Angles: When Lines Meet

An Angle is formed when two lines or surfaces meet at a common point.

Types of Angles

  • Right Angle: An angle of exactly $90^\circ$, forming a perfect L-shape (like the corner of a book or window).
  • Acute Angle: An angle that is less than a right angle ($< 90^\circ$).
  • Obtuse Angle: An angle that is greater than a right angle ($> 90^\circ$).

Rigidity of Shapes

  • Triangle: The most rigid shape. Pushing on its sides does not change its angles or shape.
  • Rectangle / Square: Not rigid. Pushing on their sides changes their angles (turning right angles into acute and obtuse angles) and alters the shape.
1.5 Circles and their Properties

A circle is a round 2D shape where all points on the boundary are at an equal distance from a fixed point.

Key Components of a Circle

  • Center: The central point inside the circle from which all points on the boundary are equidistant.
  • Radius (r): The distance or line segment from the center of the circle to any point on its boundary.
  • Diameter (d): A straight line segment that passes through the center of the circle and connects two points on the boundary.
  • Relationship: The diameter is twice the length of the radius: $$d = 2r$$
1.6 Puzzles and Classification

Applying geometric concepts to solve puzzles and classify shapes.

  • Matchstick Puzzles: Creating and transforming triangles by moving or removing a specific number of matchsticks.
  • Shape Classification: Classifying 2D shapes based on their number of sides and angles (e.g., 3 angles, 4 angles, 5 angles). The number of sides in a polygon is always equal to its number of angles.
