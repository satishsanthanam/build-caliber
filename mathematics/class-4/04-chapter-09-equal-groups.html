Class 4 — Chapter 9: Equal Groups

9.1 Animal Jumps and Multiples

Understanding multiplication through skip counting and jumps of different animals on a number line.

9.1.1 Multiples of 3 (Frog Jumps)

The frog jumps 3 steps at a time starting from 0.

  • The numbers touched by the frog are: multiples of 3 ($3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21, 24, \dots$).
  • To check if the frog will touch 67, we determine if 67 is a multiple of 3. Since $67 = 3 \times 22 + 1$, it is not a multiple of 3, so the frog will not touch 67.

9.1.2 Multiples of 4 (Squirrel Jumps)

The squirrel jumps 4 steps at a time starting from 0.

  • The numbers touched by the squirrel are: multiples of 4 ($4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, \dots$).
  • To reach 60, the number of jumps needed is: $$60 \div 4 = 15\text{ jumps}$$

9.1.3 Multiples of 6 (Rabbit Jumps)

The rabbit jumps 6 steps at a time starting from 0.

  • The numbers touched by the rabbit are: multiples of 6 ($6, 12, 18, 24, 30, \dots$).
  • The smallest 3-digit number is 100. The smallest 3-digit multiple of 6 is: $$102\text{ (since } 102 = 6 \times 17\text{)}$$
  • The number of jumps to reach 102 is $17$ jumps.

9.1.4 Multiples of 8 (Kangaroo Jumps)

The kangaroo jumps 8 steps at a time starting from 0.

  • The numbers touched by the kangaroo are: multiples of 8 ($8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, \dots$).
9.2 Common Multiples

When two or more sequences of jumps land on the same numbers, those numbers are called common multiples.

  • Common Multiples of 3 and 4: Numbers touched by both the frog and the squirrel: $12, 24, 36, 48, 60, \dots$
  • Common Multiples of 6 and 8: Numbers touched by both the rabbit and the kangaroo: $24, 48, 72, 96, \dots$
  • To reach 48:
    • The rabbit (jumping 6 steps) takes: $48 \div 6 = 8$ jumps.
    • The kangaroo (jumping 8 steps) takes: $48 \div 8 = 6$ jumps.
9.3 Gulabo's Garden: Multiplication and Division Concepts

Using real-world scenarios to understand the components of multiplication and division statements.

9.3.1 Multiplication Statement Components

In a multiplication statement:

$$\text{Multiplier (No. of groups)} \times \text{Multiplicand (Group size)} = \text{Product}$$

Example: Gulabo has 12 lily flowers, each with 3 petals. Total petals:

  • Petals in 10 lilies: $10 \times 3 = 30$ petals
  • Petals in 2 lilies: $2 \times 3 = 6$ petals
  • Petals in 12 lilies: $30 + 6 = 36$ petals ($12 \times 3 = 36$)

9.3.2 Division Statement Components

Gulabo counted 80 petals of hibiscus flowers (each flower has 5 petals). To find the number of flowers:

$$80 \div 5 = 16\text{ flowers}$$

  • $5$ petals = $1$ flower
  • $10$ petals = $2$ flowers
  • $50$ petals = $10$ flowers
  • Remaining $30$ petals = $6$ flowers
  • Total: $10 + 6 = 16$ flowers
9.4 The Doubling Magic

Exploring the mathematical properties of doubling numbers.

  • Doubling Examples:
    • Double of $32 = 64$
    • Double of $14 = 28$
    • Double of $26 = 52$
    • Double of $17 = 34$
    • Double of $39 = 78$
    • Double of $45 = 90$
  • Ones Digit Rule of Doubled Numbers: When any whole number is doubled, the ones digit of the result is always an even number ($0, 2, 4, 6, 8$).
  • It is impossible to get $3, 5, 7, 9$ as the ones digit after doubling a whole number.
9.5 Multiplication Grid and Patterns

Analyzing patterns in a $10 \times 10$ multiplication chart.

  • Symmetry: The numbers in row $7$ are identical to the numbers in column $7$ because multiplication is commutative ($a \times b = b \times a$).
  • Even Rows: In rows of even numbers (2, 4, 6, 8, 10), all products are even numbers.
  • Ones Digit Patterns:
    • In row 5, the ones digits alternate between $5$ and $0$ ($5, 0, 5, 0, \dots$).
    • In row 8, the ones digits repeat in the pattern: $8, 6, 4, 2, 0, 8, 6, 4, 2, 0$.
9.6 Multiples of Tens and Hundreds

Simplifying multiplication by breaking down numbers into tens and hundreds.

9.6.1 Multiples of 10

To multiply a number by a multiple of 10, multiply the non-zero digits and append a zero:

  • $10 \times 3 = 30$
  • $20 \times 3 = 60$
  • $40 \times 6 = 240$
  • $60 \times 8 = 480$

9.6.2 Multiplying Using 10s (Distributive Property)

To multiply a two-digit number, split it into tens and ones:

Example: $18 \times 4$

  • Split $18$ into $10 + 8$.
  • $10 \times 4 = 40$
  • $8 \times 4 = 32$
  • Total: $40 + 32 = 72$

9.6.3 Multiples of 100

To multiply by hundreds, multiply the single digits and append two zeros:

  • $100 \times 2 = 200$
  • $200 \times 2 = 400$
  • $500 \times 4 = 2000$
9.7 More Multiplication (Three-Digit Numbers)

Using the grid method to multiply three-digit numbers by single-digit numbers.

Example: $125 \times 8$

  • Split $125$ into $100 + 20 + 5$.
  • Multiply each part by $8$:
$\times$ $8$
$100$ $100 \times 8 = 800$
$20$ $20 \times 8 = 160$
$5$ $5 \times 8 = 40$

$$\text{Total} = 800 + 160 + 40 = 1000$$

9.8 Division and Partial Quotients

Division can be performed by systematically subtracting known multiples of the divisor (the partial quotient method).

Example: $58 \div 3$

  • Subtract $10$ groups of $3$ ($30$): $58 - 30 = 28$ (Quotient so far: $10$)
  • Subtract $5$ groups of $3$ ($15$): $28 - 15 = 13$ (Quotient so far: $10 + 5 = 15$)
  • Subtract $3$ groups of $3$ ($9$): $13 - 9 = 4$ (Quotient so far: $15 + 3 = 18$)
  • Subtract $1$ group of $3$ ($3$): $4 - 3 = 1$ (Quotient so far: $18 + 1 = 19$)
  • Remaining: $1$ (which is less than the divisor $3$).
  • Result: Quotient = $19$, Remainder = $1$.
