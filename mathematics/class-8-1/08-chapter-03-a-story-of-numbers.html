Class 8 (Part 1) — Chapter 3: A Story of Numbers

3.1 Reema's Curiosity & History of Modern Numerals

Origin of Modern Numbers

  • Indian Origin: The modern oral and written number system originated thousands of years ago in India.
  • Yajurveda Samhita: Ancient Indian texts listed names for numbers based on powers of 10: eka (1), dasha (10), shata (100), sahasra (1000), up to $10^{12}$ and beyond.
  • Bakhshali Manuscript: Contains the first known written instance of numbers using ten digits, including zero (notated as a dot), dating to c. 3rd century CE.
  • Aryabhata: (c. 499 CE) First mathematician to fully explain and compute with the Indian system of 10 symbols.

Transmission to the West

  • Arab World: Transmitted around 800 CE; popularized by Persian mathematician Al-Khwārizmī (c. 825 CE) and philosopher Al-Kindi (c. 830 CE).
  • Europe: Transmitted around 1100 CE. Italian mathematician Fibonacci (c. 1200 CE) advocated for its adoption, which became fully integrated by the 17th century.
  • Terminology: Commonly referred to as Hindu numerals, Indian numerals, or Hindu-Arabic numerals.
3.2 Early Number Systems & Counting Mechanisms

The Mechanism of Counting

  • One-to-One Mapping: Associating each object in a collection with a unique physical marker (e.g., sticks, pebbles) to track quantities.
  • Number System: A standard sequence of objects, names, or written symbols with a fixed order used for counting.
  • Roman Numerals: A system of landmark symbols (I=1, V=5, X=10, L=50, C=100, D=500, M=1000) used in Europe before being replaced by the Hindu system.

Early Physical Systems

  • Body Parts: Used as standard counting sequences in various cultures, such as in Papua New Guinea.
  • Tally Marks on Bones: One of the oldest methods of representation. Notable artifacts include the Ishango bone (20,000–35,000 years old) and the Lebombo bone (44,000 years old).
  • Counting in Twos: Indigenous groups like the Gumulgal (Australia), Bakairi (South America), and Bushmen (South Africa) independently developed base-2 systems (e.g., 1 = urapon, 2 = ukasar, 3 = ukasar-urapon).
3.3 The Idea of a Base

The Egyptian Number System

  • Base-10 System: Developed around 3000 BCE, utilizing distinct symbols for powers of 10 ($1, 10, 10^2, 10^3, \dots, 10^7$).
  • Shortcoming: Lacked place value; representing large numbers required repeating symbols up to 9 times each, making it highly inefficient for extremely large values.

Base-n Number Systems

  • Base-n System: A system where the first landmark number is 1, and every subsequent landmark number is obtained by multiplying the current landmark by a fixed number $n$.
  • Landmark Numbers: Powers of $n$ starting from $n^0, n^1, n^2, n^3, \dots$

The Abacus

  • Abacus: A calculating board with lines representing powers of 10, introduced around the 11th century to facilitate decimal calculations.
3.4 Place Value Representation

Mesopotamian Number System

  • Sexagesimal System: A base-60 system developed in ancient Mesopotamia.
  • Positional System: The position of a symbol determines its associated power of 60.
  • Placeholder: Later Mesopotamians introduced a placeholder symbol to denote empty positions (similar to zero), though it was rarely used at the end of numbers.

Mayan & Chinese Systems

  • Mayan System: An independent, vertical, almost base-20 system using dots (1), bars (5), and a seashell symbol as a placeholder for zero.
  • Chinese Rod Numerals: A decimal (base-10) place-value system using vertical (Zongs) and horizontal (Hengs) rod configurations to prevent positional ambiguity.

The Hindu Number System

  • Fully Developed Place Value: Uses 10 digits (0-9) where each position represents a power of 10.
  • Zero as a Number: Codified by Brahmagupta in his work Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta (628 CE), establishing zero as a number in its own right with defined arithmetic properties.
