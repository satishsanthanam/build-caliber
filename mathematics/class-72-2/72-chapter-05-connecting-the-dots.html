Chapter 5: Connecting the Dots...

5.1 Of Questions and Statements

We regularly encounter statements and questions that require statistical thinking to interpret and answer.

  • Statistical Statement: A claim or summary about some phenomenon, expressed in terms of numerical values, proportions, probabilities, or predictions.
  • Statistical Question: A question that can be answered by collecting data where variability is expected (e.g., "How tall are Grade 7 students in our school?").
  • Statistics: The study of collecting, organising, analysing, interpreting, and presenting data.
5.2 Representative Values

To represent a group of numbers with a single value, we use measures of central tendency.

Arithmetic Mean (Average)

The Arithmetic Mean (A.M.), or simply Mean, balances out the highs and lows of a dataset. It is calculated as:

$$\text{Mean} = \frac{\text{Sum of all the values in the data}}{\text{Number of values in the data}}$$

Average as Fair-Share: The average can also be understood as an equal-share distribution among all members of a group.

Historical Note: In ancient Indian mathematics, the mean was referred to as samamiti (mean measure), where 'sama' means equal. Other historical terms include samarajju (Brahmagupta, 628 CE), samīkaraṇa (Mahāvīrācārya, 850 CE), and sāmya (Śrīpati, 1039 CE).

Median

The Median is the middle number in a sorted dataset, dividing it into two equal halves.

  • To find the median, first sort the data in ascending or descending order.
  • If the number of values is odd, the median is the exact middle number.
  • If the number of values is even, the median is the average of the two middle numbers: $$\text{Median} = \frac{\text{Value}_1 + \text{Value}_2}{2}$$

Outliers

An outlier is a value that significantly deviates from the rest of the values in the data.

  • Impact on Mean: A very high or very low outlier significantly impacts the sum, shifting the mean towards the outlier.
  • Impact on Median: The median is robust and is not affected much by outliers.
  • When data is balanced, the mean and median are close. When an outlier is on the lower end, $\text{mean} < \text{median}$. When an outlier is on the higher end, $\text{mean} > \text{median}$.
5.3 Visualising Data

Visual representations help us understand data patterns and variability more clearly.

  • Dot Plot: A graph that shows data points as dots on a line, helping to visualise variability, clustering, and spread.
  • Clustered Column Graph (or Double Column Graph): A graph that displays two columns side-by-side in each category, allowing direct comparison between two groups over time or across categories.

2-Step Process for Analyzing Graphs
  1. Step 1: Identify what is given: Notice how the graph is organised, what scale is used, and what patterns the data shows.
  2. Step 2: Infer from what is given: Analyse, interpret, and draw conclusions from the observations.
5.4 Data Detective

Well-organised data can reveal interesting stories and help solve real-world mysteries.

  • Comparing Datasets: By analyzing dot plots and central tendency measures (mean and median) of different groups (e.g., heights of boys vs. girls across schools or over decades), we can identify trends and avoid false generalisations.
  • Real-world Applications: Analyzing global datasets such as daylight hours in different hemispheres (Helsinki vs. Wellington), worldwide rocket launches, and city skyscraper counts.
Summary
  • Dot plots provide a quick glimpse of data variability, range, and clustering.
  • $$\text{Arithmetic Mean} = \frac{\text{Sum of all values}}{\text{Number of values}}$$
  • The Median is the middle value of sorted data (or the average of the two middle values for an even number of data points).
  • Data can be described and compared using minimum, maximum, total, range, mean, and median.
  • Clustered bar graphs are useful for comparing values across categories and time.
