Fractions in Disguise

1.1 Fractions as Percentages

  • The symbol '%' is read as per cent, derived from Latin 'per centum' meaning 'out of hundred'.
  • Percentages are simply fractions where the denominator is 100 (e.g., 25% = 25/100).
  • To express a fraction as a percentage, multiply the fraction by 100.

1.2 Percentage of Some Quantity

  • To find a percentage of a quantity: y% of z = (y/100) × z.
  • The FDP Trio: Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages are interchangeable (e.g., 50% = 50/100 = 1/2 = 0.5).

1.3 Using Percentages

Comparing Proportions

Convert different fractions/ratios to percentages to easily compare them (e.g., test scores with different maximum marks).

Percentage Increase or Decrease

  • Used to describe the rate of change of quantities.
  • Formula: Percentage Change = (Amount of Change / Original Amount) × 100.

Profit and Loss

  • Cost Price (CP): The price paid to purchase an item.
  • Marked Price (MP): The price quoted by the shopkeeper (MRP).
  • Selling Price (SP): The price the customer pays after any discounts.
  • Profit: Occurs when SP > CP. Profit = SP - CP.
  • Loss: Occurs when CP > SP. Loss = CP - SP.
  • Percentage Profit/Loss: Calculated based on the Cost Price (CP). Profit/Loss % = (Profit or Loss / CP) × 100.

Taxes

Tax rates like GST (Goods and Services Tax) are specified as percentages and added to the bill amount.

Growth and Compounding

  • Principal (P): The initial amount deposited or borrowed.
  • Interest: Extra money paid by institutions on deposits or paid by borrowers on loans.
  • Rate of Interest (r): The percentage of the principal charged/earned per period (e.g., p.a. = per annum).
  • Without Compounding (Simple Interest): Interest is paid back every term. Principal remains the same. Amount after t years = P(1 + rt).
  • With Compounding: Interest gained is added back to the principal, increasing the base for the next term. Amount after t years = P(1 + r)^t.

Decline

Depreciation is the reduction of an item's financial value over time due to use and age.

Chapter Summary
  • Percentages are widely used in daily life and represent fractions with a denominator of 100.
  • Fractions, decimals, and percentages can be converted into one another.
  • Increases, decreases, profits, losses, and taxes can all be expressed as percentages.
  • Compounding causes a quantity to grow exponentially compared to non-compounding (linear growth).
  • Visualizing problems with rough diagrams helps in estimating and solving percentage problems.
