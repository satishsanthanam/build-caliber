Chapter 3: Proportional Reasoning-2

3.1 Proportionality—A Quick Recap

Key Concepts

  • Proportional Relationship: When two or more related quantities change by the same factor.
  • Ratio Notation: Used to represent proportional relationships (e.g., 2:1).
  • Cross-Multiplication Method: To verify if two ratios (a:b and c:d) are proportional, check if a × d = b × c.
  • General Rule: Ratios a:b and c:d are proportional if a × d = b × c, or equivalently, a/b = c/d.
3.2 Ratios in Maps

Understanding Map Ratios

  • Representative Fraction (RF): An expression showing the ratio between a distance on the map and the corresponding actual distance on the ground.
  • Example: RF 1:60,00,000 means 1 cm on the map represents 60,00,000 cm (or 60 km) in reality.
  • This ratio helps determine geographical distances, not necessarily road distances.
3.3 Ratios with More than 2 Terms

Multi-Term Ratios and Proportionality

  • Ratios can involve more than two terms (e.g., a:b:c:d).
  • A multi-term proportional relationship is maintained if all quantities change by the same factor.
  • Example: If a spice mix ratio is 8:4:2:1, and one ingredient is halved, all other ingredients must also be halved to maintain the same taste (e.g., 4:2:1:0.5).
  • Notation: Proportionality between multi-term ratios is denoted by '::' (e.g., 8:4:2:1 :: 4:2:1:0.5).
  • General Rule: If a:b:c:d :: p:q:r:s, then a/p = b/q = c/r = d/s.
3.4 Dividing a Whole in a Given Ratio

Method for Division

  • To divide a quantity 'x' into parts according to a ratio a:b:c:d...:
  • Step 1: Calculate the sum of the ratio terms (S = a+b+c+d...).
  • Step 2: Each part is found by multiplying the total quantity 'x' by the fraction of its corresponding ratio term over the sum of terms.
  • Formulas:
    • First part = x × (a / S)
    • Second part = x × (b / S)
    • And so on.
  • Example: To make 110 units of concrete with a cement:sand:gravel ratio of 1:1.5:3:
    • Sum of ratio terms = 1 + 1.5 + 3 = 5.5.
    • Cement = 110 × (1 / 5.5) = 20 units.
    • Sand = 110 × (1.5 / 5.5) = 30 units.
    • Gravel = 110 × (3 / 5.5) = 60 units.
3.5 A Slice of the Pie

Pie Charts and Angle Calculation

  • Pie Charts: Visual representations showing proportions of a whole.
  • Principle: The angle of each slice in a pie chart is proportional to the quantity it represents.
  • Total Angle: A full circle is 360°.
  • Steps to Construct:
    1. Determine the quantities for each category.
    2. Find the total sum of all quantities.
    3. Simplify the ratio of quantities (optional, but good practice).
    4. Calculate the angle for each category: (Category Quantity / Total Quantity) × 360°.
    5. Draw a circle and mark angles sequentially using a protractor.
    6. Label and color the slices.
  • Example: Grade distribution (A:B:C:D:E = 12:10:8:6:4) for 40 students.
    • Simplified ratio = 6:5:4:3:2. Sum = 20.
    • Angle for Grade A = (6/20) × 360° = 108°.
    • Angle for Grade B = (5/20) × 360° = 90°.
    • Angle for Grade C = (4/20) × 360° = 72°.
    • Angle for Grade D = (3/20) × 360° = 54°.
    • Angle for Grade E = (2/20) × 360° = 36°.
3.6 Inverse Proportions

Understanding Inverse Relationships

  • Direct Proportion: If a:b :: c:d, then d = (b × c) / a. Quantities change by the same factor in the same direction.
  • Inverse Proportion: When two quantities change in opposite directions by the same factor. If one quantity increases by a factor 'n', the other decreases by a factor 'n' (or 1/n).
  • Rule: Two quantities 'x' and 'y' are inversely proportional if their product is a constant (xy = k).
  • Example: Speed and time taken to cover a fixed distance. If speed doubles, time halves.
  • Formula: If (x1, y1) and (x2, y2) are corresponding values of inversely proportional quantities, then x1y1 = x2y2 = k. This also implies x1/x2 = y2/y1.
  • Problem Solving:
    • Example: 20 workers take 4 days to lay a road. 10 workers will take (20 × 4) / 10 = 8 days.
    • Example: 2 pumps fill a tank in 18 hours. 4 pumps will fill it in (2 × 18) / 4 = 9 hours.
