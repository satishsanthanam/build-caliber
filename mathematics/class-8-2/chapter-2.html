Chapter 2: The Baudhāyana-Pythagoras Theorem

2.1 Doubling a Square

Concept & Historical Context
  • Baudhāyana’s Śulba-Sūtra (c. 800 BCE) poses the fundamental question: How can one construct a square having double the area of a given square?
  • Simply doubling the side length of a square does not double its area; it quadruples it (Area = $2 \times 2 = 4$ times the original area).
  • Baudhāyana's Rule (Verse 1.9): "The diagonal of a square produces a square of double the area of the original square."
  • Geometric Proof: An original square of side $1$ can be divided into $2$ congruent right isosceles triangles. A square constructed on its diagonal is composed of $4$ such congruent triangles, thus having exactly double the area.
2.2 Halving a Square

Concept & Construction
  • To construct a square with half the area of a given square, the process of doubling is reversed.
  • By connecting the midpoints of the sides of a square, a tilted inner square (PQRS) is formed.
  • The area of this inner square is exactly half of the outer square because the outer square is composed of 8 congruent triangles, while the inner square contains 4 of them.
2.3 Hypotenuse of an Isosceles Right Triangle

Mathematical Derivation
  • In a right-angled triangle, the side opposite to the right angle is the hypotenuse.
  • For an isosceles right triangle with equal sides of $1$ unit:
  • The square constructed on the hypotenuse has an area equal to $2$ square units.
  • If $c$ is the length of the hypotenuse, then:
    c² = 2
    c = √2 units.

Decimal Representation of √2
  • Bounds of √2:
    • $1 < \sqrt{2} < 2$ (since $1^2 = 1$ and $2^2 = 4$)
    • $1.4 < \sqrt{2} < 1.5$ (since $1.4^2 = 1.96$ and $1.5^2 = 2.25$)
    • $1.41 < \sqrt{2} < 1.42$ (since $1.41^2 = 1.9881$ and $1.42^2 = 2.0164$)
    • $1.414 < \sqrt{2} < 1.415$ (since $1.414^2 = 1.999396$ and $1.415^2 = 2.002225$)
  • Irrationality: $\sqrt{2}$ cannot be expressed as a terminating decimal or a fraction $m/n$ where $m$ and $n$ are counting numbers. It is a non-terminating, non-recurring decimal: $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41421356...$
2.4 Combining Two Different Squares

Baudhāyana’s Theorem
  • Śulba-Sūtra (Verse 1.12): "The area of the square produced by the diagonal is the sum of the areas of the squares produced by the two sides."
  • To combine two squares of different areas (sides $a$ and $b$), construct a right-angled triangle with perpendicular sides $a$ and $b$. The square on the hypotenuse $c$ will have an area equal to the sum of the areas of the two original squares:
    a² + b² = c²
  • This is the fundamental statement of the Baudhāyana-Pythagoras Theorem.
2.5 Right-Triangles Having Integer Sidelengths

Baudhāyana Triples
  • Integer triples $(a, b, c)$ that satisfy $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ are called Baudhāyana triples (or Pythagorean triples).
  • Common examples: $(3, 4, 5)$, $(5, 12, 13)$, $(8, 15, 17)$, $(7, 24, 25)$, $(12, 35, 37)$, $(15, 36, 39)$.
  • Scaling Property: If $(a, b, c)$ is a triple, then $(ka, kb, kc)$ is also a triple for any positive integer $k$.
  • Primitive Triple: A triple with no common factor greater than $1$ (e.g., $(3, 4, 5)$ is primitive, while $(6, 8, 10)$ is a scaled version).
  • Generation Method: Triples can be generated using the sum of consecutive odd numbers:
    1 + 3 + 5 + ... + (2n - 1) = n²
2.6 A Long-Standing Open Problem

Fermat's Last Theorem
  • Inspired by Baudhāyana triples, Pierre de Fermat conjectured that the equation xⁿ + yⁿ = zⁿ has no positive integer solutions for n > 2.
  • This famous conjecture, known as Fermat's Last Theorem, remained unsolved for over 300 years until it was proven by Andrew Wiles in 1994.
2.7 Applications of the Theorem

Historical Problem: Bhāskarāchārya’s Līlāvatī
  • The Lotus Problem: A lotus stem in a lake is $1$ unit above the water. When swayed by the wind, it touches the water surface $3$ units away from its original vertical position. Find the depth of the lake.
  • Solution: Let $x$ be the depth of the lake. The total length of the stem is $x + 1$. Applying the theorem:
    3² + x² = (x + 1)²
    9 + x² = x² + 2x + 1
    9 = 2x + 1
    x = 4 units.
  • The depth of the lake is $4$ units.
