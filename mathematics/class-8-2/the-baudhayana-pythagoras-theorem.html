Chapter 2: The Baudhāyana-Pythagoras Theorem

2.1 Doubling a Square
  • Problem: Construct a square with double the area of a given square.
  • Incorrect Approach: Doubling the length of each side results in a square with 4 times the original area (2x2=4).
  • Baudhāyana's Solution (Śulba-Sūtra, c. 800 BCE): "The diagonal of a square produces a square of double the area of the original square."
  • Visual Explanation: If the original square is composed of two small triangles, the square constructed on its diagonal is composed of four such small triangles, thus having double the area.
2.2 Halving a Square
  • Problem: Construct a square with half the area of a given square.
  • Method: Reverse the doubling construction. Draw a tilted smaller square inside the larger one by connecting the midpoints of the sides of the original square.
  • Explanation: The inner square (e.g., PQRS) is formed by four right-angled triangles. The area of this inner square is half the area of the original square.
2.3 Hypotenuse of an Isosceles Right Triangle
  • Definition: The hypotenuse is the side opposite the right angle in a right triangle.
  • Problem: Find the hypotenuse of an isosceles right triangle with equal sides of length 1 unit.
  • Solution:
    • A square of side 1 unit has an area of 1 sq. unit.
    • The square constructed on its diagonal has double the area, i.e., 2 sq. units.
    • If 'c' is the length of the hypotenuse, then c² = 2.
    • Therefore, c = √2.
  • General Formula: For an isosceles right triangle with equal sides 'a' and hypotenuse 'c', the relationship is c² = 2a², which simplifies to c = a√2.
  • Example 1: If a = 12, then c = 12√2 ≈ √288 (between 16 and 17).
  • Example 2: If c = √72, then (√72)² = 2a² ⇒ 72 = 2a² ⇒ a² = 36 ⇒ a = 6.
Decimal Representation of √2
  • Bounds: 1 < √2 < 2 (since 1²=1, 2²=4).
  • Closer Bounds: 1.4 < √2 < 1.5 (since 1.4²=1.96, 1.5²=2.25).
  • Even Closer Bounds: 1.414 < √2 < 1.415 (since 1.414² ≈ 1.999, 1.415² ≈ 2.002).
  • Nature of √2:
    • √2 cannot be expressed as a terminating decimal.
    • √2 cannot be expressed as a fraction m/n (where m, n are counting numbers). This was proven by Euclid.
    • Therefore, √2 has a non-terminating, non-repeating decimal representation (e.g., 1.41421356...).
2.4 Combining Two Different Squares
  • Baudhāyana's Statement (Śulba-Sūtra, Verse 1.12): "The area of the square produced by the diagonal is the sum of the areas of the squares produced by the two sides."
  • Method: To combine two squares with side lengths 'a' and 'b':
    • Form a right-angled triangle with perpendicular sides 'a' and 'b'.
    • The square constructed on the hypotenuse of this triangle will have an area equal to the sum of the areas of the two original squares (a² + b²).
  • Visual Proof: By arranging the two squares and four congruent right-angled triangles (with sides 'a' and 'b'), a larger square is formed whose side is the hypotenuse 'c'. The area of this larger square is (a-b)² + 4 * (1/2 * a * b) = a² - 2ab + b² + 2ab = a² + b². This area is also c².
  • Baudhāyana's Theorem on Right-angled triangles: If a right-angled triangle has sidelengths 'a', 'b', and 'c' (where 'c' is the hypotenuse), then a² + b² = c². This is also known as the Pythagorean Theorem.
  • Example: For a right triangle with sides a=3 cm, b=4 cm, the hypotenuse c satisfies c² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25, so c = 5 cm.
2.5 Right-Triangles Having Integer Sidelengths
  • Baudhāyana Triples (Pythagorean Triples): Integer triples (a, b, c) that form the sidelengths of a right-angled triangle, satisfying a² + b² = c².
  • Examples: (3, 4, 5), (5, 12, 13), (8, 15, 17), (7, 24, 25), (12, 35, 37), (15, 36, 39).
  • Scaled Versions: If (a, b, c) is a Baudhāyana triple, then (ka, kb, kc) is also a Baudhāyana triple for any positive integer 'k'. This shows there are infinitely many Baudhāyana triples.
  • Primitive Baudhāyana Triple: A Baudhāyana triple (a, b, c) that does not have any common factor greater than 1. Example: (3, 4, 5) is primitive, while (9, 12, 15) is a scaled version of (3, 4, 5) and thus not primitive.
  • Generating Triples using Odd Numbers: If the nth odd number (2n-1) is a perfect square, then the equation (n-1)² + (2n-1) = n² can generate Baudhāyana triples. For example:
    • If n=5, 2n-1=9 (3²). Then (5-1)² + 9 = 5² ⇒ 4² + 3² = 5². This gives the triple (3, 4, 5).
    • If n=13, 2n-1=25 (5²). Then (13-1)² + 25 = 13² ⇒ 12² + 5² = 13². This gives the triple (5, 12, 13).
2.6 A Long-Standing Open Problem: Fermat's Last Theorem
  • Fermat's Conjecture: The equation xⁿ + yⁿ = zⁿ has no solution in positive integers when n > 2.
  • Historical Context: Fermat claimed to have a "truly marvellous proof" but stated the margin was too small to contain it.
  • Resolution: After more than 300 years of failed attempts by mathematicians, Andrew Wiles successfully proved Fermat's Last Theorem in 1994.
2.7 Further Applications of the Baudhāyana-Pythagoras Theorem

Problem from Bhāskarāchārya's Līlāvatī (Lotus Problem)

  • Scenario: A lotus stem (length x+1) has its tip 1 unit above water. When swayed, the tip touches the water 3 units away from its original position.
  • Objective: Find the depth of the lake (x).
  • Application: This forms a right-angled triangle with sides 3, x, and hypotenuse x+1.
  • Solution: Using Baudhāyana's Theorem:
    3² + x² = (x+1)²
    9 + x² = x² + 2x + 1
    9 = 2x + 1
    2x = 8
    x = 4
    The depth of the lake is 4 units.

Summary of Key Concepts

  • The Baudhāyana-Pythagoras Theorem describes the relationship between the three sides of a right-angled triangle.
  • For a right-angled triangle with sides 'a', 'b', and hypotenuse 'c', the relationship is a² + b² = c².
  • In an isosceles right triangle with equal sides 'a' and hypotenuse 'c', the relationship is c = a√2.
  • √2 is an irrational number; it cannot be expressed as a terminating decimal or a fraction m/n. Its value is approximately 1.414.
  • A Baudhāyana triple (a, b, c) consists of positive integers satisfying a² + b² = c². Examples include (3, 4, 5) and (5, 12, 13). Infinitely many such triples can be constructed.
  • Fermat's Last Theorem states that the equation aⁿ + bⁿ = cⁿ has no solution in positive integers when n > 2. This was proven by Andrew Wiles in 1994.
