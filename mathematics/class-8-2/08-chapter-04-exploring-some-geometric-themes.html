Chapter 4: Exploring Some Geometric Themes

4.1 Fractals

Introduction to Fractals
  • Fractals are self-similar geometric shapes that exhibit the same or similar patterns over and over again at smaller and smaller scales.
  • Self-similarity occurs widely in nature, such as in ferns, trees, clouds, coastlines, mountains, and lightning.

Mathematical Fractals
  • Sierpinski Carpet: Created by dividing a square into 9 smaller squares and removing the central square. This process is repeated recursively on the remaining 8 squares.
    • Number of remaining squares at step $n$: $R_n = 8^n$
    • Number of holes at step $n$: $H_n$ where $H_{n+1} = H_n + R_n$
  • Sierpinski Gasket (Triangle): Created by dividing an equilateral triangle into 4 identical equilateral triangles by joining the midpoints, and removing the central triangle recursively.
  • Koch Snowflake: Formed by starting with an equilateral triangle, dividing each side into 3 equal parts, raising an equilateral triangle over the middle part, and removing its base recursively.

Fractals in Art
  • Fractal patterns are found in historical architecture, such as the Kandariya Mahadev Temple in Khajuraho, India.
  • They also appear in traditional African art, such as Nigerian Fulani wedding blankets, and in the works of Dutch artist M.C. Escher (e.g., 'Smaller and Smaller').
4.2 Visualising Solids

Elements of 3D Solids
  • Faces: The flat plane surfaces that form the boundary of a solid.
  • Edges: The line segments where two faces meet.
  • Vertices: The corner points where edges meet.
  • For example, a standard cube or cuboid has 6 faces, 12 edges, and 8 vertices.

Prisms vs. Pyramids
  • Prism: A solid with two congruent polygonal bases as opposite faces, with all other faces being parallelograms. Named after the shape of its base (e.g., triangular prism, pentagonal prism).
  • Pyramid: A solid with a polygonal base and a single apex point outside the base, with triangular faces connecting the apex to the base vertices (e.g., triangular pyramid or tetrahedron, square pyramid).
4.3 Nets of Solids

Nets and Folding
  • A Net is a 2D flat shape that can be folded to form a 3D solid.
  • A standard cube has exactly 11 unique net structures (excluding rotations and reflections).
  • A regular tetrahedron has only 2 possible nets.
  • An octahedron has 11 different nets, while a dodecahedron has 43,380 unique nets.

Shortest Paths on a Solid
  • To find the shortest path between two points on the surface of a 3D solid (like a cuboid), unfold the solid into its 2D net.
  • The straight line connecting the two points on the flat net represents the shortest path on the 3D surface.
  • The way a cuboid is unfolded matters, as different net configurations yield different straight-line distances.
4.4 Projections and Views

Projections
  • A Projection is the representation of a 3D object's points mapped perpendicularly onto a 2D plane.
  • The projection of a pair of parallel lines will always remain parallel.

Three-View Projections

To fully represent a 3D object on a 2D surface, three mutually perpendicular views are typically used:

  • Front View: Projection onto the vertical plane.
  • Top View: Projection onto the horizontal plane.
  • Side View: Projection onto the side plane.
4.5 Isometric Projections

Isometric Drawing
  • An Isometric Projection is a 2D representation of a 3D object where the projections of all three primary axes (length, depth, and height) are scaled equally.
  • The isometric projection of a cube forms a regular hexagon.
  • An Isometric Grid is constructed by tiling a plane with hexagons, allowing easy measurement and drawing of 3D solids along three primary directions.
