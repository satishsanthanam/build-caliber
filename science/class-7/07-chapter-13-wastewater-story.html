Chapter 13: Wastewater Story

13.1 Water, Our Lifeline

Water Scarcity and Conservation
  • Wastewater: The dirty, lather-rich, oil-mixed, black-brown water that goes down the drains from sinks, showers, toilets, and laundries.
  • Clean Water Scarcity: More than one billion people worldwide lack access to safe drinking water, leading to water-related diseases and deaths.
  • Water for Life: The United Nations General Assembly proclaimed the period 2005–2015 as the International Decade for action on "Water for life" to halve the population without safe drinking water.
  • Cleaning of Water: The process of removing pollutants before wastewater enters a water body or is reused, commonly known as Sewage Treatment.
13.2 What is Sewage?

Composition of Sewage

Sewage is a complex liquid waste containing suspended solids, organic and inorganic impurities, nutrients, and disease-causing microbes:

  • Organic Impurities: Human faeces, animal waste, oil, urea (urine), pesticides, herbicides, fruit and vegetable waste.
  • Inorganic Impurities: Nitrates, phosphates, and metals.
  • Nutrients: Phosphorus and Nitrogen.
  • Bacteria: Pathogens such as Vibrio cholerae (causes cholera) and Salmonella paratyphi (causes typhoid).
  • Other Microbes: Protozoans which cause dysentery.
13.3 Sewerage System

Wastewater Transport
  • Sewers: Underground pipes that carry wastewater from its point of origin to the treatment plant.
  • Sewerage: A network of interconnected sewers forming a transport system.
  • Manholes: Access points located every 50 m to 60 m in the sewerage system, placed at junctions or points where there is a change in direction.
13.4 Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP)

Stages of Treatment
  1. Bar Screens: Wastewater is passed through screens to remove large physical objects like rags, sticks, cans, plastic packets, and napkins.
  2. Grit and Sand Removal: Water enters a tank where the flow speed is decreased, allowing sand, grit, and pebbles to settle down.
  3. Clarifier (Sedimentation): Water settles in a large sloped tank. Solid waste like faeces settles at the bottom as sludge (removed by a scraper), while floatable solids like oil and grease are removed by a skimmer to produce clarified water.
  4. Aeration: Air is pumped into the clarified water to help aerobic bacteria grow. These bacteria consume remaining organic matter, food waste, and soaps.
  5. Disinfection: Before discharge, treated water may be disinfected using chemicals like chlorine or ozone.
  • By-products: Sludge (decomposed by anaerobic bacteria to produce biogas or dried for manure) and biogas (used as fuel or to generate electricity).
13.5 Better Housekeeping Practices

Minimising Waste at Source
  • Oils and Fats: Should not be thrown down the drain as they harden and block pipes, or clog soil pores in open drains. Throw them in the dustbin.
  • Chemicals: Paints, solvents, motor oil, and medicines should not be discarded in drains as they kill water-purifying microbes.
  • Solid Wastes: Used tea leaves, solid food remains, cotton, and sanitary towels should be thrown in the dustbin to prevent choking drains and blocking oxygen flow.
13.6 Sanitation and Disease

Health Hazards and Alternative Systems
  • Diseases: Untreated human excreta pollutes surface and groundwater, leading to water-borne diseases like cholera, typhoid, polio, meningitis, hepatitis, and dysentery.
  • Vermi-processing Toilet: A novel, low-water toilet design tested in India where human excreta is treated by earthworms and converted into nutrient-rich vermi cakes.
  • On-site Sewage Disposal: Low-cost sanitation systems like septic tanks, chemical toilets, and composting pits are used in areas without sewerage systems.
  • Swachh Bharat Mission: Initiated by the Government of India in 2016 to promote proper sewage disposal and provide toilets for all.
