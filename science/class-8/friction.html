Chapter 9: Friction

9.1 Force of Friction
  • Definition: The force that opposes the relative motion between surfaces in contact is called the force of friction.
  • Direction: Friction acts in the direction opposite to the applied force or motion.
  • Example: When a book is pushed on a table, friction opposes its motion.
9.2 Factors affecting Friction
  • Dependence on Surfaces: Friction depends on the nature and smoothness of the surfaces in contact.
  • Roughness: Rougher surfaces have more friction due to greater interlocking of irregularities.
  • Interlocking of Irregularities: Even smooth surfaces have microscopic irregularities that interlock, causing friction.
  • Force of Contact: Friction increases if the two surfaces are pressed harder together.
  • Static vs. Sliding Friction: Static friction is the force required to start motion, while sliding friction is the force required to maintain motion. Sliding friction is generally less than static friction.
  • Spring Balance: A device used to measure the force acting on an object, often used to measure friction.
9.3 Friction : A Necessary Evil
  • Importance: Friction is essential for walking, writing, starting/stopping vehicles, fixing nails, and constructing buildings.
  • Undesirable Aspects: Friction causes wear and tear of materials (shoes, bearings) and generates heat, leading to energy loss.
  • Heat Generation: Rubbing hands, striking a matchstick, and running machines generate heat due to friction.
9.4 Increasing and Reducing Friction
  • Increasing Friction: Deliberately increased by making surfaces rougher (e.g., shoe soles, tire treads) or using materials that enhance grip (e.g., soil for kabaddi players, coarse powder for gymnasts).
  • Reducing Friction: Achieved by using lubricants (oil, grease, graphite) which form a thin layer between moving parts, or by polishing surfaces.
  • Lubricants: Substances that reduce friction.
  • Air Cushion: Used in some machines to reduce friction.
  • Inherent Friction: Friction can never be completely eliminated as no surface is perfectly smooth.
9.5 Wheels Reduce Friction
  • Rolling Friction: The resistance encountered when one body rolls over the surface of another.
  • Advantage: Rolling friction is significantly less than sliding friction.
  • Applications: Use of rollers on luggage, wheels on vehicles, and ball bearings in machinery (fans, bicycles) to reduce friction.
  • Ball Bearings: Devices that replace sliding with rolling friction between moving parts.
9.6 Fluid Friction
  • Definition: The frictional force exerted by fluids (gases and liquids) on objects moving through them. Also known as drag.
  • Factors Affecting Fluid Friction: Depends on the speed of the object relative to the fluid, the shape of the object, and the nature of the fluid.
  • Minimizing Fluid Friction: Objects moving through fluids (like birds, fish, aeroplanes) often have streamlined shapes to reduce drag and energy loss.
  • Streamlined Shapes: Shapes that minimize resistance when moving through fluids.
