Chapter 13: Light

13.1 & 13.2 Laws of Reflection

What Makes Things Visible?
  • We see an object only when light from it enters our eyes. This light may be emitted by the object or reflected by it.
  • A polished or shiny surface (like a mirror) changes the direction of light falling on it.

Reflection Terminology
  • Incident Ray: The light ray which strikes any reflecting surface.
  • Reflected Ray: The light ray that comes back from the surface after reflection.
  • Normal: An imaginary line drawn at an angle of 90° to the reflecting surface at the point of incidence.
  • Angle of Incidence (∠i): The angle between the normal and the incident ray.
  • Angle of Reflection (∠r): The angle between the normal and the reflected ray.

The Two Laws of Reflection
  • First Law: The angle of incidence is always equal to the angle of reflection (∠i = ∠r).
  • Second Law: The incident ray, the normal at the point of incidence, and the reflected ray all lie in the same plane.
  • Lateral Inversion: An effect where the left of an object appears on the right in its mirror image, and vice versa.
13.3 Regular and Diffused Reflection

Types of Reflection
  • Regular Reflection: Reflection from a smooth surface (like a mirror). Parallel incident rays are reflected parallel to each other, forming clear images.
  • Diffused (Irregular) Reflection: Reflection from a rough or uneven surface (like cardboard). Parallel incident rays are reflected in different directions due to surface irregularities, not because of the failure of reflection laws.

Luminous vs. Illuminated Objects
  • Illuminated Objects: Objects that shine by reflecting light from other sources (e.g., the Moon).
  • Luminous Objects: Objects that emit their own light (e.g., the Sun, candles, electric lamps).
13.4 & 13.5 Multiple Reflections & Images

Multiple Images
  • Two plane mirrors placed at an angle to each other form multiple images.
  • The number of images formed depends on the angle between the mirrors.
  • When mirrors are placed parallel to each other, an infinite number of images are formed.

Optical Instruments
  • Periscope: Uses two parallel plane mirrors inclined at 45° to enable viewing of objects out of direct line of sight (used in submarines, tanks, and bunkers).
  • Kaleidoscope: An instrument containing three rectangular mirror strips joined to form a prism. It creates beautiful, non-repeating patterns through multiple reflections of colored glass pieces.
13.6 Sunlight — White or Coloured

Dispersion of Light
  • Sunlight (white light) consists of seven constituent colors.
  • Dispersion: The splitting of white light into its individual colors (e.g., when passing through a prism or water droplets to form a rainbow).
13.7 & 13.8 Structure and Care of the Human Eye

Anatomy of the Eye
  • Cornea: The tough, transparent outer front part of the eye that protects its interior.
  • Iris: The dark muscular structure behind the cornea that gives the eye its distinctive color and controls the size of the pupil.
  • Pupil: The small opening in the iris through which light enters.
  • Lens: A crystalline structure behind the pupil that focuses light onto the back of the eye.
  • Retina: The light-sensitive inner layer containing nerve cells that transmit visual signals to the brain.
  • Optic Nerve: Transmits visual sensations from the retina to the brain.

Physiology of Vision
  • The retina contains two types of photoreceptor cells:
    • Cones: Sensitive to bright light; detect color.
    • Rods: Sensitive to dim light.
  • Blind Spot: The junction of the optic nerve and the retina where no sensory cells exist, making vision impossible at that spot.
  • Persistence of Vision: The impression of an image persists on the retina for about 1/16th of a second. If static images are flashed faster than 16 frames per second, they appear as a continuous moving picture.

Care of the Eyes & Defects
  • The most comfortable reading distance for a normal eye is about 25 cm.
  • Cataract: A condition in old age where the eye lens becomes cloudy, causing foggy vision. It is treated by surgically replacing the opaque lens with an artificial one.
  • Vitamin A Deficiency: Can lead to night blindness. Rich sources include carrots, broccoli, spinach, milk, eggs, and papaya.
13.9 & 13.10 Visually Impaired & Braille System

The Braille System
  • Developed by Louis Braille in 1821 for visually challenged individuals.
  • Consists of 63 dot patterns or characters arranged in cells of two vertical rows of three dots each.
  • Characters represent letters, combinations of letters, common words, or grammatical signs. The dots are slightly raised to allow recognition by touch.
