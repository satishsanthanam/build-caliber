Chapter 2: Acids, Bases and Salts

2.0 Introduction to Acids, Bases and Salts
  • Taste: Sour taste is due to acids, bitter taste is due to bases.
  • Acidity Remedy: Baking soda solution is a common remedy for acidity after overeating, as it is basic and nullifies acid.
2.0.1 Identifying Acids and Bases
  • Acids: Sour in taste, change blue litmus to red.
  • Bases: Bitter in taste, change red litmus to blue.
  • Litmus: A natural indicator.
  • Turmeric: Another natural indicator (stain of curry on white cloth becomes reddish-brown with soap, then yellow with water).
  • Synthetic Indicators: Methyl orange and phenolphthalein are used to test for acids and bases.
2.0.2 Litmus Solution (Do You Know?)
  • Litmus: A purple dye extracted from lichen (a plant belonging to the division Thallophyta).
  • Neutral Litmus: Its colour is purple when neither acidic nor basic.
  • Other Natural Indicators: Red cabbage leaves, turmeric, coloured petals of flowers like Hydrangea, Petunia, and Geranium.
  • Acid-Base Indicators: Substances that indicate the presence of an acid or base by changing colour.
2.0.3 Identifying Solutions with Red Litmus Paper (Question)
  • Scenario: Given three test tubes (distilled water, acidic, basic) and only red litmus paper.
  • Method:
    • Step 1: Dip red litmus paper into each solution. The solution that turns red litmus blue is the basic solution.
    • Step 2: Use the newly blue litmus paper (from the basic solution) on the remaining two test tubes.
    • Step 3: The solution that turns the blue litmus paper back to red is the acidic solution.
    • Step 4: The solution that shows no change with either red or blue litmus is distilled water.
2.1 Understanding the Chemical Properties of Acids and Bases
2.1.1 Acids and Bases in the Laboratory
  • Activity 2.1: Testing various solutions (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, CH₃COOH, NaOH, Ca(OH)₂, KOH, Mg(OH)₂, NH₄OH) with red litmus, blue litmus, phenolphthalein, and methyl orange.
  • Indicators: Tell us whether a substance is acidic or basic by changing colour.
  • Olfactory Indicators: Substances whose odour changes in acidic or basic media.
  • Activity 2.2 (Olfactory Indicators): Testing finely chopped onions, vanilla essence, and clove oil.
  • Onion: Loses its characteristic smell in basic medium, retains in acidic medium.
  • Vanilla Essence & Clove Oil: Odour is destroyed in basic medium, remains unchanged in acidic medium.
2.1.2 How do Acids and Bases React with Metals?
  • Activity 2.3 (Acids with Metals): Zinc granules react with dilute sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄) to evolve hydrogen gas.
  • Test for H₂ gas: Bubbles are formed in soap solution, and a burning candle brought near them extinguishes with a 'pop' sound.
  • Reaction Summary (Acid + Metal): Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas.
  • Example: `2HCl + Zn → ZnCl₂ + H₂`.
  • Activity 2.4 (Bases with Metals): Some metals, like zinc, react with strong bases like sodium hydroxide (NaOH) to produce hydrogen gas.
  • Example: `2NaOH(aq) + Zn(s) → Na₂ZnO₂(s) + H₂(g)` (Sodium zincate).
  • Note: Such reactions (bases with metals) are not possible with all metals.
2.1.3 How do Metal Carbonates and Metal Hydrogencarbonates React with Acids?
  • Activity 2.5: Sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃) and sodium hydrogencarbonate (NaHCO₃) react with dilute HCl to produce carbon dioxide (CO₂) gas.
  • Test tube A (Sodium Carbonate): `Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)`.
  • Test tube B (Sodium Hydrogencarbonate): `NaHCO₃(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)`.
  • Test for CO₂ gas: Pass the evolved gas through lime water (calcium hydroxide solution).
  • Lime Water Reaction: `Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l)`. (White precipitate of calcium carbonate, turns milky).
  • Excess CO₂: `CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) → Ca(HCO₃)₂(aq)`. (Calcium hydrogencarbonate, which is soluble in water, causes milkiness to disappear).
  • Forms of Calcium Carbonate: Limestone, chalk, and marble are different forms.
  • Reaction Summary: Metal carbonate / Metal hydrogencarbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water.
2.1.4 How do Acids and Bases React with each other?
  • Activity 2.6: Adding dilute HCl to NaOH solution with phenolphthalein.
  • Observation: Phenolphthalein turns pink in basic NaOH, becomes colourless upon adding HCl, and turns pink again upon adding NaOH.
  • Conclusion: Acids and bases nullify each other's effects.
  • Reaction: `NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)`.
  • Neutralisation Reaction: The reaction between an acid and a base to give a salt and water.
  • General Form: Base + Acid → Salt + Water.
2.1.5 Reaction of Metallic Oxides with Acids
  • Activity 2.7: Copper oxide (CuO) reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid (HCl).
  • Observation: Copper oxide dissolves, and the solution turns blue-green (due to formation of copper(II) chloride).
  • General Reaction: Metal oxide + Acid → Salt + Water.
  • Nature of Metallic Oxides: Since they react with acids to form salt and water, similar to bases, metallic oxides are considered basic oxides.
2.1.6 Reaction of a Non-metallic Oxide with Base
  • Example: Carbon dioxide (CO₂) reacts with calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂, lime water).
  • Reaction: `Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l)`.
  • Nature of Non-metallic Oxides: Since this reaction is similar to a neutralisation reaction (base + acid → salt + water), non-metallic oxides are considered acidic in nature.
2.1.7 Questions on Chemical Properties
  • Curd and Sour Substances: Should not be kept in brass and copper vessels because acids react with metals to produce hydrogen gas and metallic salts, which can be harmful.
  • Gas Liberated by Acid + Metal: Hydrogen gas (H₂).
  • Example: `Zn(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)`.
  • Test for H₂ gas: Bring a burning candle near the gas; it extinguishes with a 'pop' sound.
  • Metal Compound A: Reacts with dilute HCl to produce effervescence, and the gas extinguishes a burning candle. If one compound formed is calcium chloride (CaCl₂), then compound A is **calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)**.
  • Reaction: `CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)`. The gas evolved is CO₂.
2.2 What Do All Acids and All Bases Have in Common?
2.2.1 Acids and Conductivity
  • Common Property of Acids: All acids produce hydrogen gas when reacting with metals, suggesting hydrogen is common to all acids.
  • Activity 2.8 (Conductivity): Acids (HCl, H₂SO₄) conduct electricity in aqueous solutions, causing a bulb to glow. Glucose and alcohol solutions do not conduct.
  • Mechanism: Electric current is carried by ions in acidic solutions.
  • Acid Ions: Acids contain H⁺ ions as cations (e.g., Cl⁻ in HCl, NO₃⁻ in HNO₃, SO₄²⁻ in H₂SO₄, CH₃COO⁻ in CH₃COOH as anions).
  • Acidic Properties: H⁺(aq) ions produced by acids in solution are responsible for their acidic properties.
  • Bases (Alkalis): Also conduct electricity in aqueous solutions.
2.2.2 What Happens to an Acid or a Base in a Water Solution?
  • Activity 2.9 (HCl gas and Water): Dry HCl gas does not change the colour of dry litmus paper. HCl solution changes wet litmus paper colour.
  • Conclusion: Hydrogen ions in HCl are produced only in the presence of water. Separation of H⁺ from HCl molecules cannot occur without water.
  • Hydronium Ion: `HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻`. Hydrogen ions (H⁺) do not exist alone; they combine with water molecules to form hydronium ions (H₃O⁺) or are represented as H⁺(aq).
  • Bases in Water: Generate hydroxide (OH⁻) ions in water.
  • Examples: `NaOH(s) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)`, `KOH(s) → K⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)`, `Mg(OH)₂(s) → Mg²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq)`.
  • Alkalis: Bases that are soluble in water.
  • Do You Know? All bases do not dissolve in water. Alkalis are soapy, bitter, corrosive.
  • Neutralisation (Ionic): `H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)`.
2.2.3 Dilution of Acids and Bases
  • Activity 2.10 (Mixing Acid/Base with Water): Dissolving an acid or a base in water is a highly exothermic process (releases heat).
  • Safety Caution: Always add concentrated acid (or base) slowly to water with constant stirring, never water to concentrated acid. This prevents splashing and burns, and avoids container breakage from excessive heat.
  • Warning Sign: Containers of concentrated acids and bases often display a warning sign.
  • Dilution: The process of mixing an acid or base with water, resulting in a decrease in the concentration of ions (H₃O⁺/OH⁻) per unit volume. The acid or base is then said to be diluted.
2.2.4 Questions on Common Properties
  • Acidic Character: HCl, HNO₃ show acidic character in aqueous solutions because they produce H⁺(aq) ions. Alcohol and glucose do not produce H⁺ ions.
  • Conductivity of Acidic Solutions: An aqueous solution of an acid conducts electricity because the H⁺ ions and corresponding anions (e.g., Cl⁻, SO₄²⁻) act as charge carriers.
  • Dry HCl Gas: Does not change the colour of dry litmus paper because it does not dissociate into H⁺ ions in the absence of water.
  • Diluting Acids: Acid should be added to water slowly, not water to acid, to control the exothermic reaction and prevent splashing/burns.
  • Hydronium Ion Concentration (Dilution): When an acid solution is diluted, the concentration of hydronium ions (H₃O⁺) per unit volume decreases.
  • Hydroxide Ion Concentration (Excess Base): When excess base is dissolved in a sodium hydroxide solution, the concentration of hydroxide ions (OH⁻) per unit volume increases.
2.3 How Strong Are Acid or Base Solutions?
2.3.1 Universal Indicator and pH Scale
  • Universal Indicator: A mixture of several indicators that shows different colours at different concentrations of hydrogen ions in a solution, allowing quantitative measurement of ion amount.
  • pH Scale: A scale for measuring hydrogen ion concentration (H⁺) in a solution.
  • Potenz: The 'p' in pH stands for 'potenz' in German, meaning power.
  • pH Range: Generally from 0 (very acidic) to 14 (very alkaline/basic).
  • pH Value: Indicates the acidic or basic nature of a solution.
  • H⁺ Concentration & pH: Higher hydronium ion (H₃O⁺) concentration means a lower pH value.
  • Neutral Solution: pH of exactly 7.
  • Acidic Solution: pH values less than 7.
  • Basic Solution: pH values greater than 7 (from 7 to 14, indicating an increase in OH⁻ ion concentration).
  • pH Paper: Paper impregnated with universal indicator is commonly used.
  • Activity 2.11: Testing pH values of various common solutions (saliva, lemon juice, coffee, tap water, 1M NaOH, 1M HCl, etc.).
2.3.2 Strong and Weak Acids/Bases
  • Strength of Acids/Bases: Depends on the number of H⁺ ions (for acids) or OH⁻ ions (for bases) produced in solution.
  • Strong Acids: Acids that produce more H⁺ ions (e.g., hydrochloric acid, HCl).
  • Weak Acids: Acids that produce fewer H⁺ ions (e.g., acetic acid, CH₃COOH).
  • Strong Bases: Produce more OH⁻ ions.
  • Weak Bases: Produce fewer OH⁻ ions.
2.3.3 Importance of pH in Everyday Life
  • pH Sensitivity of Living Organisms: Our body works within a narrow pH range of 7.0 to 7.8. Survival is difficult outside this range.
  • Acid Rain: Rainwater with pH less than 5.6. Lowers the pH of river water, making survival difficult for aquatic life.
  • Acids in Other Planets (Do You Know?): Venus's atmosphere is thick clouds of sulphuric acid, unsuitable for life.
  • pH of Soil: Plants require a specific pH range for healthy growth. Activity 2.12 involves testing soil pH.
  • pH in Digestive System: Stomach produces hydrochloric acid (HCl) for digestion.
  • Indigestion: Excess acid production causes pain and irritation.
  • Antacids: Bases used to neutralise excess stomach acid (e.g., Magnesium hydroxide, Milk of magnesia).
  • Tooth Decay: Starts when mouth pH is lower than 5.5.
  • Tooth Enamel: Made of calcium hydroxyapatite (crystalline calcium phosphate), hardest substance in body. Corrodes below pH 5.5.
  • Cause of Decay: Bacteria in mouth produce acids by degrading sugars and food particles.
  • Prevention: Clean mouth after eating, use basic toothpastes to neutralise excess acid.
  • Self-defence by Animals and Plants:
  • Bee-sting: Leaves an acid causing pain/irritation. Relieved by mild bases like baking soda.
  • Nettle Sting: Stinging hair of nettle leaves inject methanoic acid, causing burning pain.
  • Nature Provides Neutralisation Options (Do You Know?): Rubbing with a dock plant leaf (often grows near nettle) provides relief, suggesting dock plant is basic.
  • Naturally Occurring Acids (Table 2.3):
    • Vinegar: Acetic acid
    • Orange, Lemon: Citric acid
    • Tamarind: Tartaric acid
    • Tomato: Oxalic acid
    • Sour milk (Curd): Lactic acid
    • Ant sting, Nettle sting: Methanoic acid
2.3.4 Questions on pH Importance
  • Solutions A (pH 6) and B (pH 8): Solution A has more hydrogen ion concentration (lower pH means higher H⁺ concentration). Solution A is acidic, Solution B is basic.
  • Effect of H⁺(aq) Concentration: Higher H⁺(aq) concentration makes the solution more acidic; lower H⁺(aq) concentration (and thus higher OH⁻(aq) concentration) makes it more basic.
  • Basic Solutions and H⁺(aq) Ions: Yes, basic solutions also have H⁺(aq) ions. They are basic because the concentration of hydroxide ions (OH⁻(aq)) is greater than the concentration of hydrogen ions (H⁺(aq)).
  • Soil Condition for Farmers: A farmer would treat acidic soil (low pH) with quick lime (calcium oxide), slaked lime (calcium hydroxide), or chalk (calcium carbonate) to neutralise the acidity and make it suitable for plant growth.
2.4 More About Salts
  • Salt Formation: Salts are formed during various reactions, such as acid-base neutralisation.
2.4.1 Family of Salts
  • Activity 2.13: Chemical formulae of salts (Potassium sulphate, sodium sulphate, calcium sulphate, magnesium sulphate, copper sulphate, sodium chloride, sodium nitrate, sodium carbonate, ammonium chloride). Identify parent acids and bases.
  • Salt Family: Salts having the same positive or negative radicals belong to a family.
  • Examples: NaCl and Na₂SO₄ belong to the family of sodium salts. NaCl and KCl belong to the family of chloride salts.
2.4.2 pH of Salts
  • Activity 2.14: Testing solubility, litmus action, and pH of various salt samples (sodium chloride, potassium nitrate, aluminium chloride, zinc sulphate, copper sulphate, sodium acetate, sodium carbonate, sodium hydrogencarbonate).
  • Neutral Salts: Formed from strong acid + strong base, pH value of 7.
  • Acidic Salts: Formed from strong acid + weak base, pH value less than 7.
  • Basic Salts: Formed from strong base + weak acid, pH value more than 7.
2.4.3 Chemicals from Common Salt
  • Common Salt: Sodium chloride (NaCl), formed from hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide, used in food. It is a neutral salt.
  • Source of Common Salt: Seawater, rock salt deposits (large crystals, often brown due to impurities), mined like coal.
  • Importance: Raw material for various daily use chemicals like sodium hydroxide, baking soda, washing soda, bleaching powder.
2.4.3.1 Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH)
  • Chlor-alkali Process: Electricity is passed through an aqueous solution of sodium chloride (brine).
  • Reaction: `2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl₂(g) + H₂(g)`.
  • Products: Chlorine gas (at anode), hydrogen gas (at cathode), sodium hydroxide solution (near cathode).
  • Uses of Products (Fig 2.8):
    • Hydrogen: Fuel, margarine, ammonia for fertilisers.
    • Chlorine: Water treatment, PVC, disinfectants, CFCs, pesticides.
    • NaOH: Degreasing metals, soaps, detergents, paper making, artificial fibres (rayon).
    • HCl (from H₂ + Cl₂): Cleaning steel, medicines, cosmetics.
    • Bleach (from NaOH + Cl₂): Household bleaches, bleaching fabric.
2.4.3.2 Bleaching Powder [Ca(ClO)₂]
  • Manufacture: Produced by the action of chlorine gas on dry slaked lime [Ca(OH)₂].
  • Reaction: `2Ca(OH)₂ + 2Cl₂ → Ca(ClO)₂ + CaCl₂ + 2H₂O`.
  • Composition: Actual composition is complex, often represented as `Ca(ClO)₂`.
  • Uses:
    • Bleaching cotton, linen (textile industry), wood pulp (paper factories), washed clothes (laundry).
    • Oxidising agent in many chemical industries.
    • Making drinking water free from germs.
2.4.3.3 Baking Soda (Sodium Hydrogencarbonate, NaHCO₃)
  • Common Use: In kitchen for cooking (e.g., pakoras, faster cooking).
  • Chemical Name: Sodium hydrogencarbonate.
  • Production: Uses sodium chloride as one of the raw materials.
  • Reaction: `NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ + NH₃ → NH₄Cl (Ammonium chloride) + NaHCO₃ (Sodium hydrogencarbonate)`.
  • Nature: Mild, non-corrosive basic salt (pH is slightly alkaline).
  • Reaction on Heating (during cooking): `2NaHCO₃ (Heat) → Na₂CO₃ (Sodium carbonate) + H₂O + CO₂`.
  • Uses:
    • Baking Powder: A mixture of baking soda and tartaric acid (a weak edible acid). When heated or mixed in water, it produces CO₂ gas.
    • CO₂ Production: `NaHCO₃ + H⁺ (from any acid) → CO₂ + H₂O + Sodium salt of acid`. This CO₂ causes bread/cake to rise, making them soft and spongy.
    • Antacid: Being alkaline, it neutralises excess stomach acid, providing relief.
    • Fire Extinguishers: Used in soda-acid fire extinguishers.
2.4.3.4 Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃.10H₂O)
  • Production: Obtained from sodium carbonate (which is formed by heating baking soda), followed by recrystallisation.
  • Nature: It is a basic salt.
  • Recrystallisation: `Na₂CO₃ + 10H₂O → Na₂CO₃.10H₂O`.
  • Uses:
    • In glass, soap, and paper industries.
    • In the manufacture of sodium compounds like borax.
    • As a cleaning agent for domestic purposes.
    • For removing permanent hardness of water.
2.4.4 Are the Crystals of Salts really Dry?
