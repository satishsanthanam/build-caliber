Chapter 2: Acids, Bases and Salts

2.0 Introduction to Acids, Bases and Salts
  • Acids: Substances that are sour in taste and change the colour of blue litmus to red.
  • Bases: Substances that are bitter in taste and change the colour of red litmus to blue.
  • Neutralization: Acids and bases have the ability to nullify each other’s effects.
  • Indicators: Substances used to test for the presence of acids and bases by changing colour.
  • Natural Indicators: Examples include litmus (a purple dye extracted from lichen), turmeric, red cabbage leaves, and coloured petals of flowers like Hydrangea, Petunia, and Geranium.
  • Synthetic Indicators: Examples include methyl orange and phenolphthalein.
  • Litmus solution: Purple when neither acidic nor basic, turns red in acid and blue in base.
  • Turmeric example: A curry stain (yellow) on white cloth turns reddish-brown with basic soap, then reverts to yellow when washed with water.
2.0.1 Introductory Question
  • Identifying solutions: To identify distilled water, acidic, and basic solutions with only red litmus paper:
    • Test all three solutions with red litmus paper.
    • The solution that turns red litmus blue is the basic solution.
    • Now, use the blue litmus paper (formed from the basic solution) to test the remaining two solutions.
    • The solution that turns blue litmus red is the acidic solution.
    • The solution that has no effect on either red or blue litmus paper is distilled water.
2.1 Understanding the Chemical Properties of Acids and Bases
  • This section explores how acids and bases react and interact in various chemical scenarios.
2.1.1 Acids and Bases in the Laboratory
  • Indicators: Tell us whether a substance is acidic or basic by a change in colour.
  • Olfactory Indicators: Substances whose odour changes in acidic or basic media.
  • Examples of Olfactory Indicators: Onion, vanilla essence, and clove oil.
  • Activity 2.2 (Onion test): The odour of onion disappears in basic solutions (NaOH) but remains in acidic solutions (HCl).
  • Activity 2.2 (Vanilla/Clove oil test): The odour of vanilla essence and clove oil disappears when added to a basic solution, but persists in an acidic solution.
2.1.2 How do Acids and Bases React with Metals?
  • Reaction of Acids with Metals: Acids react with active metals to displace hydrogen atoms, forming a salt and hydrogen gas.
  • General Equation: Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas
  • Example (Zinc with Sulphuric Acid): Zn(s) + H2SO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + H2(g)
  • Testing Hydrogen Gas: Hydrogen gas burns with a characteristic 'pop' sound when a burning candle is brought near it.
  • Reaction of Bases with Metals: Some metals react with strong bases to produce hydrogen gas and a salt.
  • Example (Zinc with Sodium Hydroxide): 2NaOH(aq) + Zn(s) → Na2ZnO2(s) + H2(g) (Sodium zincate)
  • Note: Such reactions are not possible with all metals.
2.1.3 How do Metal Carbonates and Metal Hydrogencarbonates React with Acids?
  • Reaction with Metal Carbonates: Metal carbonates react with acids to produce a corresponding salt, carbon dioxide gas, and water.
  • Example (Sodium Carbonate with HCl): Na2CO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g)
  • Reaction with Metal Hydrogencarbonates: Metal hydrogencarbonates also react with acids to produce a corresponding salt, carbon dioxide gas, and water.
  • Example (Sodium Hydrogencarbonate with HCl): NaHCO3(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g)
  • General Equation: Metal Carbonate/Hydrogencarbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water
  • Testing Carbon Dioxide Gas: Carbon dioxide gas is passed through lime water [Ca(OH)2 solution].
  • Lime Water Test: CO2(g) + Ca(OH)2(aq) → CaCO3(s) + H2O(l) (The formation of white precipitate of calcium carbonate makes lime water milky).
  • Excess Carbon Dioxide: If excess CO2 is passed, the white precipitate dissolves, forming soluble calcium hydrogencarbonate: CaCO3(s) + H2O(l) + CO2(g) → Ca(HCO3)2(aq).
  • Forms of Calcium Carbonate: Limestone, chalk, and marble are different forms of calcium carbonate.
2.1.4 How do Acids and Bases React with each other?
  • Neutralisation Reaction: The reaction between an acid and a base to give a salt and water is known as a neutralisation reaction.
  • General Equation: Base + Acid → Salt + Water
  • Example (Sodium Hydroxide with Hydrochloric Acid): NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)
  • Phenolphthalein in Neutralisation: Phenolphthalein changes from pink (in base) to colourless (after acid addition) and back to pink (with excess base).
2.1.5 Reaction of Metallic Oxides with Acids
  • Reaction of Metallic Oxides with Acids: Metallic oxides react with acids to form salt and water.
  • General Equation: Metal oxide + Acid → Salt + Water
  • Example (Copper Oxide with Hydrochloric Acid): CuO(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CuCl2(aq) + H2O(l) (The solution becomes blue-green due to copper(II) chloride formation).
  • Nature of Metallic Oxides: Metallic oxides are considered basic oxides because they react with acids similarly to how bases react with acids.
2.1.6 Reaction of a Non-metallic Oxide with Base
  • Reaction of Non-metallic Oxides with Bases: Non-metallic oxides react with bases to produce a salt and water.
  • Example (Carbon Dioxide with Calcium Hydroxide): CO2(g) + Ca(OH)2(aq) → CaCO3(s) + H2O(l)
  • Nature of Non-metallic Oxides: Non-metallic oxides are considered acidic oxides because their reaction with a base is similar to an acid-base neutralisation.
2.1.7 Important Questions on Chemical Properties
  • Why should curd and sour substances not be kept in brass and copper vessels? Curd and sour substances are acidic. They react with the metals (brass and copper) to form harmful or toxic compounds, making the food unfit for consumption.
  • Which gas is usually liberated when an acid reacts with a metal? Illustrate with an example. How will you test for the presence of this gas? Hydrogen gas (H2) is liberated. Example: Zn(s) + H2SO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + H2(g). Test: Bring a burning candle near the evolved gas; it extinguishes the flame with a 'pop' sound.
  • Metal compound A reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid to produce effervescence. The gas evolved extinguishes a burning candle. Write a balanced chemical equation for the reaction if one of the compounds formed is calcium chloride. The gas that extinguishes a burning candle is carbon dioxide (CO2). Since calcium chloride (CaCl2) is formed, compound A must be calcium carbonate. Equation: CaCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl2(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g).
2.2 What Do All Acids and All Bases Have in Common?
  • This section explores the fundamental components responsible for the similar chemical properties of acids and bases.
2.2.1 Conduction of Electricity by Acids and Bases
  • Acids Conduct Electricity: Aqueous solutions of acids (e.g., HCl, H2SO4) conduct electricity, causing a bulb to glow.
  • Non-Acidic Hydrogen Compounds: Solutions of compounds like glucose and alcohol (which contain hydrogen) do not conduct electricity and thus do not show acidic character.
  • Reason for Conduction: The flow of electric current through acidic solutions is due to the presence of mobile ions.
  • Ions in Acids: Acids produce H+ ions (as cations) and corresponding anions (e.g., Cl- in HCl, NO3- in HNO3, SO42- in H2SO4, CH3COO- in CH3COOH).
  • Acidic Properties: The H+(aq) ions produced in solution are responsible for the acidic properties.
  • Bases (Alkalis) Conduct Electricity: Similarly, aqueous solutions of alkalis (soluble bases like NaOH, Ca(OH)2) also conduct electricity due to the presence of ions.
2.2.2 What Happens to an Acid or a Base in a Water Solution?
  • Acidic Character Requires Water: Acids produce hydrogen ions (H+) only in the presence of water; the separation of H+ ions from acid molecules cannot occur in the absence of water.
  • Dry HCl Gas: Dry HCl gas does not change the colour of dry litmus paper because H+ ions are not produced without water.
  • Hydronium Ions: Hydrogen ions (H+) cannot exist alone; they combine with water molecules to form hydronium ions (H3O+).
  • Equation for HCl in Water: HCl(g) + H2O(l) → H3O+(aq) + Cl-(aq) (often simplified as H+(aq)).
  • Bases in Water: Bases dissolve in water to generate hydroxide (OH-) ions.
  • Examples: NaOH(s) → Na+(aq) + OH-(aq); KOH(s) → K+(aq) + OH-(aq); Mg(OH)2(s) → Mg2+(aq) + 2OH-(aq).
  • Alkalis: Bases that are soluble in water are called alkalis. They are soapy, bitter, and corrosive.
  • Ionic Neutralisation Reaction: The common reaction for neutralisation in water is H+(aq) + OH-(aq) → H2O(l).
2.2.3 Dilution of Acids and Bases
  • Exothermic Process: The process of dissolving an acid or a base in water is a highly exothermic one (releases significant heat).
  • Safety Precaution: Always add concentrated acid (or base) slowly to water with constant stirring. Never add water to a concentrated acid.
  • Reason for Safety: If water is added to a concentrated acid, the heat generated can cause the mixture to splash out, leading to burns, or the glass container may break due to excessive local heating.
  • Dilution: Mixing an acid or base with water results in a decrease in the concentration of ions (H3O+ or OH-) per unit volume.
  • Diluted Solution: An acid or base is said to be diluted after this process.
2.2.4 Important Questions on Common Properties
  • Why do HCl, HNO3, etc., show acidic characters in aqueous solutions while solutions of compounds like alcohol and glucose do not show acidic character? Acids like HCl and HNO3 dissociate in aqueous solutions to produce H+(aq) ions, which are responsible for their acidic character. Alcohol and glucose, despite containing hydrogen, do not dissociate to produce H+ ions in water.
  • Why does an aqueous solution of an acid conduct electricity? An aqueous solution of an acid conducts electricity because acids dissociate in water to produce free H+ ions (as H3O+) and corresponding anions, which can carry electric charge.
  • Why does dry HCl gas not change the colour of the dry litmus paper? Dry HCl gas does not change the colour of dry litmus paper because in the absence of water, it cannot dissociate to produce H+ ions. Acidity is exhibited only when H+ ions are formed.
  • While diluting an acid, why is it recommended that the acid should be added to water and not water to the acid? Diluting an acid is a highly exothermic process. If water is added to a concentrated acid, the large amount of heat generated suddenly can cause the mixture to splash out, causing severe burns, or the glass container may crack due to extreme local heating. Adding acid slowly to water ensures that the heat is distributed and absorbed by the larger volume of water.
  • How is the concentration of hydronium ions (H3O+) affected when a solution of an acid is diluted? When a solution of an acid is diluted, the concentration of hydronium ions (H3O+) per unit volume decreases.
  • How is the concentration of hydroxide ions (OH-) affected when excess base is dissolved in a solution of sodium hydroxide? When excess base is dissolved in a solution of sodium hydroxide, the concentration of hydroxide ions (OH-) per unit volume increases.
2.3 How Strong are Acid or Base Solutions?
  • This section introduces quantitative measures to determine the strength of acids and bases.
2.3.1 The pH Scale
  • Universal Indicator: A mixture of several indicators that shows different colours at different concentrations of hydrogen ions in a solution.
  • pH Scale: A scale developed to measure the hydrogen ion concentration in a solution. The 'p' in pH stands for 'potenz' (German for power).
  • pH Range: Generally measures pH from 0 (very acidic) to 14 (very alkaline).
  • pH and Acidity: Higher the hydronium ion (H3O+) concentration, lower is the pH value (more acidic).
  • Neutral Solution: Has a pH of exactly 7.
  • Acidic Solution: Has a pH value less than 7.
  • Basic (Alkaline) Solution: Has a pH value more than 7. As pH increases from 7 to 14, it indicates an increase in OH- ion concentration and thus an increase in the strength of the alkali.
  • Measuring pH: Universal indicator paper is commonly used for measuring pH.
  • Strength of Acids and Bases: Depends on the number of H+ ions (for acids) or OH- ions (for bases) produced, respectively.
  • Strong Acids: Acids that give rise to more H+ ions (e.g., HCl, H2SO4).
  • Weak Acids: Acids that give less H+ ions (e.g., CH3COOH).
  • Strong Bases: Bases that produce more OH- ions (e.g., NaOH, KOH).
  • Weak Bases: Bases that produce less OH- ions (e.g., Mg(OH)2, NH4OH).
2.3.2 Importance of pH in Everyday Life
  • pH Sensitivity of Living Organisms: Plants and animals survive within a narrow pH range (e.g., human body: 7.0 to 7.8).
  • Acid Rain: Rainwater with pH less than 5.6 is called acid rain. It lowers the pH of river water, making it difficult for aquatic life to survive.
  • Soil pH: Plants require a specific pH range for healthy growth. Farmers adjust soil pH if it is too acidic or too basic.
  • pH in our Digestive System: The stomach produces hydrochloric acid (HCl) for digestion. During indigestion, excess acid causes pain and irritation.
  • Antacids: Mild bases like magnesium hydroxide (Milk of Magnesia) are used as antacids to neutralise excess stomach acid, providing relief.
  • pH Change as Cause of Tooth Decay: Tooth decay starts when the pH of the mouth is lower than 5.5.
  • Tooth Enamel: Made of calcium hydroxyapatite (hardest substance in the body), which corrodes when mouth pH falls below 5.5.
  • Prevention of Tooth Decay: Bacteria in the mouth produce acids from sugar and food particles. Using basic toothpastes neutralises these acids and prevents decay.
  • Self-defence by Animals and Plants:
    • Bee-sting: Leaves an acid (formic acid/methanoic acid) causing pain and irritation. Relieved by a mild base like baking soda.
    • Nettle leaves: Stinging hairs inject methanoic acid, causing burning pain. A traditional remedy is rubbing the area with the dock plant leaf (which is basic).
  • Naturally Occurring Acids:
    • Vinegar: Acetic acid
    • Orange/Lemon: Citric acid
    • Tamarind: Tartaric acid
    • Tomato: Oxalic acid
    • Sour milk (Curd): Lactic acid
    • Ant sting/Nettle sting: Methanoic acid
2.3.3 Important Questions on pH Strength and Importance
  • You have two solutions, A and B. The pH of solution A is 6 and pH of solution B is 8. Which solution has more hydrogen ion concentration? Which of this is acidic and which one is basic? Solution A (pH 6) has more hydrogen ion concentration. Solution A is acidic, and Solution B (pH 8) is basic.
  • What effect does the concentration of H+(aq) ions have on the nature of the solution? Higher the concentration of H+(aq) ions, the more acidic the solution is (lower pH). Lower the concentration of H+(aq) ions, the more basic the solution is (higher pH).
  • Do basic solutions also have H+(aq) ions? If yes, then why are these basic? Yes, basic solutions also have H+(aq) ions. However, they are basic because the concentration of hydroxide ions (OH-) is greater than the concentration of hydrogen ions (H+) in them.
  • Under what soil condition do you think a farmer would treat the soil of his fields with quick lime (calcium oxide) or slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) or chalk (calcium carbonate)? A farmer would treat the soil with quick lime, slaked lime, or chalk when the soil is acidic. These compounds are basic and help to neutralise the excess acidity in the soil, making it suitable for plant growth.
2.4 More About Salts
  • This section delves deeper into the preparation, properties, and various uses of salts.
2.4.1 Family of Salts
  • Salt Formation: Salts are formed from the reaction of acids and bases.
  • Salt Families: Salts having the same positive radical (cation) or negative radical (anion) are said to belong to a family.
  • Examples: NaCl and Na2SO4 belong to the family of sodium salts (same positive radical, Na+). NaCl and KCl belong to the family of chloride salts (same negative radical, Cl-).
2.4.2 pH of Salts
  • Salts of Strong Acid and Strong Base: These salts are neutral, with a pH value of 7 (e.g., NaCl, KNO3).
  • Salts of Strong Acid and Weak Base: These salts are acidic in nature, with a pH value less than 7 (e.g., AlCl3, ZnSO4, CuSO4).
  • Salts of Weak Acid and Strong Base: These salts are basic in nature, with a pH value more than 7 (e.g., CH3COONa, Na2CO3, NaHCO3).
2.4.3 Chemicals from Common Salt
  • Common Salt (Sodium Chloride, NaCl): Formed by the combination of hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide, it is a neutral salt used in food.
  • Sources: Obtained from seawater and rock salt deposits (large, often brown crystals due to impurities, mined like coal).
  • Importance: Common salt is a crucial raw material for various chemicals like sodium hydroxide, baking soda, washing soda, and bleaching powder.
2.4.3.1 Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH)
  • Production: When electricity is passed through an aqueous solution of sodium chloride (called brine), it decomposes to form sodium hydroxide.
  • Process Name: This process is called the chlor-alkali process (because of the products: 'chlor' for chlorine and 'alkali' for sodium hydroxide).
  • Equation: 2NaCl(aq) + 2H2O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl2(g) + H2(g)
  • Product Locations: Chlorine gas (Cl2) is given off at the anode, hydrogen gas (H2) at the cathode, and sodium hydroxide solution (NaOH) is formed near the cathode.
  • Uses: All three products are useful. NaOH is used for degreasing metals, in the manufacture of soaps, detergents, paper, and artificial fibres.
2.4.3.2 Bleaching Powder [Ca(ClO)2]
  • Production: Manufactured by the action of chlorine gas (produced during chlor-alkali process) on dry slaked lime [Ca(OH)2].
  • Equation: 2Ca(OH)2 + 2Cl2 → Ca(ClO)2 + CaCl2 + 2H2O (The actual composition is complex).
  • Uses:
    • For bleaching cotton, linen (textile industry), wood pulp (paper factories), and washed clothes (laundry).
    • As an oxidising agent in many chemical industries.
    • To make drinking water free from germs (disinfectant).
2.4.3.3 Baking Soda (Sodium Hydrogencarbonate, NaHCO3)
  • Common Use: Commonly used in the kitchen for making crispy pakoras and for faster cooking.
  • Chemical Name: Sodium hydrogencarbonate.
  • Formula: NaHCO3.
  • Production: Produced using sodium chloride as one of the raw materials: NaCl + H2O + CO2 + NH3 → NH4Cl + NaHCO3 (Ammonium chloride + Sodium hydrogencarbonate).
  • Nature: It is a mild, non-corrosive basic salt, which can neutralise acids.
  • Heating Reaction: When heated during cooking, it decomposes: 2NaHCO3 (Heat)→ Na2CO3 + H2O + CO2 (forms sodium carbonate, water, and carbon dioxide).
  • Uses:
    • Baking powder: A mixture of baking soda and a mild edible acid (e.g., tartaric acid). When heated or mixed in water, it produces CO2, which makes bread or cake soft and spongy by causing them to rise.
    • Antacid: Being alkaline, it neutralises excess acid in the stomach and provides relief from indigestion.
    • Fire extinguishers: Used in soda-acid fire extinguishers to produce CO2, which helps to smother fires.
2.4.3.4 Washing Soda (Sodium Carbonate Decahydrate, Na2CO3.10H2O)
  • Production: Obtained from sodium carbonate (which is formed by heating baking soda) through recrystallisation.
  • Formula: Na2CO3.10H2O.
  • Nature: It is a basic salt.
  • Uses:
    • In glass, soap, and paper industries.
    • In the manufacture of sodium compounds like borax.
    • As a cleaning agent for domestic purposes.
    • For removing the permanent hardness of water.
2.4.4 Are the Crystals of Salts really Dry?
  • Water of Crystallisation: The fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt. Crystals that appear dry may contain this water.
  • Copper Sulphate Example: Hydrated copper sulphate (CuSO4.5H2O) crystals are blue. When heated, they lose water and turn white (anhydrous copper sulphate). Adding water restores the blue colour.
  • Gypsum: A salt that possesses water of crystallisation, with the chemical formula CaSO4.2H2O (two water molecules).
  • Plaster of Paris (PoP): Calcium sulphate hemihydrate (CaSO4.½H2O).
  • Production of PoP: Heating gypsum at 373 K (100°C) causes it to lose water molecules.
  • Setting of PoP: Plaster of Paris is a white powder that, on mixing with water, changes back to gypsum, giving a hard solid mass.
  • Equation for Setting: CaSO4.½H2O + 1½H2O → CaSO4.2H2O
  • Why ½H2O? It indicates that two formula units of CaSO4 share one molecule of water.
  • Uses of PoP: Used by doctors for supporting fractured bones, for making toys, decorative materials, and for making surfaces smooth.
2.4.5 Important Questions on Salts
  • What is the common name of the compound Ca(ClO)2? Bleaching powder.
  • Name the substance which on treatment with chlorine yields bleaching powder. Dry slaked lime [Ca(OH)2].
  • Name the sodium compound which is used for softening hard water. Washing soda (Na2CO3.10H2O).
  • What will happen if a solution of sodium hydrogencarbonate is heated? Give the equation of the reaction involved. If a solution of sodium hydrogencarbonate (baking soda) is heated, it decomposes to form sodium carbonate, water, and carbon dioxide gas. Equation: 2NaHCO3 (Heat)→ Na2CO3 + H2O + CO2.
  • Write an equation to show the reaction between Plaster of Paris and water. CaSO4.½H2O + 1½H2O → CaSO4.2H2O (Plaster of Paris reacts with water to form Gypsum).
2.5 What You Have Learnt (Chapter Summary)
  • Acid-base indicators: Dyes or mixtures of dyes used to indicate the presence of acids and bases through colour change.
  • Acidic nature: Due to the formation of H+(aq) ions in solution.
  • Basic nature: Due to the formation of OH-(aq) ions in solution.
  • Acid + Metal reaction: Evolves hydrogen gas and forms a corresponding salt.
  • Base + Metal reaction: Forms a salt with a negative ion composed of the metal and oxygen, along with the evolution of hydrogen gas.
  • Acid + Metal Carbonate/Hydrogencarbonate reaction: Gives the corresponding salt, carbon dioxide gas, and water.
  • Electrical conductivity: Acidic and basic solutions in water conduct electricity because they produce hydrogen and hydroxide ions, respectively.
  • pH scale: A scale (0-14) that gives the measure of hydrogen ion concentration in a solution, used to test the strength of an acid or an alkali.
  • pH values: A neutral solution has a pH of exactly 7, an acidic solution has a pH less than 7, and a basic solution has a pH more than 7.
  • Living beings and pH: Carry out their metabolic activities within an optimal pH range.
  • Dilution: Mixing concentrated acids or bases with water is a highly exothermic process, requiring careful procedure.
  • Neutralisation: Acids and bases neutralise each other to form corresponding salts and water.
  • Water of crystallisation: The fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt.
  • Salts: Have various uses in everyday life and in industries.
