Chapter 1: Chemical Reactions and Equations

1.0 Introduction to Chemical Reactions
  • Daily Life Observations: Many everyday situations involve chemical changes, indicating chemical reactions.
  • Chemical Change: Any event where the nature and identity of initial substances change.
  • Chemical Reaction Indicators: Observations that suggest a chemical reaction has taken place.
1.0.1 Activity 1.1: Burning of a Magnesium Ribbon
  • Procedure: Clean a 3-4 cm magnesium ribbon, hold with tongs, burn with a spirit lamp, and collect the ash.
  • Caution: Requires teacher assistance, suitable eyeglasses, burn away from eyes.
  • Observation: Magnesium ribbon burns with a dazzling white flame and changes into a white powder.
  • Product: The white powder is magnesium oxide (MgO), formed from magnesium and oxygen in air.
1.0.2 Activity 1.2: Lead Nitrate and Potassium Iodide Solutions
  • Procedure: Add potassium iodide solution to lead nitrate solution in a test tube.
  • Observation: Formation of a yellow precipitate, which is lead(II) iodide (PbI2).
1.0.3 Activity 1.3: Zinc Granules with Dilute Acids
  • Procedure: Take zinc granules in a conical flask, add dilute hydrochloric acid or sulphuric acid.
  • Caution: Handle acid with care.
  • Observation: Bubbles of gas are formed around zinc granules. The flask or test tube feels warm (change in temperature).
  • Product: Hydrogen gas (H2) is evolved, and zinc salt (e.g., zinc sulphate) is formed.
1.0.4 Identifying Chemical Reactions
  • Indications: A chemical reaction can be determined by:
  • Change in State: Reactants and products may have different physical states.
  • Change in Colour: A new substance with a different colour may form.
  • Evolution of a Gas: Formation of gas bubbles.
  • Change in Temperature: The reaction mixture may become warmer or colder.
1.1 Chemical Equations
  • Purpose: Chemical equations represent chemical reactions concisely using symbols.
1.1.1 Writing a Chemical Equation
  • Word-Equation: A simple way to represent a reaction, showing reactants turning into products.
  • Reactants: Substances that undergo chemical change, written on the Left-Hand Side (LHS) with a plus (+) sign between them.
  • Products: New substances formed during the reaction, written on the Right-Hand Side (RHS) with a plus (+) sign between them.
  • Arrow (→): Points towards products, indicating the direction of the reaction.
  • Example (Word): Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide
  • Chemical Formulae: Using chemical symbols and formulae makes equations more concise and useful.
  • Example (Formula): Mg + O2 → MgO
  • Skeletal Chemical Equation: An unbalanced chemical equation where the number of atoms of each element is not the same on both sides.
1.1.2 Balanced Chemical Equations
  • Law of Conservation of Mass: Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.
  • Implication: The total mass and number of atoms of each element must be equal on both reactant and product sides.
  • Balancing Necessity: Skeletal equations must be balanced to obey the law of conservation of mass.
  • Hit-and-Trial Method: A method of balancing chemical equations by using the smallest whole number coefficients through trials.
1.1.2.1 Step I: Drawing Boxes
  • Action: Draw boxes around each chemical formula in the equation.
  • Rule: Do not change anything inside the boxes while balancing.
  • Example: Fe + H2O → Fe3O4 + H2
1.1.2.2 Step II: Listing Atoms
  • Action: List the number of atoms of each element in reactants and products.
  • Purpose: Identify which elements are unbalanced.
  • Example (Fe + H2O → Fe3O4 + H2):
    • Fe: 1 (LHS), 3 (RHS)
    • H: 2 (LHS), 2 (RHS)
    • O: 1 (LHS), 4 (RHS)
1.1.2.3 Step III: Balancing Oxygen Atoms
  • Strategy: Start with the compound containing the maximum number of atoms, and then select the element within it that has the maximum number of atoms.
  • Application: In Fe3O4, oxygen has 4 atoms on RHS, 1 on LHS (in H2O).
  • Action: Place coefficient '4' before H2O on LHS.
  • Result: Fe + 4H2O → Fe3O4 + H2
1.1.2.4 Step IV: Balancing Hydrogen Atoms
  • Current State: H atoms: 8 (LHS in 4H2O), 2 (RHS in H2).
  • Action: Place coefficient '4' before H2 on RHS.
  • Result: Fe + 4H2O → Fe3O4 + 4H2
1.1.2.5 Step V: Balancing Iron Atoms
  • Current State: Fe atoms: 1 (LHS in Fe), 3 (RHS in Fe3O4).
  • Action: Place coefficient '3' before Fe on LHS.
  • Result: 3Fe + 4H2O → Fe3O4 + 4H2
1.1.2.6 Step VI: Checking Balance
  • Action: Count atoms of each element on both sides.
  • Result (3Fe + 4H2O → Fe3O4 + 4H2):
    • Fe: 3 (LHS), 3 (RHS)
    • H: 8 (LHS), 8 (RHS)
    • O: 4 (LHS), 4 (RHS)
  • Conclusion: The equation is now balanced.
1.1.2.7 Step VII: Writing Symbols of Physical States
  • Purpose: To make chemical equations more informative.
  • Notations:
    • (g): Gaseous state
    • (l): Liquid state
    • (aq): Aqueous solution (solution in water)
    • (s): Solid state
  • Example: 3Fe(s) + 4H2O(g) → Fe3O4(s) + 4H2(g) (H2O(g) indicates steam)
  • Note: Physical states are usually included only when necessary for specificity.
1.1.2.8 Indicating Reaction Conditions
  • Placement: Conditions like temperature, pressure, or catalyst are shown above and/or below the arrow.
  • Example (Pressure): CO(g) + 2H2(g) →2340atm CH3OH(l)
  • Example (Light & Catalyst): 6CO2(aq) + 12H2O(l) →SunlightChlorophyll C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) + 6H2O(l)
1.2 Types of Chemical Reactions
  • Fundamental Principle: Atoms do not change into other elements; reactions involve breaking and making bonds to form new substances.
1.2.1 Combination Reaction
  • Definition: A reaction in which two or more substances (elements or compounds) combine to form a single new product.
1.2.1.1 Activity 1.4: Reaction of Calcium Oxide with Water
  • Procedure: Add water slowly to calcium oxide (quick lime) in a beaker.
  • Observation: Vigorous reaction, beaker becomes warm (heat released).
  • Reaction: CaO(s) + H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq) + Heat
  • Products: Slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) is formed.
1.2.1.2 Applications and Examples of Combination Reactions
  • Whitewashing: Slaked lime solution (Ca(OH)2) is used for whitewashing walls.
  • Carbonation: Ca(OH)2 reacts slowly with CO2 in air to form a thin layer of calcium carbonate (CaCO3), giving a shiny finish.
  • Marble Formula: The chemical formula for marble is also CaCO3.
  • Burning of Coal: C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g)
  • Formation of Water: 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l)
1.2.1.3 Exothermic Reactions
  • Definition: Reactions in which heat is released along with the formation of products, making the reaction mixture warm.
  • Example (Activity 1.4): The reaction of quick lime with water is exothermic.
  • Burning of Natural Gas: CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)
  • Respiration: A process where glucose combines with oxygen in cells to provide energy; C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) → 6CO2(aq) + 6H2O(l) + energy.
  • Decomposition of Vegetable Matter: Forming compost is also an exothermic reaction.
1.2.2 Decomposition Reaction
  • Definition: A single reactant breaks down to give simpler products.
1.2.2.1 Activity 1.5: Thermal Decomposition of Ferrous Sulphate
  • Procedure: Heat ferrous sulphate crystals in a dry boiling tube.
  • Observation: Green colour changes, and characteristic odour of burning sulphur is detected.
  • Reaction: 2FeSO4(s) →Heat Fe2O3(s) + SO2(g) + SO3(g)
  • Products: Ferric oxide (solid), sulphur dioxide (gas), and sulphur trioxide (gas).
  • Thermal Decomposition: Decomposition reactions carried out by heating.
1.2.2.2 Industrial Applications and Examples of Thermal Decomposition
  • Limestone Decomposition: CaCO3(s) →Heat CaO(s) + CO2(g)
  • Products: Calcium oxide (lime or quick lime) and carbon dioxide.
  • Uses of Quick Lime: Used in various industries, including cement manufacture.
1.2.2.3 Activity 1.6: Thermal Decomposition of Lead Nitrate
  • Procedure: Heat lead nitrate powder in a boiling tube.
  • Observation: Emission of brown fumes.
  • Products: Brown fumes are nitrogen dioxide (NO2).
  • Reaction: 2Pb(NO3)2(s) →Heat 2PbO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)
1.2.2.4 Activity 1.7: Electrolytic Decomposition of Water
  • Procedure: Electrolysis of water with dilute sulphuric acid using carbon electrodes connected to a 6-volt battery.
  • Observation: Bubbles form at both electrodes, displacing water in test tubes. Volume of gas at one electrode is double that at the other.
  • Gases Formed: Hydrogen gas (H2) is collected at the cathode (double volume), and oxygen gas (O2) is collected at the anode.
  • Reaction: 2H2O(l) →Electricity 2H2(g) + O2(g)
1.2.2.5 Activity 1.8: Photolytic Decomposition of Silver Chloride
  • Procedure: Place silver chloride in a china dish in sunlight.
  • Observation: White silver chloride turns grey.
  • Reaction: 2AgCl(s) →Sunlight 2Ag(s) + Cl2(g)
  • Similar Reaction: Silver bromide (AgBr) also decomposes by light: 2AgBr(s) →Sunlight 2Ag(s) + Br2(g)
  • Application: These reactions are used in black and white photography.
1.2.2.6 Endothermic Reactions
  • Definition: Reactions in which energy (heat, light, or electricity) is absorbed to break down reactants.
  • Example: Decomposition reactions generally require energy absorption.
  • Example (Barium Hydroxide + Ammonium Chloride): Mixing these causes the test tube to feel cold, indicating an endothermic reaction.
1.2.3 Displacement Reaction
  • Definition: A reaction where a more reactive element displaces or removes another less reactive element from its compound.
1.2.3.1 Activity 1.9: Iron Nails in Copper Sulphate Solution
  • Procedure: Dip iron nails in copper sulphate solution (Test tube B) and compare with a control (Test tube A).
  • Observation: Iron nails become brownish, and the blue colour of copper sulphate solution fades.
  • Explanation: Iron is more reactive than copper and displaces it.
  • Reaction: Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
  • Products: Iron sulphate (greenish solution) and copper metal (brown coating).
1.2.3.2 Examples of Displacement Reactions
  • Zinc and Copper Sulphate: Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
  • Lead and Copper Chloride: Pb(s) + CuCl2(aq) → PbCl2(aq) + Cu(s)
  • Reactivity: Zinc and lead are more reactive than copper, displacing it from its compounds.
1.2.4 Double Displacement Reaction
  • Definition: Reactions in which there is an exchange of ions between the reactants.
1.2.4.1 Activity 1.10: Sodium Sulphate and Barium Chloride Solutions
  • Procedure: Mix sodium sulphate solution with barium chloride solution.
  • Observation: A white insoluble substance is formed.
  • Precipitate: This insoluble substance is called a precipitate.
  • Precipitation Reaction: Any reaction that produces a precipitate.
  • Reaction: Na2SO4(aq) + BaCl2(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2NaCl(aq)
  • Explanation: Ba2+ and SO42- ions combine to form insoluble BaSO4. Sodium chloride (NaCl) remains in solution.
1.2.4.2 Further Examples
  • Lead(II) Nitrate and Potassium Iodide (Activity 1.2): Pb(NO3)2(aq) + 2KI(aq) → PbI2(s) + 2KNO3(aq). This is also a double displacement reaction, producing a yellow precipitate of lead(II) iodide.
1.2.5 Oxidation and Reduction
  • Redox Reactions: Reactions where one reactant gets oxidised while the other gets reduced.
1.2.5.1 Activity 1.11: Heating Copper Powder
  • Procedure: Heat copper powder in a china dish.
  • Observation: Copper surface becomes coated with black copper(II) oxide.
  • Oxidation of Copper: 2Cu + O2 →Heat 2CuO. Copper gains oxygen.
  • Reduction of Copper Oxide: If hydrogen gas is passed over heated CuO, the black coating turns brown (copper is obtained).
  • Reaction: CuO + H2 →Heat Cu + H2O. Copper(II) oxide loses oxygen (reduced), hydrogen gains oxygen (oxidised).
1.2.5.2 Definitions of Oxidation and Reduction
  • Oxidation (Oxygen Perspective): A substance gains oxygen during a reaction.
  • Reduction (Oxygen Perspective): A substance loses oxygen during a reaction.
  • Oxidation (Hydrogen Perspective): A substance loses hydrogen during a reaction.
  • Reduction (Hydrogen Perspective): A substance gains hydrogen during a reaction.
1.2.5.3 Examples of Redox Reactions
  • Zinc Oxide and Carbon: ZnO + C → Zn + CO. Carbon is oxidised to CO, ZnO is reduced to Zn.
  • Manganese Dioxide and Hydrochloric Acid: MnO2 + 4HCl → MnCl2 + 2H2O + Cl2. HCl is oxidised to Cl2, MnO2 is reduced to MnCl2.
  • Magnesium Burning: In Activity 1.1, magnesium is oxidised as it gains oxygen to form magnesium oxide.
1.3 Effects of Oxidation Reactions in Everyday Life
1.3.1 Corrosion
  • Definition: The process by which metals are attacked by substances around them (moisture, acids, etc.), leading to their deterioration.
  • Rusting of Iron: Iron articles develop a reddish-brown powder (rust) when exposed to humid air.
  • Black Coating on Silver: An example of silver corrosion.
  • Green Coating on Copper: An example of copper corrosion.
  • Impact: Causes significant damage to infrastructure like car bodies, bridges, iron railings, and ships, incurring high replacement costs.
  • Prevention: Painting iron articles prevents direct contact with moisture and oxygen, thus preventing corrosion.
1.3.2 Rancidity
  • Definition: The process where fats and oils in food materials get oxidised, leading to a change in their smell and taste.
  • Prevention Methods:
    • Antioxidants: Adding substances that prevent oxidation to food.
    • Air-tight Containers: Keeping food in air-tight containers slows down oxidation.
    • Nitrogen Flushing: Manufacturers flush bags of chips with inert gases like nitrogen to prevent the chips from getting oxidised.
1.4 Summary: What You Have Learnt
  • Chemical Equation: Represents reactants, products, and their physical states symbolically.
  • Balanced Equation: Ensures equal numbers of atoms of each type on reactant and product sides, adhering to the law of conservation of mass.
  • Combination Reaction: Two or more substances combine to form a single new substance.
  • Decomposition Reaction: A single substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances (opposite of combination).
  • Exothermic Reaction: Releases heat along with product formation.
  • Endothermic Reaction: Absorbs energy from the surroundings.
  • Displacement Reaction: One element displaces another from its compound.
  • Double Displacement Reaction: Exchange of ions between reactants, often producing a precipitate.
  • Precipitation Reaction: Produces insoluble salts.
  • Oxidation: Gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen.
  • Reduction: Loss of oxygen or gain of hydrogen.

1.6.2 Short Answer and Explanatory Questions
  • 4. Balanced Chemical Equation: A chemical equation where the number of atoms of each element is equal on both sides of the reaction. Equations should be balanced to satisfy the Law of Conservation of Mass, which states mass cannot be created or destroyed.
  • 5. Balanced Chemical Equations from Statements:
    • (a) Hydrogen gas combines with nitrogen to form ammonia: 3H2(g) + N2(g) → 2NH3(g)
    • (b) Hydrogen sulphide gas burns in air to give water and sulphur dioxide: 2H2S(g) + 3O2(g) → 2H2O(l) + 2SO2(g)
    • (c) Barium chloride reacts with aluminium sulphate to give aluminium chloride and a precipitate of barium sulphate: 3BaCl2(aq) + Al2(SO4)3(aq) → 2AlCl3(aq) + 3BaSO4(s)
    • (d) Potassium metal reacts with water to give potassium hydroxide and hydrogen gas: 2K(s) + 2H2O(l) → 2KOH(aq) + H2(g)
  • 6. Balancing Chemical Equations:
    • (a) HNO3 + Ca(OH)2 → Ca(NO3)2 + H2O: 2HNO3 + Ca(OH)2 → Ca(NO3)2 + 2H2O
    • (b) NaOH + H2SO4 → Na2SO4 + H2O: 2NaOH + H2SO4 → Na2SO4 + 2H2O
    • (c) NaCl + AgNO3 → AgCl + NaNO3: NaCl + AgNO3 → AgCl + NaNO3 (Already balanced)
    • (d) BaCl2 + H2SO4 → BaSO4 + HCl: BaCl2 + H2SO4 → BaSO4 + 2HCl
  • 7. Balanced Chemical Equations:
    • (a) Calcium hydroxide + Carbon dioxide → Calcium carbonate + Water: Ca(OH)2(aq) + CO2(g) → CaCO3(s) + H2O(l)
    • (b) Zinc + Silver nitrate → Zinc nitrate + Silver: Zn(s) + 2AgNO3(aq) → Zn(NO3)2(aq) + 2Ag(s)
    • (c) Aluminium + Copper chloride → Aluminium chloride + Copper: 2Al(s) + 3CuCl2(aq) → 2AlCl3(aq) + 3Cu(s)
    • (d) Barium chloride + Potassium sulphate → Barium sulphate + Potassium chloride: BaCl2(aq) + K2SO4(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2KCl(aq)
  • 8. Balanced Chemical Equations and Reaction Types:
    • (a) Potassium bromide(aq) + Barium iodide(aq) → Potassium iodide(aq) + Barium bromide(s): 2KBr(aq) + BaI2(aq) → 2KI(aq) + BaBr2(s). Type: Double Displacement Reaction.
    • (b) Zinc carbonate(s) → Zinc oxide(s) + Carbon dioxide(g): ZnCO3(s) → ZnO(s) + CO2(g). Type: Decomposition Reaction.
    • (c) Hydrogen(g) + Chlorine(g) → Hydrogen chloride(g): H2(g) + Cl2(g) → 2HCl(g). Type: Combination Reaction.
    • (d) Magnesium(s) + Hydrochloric acid(aq) → Magnesium chloride(aq) + Hydrogen(g): Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl2(aq) + H2(g). Type: Displacement Reaction.
  • 9. Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions:
    • Exothermic Reactions: Release heat energy. Example: Burning of natural gas (CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g) + Heat).
    • Endothermic Reactions: Absorb heat energy. Example: Decomposition of calcium carbonate (CaCO3(s) →Heat CaO(s) + CO2(g)).
  • 10. Respiration as Exothermic Reaction: Respiration breaks down glucose in the presence of oxygen to release energy for life processes. Since energy is released, it is considered an exothermic reaction (C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) → 6CO2(aq) + 6H2O(l) + Energy).
  • 11. Decomposition vs. Combination Reactions: Decomposition reactions are opposite to combination reactions. In combination, two or more substances combine to form a single product (A + B → AB). In decomposition, a single reactant breaks down into two or more products (AB → A + B).
  • 12. Decomposition Reactions with Energy Forms:
    • Heat (Thermal): 2Pb(NO3)2(s) →Heat 2PbO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)
    • Light (Photolytic): 2AgCl(s) →Sunlight 2Ag(s) + Cl2(g)
    • Electricity (Electrolytic): 2H2O(l) →Electricity 2H2(g) + O2(g)
  • 13. Displacement vs. Double Displacement Reactions:
    • Displacement Reaction: A more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. Example: Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s).
    • Double Displacement Reaction: Two compounds exchange ions to form two new compounds. Example: Na2SO4(aq) + BaCl2(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2NaCl(aq).
  • 14. Silver Refining Reaction: In the refining of silver from silver nitrate solution by copper metal, copper displaces silver: Cu(s) + 2AgNO3(aq) → Cu(NO3)2(aq) + 2Ag(s).
  • 15. Precipitation Reaction: A reaction that produces an insoluble substance (precipitate). Example: When barium chloride solution is mixed with sodium sulphate solution, a white precipitate of barium sulphate forms: BaCl2(aq) + Na2SO4(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2NaCl(aq).
  • 16. Oxidation and Reduction (Gain/Loss of Oxygen):
    • (a) Oxidation: Gain of oxygen. Examples: 2Cu + O2 → 2CuO; 4Na + O2 → 2Na2O.
    • (b) Reduction: Loss of oxygen. Examples: CuO + H2 → Cu + H2O; ZnO + C → Zn + CO.
  • 17. Element 'X' and Black Compound: The shiny brown element 'X' is Copper (Cu). On heating in air, it reacts with oxygen to form black copper(II) oxide (CuO).
  • 18. Painting Iron Articles: Painting iron articles prevents corrosion (rusting) by creating a barrier between the iron metal and atmospheric oxygen and moisture.
  • 19. Flushing Oil and Fat Containing Food with Nitrogen: Nitrogen is an inert gas that prevents the oxidation of fats and oils in food items. Oxidation leads to rancidity, causing bad smell and taste.
  • 20. Terms with Examples:
    • (a) Corrosion: The process by which metals are gradually eaten away by the action of air, moisture, or a chemical on their surface. Example: Rusting of iron, where iron reacts with oxygen and moisture to form reddish-brown iron oxides.
    • (b) Rancidity: The unpleasant smell and taste that develops in oil and fat-containing foods due to their oxidation when exposed to air for a prolonged period. Example: The stale smell of old chips or butter left in the open.
