Chapter 1: Chemical Reactions and Equations

1.0 Introduction to Chemical Reactions
  • Daily Life Examples: Situations where the nature and identity of initial substances change (e.g., milk souring, iron rusting, food cooking).
  • Chemical Change: Occurs whenever a chemical reaction takes place.
  • Fundamental Question: How do we come to know that a chemical reaction has taken place?
1.0.1 Activities to Identify Chemical Reactions
  • Activity 1.1 (Magnesium Ribbon):
    • Procedure: Clean a 3-4 cm magnesium ribbon, burn it with a spirit lamp, collect ash in a watch-glass.
    • Observation: Burns with a dazzling white flame, forming a white powder.
    • Product: The white powder is magnesium oxide, formed by the reaction of magnesium and oxygen.
  • Activity 1.2 (Lead Nitrate & Potassium Iodide):
    • Procedure: Add potassium iodide solution to lead nitrate solution in a test tube.
    • Observation: Formation of a yellow precipitate of lead iodide.
  • Activity 1.3 (Zinc Granules & Acid):
    • Procedure: Add dilute hydrochloric or sulphuric acid to zinc granules in a conical flask.
    • Observation: Bubbles of gas (hydrogen) evolve around the zinc granules.
    • Temperature Change: The flask/test tube feels warm, indicating a change in temperature.
  • Key Observations Indicating a Chemical Reaction:
    • Change in State: Reactants and products may have different physical states.
    • Change in Colour: The substances involved may change color.
    • Evolution of a Gas: A gas may be produced.
    • Change in Temperature: The reaction mixture may become hotter or colder.
1.1 Chemical Equations
  • Describing Reactions: A chemical reaction described in sentence form can be quite long.
  • Word-Equation: A shorter way to represent a chemical reaction (e.g., Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide).
  • Reactants: Substances that undergo chemical change (written on the Left-Hand Side, LHS).
  • Products: New substances formed during the reaction (written on the Right-Hand Side, RHS).
  • Arrow (→): Placed between reactants and products, points towards products, indicating the direction of the reaction.
  • Plus Sign (+): Used between multiple reactants or multiple products.
1.1.1 Writing a Chemical Equation
  • Concise Representation: Chemical equations use chemical formulae instead of words.
  • Example (Skeletal Equation): Mg + O2 → MgO.
  • Skeletal Chemical Equation: An unbalanced equation where the number of atoms of each element is not the same on both sides.
1.1.2 Balanced Chemical Equations
  • Law of Conservation of Mass (Class 9): Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.
  • Balancing Necessity: The total mass and number of atoms of each element must be equal on both sides of the equation.
  • Balanced Equation Example: Zn(s) + H2SO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + H2(g).
  • Hit-and-Trial Method (Steps for balancing Fe + H2O → Fe3O4 + H2):
    • Step I (Boxing): Draw boxes around each formula; do not change anything inside.
    • Step II (List Atoms): List the number of atoms of different elements on LHS and RHS.
    • Step III (Balance Max Atoms): Start balancing with the compound/element having the maximum number of atoms (e.g., oxygen in Fe3O4).
    • Step IV (Balance H Atoms): Balance hydrogen atoms.
    • Step V (Balance Fe Atoms): Balance the remaining element, iron.
    • Step VI (Final Check): Count atoms of each element on both sides to confirm balance (e.g., 3Fe + 4H2O → Fe3O4 + 4H2).
  • Step VII (Physical States): Mention physical states of reactants and products to make the equation more informative.
  • Physical State Notations: (g) for gaseous, (l) for liquid, (aq) for aqueous (solution in water), (s) for solid.
  • Steam Notation: H2O(g) indicates water in the form of steam in a reaction.
  • Reaction Conditions: Temperature, pressure, catalyst can be indicated above or below the arrow in the equation.
    • Example 1: CO(g) + 2H2(g) 2340atm→ CH3OH(l).
    • Example 2: 6CO2(aq) + 12H2O(l) Sunlight/Chlorophyll→ C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) + 6H2O(l).
1.2 Types of Chemical Reactions
  • Atomic Changes: During a chemical reaction, atoms of one element do not change into another; rather, bonds break and form to produce new substances.
1.2.1 Combination Reaction
  • Definition: A reaction in which a single product is formed from two or more reactants.
  • Activity 1.4 (Calcium Oxide + Water):
    • Procedure: Add water slowly to calcium oxide (quick lime) in a beaker.
    • Observation: Vigorous reaction, beaker becomes warm.
    • Reaction: CaO(s) + H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq) + Heat.
    • Product: Slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) is formed.
  • Whitewashing Use: A solution of slaked lime is used for whitewashing walls.
  • Carbonation Reaction: Ca(OH)2(aq) + CO2(g) → CaCO3(s) + H2O(l) (forms a thin layer of calcium carbonate, giving a shiny finish).
  • Other Examples:
    • Burning of Coal: C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g).
    • Formation of Water: 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l).
  • Exothermic Reactions: Reactions that release a large amount of heat along with the formation of products.
  • Exothermic Examples:
    • Burning of Natural Gas: CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g).
    • Respiration: C6H12O6(aq) + 6O2(aq) → 6CO2(aq) + 6H2O(l) + energy.
    • Decomposition of Vegetable Matter: Into compost.
1.2.2 Decomposition Reaction
  • Definition: A single reactant breaks down to give two or more simpler products.
  • Activity 1.5 (Ferrous Sulphate Decomposition):
    • Procedure: Heat ferrous sulphate crystals in a dry boiling tube.
    • Observation: Green crystals change color, characteristic odor of burning sulphur.
    • Reaction: 2FeSO4(s) Heat→ Fe2O3(s) + SO2(g) + SO3(g).
    • Products: Ferric oxide (solid), sulphur dioxide (gas), and sulphur trioxide (gas).
  • Thermal Decomposition: Decomposition reactions carried out by heating.
  • Example (Limestone): CaCO3(s) Heat→ CaO(s) + CO2(g).
  • Industrial Use: Calcium oxide (quick lime) is used in the manufacture of cement.
  • Activity 1.6 (Lead Nitrate Decomposition):
    • Procedure: Heat lead nitrate powder in a boiling tube.
    • Observation: Emission of brown fumes (nitrogen dioxide).
    • Reaction: 2Pb(NO3)2(s) Heat→ 2PbO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g).
  • Activity 1.7 (Electrolysis of Water):
    • Procedure: Drill holes in a plastic mug, fit carbon electrodes connected to a 6-volt battery, fill with acidified water, and invert test tubes over electrodes.
    • Observation: Bubbles form at both electrodes, displacing water in test tubes.
    • Gases Collected: Volume of gas at one electrode (hydrogen) is double that at the other (oxygen).
  • Activity 1.8 (Silver Chloride Decomposition):
    • Procedure: Place silver chloride in a china dish in sunlight.
    • Observation: White silver chloride turns grey.
    • Reaction: 2AgCl(s) Sunlight→ 2Ag(s) + Cl2(g).
    • Similar Reaction: 2AgBr(s) Sunlight→ 2Ag(s) + Br2(g).
    • Application: These reactions are used in black and white photography.
  • Endothermic Reactions: Reactions in which energy (heat, light, or electricity) is absorbed.
  • Example: Reaction between barium hydroxide and ammonium chloride (test tube feels cold).
1.2.3 Displacement Reaction
  • Definition: A reaction where one element displaces or removes another element from its compound.
  • Activity 1.9 (Iron Nails in Copper Sulphate Solution):
    • Procedure: Immerse two iron nails in copper sulphate solution (Test Tube B), keep one aside for comparison.
    • Observation: Iron nails turn brownish, blue color of copper sulphate solution fades.
    • Reaction: Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s).
    • Explanation: Iron, being more reactive, displaces copper from copper sulphate solution.
  • Other Examples:
    • Zinc & Copper Sulphate: Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s).
    • Lead & Copper Chloride: Pb(s) + CuCl2(aq) → PbCl2(aq) + Cu(s).
  • Reactivity Principle: More reactive elements (like zinc and lead) displace less reactive elements (like copper) from their compounds.
1.2.4 Double Displacement Reaction
  • Definition: Reactions in which there is an exchange of ions between the reactants.
  • Activity 1.10 (Sodium Sulphate & Barium Chloride):
    • Procedure: Mix sodium sulphate solution with barium chloride solution.
    • Observation: Formation of a white substance insoluble in water.
    • Precipitate: The insoluble substance formed is known as a precipitate (e.g., BaSO4).
    • Reaction: Na2SO4(aq) + BaCl2(aq) → BaSO4(s) + 2NaCl(aq).
  • Precipitation Reaction: Any reaction that produces a precipitate.
  • Recall Activity 1.2: The reaction between lead(II) nitrate and potassium iodide is also a double displacement reaction.
1.2.5 Oxidation and Reduction
  • Activity 1.11 (Heating Copper Powder):
    • Procedure: Heat copper powder in a china dish.
    • Observation: The surface of copper powder becomes coated with black copper(II) oxide.
    • Reason: Oxygen is added to copper.
    • Reaction: 2Cu + O2 Heat→ 2CuO.
  • Reverse Reaction: Passing hydrogen gas over heated copper(II) oxide turns the black coating brown, obtaining copper.
  • Reaction: CuO + H2 Heat→ Cu + H2O.
  • Oxidation (First Definition): If a substance gains oxygen during a reaction.
  • Reduction (First Definition): If a substance loses oxygen during a reaction.
  • Redox Reactions (Oxidation-Reduction Reactions): Reactions where one reactant gets oxidised while the other gets reduced.
  • Other Examples of Redox Reactions:
    • Zinc Oxide & Carbon: ZnO + C → Zn + CO (Carbon is oxidised to CO, ZnO is reduced to Zn).
    • Manganese Dioxide & Hydrochloric Acid: MnO2 + 4HCl → MnCl2 + 2H2O + Cl2 (HCl is oxidised to Cl2, MnO2 is reduced to MnCl2).
  • Oxidation (Second Definition): If a substance gains oxygen or loses hydrogen.
  • Reduction (Second Definition): If a substance loses oxygen or gains hydrogen.
  • Magnesium Ribbon (Activity 1.1): Magnesium is oxidised as it gains oxygen to form magnesium oxide.
1.3 Effects of Oxidation Reactions in Everyday Life
1.3.1 Corrosion
  • Definition: When a metal is attacked by substances around it (moisture, acids), it corrodes.
  • Rusting of Iron: Iron articles get coated with a reddish-brown powder (rust) when exposed to humid atmosphere.
  • Other Examples: Black coating on silver, green coating on copper.
  • Damage Caused: Corrosion damages car bodies, bridges, iron railings, ships, and other metal objects.
  • Economic Impact: Corrosion of iron is a serious problem, leading to significant financial losses for replacement.
1.3.2 Rancidity
  • Definition: When fats and oils in food materials are oxidised, they become rancid.
  • Effects: Changes in the smell and taste of food.
  • Prevention (Antioxidants): Substances that prevent oxidation are added to foods containing fats and oil.
  • Prevention (Airtight Containers): Keeping food in airtight containers helps to slow down oxidation.
  • Prevention (Nitrogen Flushing): Chips manufacturers flush bags of chips with gases like nitrogen to prevent oxidation.
What You Have Learnt
  • Chemical Equation: Represents reactants, products, and their physical states symbolically.
  • Balanced Equation: Ensures equal numbers of atoms of each type on both reactant and product sides.
  • Combination Reaction: Two or more substances combine to form a new single substance.
  • Decomposition Reaction: A single substance decomposes to give two or more substances (opposite of combination reactions).
  • Exothermic Reactions: Release heat along with product formation.
  • Endothermic Reactions: Absorb energy (heat, light, or electricity).
  • Displacement Reaction: One element displaces another from its compound.
  • Double Displacement Reaction: Involves the exchange of two different atoms or groups of atoms (ions) between reactants.
  • Precipitation Reactions: Produce insoluble salts (often a type of double displacement reaction).
  • Oxidation: Gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen.
  • Reduction: Loss of oxygen or gain of hydrogen.
