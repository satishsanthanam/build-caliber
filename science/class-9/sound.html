Chapter 10: Sound

10.1 Sound is Produced by a Vibrating Body
10.1.1 Production of Sound through Vibrations
  • Vibration: The to-and-fro or back-and-forth motion of an object is called vibration.
  • When a stretched rubber band is plucked, it vibrates and produces sound. It stops producing sound when it stops vibrating.
  • Vibrating objects produce sound. The vibrations are sometimes easily visible and sometimes felt.
  • Musical instruments produce sound through vibrating parts (e.g., stretched strings, membranes).
10.1.2 Sound Produced by Humans
  • Humans produce sound using the voice box, also known as the larynx.
  • The larynx is located at the upper end of the windpipe and contains two vocal cords stretched across it.
  • When air is forced through the slit between the vocal cords, they vibrate and produce sound.
  • The tightness and thickness of the vocal cords affect the type and quality of the voice.
  • Vocal cords in men are about 20 mm long, in women about 15 mm, and shorter in children, leading to different voice pitches.
10.2 Sound Needs a Medium for Propagation
  • Sound requires a medium (gas, liquid, or solid) to travel. It cannot travel in a vacuum.
  • Medium: The substance through which sound travels.
  • When air is removed from a container, a vacuum is created, and sound cannot propagate through it.
  • Sound can travel through liquids, as demonstrated by shaking a bell underwater and listening to it.
  • Sound can travel through solids, such as metal rods or wooden tables, as experienced by tapping one end and listening at the other.
  • Toy telephones demonstrate that sound can travel through strings (solids).
10.3 We Hear Sound through Our Ears
10.3.1 The Human Ear Structure and Function
  • The outer ear collects sound waves and channels them down a canal.
  • At the end of the canal is the eardrum, a thin, stretched membrane.
  • Sound vibrations cause the eardrum to vibrate.
  • The vibrating eardrum transmits signals to the inner ear, which then sends them to the brain.
  • This process, from vibration sensing to brain signal, is how we hear.
  • Caution: Never insert sharp objects into the ear, as it can damage the eardrum and impair hearing.
10.5 Amplitude, Time Period and Frequency of a Vibration
10.5.1 Vibration and Oscillation
  • Vibration/Oscillation: The to-and-fro motion of an object.
  • Time Period: The time taken by an object to complete one oscillation.
  • Frequency: The number of oscillations per second. It is expressed in Hertz (Hz). 1 Hz = 1 oscillation per second.
10.5.2 Amplitude and Loudness
  • Amplitude: The magnitude of the maximum displacement of a vibrating object from its equilibrium position.
  • Loudness of sound is proportional to the square of the amplitude. A larger amplitude produces a louder sound.
  • Loudness is measured in decibels (dB).
  • Sounds above 80 dB can be physically painful.
10.5.3 Frequency and Pitch
  • Pitch: A characteristic of sound that determines how shrill or grave it is.
  • Pitch is determined by the frequency of vibration. Higher frequency means higher pitch (shriller sound).
  • Lower frequency means lower pitch (graver sound).
  • A bird's sound has a higher pitch than a lion's roar.
  • The voice of a child typically has a higher frequency than that of an adult. Women's voices generally have higher frequencies than men's.
10.6 Audible and Inaudible Sounds
  • The human ear can detect sound frequencies roughly between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz.
  • Audible Sounds: Sounds with frequencies within the audible range (approx. 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz).
  • Inaudible Sounds: Sounds with frequencies below 20 Hz (infrasound) or above 20,000 Hz (ultrasound).
  • Some animals, like dogs, can hear frequencies higher than 20,000 Hz (ultrasound).
  • Ultrasound equipment used in medicine operates at frequencies above 20,000 Hz.
10.7 Noise and Music
  • Noise: Unpleasant and jarring sounds that cause discomfort (e.g., sounds from construction sites, horns).
  • Music: Pleasant sounds that are pleasing to the ear (e.g., sounds from musical instruments).
  • A musical sound can become noise if it is too loud.
10.8 Noise Pollution
  • Noise Pollution: The presence of excessive or unwanted sounds in the environment.
  • Sources: Sounds of vehicles, explosions, machines, loudspeakers, high-volume TV/radio, some home appliances.
  • Harmful Effects: Lack of sleep, hypertension, anxiety, temporary or permanent hearing impairment.
  • Measures to Limit Noise Pollution:
  • Install silencing devices in engines and machines.
  • Conduct noisy operations away from residential areas.
  • Minimize use of automobile horns.
  • Play TV and music systems at low volumes.
  • Plant trees along roadsides and around buildings to absorb sound.
Hearing Impairment
  • Total hearing impairment is usually congenital; partial impairment can result from disease, injury, or age.
  • Children with impaired hearing often need special care and may have defective speech.
  • Sign language and technological devices can help hearing-impaired individuals communicate and improve their quality of life.
