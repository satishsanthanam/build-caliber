Chapter 3: Tissues in Action

3.1 Understanding Tissues and Cellular Organization

3.1.1 Organization Hierarchy and Division of Labour

  • Tissue is a group of structurally similar cells working together to perform a specific function, establishing the basic hierarchy from cell to multicellular organism.
  • The formation of different types of tissues leads to the division of labour, which increases body efficiency.
  • In unicellular organisms like amoeba, a single cell performs all life processes, while multicellular organisms use specialized tissues.

3.1.2 Distinctive Differences Between Plant and Animal Tissues

  • Plants are fixed and require rigidity, facilitated by cells with strong, rigid cell walls, whereas animals move and exhibit cellular flexibility for locomotion.
  • Plants synthesize food components via photosynthesis powered by solar energy, while animals possess specialized digestive tissues to process food.
  • Plant growth is localized to specific regions containing dividing tissues, whereas animal growth is generally more uniform.
3.2 Plant Growth and Meristematic Tissues

3.2.1 Characteristics of Meristematic Tissues

  • Meristematic tissue consists of actively dividing cells characterized by thin cell walls, dense cytoplasm, a large prominent nucleus, and typically lacking vacuoles.
  • The process of differentiation occurs when newly formed meristematic cells undergo structural and functional changes to become specialized, non-dividing permanent tissues.

3.2.2 Classification of Meristems

  • Apical meristem is located at the tips of shoots and roots, helping the plant grow in length.
  • Lateral meristem is arranged in a ring in the stem and increases the girth (diameter) of the plant, forming annual growth rings.
  • Intercalary meristem is located at the base of internodes or nodes, enabling plant regeneration and branching.
3.3 Permanent Plant Tissues and Tissue Systems

3.3.1 Simple Permanent Tissues

  • Parenchyma consists of living, thin-walled, loosely packed cells that mainly store food and perform photosynthesis.
  • Collenchyma consists of living cells with unevenly thickened corners due to pectin deposition, providing mechanical support and flexibility.
  • Sclerenchyma consists of dead, narrow cells with highly thickened, lignified walls made of lignin, providing rigidity and structural strength.
  • The outer protective layer of the plant is the epidermis, covered with a waxy cuticle to minimize water loss and featuring stomata for gas exchange and transpiration.

3.3.2 Complex Permanent Tissues

  • Xylem transports water and minerals upward from roots, composed of tracheids, vessels, xylem parenchyma, and xylem fibres.
  • Phloem conducts synthesized organic food from leaves, composed of sieve tubes, companion cells, phloem parenchyma, and phloem fibres.

3.3.3 Plant Tissue Systems

  • The dermal tissue system forms the protective outer covering of the plant.
  • The ground tissue system makes up the main body between dermal and vascular tissues.
  • The vascular tissue system consists of conducting tissues (xylem and phloem).
3.4 Animal Tissues

3.4.1 Epithelial Tissues

  • Epithelial tissue forms closely packed cells that protect body surfaces and line internal organs, serving functions in absorption, secretion, and exchange.
  • Squamous epithelium consists of thin, flat cells optimized for diffusion (lungs) and protection (skin).
  • Columnar and cuboidal epithelium are specialized for absorption and secretion in glands and digestive tract linings.

3.4.2 Connective Tissues

  • A connective tissue supports and connects other tissues, composed of cells embedded in an extracellular matrix.
  • Blood is a fluid connective tissue containing a liquid plasma matrix that transports red blood cells (RBCs), white blood cells (WBCs), and platelets.
  • Bone is a rigid skeletal tissue with a hard matrix rich in calcium and phosphorus.
  • Cartilage is a flexible connective tissue with a resilient matrix that cushions joints.
  • Tendons connect muscle to bone, while ligaments connect bone to bone to stabilize joints.

3.4.3 Muscular Tissues

  • Skeletal muscle fibres are striated, multinucleated, unbranched, and under voluntary control.
  • Smooth muscle cells are spindle-shaped, non-striated, and regulate involuntary movements in organs like the stomach.
  • Cardiac muscle fibres are striated, branched, and involuntary, working rhythmically without fatigue.

3.4.4 Nervous Tissue

  • Nervous tissue acts as the body's control network, composed of neurons containing a cell body, dendrites, and an axon to transmit electrical signals.
3.5 The Musculoskeletal System and Joints

3.5.1 Types of Skeletal Joints

  • Ball and socket joint allows multi-directional movement (e.g., shoulder joint).
  • Hinge joint restricts movement to one plane (e.g., knee and elbow joints, protected by the kneecap).
  • Pivot joint allows rotational movement (e.g., neck joint).
  • Fixed joint is immobile, providing protection to vital areas like the skull.

3.5.2 Vertebral Column and Rib Cage

  • The vertebral column is made of individual vertebrae separated by cartilage discs to protect the spinal cord and allow torso flexibility.
  • The rib cage consists of 12 pairs of ribs connected to the sternum by flexible cartilage, expanding during breathing.
3.6 Scientific Concepts and Cell Totipotency

3.6.1 Totipotency and Crown Gall Disease

  • Totipotency is the cellular capacity to dedifferentiate, divide, and redifferentiate into a complete organism, demonstrated by F.C. Steward with carrot phloem.
  • Agrobacterium tumefaciens causes crown gall disease and is utilized by scientists in genetic engineering to introduce useful genes into crops.
