Chapter 7: Temperature and its Measurement

"Wrong measurements are worse than no measurements at all." — Anna Mani
7.1 Hot or Cold?
  • Our sense of touch is unreliable for judging hotness or coldness accurately.
  • An object may feel warm to one hand and cold to another depending on previous exposure (as demonstrated in Activity 7.1).
7.2 Temperature
  • Temperature: A reliable measure of the degree of hotness or coldness of a body.
  • Thermometer: A device used to measure temperature.
  • There are two main types of thermometers: clinical thermometers (for body temperature) and laboratory thermometers (for general scientific use).
7.3 Measuring Temperature

7.3.1 Clinical Thermometer
  • Used exclusively to measure human body temperature.
  • Digital Clinical Thermometer: Modern battery-operated device that displays temperature digitally using heat sensors, eliminating the risk of toxic mercury.
  • Celsius Scale: Denoted by °C. Normal human body temperature is 37.0 °C.
  • Fahrenheit Scale: Denoted by °F. Normal human body temperature is 98.6 °F.
  • Kelvin Scale: Denoted by K. It is the SI unit of temperature.
  • Conversion Formula: Temperature in Kelvin (K) = Temperature in Celsius (°C) + 273.15.
  • Infrared Thermometers: Non-contact thermometers that measure temperature from a distance, widely used during the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • Precautions: Wash the tip before and after use; do not hold by the tip; keep the digital display out of water.

7.3.2 Laboratory Thermometer
  • Used to measure temperatures in laboratory experiments.
  • Range: Typically from –10 °C to 110 °C.
  • Working Liquid: Generally contains red-coloured alcohol or mercury.
  • Correct Usage:
    • Must be held vertically and not tilted.
    • The bulb must be fully immersed in the substance but should not touch the bottom or sides of the container.
    • The reading must be taken while the thermometer is still immersed in the substance.

7.3.3 Air Temperature
  • Measured using room thermometers to monitor indoor environments.
  • Weather stations use specialized thermometers to record the daily maximum and minimum air temperatures for weather forecasting.
Key Scientists & Facts
  • Anna Mani (1918–2001): Known as the "Weather Woman of India". She was an Indian scientist who designed and built numerous weather measurement instruments, promoting self-reliance in meteorology and pioneering wind and solar energy research in India.
  • Absolute Zero: The lowest possible temperature that can theoretically be achieved, equal to –273.15 °C (0 K).
  • The temperature at the core of the Sun reaches about 15 million degrees Celsius.
