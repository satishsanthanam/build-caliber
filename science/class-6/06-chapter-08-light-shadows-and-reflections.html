Chapter 8: A Journey through States of Water

8.1 Investigating Water's Disappearing Act

Evaporation

  • Evaporation is the process of conversion of water (liquid state) into water vapour (gaseous state).
  • This process takes place continuously, even at room temperature.
  • Common examples include drying of wet clothes, mopping floors, and sweat drying on our body.

Factors Affecting Evaporation

  • Exposed Surface Area: Larger surface area leads to faster evaporation (e.g., water evaporates faster from a plate than a bottle cap).
  • Temperature: Higher temperatures increase the rate of evaporation (e.g., clothes dry faster in direct sunlight than in shade).
  • Wind Speed: Increased air movement accelerates evaporation (e.g., clothes dry faster on a windy day).
  • Humidity: Higher humidity (moisture in the air) slows down evaporation (e.g., clothes dry slowly on rainy days).
8.2 Another Mystery (Condensation)

Condensation

  • Condensation is the process of conversion of water vapour (gaseous state) into its liquid state.
  • It occurs when water vapour in the air comes into contact with a cold surface (e.g., water droplets forming on the outer surface of a glass containing ice-cold water).
  • Humidity refers to the amount of water vapour present in the air.
8.3 What are the Different States of Water?

Three States of Water

  • Solid State (Ice): Retains its shape irrespective of the container. It does not flow or spread.
  • Liquid State (Water): Takes the shape of its container but maintains a constant volume. It has the ability to flow and spread.
  • Gaseous State (Water Vapour): Has no fixed shape or volume. It spreads out to fill the entire available space and is invisible.
8.4 Changing the States of Water

Phase Transitions

  • Melting: The process of conversion of a solid (ice) into a liquid (water) by heating.
  • Freezing: The process of conversion of a liquid (water) into a solid (ice) by cooling.
  • Water changes its state continuously in nature through heating (evaporation) and cooling (condensation).
8.5 Cooling Effect of Evaporation

How Evaporation Causes Cooling

  • When water evaporates, it absorbs heat from its surroundings, imparting a cooling effect.
  • Earthen Pots (Matka/Surahi): Water seeps through the porous walls of the clay pot and evaporates from the outer surface, cooling the water inside.
  • Sweating: Wind from a fan helps sweat evaporate from our skin, which cools our body.
8.6 How Do Clouds Give Us Rain?

Cloud Formation and the Water Cycle

  • Water vapour is lighter than air, causing it to rise up into the atmosphere.
  • As air moves higher, it cools down. At certain heights, water vapour condenses around tiny dust particles to form clouds.
  • These tiny droplets join together to form larger, heavier drops. When they become too heavy to float, they fall as rain, snow, or hail.
  • The continuous circulation of water from the Earth's surface to the atmosphere and back is called the water cycle.
