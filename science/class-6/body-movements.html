Chapter 5: Measurement of Length and Motion

5.1 How do we Measure?
  • Measurement: The comparison of an unknown quantity with some known fixed quantity of the same kind.
  • Unit: A standard quantity used for measurement. A complete measurement is expressed in two parts: a number and a unit (e.g., 13 handspans).
  • Non-Standard Units: Traditional units based on body parts, such as handspan (balisht), angula (finger width), arm length, and strides. These vary from person to person and are not reliable for precise measurements.
5.2 Standard Units
  • SI Units: The International System of Units, adopted globally for uniformity in measurements.
  • Metre (m): The standard SI unit of length.
  • Unit Conversions:
    • 1 kilometre (km) = 1000 metres (m)
    • 1 metre (m) = 100 centimetres (cm)
    • 1 centimetre (cm) = 10 millimetres (mm)
5.3 Correct Way of Measuring Length
  • Scale Placement: Place the scale in direct contact with the object along its entire length.
  • Eye Position: Your eye must be positioned directly above the point of measurement to avoid parallax errors.
  • Measuring with Broken Scales: If the zero mark is broken or unclear, align the object with any other full mark (e.g., 1.0 cm) and subtract this initial reading from the final reading at the other end.
5.4 Measuring the Length of a Curved Line
  • A curved line cannot be measured directly with a rigid metre scale.
  • Method: Use a flexible thread to trace along the curved path, mark the start and end points on the thread, and then measure the straightened thread using a standard scale.
5.5 Describing Position
  • Reference Point: A fixed point or object used to describe the position or distance of another object. Position is always relative to the chosen reference point.
5.6 Moving Things
  • Motion: An object is in motion if its position changes with respect to a reference point over time.
  • Rest: An object is at rest if its position does not change with respect to a reference point over time.
5.7 Types of Motion

Classification of Motion
  • Linear Motion: Motion of an object along a straight line (e.g., a falling apple, march-past of soldiers).
  • Circular Motion: Motion of an object along a circular path where its distance from a central point remains constant (e.g., a stone whirled on a string, a merry-go-round).
  • Oscillatory Motion: To-and-fro motion of an object about a fixed central position (e.g., a swing, a hanging pendulum).
  • Periodic Motion: Any motion that repeats itself at regular, fixed intervals of time (e.g., circular and oscillatory motions).
