Chapter 9: Methods of Separation in Everyday Life

9.1 Introduction to Mixtures

In everyday life, we often need to separate substances from a mixture (a combination of two or more substances) to remove impurities, discard non-useful components, or retrieve multiple useful components.

9.2 Separation of Solid-Solid Mixtures

9.2.1 Handpicking

  • Handpicking is the method of separating components from a mixture by hand.
  • Basis: Differences in size, color, or shape of the particles.
  • Application: Removing small stones, husk, or dirt from wheat, rice, and pulses when present in small quantities.

9.2.2 Threshing

  • Threshing is the process of separating grains from harvested stalks.
  • Method: Beating dry stalks against a hard surface (like a wooden log) to free the grains.
  • Modern Alternative: Motorized machines called threshers perform threshing and winnowing simultaneously.

9.2.3 Winnowing

  • Winnowing is the method of separating lighter components from heavier components of a mixture using wind or blowing air.
  • Method: Dropping the mixture from a height; the wind blows away the lighter husk, while the heavier grains fall straight down.
  • Traditional Tool: A bamboo tray called a soop.

9.2.4 Sieving

  • Sieving is used when components of a solid-solid mixture have different particle sizes.
  • Method: The mixture is passed through a sieve. Fine particles pass through the holes, while larger particles (like bran or stones) remain on top.
  • Application: Separating bran from flour, or separating pebbles and stones from sand at construction sites.

9.2.5 Magnetic Separation

  • Magnetic Separation is the process of separating magnetic substances from non-magnetic substances using a magnet.
  • Application: Separating iron nails from sawdust, or extracting scrap iron from industrial waste heaps using electromagnetic cranes.
9.3 Separation of Insoluble Solids from Liquids

9.3.1 Sedimentation and Decantation

  • Sedimentation is the process where heavier, insoluble solid particles in a liquid settle down at the bottom when left undisturbed.
  • Decantation is the careful pouring out of the clear upper liquid layer without disturbing the settled sediment.
  • Application: Washing rice or pulses (dirt and dust dissolve/float and are poured off), or separating oil from water.

9.3.2 Filtration

  • Filtration is the process of separating insoluble solid particles from a liquid by passing the mixture through a filter.
  • Filters used: Fine cloth, tea strainers, or specialized filter paper with microscopic pores.
  • Terms: The clean liquid that passes through is the filtrate; the solid particles left behind on the filter are the residue.
9.4 Separation of Soluble Solids from Liquids

9.4.1 Evaporation

  • Evaporation is the process in which a liquid converts into its vapor state.
  • Application: Used to recover a dissolved solid from a liquid solution (e.g., obtaining common salt from seawater by letting water evaporate in shallow pits under the sun).
9.5 Other Separation Methods

9.5.1 Churning

  • Churning is the process of vigorously shaking or spinning a liquid mixture to separate components of different densities.
  • Application: Churning curd or milk using a mathni (churner) to separate butter (which is lighter and floats to the top) from buttermilk.
