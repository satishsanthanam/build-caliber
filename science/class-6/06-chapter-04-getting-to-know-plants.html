Chapter 4: Exploring Magnets

4.1 Magnetic and Non-magnetic Materials

Types of Magnets

  • Natural Magnets: Magnets found in nature, such as lodestones (magnetite).
  • Artificial Magnets: Man-made magnets prepared from iron and other materials. They come in various shapes:
    • Bar magnet
    • U-shaped magnet
    • Ring magnet
    • Cylindrical magnet
    • Disc magnet
    • Spherical magnet

Magnetic vs. Non-magnetic Materials

  • Magnetic Materials: Materials attracted towards a magnet. Examples: Iron, Nickel, Cobalt.
  • Non-magnetic Materials: Materials not attracted towards a magnet. Examples: Wood, Plastic, Glass, Rubber.
4.2 Poles of a Magnet

Magnetic Poles

  • Poles: The regions near the ends of a magnet where the magnetic attraction is strongest.
  • Every magnet has two poles: the North pole (N) and the South pole (S).
  • Inseparability of Poles: Magnetic poles always exist in pairs. A single isolated pole (monopole) cannot exist. If a magnet is broken, each piece becomes a complete magnet with its own N and S poles.
4.3 Finding Directions

Directive Property

  • A freely suspended bar magnet always comes to rest pointing in the North-South direction.
  • The end pointing North is the North-seeking pole (North pole).
  • The end pointing South is the South-seeking pole (South pole).
  • This happens because the Earth itself behaves like a giant magnet.

The Magnetic Compass

  • Magnetic Compass: A device developed for finding directions, containing a magnetized needle that rotates freely on a pivot.
  • The needle always aligns itself in the North-South direction. The North-seeking end is typically painted red.
  • Matsya-yantra: An ancient Indian navigation device consisting of a magnetized fish-shaped iron piece kept in a vessel of oil, used for sea navigation.
4.4 Attraction and Repulsion

Laws of Magnetic Poles

  • Like poles repel: North-North and South-South poles push each other away.
  • Unlike poles attract: North-South poles pull towards each other.
  • Sure Test of Magnetism: Repulsion is the only sure test to identify a magnet, as an unmagnetized magnetic material (like iron) will be attracted to both poles of a magnet, but only another magnet can experience repulsion.

Magnetic Penetration

  • Magnetic force can act through non-magnetic materials (like wood, cardboard, plastic, glass, or water) without significant loss of effect.
4.5 Care and Safety of Magnets

How to Keep Magnets Safe

  • Store bar magnets in pairs with unlike poles on the same side, separated by a piece of wood, with soft iron pieces (keepers) across their ends.
  • For a U-shaped magnet, place a single piece of iron across its poles.
  • Avoid heating, dropping, or hammering magnets, as they lose their magnetic properties (demagnetization).
  • Keep magnets away from electronic devices like mobile phones, computers, and remote controls.
