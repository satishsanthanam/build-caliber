Chapter 12: Beyond Earth

12.1 Stars and Constellations

Stars and Constellations
  • Stars: Celestial bodies that shine with their own light.
  • Constellations: Defined regions of the sky containing groups of stars that form recognizable patterns. The International Astronomical Union (IAU) officially lists 88 constellations.
  • Orion: Represented as a hunter. Its middle three stars represent the belt. Contains the star Betelgeuse (known as Ārdrā in Indian astronomy).
  • Canis Major: The constellation representing the hunter's dog. Contains Sirius, the brightest star in the night sky.
  • Taurus: Represented as a bull. Contains the star Aldebaran (Rohiṇī) and the star cluster Pleiades (Kṛittikā).
  • Nakṣhatra: Term used in Indian astronomy to denote a specific star or group of stars.
  • Big Dipper: A prominent pattern of seven stars (known as Saptaṛiṣhi in India) belonging to the constellation Ursa Major.
  • Little Dipper: A star pattern belonging to the constellation Ursa Minor.
  • Pole Star: Also called Polaris (or Dhruva tārā), it is part of the Little Dipper and remains nearly stationary in the North direction, serving as a vital navigation aid.
12.2 Night Sky Watching

Observing the Night Sky
  • Light Pollution: Excessive artificial light at night that reduces the visibility of stars and celestial objects.
  • Sky Mapping Apps: Digital tools like Sky Map and Stellarium used to identify stars, planets, and constellations.
  • Preparation: Sky watching requires a dark, open area away from tall buildings, trees, and artificial lights, preferably on a clear, moonless night.
12.3 Our Solar System

The Sun and Planets
  • The Sun: The closest star to Earth, composed of extremely hot gases. It is the primary source of heat and light energy for the Solar System.
  • Astronomical Unit (au): A unit of distance approximately equal to the distance between the Sun and the Earth (about 150 million km).
  • Proxima Centauri: The nearest star to Earth after the Sun, located at a distance of about 269,000 au.
  • Revolution: The orbital movement of a celestial object around the Sun.
  • Rotation: The spinning of a celestial object on its own axis. Earth takes about 24 hours for one full rotation.
  • Planets: Large, nearly spherical objects revolving around the Sun. The eight planets in order of distance are: Mercury (Budha), Venus (Śhukra), Earth (Pṛithvī), Mars (Mangala), Jupiter (Bṛihaspati), Saturn (Śhani), Uranus, and Neptune.
  • Inner Planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars. They are smaller, rocky, and have solid surfaces.
  • Outer Planets: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. They are giant planets made mostly of gas and ice, featuring flat ring-like structures.
  • Dwarf Planets: Smaller celestial bodies like Pluto that orbit the Sun but do not meet all criteria to be classified as major planets.

Satellites, Asteroids, and Comets
  • Natural Satellites: Celestial bodies that orbit planets (commonly called moons).
  • The Moon: Earth's only natural satellite, located about 3,84,000 km away. It takes about 27 days to complete one revolution around Earth. Its surface is covered with circular bowl-like structures called craters.
  • Chandrayaan Missions: India's lunar exploration program. Chandrayaan-3 successfully soft-landed near the Moon's south pole on 23 August 2023, which is celebrated as National Space Day.
  • Asteroids: Small, rocky, irregularly shaped objects orbiting the Sun, primarily found in the Asteroid Belt between Mars and Jupiter.
  • Comets: Icy-rocky bodies from the outer solar system that develop long, glowing tails of evaporating gas and dust as they approach the Sun. Halley's Comet appears once every 76 years.
12.4 & 12.5 The Milky Way and The Universe

Galaxies and the Cosmos
  • Milky Way Galaxy: Also known as Ākāśha Gangā, it is our home galaxy containing millions to billions of stars, including our Solar System.
  • The Universe: The vast, infinite expanse containing all matter, energy, and billions of galaxies.
  • Exoplanets: Planets discovered revolving around stars outside our Solar System.
