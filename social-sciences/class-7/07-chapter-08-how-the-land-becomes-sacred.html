Chapter 8: How the Land Becomes Sacred

8.1 What is 'Sacredness'?

Sacredness refers to finding something of deep religious or spiritual significance, worthy of respect, reverence, holy, or divine. It can be a location, a shrine, a journey, or the land itself.

Key Definitions

  • Pilgrimage: A journey to a sacred place that is significant within a religion or belief system.
  • Shrine: A place regarded as holy because of its associations with the divine, a sacred relic, or a spiritual figure.
  • Relic: A part of a saint's or spiritual figure's body, or sometimes one of their belongings, kept as an object of reverence.

Sacred Places Across Religions

  • Religions originating outside India: Places like the Dargah Sharif of Ajmer (Islam) or the Velankanni Church in Tamil Nadu (Christianity) are revered by followers of various faiths.
  • Buddhism: Sacred sites are often associated with the Buddha's life or where his relics are kept, such as the Great Stūpa at Sanchi (relic stūpa) and the Mahabodhi Stūpa in Bodh Gaya (where he attained enlightenment).
  • Sikhism: Spiritual authority is centered around Takhts (seats of authority), such as Takht Sri Patna Sahib, the Akal Takht (Golden Temple, Amritsar), and Takht Sri Keshgarh Sahib (Anandpur). Gurus like Guru Nanak also conducted pilgrimages to various sites.
8.2 Pilgrimages and Tīrthas

Pilgrimage in India is an ancient and continuous tradition of physical and inner journeys requiring a specified code of conduct.

The Concept of Tīrtha

Tīrtha literally means a place where one can cross a river or body of water. Symbolically, it represents crossing from ordinary worldly life to a higher, spiritual life.

Jain and Other Traditions

  • Jainism: Tīrthas are associated with places where Tīrthankaras (supreme preachers of dharma who guide the crossing to higher life) attained liberation or performed meditation (e.g., Mount Abu, Girnar, Shatrunjaya hill).
  • Sabarimala (Kerala): A hilltop shrine dedicated to deity Ayyappa, reached via a challenging trek symbolizing the difficulties of the inner path.
  • Pandharpur Wārī: An 800-year-old annual pilgrimage tradition in Maharashtra where pilgrims walk for 21 days to the Vithoba temple.
8.3 Sacred Geography and Cultural Integration

Interconnected networks of sacred sites crisscross the Indian Subcontinent, creating a shared sacred geography that fosters cultural integration.

Major Sacred Networks

  • Chār Dhām Yātrā: Four highly revered sites deliberately located in the northern, southern, eastern, and western corners of India.
  • 12 Jyotirlingas: Sacred shrines dedicated to Lord Śhiva, each with unique mythology.
  • 51 Shakti Pīṭhas: Shrines representing the body parts of the divine mother Satī. Symbolically, the entire land represents the body of the divine mother.

From Pilgrimage to Trade

Pilgrimage routes and trade routes historically overlapped. Major ancient trade routes like the Uttarapatha and Dakṣhiṇapātha facilitated the exchange of goods (precious stones, gold, spices, sandalwood) alongside ideas, enriching the cultural fabric of the subcontinent.

8.4 Sacred Ecology: Rivers, Mountains, and Nature Worship

In Hindu, folk, and tribal traditions, elements of Nature are worshipped as divine, reflecting the perception of a divine presence in all of Nature (Mother Earth or Bhūdevī).

Rivers and Sangams

  • Rivers have been worshipped since Vedic times. The Nadīstuti Sūkta of the Rigveda is a hymn praising 19 major rivers.
  • A popular ritual invocation names seven sacred rivers: Ganga, Yamuna, Godavari, Sarasvati, Narmada, Sindhu, and Kaveri.
  • Kumbh Mela: Originating from the legend of the churning of the ocean (amṛita manthana), it is held at four places where drops of nectar fell: Haridwar, Prayagraj (confluence of Ganga, Yamuna, and invisible Sarasvati), Nashik, and Ujjain. It is recognized by UNESCO as an intangible cultural heritage.

Mountains and Tribal Traditions

  • Mountains are viewed as symbolic gateways from earth to heaven (e.g., Mount Kailash, Vaishno Devi, Tiruvannamalai, Tirumala hills).
  • Niyam Dongar (Odisha): Sacred to the Dongria Khond tribe as the abode of Niyam Raja; cutting trees is strictly prohibited.
  • Todas (Nilgiris, Tamil Nadu): Regard mountain peaks, Shola forests, and specific trees as sacred.
8.5 Trees, Forests, and Sacred Groves

Sacred Trees and Epics

  • The Peepul tree (Ficus religiosa or ashvattha) is sacred across Hinduism, Buddhism, Sikhism, and Jainism.
  • The epics Mahābhārata and Rāmāyaṇa describe sacred landscapes. Local traditions across India claim epic heroes passed through their regions, creating local shrines (e.g., Bastar, Chhattisgarh for Rāma).

Sacred Groves

Sacred Groves are community-protected natural forests believed to be the abodes of deities, preserving rich biodiversity and water resources.

8.6 Global Perspectives and Conservation
  • Global Sacred Geography: Ancient Greece had sacred groves; Native Americans share a deep bond with Nature; the Maoris of New Zealand successfully advocated for the Taranaki Maunga mountain to be granted legal human rights.
  • Restoration: Modern pollution and encroachment threaten sacred rivers (Yamuna, Mahanadi, Kaveri) and groves. Preserving these sites is vital for ecological sustainability and is a constitutional duty in India.
