Class 7 — Chapter 2: Understanding the Weather

2.1 Weather and its Elements

Our bodies constantly sense and respond to the weather, whether shivering on a cold winter morning or seeking light clothing in the summer. But what exactly is weather?

What is Weather?

Weather is the state of the Earth's atmosphere at a particular time and place. The atmosphere is the layer of gases (commonly called 'air') that surrounds the Earth.

The atmosphere can be compared to a layered cake. The layer closest to the Earth's surface is the troposphere. This is where all land-based plants and animals live, and where almost all weather phenomena take place. The troposphere extends to a height of $6$ to $18\text{ km}$ from the ground; it is thinner at the poles (where cold air contracts) and thicker in the tropical zone (where warm air expands).

The Five Elements of Weather

We experience weather through its core elements:

  • Temperature: How hot or cold the atmosphere is.
  • Precipitation: Any form of water that falls from the sky, such as rain, snow, sleet (frozen or partly frozen rain), or hail (small, hard balls of ice).
  • Atmospheric Pressure: The weight of the air above us, felt on the Earth's surface.
  • Wind: The movement of air, characterized by its speed and direction.
  • Humidity: The amount of water vapour (water in gaseous form) present in the air.
2.2 Observing Nature's Clues

Before modern instruments, humans relied on observing nature to forecast (predict in advance) the weather. Traditional indicators include:

  • Ants shifting their eggs to higher ground, indicating expected heavy rain.
  • Frogs croaking loudly in expectation of rain.
  • Pine cones closing in humid conditions to protect their seeds, and opening in dry conditions to release them.

Today, the systematic study of weather and its evolution is known as meteorology, and the scientists who study and predict it are called meteorologists.

2.3 Weather Instruments

To measure and monitor weather elements with precision, meteorologists use specialized instruments.

a) Temperature

Temperature is measured using thermometers on scales like Celsius ($^\circ\text{C}$) or Fahrenheit ($^\circ\text{F}$). For example, a cool temperature of $15^\circ\text{C}$ is equivalent to $59^\circ\text{F}$.

Key temperature statistics include:

  • Ambient temperature: The temperature of the immediate surroundings.
  • Range of temperature: The maximum temperature minus the minimum temperature during a specific period (usually 24 hours).
  • Mean daily temperature: The average of the maximum and minimum temperatures of the day: $$\text{Mean Daily Temperature} = \frac{\text{Maximum Temp} + \text{Minimum Temp}}{2}$$

Note: The India Meteorological Department (IMD), established in 1875, uses the motto "ādityāt jāyate vriṣhti", meaning "From the sun arises rain."

b) Precipitation

Rainfall is measured using an instrument called a rain gauge. When it rains, water falls into a collecting funnel and accumulates in a measuring tube. A scale measures the depth of the collected water in millimetres (mm).

c) Atmospheric Pressure

Atmospheric pressure is the force exerted by the weight of the air above us. It is measured using a barometer, typically in millibars (mb).

  • Normal atmospheric pressure at sea level is around $1013\text{ mb}$.
  • A pressure reading below $1000\text{ mb}$ indicates a low-pressure system or "depression," which can develop into storms or cyclones.
  • Pressure decreases with altitude. At high altitudes, the air is thinner, containing less oxygen, requiring travelers to pause and acclimatise (adjust to the new climate/conditions). For example, at Khardung La in Ladakh ($5600\text{ m}$ above sea level), the pressure is only about $650\text{ mb}$.

d) Wind

Wind is the movement of air from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure.

  • Direction: Measured using a wind vane (or weather vane) or a wind sock (commonly used on airport tarmacs to assist pilots during take-off and landing).
  • Speed: Measured using an anemometer, which features rotating metal cups that spin faster as wind speed increases, calculating speed in kilometres per hour (km/h).

e) Humidity

Humidity is measured as relative humidity, expressed as a percentage:

  • $0\%$ represents air with absolutely no water vapour.
  • $100\%$ represents air completely saturated with water vapour.
  • Dry weather typically ranges between $20\%$ and $40\%$, while humid weather falls between $60\%$ and $80\%$.
  • Relative humidity is measured using a hygrometer.
2.4 Weather Stations and Predictions

Weather Stations

A weather station brings together multiple instruments to measure and track weather elements simultaneously at regular intervals.

An Automated Weather Station (AWS) is a self-operating system that uses electronic sensors to record and transmit weather data without requiring human intervention. These are crucial for agriculture, aviation, and disaster management.

Predicting the Weather

Meteorologists analyze long-term data to predict weather patterns. Accurate predictions are vital for preparing for extreme events like droughts, floods, heat waves, and cyclones.

The IMD issues color-coded alerts to help the public and local governments prepare:

  • Green: No Warning
  • Yellow: Watch (Be Updated)
  • Orange: Alert (Be Prepared)
  • Red: Warning (Take Action)
