Chapter 3: Climates of India

3.1 Introduction & The Big Questions

The chapter begins with a traditional Subhashita quote emphasizing the importance of timely rains and a prosperous, peaceful Earth:

"kāle varṣhatu parjanyaha pṛithivī sasyaśhālinī deśhoyam kṣhobharahitah brāhmaṇāsantu nirbhayāh"
May the rains be timely, may the Earth be lush with vegetation. May this country be free from turmoil, may good people be fearless!

The Big Questions

  • What makes India’s climate so diverse?
  • What are the monsoons? How are they formed?
  • What is the effect of climate on economy, culture and society?
  • How can understanding the climate help us to prepare for natural disasters?
  • What is climate change? What are its consequences?
3.2 Weather, Seasons and the Climate

People often confuse 'weather' and 'climate', but they represent different timescales of atmospheric conditions.

Weather vs. Climate

  • Weather is what we experience every hour or day (e.g., rain, bright sunshine, wind). It keeps changing constantly.
  • Climate is the pattern of weather an area or a region experiences over a long period of time—several decades at least. This pattern varies from region to region.

Seasons and the Traditional Indian Calendar

Seasons occur as the Earth revolves around the Sun. Each season lasts for a few months and recurs every year in a cycle.

Traditionally, in many parts of India, the year is divided into six seasons or ritus:

  • Vasanta (spring)
  • Grīṣhma (summer)
  • Varṣhā (rainy season)
  • Śharad (autumn)
  • Hemanta (pre-winter)
  • Śhiśhir (winter)

Specific rituals and festivals are associated with these six ritus, such as Vasanta Pañchamī or Śharad Pūrṇima. Human, plant, and animal life are in rhythm with these seasons, affecting the crops grown, food eaten, and clothes worn.

3.3 Types of Climates in India

India is a land of immense climatic diversity, featuring several distinct climate zones:

  • Alpine Climate: Found in the northern Himalayan mountains, characterized by cold, snowy winters and cool summers.
  • Temperate Climate: Found lower in the Himalayas and in many hilly areas (hill stations), where winters are moderately cold and summers are not too hot.
  • Subtropical Climate: Found in the northern plains, featuring very hot summers and cold winters. This region is where most of India's wheat is grown.
  • Arid Climate: Found in the Thar Desert (west), featuring extremely hot days, cool nights, and very little rainfall.
  • Tropical Wet Climate: Found along the western coastal strip, which receives heavy rainfall during monsoon months, favorable for growing rice and spices.
  • Semi-Arid Climate: Found in the central Deccan Plateau, with hot summers, mild winters, and moderate rainfall during the rainy season.
  • Tropical Climate: Found in Eastern India and the southern peninsula, featuring mild winters and distinct wet and dry periods controlled by monsoon winds.
3.4 Factors Determining the Climate

Several global, regional, and local factors work together to shape India's diverse climates:

a) Latitude

Latitudes measure the distance from the Equator. Places near the Equator (low latitudes) are warmer because the sun's rays hit nearly perpendicularly, focusing energy on a smaller area. Near the poles (high latitudes), the rays are oblique, distributing energy over a larger surface and passing through more atmosphere. In India, southern places like Kanniyakumari and the Nicobar Islands are warm year-round, while northern places like Srinagar are much cooler.

b) Altitude

Temperature decreases as altitude increases. This explains why hill stations (e.g., Munnar, Ooty, Shimla, Darjeeling) are cooler than the plains below. This happens because:

  1. Atmospheric pressure and air density decrease with altitude; less dense air gets cooler.
  2. The sun heats the Earth's surface, so air farther from the surface is less hot.

c) Proximity to the Sea

The sea acts as a moderator for temperature. Coastal areas do not experience extreme temperature variations because land absorbs and loses heat quickly, while the sea absorbs and loses heat slowly. Moving inland leads to extreme temperatures. For example, coastal Mumbai has cooler summers (around $32^ ext{C}$) and milder winters (around $18^ ext{C}$), while inland Nagpur experiences up to $44^ ext{C}$ in summer and $10^ ext{C}$ in winter.

d) Winds

Winds move masses of warmer or cooler air. Western winds traveling over deserts from Arabia and Afghanistan bring dry, hot air (heat waves) to northwestern and central India in summer. In winter, cold winds from across the Himalayas bring cold waves. Winds from the sea bring moisture, resulting in rainfall.

e) Topography

The physical surface features (topography) play a major role. The Himalayas and Karakoram ranges protect the Indian subcontinent from cold Central Asian winds. Conversely, the flat topography of the Thar Desert offers no protection from hot, dry winds.

Microclimates and Urban Heat Islands

A microclimate is a climate localized in a small area (like enclosed valleys or forests) that differs from the surrounding region. Cities with dense concrete buildings and minimal vegetation trap heat, creating warmer zones known as urban heat islands.

3.5 The Monsoons

The word monsoon comes from the Arabic word mausim, meaning 'season'. It refers to seasonal winds blowing over a large area of the Indian Ocean and surrounding regions.

Mechanism of the Monsoon

The monsoon is driven by the difference in heating rates between land and water:

  • Summer (Southwest Monsoon): The Asian landmass heats up rapidly, creating a powerful low-pressure system. Winds are drawn from the cooler, high-pressure ocean towards the hot land, carrying moisture that condenses and falls as heavy rain. It typically advances from the southern tip of India in early June and covers the subcontinent by mid-July.
  • Winter (Northeast Monsoon): The landmass cools down faster than the ocean, reversing the pressure systems. Dry winds blow from the high-pressure land towards the warmer, low-pressure ocean. However, as they pass over the Bay of Bengal, they pick up moisture and bring winter rainfall to parts of eastern and southern India.

Key Monsoon Facts

  • Mawsynram, located in Meghalaya, receives the highest average annual rainfall in the world, about $11,000 ext{ mm}$ (11 meters).
  • Monsoons have inspired classical Indian ragas, such as Meghamalhār and Amruthavarshini.
3.6 Climate and our Lives

Human lives, cultures, and economies are deeply intertwined with the climate.

Traditional Knowledge

Our ancestors developed local traditional knowledge to predict monsoons:

  • Fishermen on the Konkan coast predict monsoons when deep-water fish come to the surface.
  • In southern India, the monsoon is expected within 50 days of the blossoming of the Golden Shower tree (Cassia fistula).
  • Low nests built by crows indicate heavy rainfall, while high nests indicate less rain.

Festivals and Economy

Many Indian festivals are linked to agricultural cycles and seasons, including Bihu, Makar Sakranti, Baisakhi, Pongal, Onam, Lohri, Gudi Padwa, and Chhath Puja. Economically, a 'monsoon failure' leads to agricultural distress, water scarcity, migration of laborers, and food inflation.

3.7 Climates and Disasters

Extreme weather patterns in India can lead to devastating natural disasters:

a) Cyclones

Formed when atmospheric pressure near the sea becomes exceptionally low, drawing in high-speed, moisture-laden winds that rotate inward around a cloudless center called the eye of the cyclone. The India Meteorological Department (IMD) tracks these events.

b) Floods

Occur when water overflows onto dry land due to heavy rainfall or breached riverbanks. In the Himalayas, floods can be caused by glacial bursts (e.g., the 2013 Uttarakhand disaster near Kedarnath). Urban flooding is often caused by overburdened drainage systems and concrete surfaces that prevent water absorption.

c) Landslides

The sudden collapse of rock, soil, or debris, common in hilly regions like the Himalayas and Western Ghats during monsoons. Human activities like deforestation and unplanned construction increase landslide risks.

d) Forest Fires

Uncontrolled fires spread across vegetation, fueled by dry conditions, droughts, or high winds, often triggered by human carelessness.

The National Disaster Response Force (NDRF) is a specialized force trained to respond to these natural and human-made disasters across India.

3.8 Climate Change

Climate Change refers to significant, long-term shifts in global or regional climate patterns. While historically driven by natural processes, since the 19th century it has been primarily driven by human activities.

Causes and Effects

  • Greenhouse Effect: Gases like carbon dioxide ($ ext{CO}_2$) trap heat from the Sun to keep the Earth warm. However, burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial practices have released excessive greenhouse gases, causing rapid global warming.
  • Impact in India: Rising temperatures are shortening and milding winters (e.g., early 2025 temperatures were $1 ext{ to }3^ ext{C}$ above normal), disrupting agriculture and small-scale industries.

Key Strategies

  • Resilience: The capacity of communities to withstand or recover quickly from climate difficulties.
  • Mitigation: Adopting steps to slow down global warming, such as reducing emissions, planting trees, and boosting renewable energy.
  • Sustainable Lifestyles: Adopting practices that can be maintained over a long period of time without harming the environment.
