Chapter 9: From the Rulers to the Ruled: Types of Governments

9.1 What is Government and its Functions?

The government plays an important role in maintaining law and order, ensuring peace, managing foreign relations, national defence, delivering essential services, and working for public welfare.

Three Core Functions of Government

  • Legislative Function: The creation of rules and laws that serve as the framework for running the country.
  • Executive Function: The implementation of rules and the administration of the country according to them.
  • Judicial Function: Ensuring that the rules are followed and resolving disputes (adjudicating).
9.2 What Makes Governments Different?

Governments evolve over time based on history, culture, and aspirations. They differ across four key aspects:

  • Source of Authority: Who gets to decide that "this is the government"? (e.g., the people in a democracy, religious beliefs in a theocracy).
  • Formation: How is the government formed? (e.g., elections in a democracy, hereditary succession in a monarchy).
  • Structure and Parts: What are the different parts of the government and how do they interact? (often defined in a written constitution).
  • Goals and Values: What is the government working for? (e.g., equality and prosperity for all, or the prosperity of a select few).
9.3 Democratic Governments around the World

Democracy is defined as the "rule of the people," where the source of power and authority lies with the citizens.

Fundamental Principles of Democracy

  • Equality: Every person has the right to be treated equally, with equal access to facilities and equality before the law.
  • Freedom: Citizens have the right to make their own choices and express their opinions.
  • Representative Participation: Every person has a right to choose and elect their representatives through elections.
  • Universal Adult Franchise: Grants every citizen above a certain age the right to vote.
  • Fundamental Rights: Core rights (e.g., equality, freedom of speech, protection against exploitation) provided and protected by the state.
  • Independent Judiciary: Ensures citizens' rights are protected and laws are followed without interference from other branches.

Forms of Democratic Governments

  • Direct Democracy: Citizens are directly responsible for all rules, regulations, and decisions (e.g., Switzerland). Hard to practice in large countries.
  • Representative Democracy: People elect representatives to govern on their behalf, and the government remains accountable to the people.
    • Parliamentary Democracy: Members of the executive are also part of the legislature (e.g., India, Australia). The executive is accountable to the legislature.
    • Presidential Democracy: The executive works independently of the legislature, and the president is elected directly by the people (e.g., USA, South Korea).
9.4 A Peek into History: Early Republics

The voice of the common people and collective decision-making have deep roots in history:

  • Ancient India's Mahājanapadas: In the Vajji (or Vṛiji) mahājanapada, the Lichchhavi clan practiced collective decision-making, choosing leaders based on merit rather than birth. These are known as early republics.
  • Chola Period Inscriptions: The 10th-century CE Uttaramerur inscriptions in Tamil Nadu detail the democratic election of members to the village sabhā (local administrative body) using sealed ballot boxes.
  • Global History: Ancient Rome and Greece had early republics, though voting rights were often restricted to free men, excluding women, workers, and slaves.
9.5 Other Forms of Government

1. Monarchy

Ruled by a king or queen (monarch) who inherits power hereditarily.

  • Absolute Monarchy: The monarch has complete control over lawmaking, enforcement, and adjudication (e.g., Saudi Arabia).
  • Constitutional Monarchy: The monarch has only nominal/ceremonial power, while real executive and legislative powers are exercised by an elected parliament and prime minister (e.g., United Kingdom).

2. Theocracy

A form of government where the country is ruled by religious leaders and religious laws (e.g., Iran, Vatican City, Afghanistan).

3. Dictatorship

A form of government where one person or a small group possesses absolute power without constitutional limits (e.g., Adolf Hitler in Germany, Idi Amin in Uganda, North Korea).

4. Oligarchy

A type of government where a small, powerful, and usually wealthy group of families or influential people makes all important decisions.

9.6 Why Democracy Matters & Its Challenges

Democracy is widely considered the best form of government because it allows citizens to lead their lives freely, choose their leaders, and hold the government accountable. However, democracies face significant challenges:

  • Corruption
  • Wealth disparity
  • Excessive control over democratic institutions by a few
  • Erosion of judicial independence
  • Manipulation of information channels

Citizens must remain vigilant to minimize these hurdles and protect democratic values.
