Class 7 — Chapter 7: The Gupta Era: An Age of Tireless Creativity

7.1 Introduction & The Big Questions

The Gupta Era is often characterized as a period of tireless creativity, wisdom, and knowledge in Indian history. Key questions explored in this chapter include:

  • Who were the Guptas, and why is their period called the classical age of India?
  • What was happening in the rest of the subcontinent during this time?
  • Who were the great figures of this period, and why do their stories matter today?
7.2 A New Power Emerges

By the $3\text{rd}$ century CE, the Kushana Empire in the north and northwest began to weaken, paving the way for the rise of the Gupta Dynasty near present-day Uttar Pradesh.

7.2.1 Rulers of the Gupta Dynasty

  • Chandragupta I: Played a crucial role in the early expansion of the empire; remembered for his strategic alliances and gold coins featuring him with his queen, Kumaradevi.
  • Chandragupta II (Vikramaditya): Grandson of Chandragupta I, a renowned ruler and devotee of Vishnu. His reign is marked by significant developments in art, literature, and science.

7.2.2 The Iron Pillar of Mehrauli

Located in Delhi, this $6\text{-tonne}$ iron pillar is over $1,600$ years old and stands without rusting. It was erected during the reign of Chandragupta II, originally in front of the Udayagiri caves. Its rust-resistance is due to a unique thin protective passive layer formed by the specific iron composition and oxygen.

7.3 The Warrior King: Samudragupta

Samudragupta, father of Chandragupta II, was a powerful conqueror whose achievements are recorded in the Prayaga Prashasti (Allahabad Pillar Inscription).

  • The Court Poet: Written by Harisena, the inscription praises Samudragupta's ambition to be dharani-bandha ("unifier of the Earth").
  • Military Campaigns: He defeated many kings, reinstating some as tributaries, and expanded the empire's influence.
  • Cultural Patronage: Samudragupta supported art and learning; he is depicted on gold coins playing the veena.
  • Ashvamedha Yajna: He performed the sacrificial horse ritual to commemorate his imperial conquests.
7.4 Society, Governance & Administration

7.4.1 Account of Faxian

The Chinese Buddhist pilgrim Faxian visited India in the early $5\text{th}$ century CE. His travelogue, A Record of Buddhistic Kingdoms, describes:

  • A happy, numerous population with minimal bureaucratic interference.
  • Wealthy cities in the Middle Kingdom (Gangetic plains) where charity houses, hospitals, and free medicine were provided by Vaishya families.
  • The harsh social reality of chandalas, who were treated as outcastes and forced to live outside city limits.

7.4.2 Governance and Imperial Titles

Gupta rulers adopted grand titles to project supreme authority, such as maharajadhiraja, samrat, and chakravartin.

  • Decentralized Administration: The empire was divided into provinces. Land grants were given to local rulers, priests, and chieftains, recorded on copper plates.
  • Prabhavati Gupta: Daughter of Chandragupta II, married into the southern Vakataka dynasty. After her husband's early death, she ruled as a powerful regent ruler, maintaining strong ties with the Guptas and constructing temples dedicated to Vishnu.
7.5 Economy & Thriving Trade
  • Revenue Sources: Land tax was the primary source of revenue, supplemented by fines, taxes on mines, irrigation, trade, and crafts.
  • International Trade: India traded with the Mediterranean world, Southeast Asia, and China, exporting textiles, spices, ivory, and gemstones.
  • Socotra Island: Located strategically in the Arabian Sea, archaeological evidence (Brahmi inscriptions, pottery, Buddhist stupa designs) proves the long-term presence of Indian traders here.
7.6 Science, Literature & Medicine (The Classical Age)

The stability of the Gupta period fostered a golden age of intellectual achievements.

7.6.1 Great Scholars and Scientists

  • Aryabhata: Lived in Kusumapura (near Patna) around $500\text{ CE}$ and wrote the Aryabhatiya. He proposed that the Earth rotates on its axis, explained eclipses, and calculated the length of a year as $365\text{ days}, 6\text{ hours}, 12\text{ minutes}, \text{and } 30\text{ seconds}$.
  • Varahamihira: A pioneer scientist from Ujjayini who wrote the encyclopedic Brihat Samhita, covering astronomy, astrology, weather forecasting, architecture, and agriculture.
  • Kalidasa: Renowned Sanskrit poet and dramatist, famous for works like Meghadutam ("The Cloud Messenger") and Raghuvansham.

7.6.2 Codification of Ayurveda

Traditional Indian medicine was codified during this era. Texts like the Charaka Samhita and Sushruta Samhita were compiled into their final forms, detailing disease diagnosis, diet, surgery, and holistic healing.

7.6.3 Nalanda University

The Guptas patronized Buddhist institutions, including the world-renowned Nalanda University, which served as a major hub of higher learning.

7.7 Art & Architecture (The Quest for Beauty)

Gupta art set high aesthetic standards that influenced subsequent generations across Asia.

  • Key Centers: Sarnath (famous for Buddha sculptures), Ajanta Caves (renowned for murals/paintings of Bodhisattva Padmapani), and Udayagiri (rock-cut caves).
  • Deogarh Temple: The Dashavatara temple in Uttar Pradesh features exquisite carvings, including Vishnu reclining on the serpent Sheshnag.
  • River Deities: Terracotta sculptures of Ganga (standing on a makara) and Yamuna (standing on a tortoise) from Ahichchhatra represent the sacred rivers of India.
7.8 Decline & Contemporary Kingdoms

7.8.1 Decline of the Guptas

By the $6\text{th}$ century CE, the empire declined due to repeated invasions by the fierce Huna tribe from Central Asia and the rise of independent regional rulers.

7.8.2 The Pallavas of Kanchipuram

In the south, the Pallavas consolidated power in Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Telangana, and Andhra Pradesh. Their capital, Kanchipuram, became a "city of a thousand temples" and a major center of learning with institutions called ghatikas.

7.8.3 The Kamarupa Kingdom

In the northeast, the Kamarupa kingdom (ruled by the Varman dynasty) flourished in the Brahmaputra valley (Assam). Historically known as Pragjyotisha, it was a prominent cultural and political center mentioned in the epics and the Prayaga Prashasti.
