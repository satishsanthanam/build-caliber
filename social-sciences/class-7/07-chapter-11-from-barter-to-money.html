Chapter 11: From Barter to Money

11.1 Introduction to the Barter System

Before the invention of modern money, people exchanged goods and services directly for other goods and services. This method of exchange is known as the barter system.

Key Definitions

  • Barter System: A way of exchanging goods and services without using money.
  • Money: The common tool that everybody accepts and uses in order to make or receive payments in exchange for goods and services.
  • Transaction: A piece of business that is done between people, especially an act of buying or selling.
  • Commodities: Products or goods that can be traded, bought, and sold.

Early Commodities Used as Money

Historically, various communities used highly valued local commodities as early forms of money:

  • Cowrie shells, salt, tea, tobacco, and cloth.
  • Cattle (cows, goats, horses, sheep) and seeds.
  • Rai stones: Giant discs of rock used as money on Yap Island in Micronesia.
  • Tajadero: Copper chopping knives used as a form of money in Central Mexico.
  • Tevau: Red feather coils used as money in the Solomon Islands.
11.2 Limitations of the Barter System

While simple, the barter system presented significant challenges in daily transactions, which eventually led to the necessity of money.

1. Double Coincidence of Wants

An economic concept describing a situation where two people each have something the other wants, allowing a direct exchange. Finding such a perfect match is highly difficult in practice.

2. Lack of a Common Standard Measure of Value

Without a single standard, it is difficult to compare the value of one good against another to determine a fair exchange proportion.

3. Divisibility

Many commodities cannot be divided into smaller parts without losing their value (e.g., one cannot exchange half an ox for a smaller item like a sweater).

4. Portability

Carrying heavy or bulky commodities (like oxen or multiple bags of wheat) over long distances for trade is highly impractical.

5. Durability

Many commodities are perishable. For example, wheat can rot or be eaten by pests, making it a poor medium for storing wealth over time.

Modern Survivals of Barter

  • Junbeel Mela: A three-day annual socio-cultural fair in Morigaon, Assam, where tribal communities (Tiwa, Karbi, Khasi, Jaintia) gather to exchange local produce without money.
  • Book Exchange: Informal clubs where people swap books directly.
  • Old Clothes for Utensils: A traditional practice where vendors exchange new household utensils for used clothes.
11.3 Basic Functions of Money

Money resolved the limitations of barter by serving four fundamental functions:

  • Medium of Exchange: Facilitates trade by acting as an intermediary accepted by all parties for transactions.
  • Store of Value: Allows wealth to be saved and retrieved in the future without decaying.
  • Common Denomination (Measure of Value): Provides a standard unit to measure and compare the prices of different goods and services.
  • Standard of Deferred Payment: Enables the agreement of payments to be made in the future.
11.4 The Journey of Money

Money has evolved through several distinct phases over thousands of years.

1. Coinage (Metallic Money)

Coins were among the earliest forms of standardized money, minted under the control of rulers.

  • Minting: The process of producing coins in a specialized manufacturing facility (a mint).
  • Alloys: Metals made by combining two or more metallic elements to make coins stronger. Ancient coins used alloys of gold, silver, and copper.
  • Historical Terms: Ancient Indian coins were called kārṣhāpaṇas or paṇas, with punched symbols called rūpas.
  • Obverse and Reverse: The front (head) of a coin is the obverse, and the back (tail) is the reverse. They featured motifs of nature, deities, or rulers.
  • Historical Examples: Chalukya coins featured the Varaha (avatar of Vishnu); Chola coins featured a tiger emblem; Roman gold coins excavated in South India (Pudukkottai) indicate active maritime trade.
  • Pre-decimal Currency: In 1947, $1$ anna was equal to $\frac{1}{16}$ of a rupee.
  • Rupee Symbol (₹): Adopted by the Government of India in 2010, designed by Udaya Kumar. It combines the Devanagari "Ra" and Roman "R".

2. Paper Money

As carrying heavy metallic coins became difficult, paper money emerged.

  • First used in China; introduced in India in the late 18th century (e.g., Bank of Bengal, Bank of Bombay).
  • Today, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is the sole central authority authorized to issue and control currency in India.
  • Denominations: Units in which currency is classified (e.g., coins of $\text{₹}1, \text{₹}2, \text{₹}5, \text{₹}10, \text{₹}20$ and notes of $\text{₹}10, \text{₹}20, \text{₹}50, \text{₹}100, \text{₹}200, \text{₹}500$).

3. New Forms of Money (Digital Money)

Modern technology has introduced intangible, electronic forms of money.

  • Digital Money: Money existing in electronic form, enabling cashless transactions.
  • QR Code: Quick Response codes consisting of black and white squares that store receiver bank details for instant mobile scanning.
  • Payment Methods: Debit cards, credit cards, net banking, and UPI (Unified Payments Interface), which directly transfer funds between bank accounts.
