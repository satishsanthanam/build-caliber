Class 7 — Chapter 12: Understanding Markets

12.1 Introduction to Markets

Prosperity emanates from the market that develops when people need goods and services that they can’t create themselves. — Adam Smith, 18th century economist

The Big Questions

  • What are markets and how do they function?
  • What is the role of markets in people’s lives?
  • What role does the government play in markets?
  • How can consumers assess the quality of goods and services they purchase?
12.2 What is a Market?

A market is a place where people buy and sell goods. It is also known as bazaar, haat (in Hindi), and mārukatté (in Kannada). Markets can exist at a physical place or online. Through markets, goods and services become available to individuals, households, and businesses.

  • Needs: In economics, a need is something that a person requires to survive, such as food, water, clothing, and shelter.
  • Wants: In economics, a want is something that a person desires but is not essential for survival.
  • Trade: Buying and selling or exchange of goods and services between people or countries.

Case Study: The Glorious Hampi Bazaar, Karnataka

The Hampi Bazaar in Karnataka was a prosperous market in the Vijayanagara Empire (16th century) and a centre of flourishing trade. Located opposite the Virupaksha temple, its abundance was documented by foreign travellers:

  • Domingos Paes called Hampi "the best-provided city in the world" due to the wide variety of products traded, including grains, seeds, milk, oil, silk, animals, and birds.
  • Fernao Nuniz wrote about the streets filled with craftsmen making golden jewels, rubies, diamonds, pearls, and cotton cloths.
12.3 Prices and Markets

Every market requires a buyer, a seller, and an agreement on a price.

Price is the amount at which a buyer is willing to buy, and a seller is willing to sell particular goods or services. Buyers and sellers negotiate and bargain to arrive at an acceptable price.

Price Determination Scenarios

  1. Price is fixed too high: If a seller sets a price too high (e.g., $\text{₹}80$ per kg for guavas), buyers may refuse to buy, leading to unsold stock.
  2. Price is fixed too low: If the price is set too low (e.g., $\text{₹}20$ per kg), demand will be extremely high, but the seller may not make a profit or sustain the business.
  3. Equilibrium Price: Over time, the price is fixed at a mutually agreeable point (e.g., $\text{₹}40$ per kg) that is high enough for the seller to make a profit and low enough for the buyer to purchase.
12.4 Markets Around Us

Physical and Online Markets

  • Physical Market: A market where buyers meet sellers physically to purchase goods or services in exchange for money (e.g., weekly markets, local shops, malls).
  • Online Market: Transactions conducted virtually using shopping applications or websites without meeting in person.
  • Manufacturer: A person or company that makes goods for sale.
  • Inputs for production: Materials or resources used in the production of goods and services.

Domestic and International Markets

  • Domestic Market: A market where goods and services are bought and sold within the geographical boundaries of a country.
  • International Market: Trade occurring across national borders.
    • Export: Selling goods or services produced in one country to a buyer in another country.
    • Import: Buying goods or services from other countries and bringing them into one's own country.

Wholesale and Retail Markets

  • Wholesalers: Buy goods in large quantities directly from producers or manufacturers, store them in warehouses (godowns) or cold storage facilities, and sell them to retailers.
  • Retailers: Shopkeepers and vendors who buy goods from wholesalers and sell them in smaller quantities to final consumers.
  • Distributors: Individuals or businesses who supply goods from manufacturers and wholesalers to retailers, bridging geographical gaps.
  • Aggregators: Websites or mobile applications that organize and combine offers from multiple sellers and sell them to consumers at one place.

Case Study: The Surat Textile Market, Gujarat

Surat is Asia's oldest textile market and a major textile hub. The supply chain functions as follows:

Raw cotton from cotton mandis in Maharashtra and Gujarat $\rightarrow$ Weaving on power looms $\rightarrow$ Dyeing in processing units $\rightarrow$ Wholesale markets $\rightarrow$ Retailers $\rightarrow$ Final consumers.

12.5 The Role of Markets in People’s Lives

Markets facilitate transactions between producers and consumers, helping households access essential goods and services. They also foster social relationships and community traditions.

Case Study: Ima Keithal (Mother’s Market), Manipur

Located in Imphal, Ima Keithal is a unique market run entirely by about 3,000 women. It provides employment, serves as an important source of income, and acts as a cultural melting pot where different communities exchange ideas and traditions.

12.6 Government’s Role in the Market

Markets function through the interaction of demand (quantity consumers are willing to buy) and supply (quantity sellers are willing to sell). The government intervenes to ensure fairness and stability.

Key Government Interventions

  • Controlling Prices: Setting maximum price limits (price caps) on essential goods like lifesaving drugs, and minimum support prices (MSP) for agricultural products (wheat, paddy, maize) to protect farmers.
  • Ensuring Quality and Safety: Setting standards and conducting sample testing (e.g., pharmaceutical approvals) to protect consumer health.
  • Mitigating External Effects: Implementing regulations to control pollution and negative environmental impacts caused by factories (e.g., single-use plastics ban).
  • Providing Public Goods: Providing services or commodities accessible to all members of society that do not diminish with use (e.g., public parks, roads, policing).

Historical Note: Kauṭilya’s Arthaśhāstra mentions that traders in clarified butter (ghee) must give buyers $\frac{1}{50}$ part more as mānasrāva to compensate for quantity lost sticking to the measuring can.

12.7 Assessing Quality of Products and Services

Consumers can assess product quality through government-certified quality marks:

  • FSSAI (Food Safety & Standards Authority of India): Indicates that food has been tested and is safe to consume.
  • ISI Mark: Issued by the Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) for electrical appliances, construction materials, tires, etc.
  • AGMARK: Certification mark for agricultural products like vegetables, fruits, cereals, pulses, spices, and honey.
  • BEE STAR Rating: Issued by the Bureau of Energy Efficiency; higher stars indicate better energy efficiency and lower electricity consumption.
