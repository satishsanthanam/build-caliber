Class 7 — Chapter 1: Geographical Diversity of India

1.1 Introduction to India's Geography

India is the seventh-largest country in the world and a key part of Asia. Together with its neighbours (Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, Bhutan, Sri Lanka, and Myanmar), it forms the region known as the Indian Subcontinent.

The country is naturally bounded by:

  • North: The Himalayan Mountain range
  • West: The Thar Desert and the Arabian Sea
  • South: The Indian Ocean
  • East: The Bay of Bengal

India is broadly divided into five major geographical zones:

  1. The great mountain zone
  2. The plains of the Ganga and the Indus
  3. The desert region
  4. The southern peninsula
  5. The islands
1.2 The Himalayas

The Himalayan range acts as a massive northern wall stretching about $2500\text{ km}$ in length. It contains peaks over $8000\text{ metres}$ high, collectively known as the Eight Thousanders. It is often called the Water Tower of Asia because its melting snow feeds major perennial rivers like the Ganga, Indus, and Brahmaputra.

1.2.1 Formation of the Himalayas

Millions of years ago, the Indian landmass was part of a larger supercontinent called Gondwana. After breaking away, it drifted north and collided with the Eurasian Plate about 50 million years ago. The collision crumpled the land between them, creating folded mountains. This tectonic movement continues today, with the Himalayas growing taller by about $5\text{ mm}$ each year.

1.2.2 Three Main Ranges of the Himalayas
  • The Himadri (Greater Himalayas): The highest, most rugged northernmost range, home to Mount Everest and Kanchenjunga. It remains permanently snow-covered.
  • The Himachal (Lower Himalayas): Located south of the Himadri, featuring a moderate climate, rich biodiversity, and popular hill stations like Nainital, Darjeeling, Shimla, and Mussoorie.
  • The Shivalik Hills (Outer Himalayas): The lowest, southernmost range consisting of rolling hills and dense forests, serving as a transition zone to the Gangetic Plains.

1.2.3 Traditional Architecture

The western Himalayan region features a traditional earthquake-resistant house construction style known as kath-kuni or dhajji-dewari, which utilizes a combination of locally sourced stone and wood.

1.3 The Cold Desert of India: Ladakh

Ladakh is a high-altitude cold desert in India where winter temperatures can drop below $-30^\circ\text{C}$. It features rugged, rocky terrain, deep valleys, and high-altitude lakes like Pangong Tso (a saltwater lake). Due to its unique geological formations resembling lunar landscapes, it is also referred to as moonland.

1.4 The Gangetic Plains

Located south of the Himalayas, the Gangetic Plains are vast, flat, and highly fertile lowlands. They are nourished by the alluvial deposits of the Ganga, Indus, and Brahmaputra river systems, making the region ideal for intensive agriculture and supporting a large portion of India's population.

1.5 The Great Indian Desert (Thar Desert)

The Thar Desert is a vast arid region spanning Rajasthan, Gujarat, Punjab, and Haryana. It is characterized by wind-sculpted sand dunes that can reach heights of up to $150\text{ metres}$. Due to extreme water scarcity, local communities have developed ingenious rainwater harvesting systems called taanka or kunds.

1.6 The Aravalli Hills

The Aravalli Hills are among the oldest mountain ranges in the world, dating back approximately 2.5 billion years. They act as a crucial natural barrier preventing the eastward expansion of the Thar Desert. The range is rich in minerals like marble, granite, zinc, and copper; ancient mines at Zawar indicate that Indians were among the first to master zinc extraction over eight centuries ago.

1.7 The Peninsular Plateau

The southern part of India forms a triangular Peninsular Plateau surrounded by water on three sides. It is bordered by two mountain ranges:

  • Western Ghats (Sahyadri Hills): A continuous, tall wall-like range running along the western coast.
  • Eastern Ghats: A lower, discontinuous range broken by river valleys along the eastern coast.

Between these ranges lies the Deccan Plateau. Major east-flowing rivers (Godavari, Krishna, Mahanadi, Kaveri) drain into the Bay of Bengal, while west-flowing rivers (Narmada, Tapti) empty into the Arabian Sea.

1.8 India's Coastlines

India has a coastline stretching over $7500\text{ km}$:

  • The West Coast: Stretches from Gujarat to Kerala. It is narrow, characterized by steep cliffs, coves, creeks, and fast-flowing rivers that form estuaries.
  • The East Coast: Lies between the Eastern Ghats and the Bay of Bengal. It features wide plains and large, fertile river deltas formed by the accumulation of river sediments. It also contains major saltwater lagoons like Chilika Lake and Pulicat Lake.
1.9 Indian Islands

India possesses two major offshore island groups:

  • Lakshadweep Islands: An archipelago of 36 coral islands located in the Arabian Sea, close to the Malabar Coast of Kerala.
  • Andaman and Nicobar Islands: A chain of more than 500 volcanic islands in the Bay of Bengal. It contains Barren Island, the only active volcano in India.
1.10 The Delta in West Bengal and the Sundarbans

The Sundarbans is the world's largest delta, formed by the confluence of the Ganga and Brahmaputra rivers. It is a UNESCO World Heritage site characterized by dense mangrove forests and is a famous habitat for the Royal Bengal Tiger.

1.11 The Hills of the Northeast

The northeastern hills include the Garo, Khasi, and Jaintia hills, which form part of the Meghalaya Plateau. This region experiences some of the highest rainfall in the world. It is famous for:

  • Mawlynnong Village: Renowned as the cleanest village in Asia.
  • Living Root Bridges: Natural suspension bridges grown and woven from the roots of rubber trees by local tribes over many years.
