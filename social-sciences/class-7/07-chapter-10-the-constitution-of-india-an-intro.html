Class 7 — Chapter 10: The Constitution of India — An Introduction

10.1 What Is a Constitution?

A constitution is a document that spells out a nation's basic principles and laws. It lays out the fundamental framework of governance and defines the relationship between the state and its citizens.

Core Functions of a Constitution

  • Establishes the framework of the three organs of government: legislature, executive, and judiciary.
  • Defines the roles, responsibilities, and limits of these organs.
  • Creates a system of checks and balances to ensure fairness and accountability.
  • Guarantees the fundamental rights and outlines the duties of citizens.
  • Outlines the long-term goals and aspirations of the nation.
10.2 Why Do We Need a Constitution?

Just like a sports match requires an official rulebook to resolve disputes and ensure fair play, a country needs a constitution to serve as its ultimate rulebook. It determines how the government is formed, how laws are made and implemented, and how disputes are resolved. It also enshrines the core values and ideals that the society commits to uphold.

10.3 Writing the Constitution of India

The Indian Constitution was drafted during a period of great transition as India gained independence from British rule.

The Constituent Assembly

  • Formed in 1946 to draft the Constitution.
  • Initially consisted of 389 members, which was reduced to 299 members after the Partition of India.
  • Included 15 women and represented diverse regions, professions, and social groups.
  • Dr. Rajendra Prasad served as the Chairman of the Constituent Assembly.

The Drafting Committee & Timeline

  • The initial text was prepared by a Drafting Committee chaired by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, the first Law and Justice minister of independent India.
  • The drafting process took almost three years, starting on 9 December 1946.
  • The Constitution was adopted on 26 November 1949.
  • It came into effect on 26 January 1950, a day celebrated annually as Republic Day.
10.4 What Shaped and Influenced the Indian Constitution?

The makers of the Indian Constitution drew inspiration from multiple sources, blending historical heritage with global democratic practices.

Influence of the Indian Freedom Movement

The values of the freedom struggle, such as equality, justice, freedom, and fraternity, became the cornerstone of the Constitution. Leaders carried their lived experiences of fighting oppression to ensure the new state would protect individual liberties.

India's Civilisational Heritage and History

  • Embedded the idea of unity in diversity and cultural synthesis.
  • Incorporated ancient principles of tolerance, respect for women, and harmony with nature.
  • Reflected traditional concepts like vasudhaiva kutumbakam ("the world is one family") and sarve bhavantu sukhinah (well-being of all).
  • Drew lessons from historical governance systems, including janapadas, sanghas, Kautilya's saptanga, and rajadharma.

Learnings from Across the World

  • France: Ideals of liberty, equality, and fraternity.
  • Ireland: Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP).
  • USA: Concept of an independent judiciary and Fundamental Rights.
  • UK & Australia: Parliamentary system and other legislative procedures.
10.5 Key Features of the Constitution of India

The Constitution establishes a robust framework for governance, balancing state power with citizen rights.

Three Organs of Government

  • Legislature: Responsible for making laws.
  • Executive: Responsible for implementing laws, headed by the Prime Minister.
  • Judiciary: Ensures laws align with the Constitution and resolves legal disputes.

Three-Tier System of Government

Power is distributed across three levels: Central Government, State Government, and Local Government (Panchayati Raj system).

Fundamental Rights, Duties, and DPSPs

  • Fundamental Rights: Enforceable promises guaranteed to citizens (e.g., Article 14: Right to Equality, Article 21: Protection of life and personal liberty, Article 21-A: Right to Education).
  • Fundamental Duties: Moral obligations of citizens to respect the nation, its symbols, and the environment.
  • Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP): Non-enforceable guidelines that direct the government toward establishing a welfare state (e.g., Article 41: Welfare Government, Article 44: Uniform Civil Code).
10.6 The Constitution as a Living Document

The Constitution is not static; it is a living document designed to adapt to changing times through amendments.

  • Amendments are rigorously debated in Parliament and sometimes state assemblies before acceptance.
  • Example: Part IV-A (Fundamental Duties) was added in 1976.
  • Example: The Panchayati Raj System was integrated in 1992 via the 73rd Constitutional Amendment Act.
  • The original document is a work of art, handwritten by calligrapher Prem Behari Narain Raizada and illustrated with historical scenes by artist Nandalal Bose and his team.
10.7 Understanding the Preamble

The Preamble is the introductory statement of the Constitution, outlining its core values and objectives:

  • We, the People of India: Indicates that the ultimate sovereignty lies with the citizens.
  • Sovereign: India is completely independent of external control.
  • Socialist: Wealth should be shared socially to reduce socio-economic inequalities.
  • Secular: The state has no official religion and treats all faiths with equal respect.
  • Democratic: A government elected by the people, run by basic rules.
  • Republic: The head of state is an elected representative, not a hereditary monarch.
  • Justice: Ensuring social, economic, and political fairness for all.
  • Liberty: Freedom of thought, expression, belief, faith, and worship.
  • Equality: All citizens are equal before the law with equal opportunities.
  • Fraternity: Promoting brotherhood and assuring the dignity of the individual.
