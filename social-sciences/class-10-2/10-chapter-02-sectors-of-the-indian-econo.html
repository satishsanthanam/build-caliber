Class 10 (Part 2) — Chapter 2: Sectors of the Indian Economy

2.1 Sectors of Economic Activities

People are engaged in various economic activities that produce either goods or services. These activities are classified into three main sectors:

  • Primary Sector: Activities undertaken by directly exploiting natural resources. Examples include agriculture, dairy, fishing, forestry, and mining. It is also called the agriculture and related sector as it forms the base for all other products.
  • Secondary Sector: Covers activities where natural products are changed into other forms through manufacturing processes. Examples include spinning yarn from cotton, making sugar from sugarcane, and converting earth into bricks. It is also called the industrial sector.
  • Tertiary Sector: Activities that do not produce goods directly but provide aid or support for the production process. Examples include transport, storage, communication, banking, and trade. It is also called the service sector and includes essential services like teachers, doctors, lawyers, and IT-based services (internet cafes, ATM booths, software companies).
2.2 Comparing the Three Sectors

To understand the total production in each sector, economists suggest using the value of goods and services rather than adding up actual physical quantities.

Counting Final Goods and Services

Only the value of final goods and services (goods that reach the consumers) is included in calculations. Intermediate goods (like wheat and flour used to make biscuits) are excluded because their value is already included in the final product. Counting them separately would lead to double counting.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

The Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of a country is the value of all final goods and services produced within a country during a particular year. It shows how big the economy is. In India, the task of measuring GDP is undertaken by a central government ministry with the help of state and union territory departments.

Recently, the Indian Government began using Gross Value Added (GVA) to measure the contribution of the three sectors after adjusting for taxes and subsidies, aligning with global practices.

Historical Change in Sectors

Historically, developed countries show a clear pattern of transition over time:

  • Initial Stage: The primary sector is the most important sector of economic activity and employment.
  • Industrial Stage: With new manufacturing methods, factories expand, and the secondary sector becomes dominant in production and employment.
  • Service Stage: In the past 100 years, a further shift to the tertiary sector has occurred, making the service sector the most important in terms of total production and employment.
2.3 Primary, Secondary and Tertiary Sectors in India

In India, while there has been a massive shift in production share towards the tertiary sector, a similar shift has not occurred in employment.

Rising Importance of the Tertiary Sector

The tertiary sector has emerged as the largest producing sector in India, replacing the primary sector due to:

  • Demand for basic services like hospitals, educational institutions, defense, and banks.
  • Development of agriculture and industry, which increases demand for services like transport and trade.
  • Rise in income levels, leading to demand for tourism, dining out, and private schooling.
  • Introduction of new services based on information and communication technology.

Where are Most People Employed?

The primary sector continues to be the largest employer in India. This is because not enough jobs were created in the secondary and tertiary sectors, even though industrial output went up by more than nine times and service output by fourteen times.

Underemployment and Disguised Unemployment

More than half of the workers in India are in the primary sector, producing only about one-sixth of the GVA. This indicates underemployment or disguised unemployment, where more people are working than necessary, and their labor effort is divided. If some people are moved out, agricultural production will not suffer, and their incomes can increase elsewhere. This issue also exists in urban areas among casual workers, painters, plumbers, and street vendors.

2.4 How to Create More Employment?

Employment can be increased through targeted government investments and policy measures:

  • Agricultural Support: Constructing wells and dams for irrigation, providing cheap agricultural credit through banks, and improving rural roads and storage facilities.
  • Promoting Local Industries: Setting up dal mills, cold storages, and honey collection centers in semi-rural areas.
  • Improving Social Infrastructure: Creating jobs in the education sector (nearly 20 lakh jobs estimated by NITI Aayog) and health sector (doctors, nurses in rural areas).
  • Promoting Tourism and IT: Developing regional craft industries and tourism (which can provide employment to over 35 lakh people annually).
  • MGNREGA 2005 / Viksit Bharat-G RAM G 2025: A legislative measure implementing the 'Right to Work' in rural districts, guaranteeing 100 days of employment per year or providing unemployment allowances if the government fails to do so.
2.5 Division of Sectors as Organised and Unorganised

Economic activities can also be classified based on employment conditions:

  • Organised Sector: Enterprises where terms of employment are regular and registered with the government. They follow laws like the Factories Act and Minimum Wages Act. Workers enjoy job security, fixed working hours, overtime pay, paid leave, provident fund, medical benefits, and safe working environments.
  • Unorganised Sector: Small, scattered units largely outside government control. Rules exist but are not followed. Jobs are low-paid, irregular, and lack security. There are no provisions for paid leave, medical benefits, or pensions.

How to Protect Workers in the Unorganised Sector

Protection and support are essential for vulnerable groups in this sector:

  • Rural Areas: Landless agricultural laborers, small/marginal farmers, and artisans need timely delivery of seeds, agricultural inputs, credit, and marketing outlets.
  • Urban Areas: Workers in small-scale industries, casual construction workers, and street vendors need support for procuring raw materials and marketing output.
  • Social Protection: Majority of workers from scheduled castes, tribes, and backward communities are in this sector and face both economic exploitation and social discrimination.
2.6 Sectors in Terms of Ownership: Public and Private Sectors

Economic activities can also be classified based on who owns assets and is responsible for service delivery:

  • Public Sector: Owned and controlled by the government. Examples include Indian Railways and Post Offices. The primary purpose is public welfare and providing basic services, funded through taxes.
  • Private Sector: Owned and managed by private individuals or companies. Examples include Tata Iron and Steel Company (TISCO) and Reliance Industries Limited (RIL). Activities are guided primarily by the profit motive.

Role of Government in Public Infrastructure

Many essential services require large sums of money beyond the capacity of the private sector (e.g., construction of roads, bridges, railways, harbors, and dams). The government must undertake these heavy spendings to ensure they are available to everyone at reasonable costs. Additionally, the government must support critical activities (like supplying electricity at affordable rates and purchasing food grains at fair prices) and prioritize primary responsibilities like health, safe drinking water, and elementary education.
