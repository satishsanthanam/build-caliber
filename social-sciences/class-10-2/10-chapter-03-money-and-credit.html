Class 10 (Part 2) — Chapter 3: Money and Credit

3.1 Money as a Medium of Exchange

The use of money spans a very large part of our everyday life. It acts as an intermediary in transactions, eliminating the difficulties of direct exchange.

  • Barter System: A system where goods are directly exchanged without the use of money. In this system, double coincidence of wants is an essential feature.
  • Double Coincidence of Wants: A situation where what a person desires to sell is exactly what the other wishes to buy.
  • Medium of Exchange: Since money acts as an intermediate in the exchange process, it is called a medium of exchange. It eliminates the need for double coincidence of wants.
3.2 Modern Forms of Money

Modern forms of money include currency (paper notes and coins) and bank deposits.

3.2.1 Currency

  • Modern currency is not made of precious metals (like gold, silver, copper) and has no use of its own.
  • It is accepted as a medium of exchange because it is authorised by the government of the country.
  • In India, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) issues currency notes on behalf of the Central Government.
  • By law, no other individual or organisation is allowed to issue currency, and the rupee is widely accepted as a legal medium of payment that cannot be refused in settling transactions.

3.2.2 Deposits with Banks

  • People hold extra cash as deposits with banks by opening a bank account in their name.
  • Banks accept deposits and pay an interest rate on them, keeping the money safe.
  • Demand Deposits: Deposits in bank accounts that can be withdrawn on demand.
  • Cheque: A paper instructing the bank to pay a specific amount from the person's account to the person in whose name the cheque has been issued. It allows direct settlement of payments without using cash.
3.3 Loan Activities of Banks

Banks act as mediators between depositors (who have surplus funds) and borrowers (who need funds).

  • Banks in India hold a small proportion of their deposits as cash (about $5\%$) to pay depositors who might withdraw money on any given day.
  • The major portion of deposits is used to extend loans for various economic activities.
  • Source of Income: Banks charge a higher interest rate on loans than what they offer on deposits. The difference between these two rates is the bank's main source of income.
3.4 Two Different Credit Situations

Credit (Loan) refers to an agreement in which the lender supplies the borrower with money, goods, or services in return for the promise of future payment.

  • Situation 1: Festival Season (Salim): Salim obtains credit for working capital to complete a large shoe order. He completes production on time, makes a good profit, and repays the loan. Here, credit plays a vital and positive role.
  • Situation 2: Swapna's Problem: Swapna, a small farmer, takes a loan for crop cultivation. Due to pest attack, the crop fails. She is unable to repay and gets caught in a debt-trap, forcing her to sell a part of her land. Here, credit pushes the borrower into a painful situation.
3.5 Terms of Credit

Every loan agreement specifies certain conditions that the borrower must fulfill, collectively known as the terms of credit.

  • Collateral: An asset that the borrower owns (such as land, building, vehicle, livestock, bank deposits) and uses as a guarantee to a lender until the loan is repaid. If the borrower fails to repay, the lender has the right to sell the collateral.
  • Components of Terms of Credit:
    1. Interest rate
    2. Collateral
    3. Documentation requirement
    4. Mode of repayment
3.6 Variety of Credit Arrangements

Different people in rural areas obtain credit from various sources depending on their economic status:

  • Shyamal (Small Farmer): Borrows from agricultural traders at $3\%$ per month ($36\%$ per annum) or moneylenders at $5\%$ per month ($60\%$ per annum). Traders also force farmers to sell crops to them at low prices.
  • Arun (Medium Farmer): Receives a bank loan at $8.5\%$ per annum, which can be repaid anytime in three years. He can also get a fresh loan against cold storage receipts.
  • Rama (Landless Agricultural Labourer): Depends on her landowner-employer for credit at $5\%$ per month. She is often caught in a cycle of debt.
  • Loans from Cooperatives: Cooperative societies (like Krishak Cooperative) pool resources from members (e.g., farmers, weavers) to obtain bank loans and provide cheap credit to their members.
3.7 Formal Sector Credit in India

Loans can be grouped into formal and informal sectors.

3.7.1 Formal vs. Informal Sector

  • Formal Sector: Includes loans from banks and cooperatives. Supervised by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI). RBI ensures banks maintain cash balance and lend to small cultivators/borrowers, not just profit-making businesses.
  • Informal Sector: Includes moneylenders, traders, employers, relatives, and friends. There is no supervising organisation. They charge very high interest rates, leading to higher cost of borrowing and potential debt traps.

3.7.2 Who Gets What?

  • Rich households primarily avail cheap credit from formal sources (e.g., $83\%$ of loans for rich urban households are formal).
  • Poor households depend heavily on informal sources (e.g., $54\%$ of loans for poor urban households are informal).
  • Key Requirements for Development:
    1. Banks and cooperatives must expand formal lending in rural areas to reduce dependence on informal sources.
    2. Formal credit must be distributed more equally so that the poor can benefit from cheaper loans. Cheap and affordable credit is crucial for the country's development.
3.8 Self-Help Groups (SHGs) for the Poor

SHGs help poor borrowers, especially women, overcome the lack of collateral and access formal credit.

  • Structure: A typical Self-Help Group (SHG) consists of $15\text{ to }20$ members, usually from the same neighborhood, who meet and save regularly (ranging from $\text{Rs }25\text{ to Rs }100$ or more).
  • Functions:
    • Members can take small, low-interest loans from the group's savings.
    • If the group is regular in savings for $1\text{ to }2$ years, it becomes eligible to apply for a bank loan in the name of the group.
    • Decisions regarding savings and loans are taken collectively by the group members.
  • Benefits: Helps women become financially self-reliant, provides a platform to discuss social issues (health, nutrition, domestic violence), and overcomes the collateral barrier.
  • Grameen Bank of Bangladesh: Founded by Professor Muhammad Yunus (recipient of the 2006 Nobel Peace Prize), it is a major success story, reaching over $9\text{ million}$ members across $81,600$ villages with reasonable credit.
