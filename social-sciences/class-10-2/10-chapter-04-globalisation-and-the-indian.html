Class 10 (Part 2) — Chapter 4: Globalisation and the Indian Economy

Introduction: The Globalised Market

In today's world, consumers have a wide choice of goods and services. The latest models of digital cameras, mobile phones, and automobiles from leading global manufacturers are easily accessible. This rapid transformation of markets has occurred over the last few decades, changing how we understand production and trade.

1. Production Across Countries

Until the middle of the twentieth century, production was largely organised within countries. Trade in raw materials, foodstuff, and finished products was the main channel connecting distant nations.

Multinational Corporations (MNCs)

A Multinational Corporation (MNC) is a company that owns or controls production in more than one nation. MNCs set up offices and factories for production in regions where they can get cheap labour and other resources to minimise production costs and maximise profits.

Spreading of Production

MNCs do not just sell their finished products globally; their goods and services are also produced globally. Production is organised in increasingly complex ways, divided into small parts and spread across the globe:

  • China: Provides cheap manufacturing locations.
  • Mexico & Eastern Europe: Useful for closeness to major markets in the US and Europe.
  • India: Provides highly skilled engineers, technical support, and English-speaking youth for customer care services (call centres).
2. Interlinking Production Across Countries

MNCs set up production where it is close to markets, where cheap skilled and unskilled labour is available, and where other factors of production are assured.

Investment and Foreign Investment

The money spent to buy assets such as land, buildings, machines, and other equipment is called investment. Investment made by MNCs is called foreign investment, made with the hope of earning profits.

Routes of MNC Expansion
  • Joint Ventures: Setting up production jointly with local companies. Local companies benefit from additional investment (for new machines) and access to the latest technology.
  • Acquisitions (Buying Local Companies): The most common route. Wealthy MNCs buy out established local companies to expand production rapidly (e.g., American MNC Cargill Foods buying India's Parakh Foods).
  • Contract Manufacturing (Placing Orders): Large MNCs in developed countries place orders with small producers around the world for garments, footwear, and sports items, selling them under their own brand names.
3. Foreign Trade and Integration of Markets

Foreign trade has historically been the main channel connecting countries, creating opportunities for producers to reach beyond domestic markets and offering buyers choices beyond domestically produced goods.

Case Study: Chinese Toys in India

Chinese manufacturers exported cheap, plastic toys to India. Because of cheaper prices and new designs, Chinese toys quickly replaced Indian toys, capturing 70 to 80 percent of the toy shops within a year. This illustrates how trade leads to competition and price equalisation across markets.

Integration of Markets

Foreign trade results in connecting the markets or integration of markets in different countries. Goods travel from one market to another, choices rise, and prices of similar goods tend to become equal.

4. What is Globalisation?

Globalisation is the process of rapid integration or interconnection between countries. MNCs play a major role in this process, facilitating the movement of goods, services, investments, technology, and people across borders.

5. Factors That Have Enabled Globalisation

Technology
  • Transportation: Rapid improvements in transportation, such as the use of containers for cargo, have reduced port handling costs and enabled faster delivery of goods across long distances.
  • Information and Communication Technology (IT): Telecommunications, computers, the internet, and e-banking have made it possible to access information instantly and coordinate production globally.

Liberalisation of Foreign Trade and Investment Policy

Governments use trade barriers (like taxes on imports or quotas) to regulate foreign trade and protect domestic industries. Removing these barriers or restrictions is known as liberalisation.

In India, far-reaching policy changes were introduced around 1991, removing barriers to foreign trade and investment to encourage domestic producers to compete globally and improve quality.

6. World Trade Organisation (WTO)

The World Trade Organisation (WTO) is an international organisation whose aim is to liberalise international trade. It establishes rules regarding international trade and ensures they are obeyed.

Debate on Trade Practices

While WTO aims for free trade, developing countries argue that developed nations have unfairly retained trade barriers (e.g., massive agricultural subsidies in the US) while forcing developing nations to remove theirs, raising questions about "free and fair trade."

7. Impact of Globalisation in India

The impact of globalisation has not been uniform across different sections of society.

Positive Impacts
  • Consumers: Enjoy greater choice, improved quality, lower prices, and higher standards of living, particularly the urban well-off.
  • MNCs & Local Suppliers: Increased investments in industries like electronics, cell phones, automobiles, and fast food have created new jobs and boosted local raw material suppliers.
  • Indian Multinationals: Top Indian companies (e.g., Tata Motors, Infosys, Ranbaxy) have expanded globally and benefited from advanced technology.

Government Initiatives: SEZs

To attract foreign investment, central and state governments are setting up Special Economic Zones (SEZs) with world-class infrastructure. Companies in SEZs enjoy tax exemptions for an initial period of five years and flexibility in labour laws (hiring workers "flexibly" on a temporary basis).

Negative Impacts and Challenges
  • Small Producers: Small-scale industries (e.g., batteries, capacitors, plastics, toys) have been hit hard by cheap imports, leading to unit closures and job losses.
  • Workers and Employment: Rising competition has led to highly insecure and "flexible" employment. Workers in garment export industries face long hours, low wages, and lack of basic benefits.
8. The Struggle for a Fair Globalisation

Fair globalisation would create opportunities for all and ensure that its benefits are shared better.

Role of the Government

The government can play a major role by:

  • Ensuring proper implementation of labour laws to protect workers' rights.
  • Supporting small producers until they are strong enough to compete.
  • Using trade and investment barriers effectively.
  • Negotiating for fairer rules at the WTO and aligning with other developing nations.

Role of People's Campaigns

Massive public campaigns and representations by people's organisations have successfully influenced critical decisions related to trade and investment at the WTO, proving that citizens can play an active role in the struggle for fair globalisation.
