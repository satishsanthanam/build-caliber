Chapter 8: Clothes—How Things are Made

8.1 Patterns with Threads

Nature is full of hidden artists—animals, birds, and insects who weave, stitch, design, and glue things together long before humans did.

8.1.1 Weaving in Nature

The baya weaver is a weaverbird that builds beautiful hanging nests from grass. It weaves strands over and under to make the nest strong and pouch-shaped. Expert weaverbirds weave very fine nests, while younger ones make rougher ones.

8.1.2 What is Weaving?

Weaving combines strips or threads of a material into a patterned fabric like cloth. One set of threads is placed vertically, and the other goes horizontally, crossing over and under each other to form a unified fabric (such as a mat, basket, or cloth).

Common natural materials used for weaving include:

  • Coconut fibre
  • Palm reeds
  • Bamboo
  • Grass
  • Jute
  • Cotton
  • Silk

Activity 1: Paper Weaving

An activity to understand weaving using blue and yellow paper strips woven in an over-under pattern to create a paper mat.

8.2 Traditions of Weaving

People in India have known how to weave for over 4,000 years. Traditional weaving is done by hand on an instrument called a loom, and the resulting cloth is called handloom fabric.

8.2.1 Famous Handloom Traditions in India
  • Kanjeevaram: Tamil Nadu
  • Pashmina: Kashmir (made from the wool of the Changthangi goat in Ladakh)
  • Ikat: Odisha and Gujarat
  • Patola: Patan, Gujarat (highly complex, taking 6 months to 1 year to weave a single saree)
  • Indian Muslin: Historically known as 'woven air' because it was so fine a whole saree could pass through a ring.

8.2.2 Importance of Weaving

Weaving provides livelihood to many families and keeps traditional skills alive. The Indian handloom sector employs over 45 lakh people, especially women and rural artisans, making it one of the largest cottage industries in the world.

8.3 Thread and Spinning

Before weaving cloth, threads must be made from raw materials.

8.3.1 Spinning and Fibres

A fibre is a thin, hair-like strand obtained from natural or artificial sources. The process of twisting fibres together to make thread or yarn is called spinning.

A charkha (spinning wheel) is a traditional tool used to spin thread from cotton.

8.3.2 Khadi and Self-Sufficiency

Mahatma Gandhi promoted spinning thread and weaving cloth by hand as a symbol of the freedom struggle and self-reliance (atmanirbhar). Hand-spun and hand-woven cloth is known as khadi.

8.4 Types of Fibres

Fibres are broadly classified into natural and synthetic categories.

8.4.1 Natural Fibres

Obtained from plants and animals. Examples include:

  • Bamboo
  • Cotton (India was the first country to cultivate and use cotton)
  • Linen
  • Wool
  • Silk: Obtained from the cocoon of the silk moth.

Life Cycle of a Silkworm:

  1. Silkworm eggs
  2. Caterpillar (larva)
  3. Cocoon (spun by the caterpillar around itself)
  4. Moth (adult emerges from the cocoon)

8.4.2 Synthetic Fibres

Made by humans using artificial materials. Examples include:

  • Nylon
  • Rayon
  • Polyester
  • Terylene
8.5 Crafting with Needle and Thread

Stitching is the process of joining pieces of fabric together using a needle and thread.

8.5.1 The Tailorbird

The tailorbird is a tiny green bird that stitches its own nest. Using its beak as a needle, it sews the edges of big leaves together using plant fibres or spider silk, padding it to make a safe nest.

8.5.2 Basic Stitching

The basic stitch used to join fabrics is the running stitch, which moves the needle up and down through the cloth in a straight line.

8.6 Stitch and Decorate

Stitches are used not only to join cloth but also to decorate it beautifully. Different regions of India have unique traditional embroideries.

8.6.1 Traditional Embroideries of India
  • Chikan or Chikankari: Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh
  • Banjara: Rajasthan
  • Kantha: West Bengal, Odisha, and Tripura
  • Gota: Rajasthan
  • Phulkari: Punjab
  • Toda: Tamil Nadu
  • Kashmiri: Kashmir
  • Khneng embroidery: Meghalaya
  • Bandhani: A type of tie-dye where small parts of the cloth are tied and dyed to make patterns (Gujarat and Rajasthan).
8.7 Recycle and Sustainability

In India, there is a strong tradition of reusing and recycling clothes rather than throwing them away.

  • Old clothes are passed down to younger siblings or others.
  • Old clothes are repurposed into quilts, mats, or cleaning cloths.
  • Kala (black) cotton: A type of cotton grown in Gujarat that grows without chemicals or extra water, woven into strong, eco-friendly fabric.
