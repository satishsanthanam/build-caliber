Class 5 — Chapter 1: Water—The Essence of Life

1.1 Introduction: Water on Earth

Although most of the Earth's surface is covered with water, the majority of it is salty. Only a very small fraction is freshwater. All living beings—people, animals, birds, and plants—depend on freshwater to survive. It is essential for drinking, growing crops, and carrying out daily activities.

The Freshwater Ratio

To understand the distribution of water on Earth, imagine if all the water on Earth were in a single glass ($200\text{ ml}$), then the available freshwater would only be as much as in a single teaspoon ($5\text{ ml}$)!

Traditional Water Conservation

Johads in Rajasthan are traditional small earthen dams built to collect rainwater and recharge groundwater.

1.2 Different Forms of Water

Water exists in three distinct forms in nature:

  • Liquid: As seen in rain, rivers, and oceans.
  • Solid: As seen in ice, snow, and glaciers.
  • Vapour: As seen in steam and moisture in the air.

Observing Changes in States of Water

Water changes its form depending on temperature:

  • Melting: Solid ice turns into liquid water when left at room temperature.
  • Boiling: Liquid water turns into water vapour (steam) when heated.
  • Evaporation: Water left in sunlight slowly turns into vapour and disappears into the air.
1.3 The Water Cycle

The constant circular movement of water in nature is called the water cycle.

  • The Sun's heat causes water from oceans, rivers, and lakes to evaporate into water vapour.
  • As water vapour rises, it cools down and condenses to form clouds.
  • Eventually, these water droplets in the clouds fall back to the Earth as rain, snow, or hail.
  • This water flows back into rivers, lakes, and oceans, repeating the cycle continuously.
1.4 Groundwater and Recharge

When it rains, some water is soaked up by the soil and sinks through the layers of soil and rocks. This water stored deep underground is called groundwater.

  • We access groundwater by digging wells, borewells, tube wells, and using handpumps.
  • In cities, cemented and concrete surfaces prevent rainwater from seeping into the ground, stopping groundwater recharge.
  • Open, uncovered areas, green lawns, soak pits, ponds, and planting trees help rainwater return underground.
1.5 Surface Water and River Flows

Surface water includes rivers, ponds, and lakes. Rivers typically begin their journey high up in the mountains from melting glaciers and flow down across the land.

  • Water flows, stops, or curves according to the shape of the land formations in its path.
  • The flow of water can also actively shape and change the landforms over time.
  • The Luni River: Originating in the Aravalli Range in Rajasthan, it is the only major Indian river that does not drain into a sea. Instead, it ends in the marshy lands of the Rann of Kutch in Gujarat.
1.6 Life in Water (Aquatic Ecosystems)

Freshwater habitats support a rich variety of plants and animals, each with unique adaptations to survive in water.

Aquatic Animals and Plants

  • Dragonfly: Flies fast and lays eggs near water; eats mosquitoes.
  • Water Scorpion: An insect with pincers that lives underwater and breathes using a tail tube.
  • Pond Heron: A bird with long legs that stands still to catch fish.
  • Freshwater Turtle: A soft-shelled turtle that lives in ponds and rivers.
  • Water Snake: A harmless water snake that eats fish and frogs.
  • Lotus and Water Lily: Rooted floating plants whose flowers float on water while roots stay in the pond bed. Their leaves have a waxy coating that makes them waterproof, preventing damage from excess water.
  • Water Hyacinth: A fast-growing, free-floating plant that spreads rapidly on the water surface.
The River Food Chain

Organisms in aquatic ecosystems depend on each other for food, forming a food chain (e.g., plants are eaten by small fish, which are eaten by big fish, which are then eaten by birds or crocodiles).

1.7 Let Us Reflect
  • Matching Concepts: Ocean water is not fit for drinking; Snow is the solid form of water; Steam is the vapour form of water; Rainwater is a source of freshwater.
  • Water Conservation: Designing houses and schools with rainwater harvesting systems helps conserve water wisely.
