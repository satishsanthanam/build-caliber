Class 8 — Chapter 7: Factors of Production

7.1 Introduction

Every product around us goes through a production process before it reaches us. This process involves using resources or inputs required to produce the final product. The resources or inputs used in producing goods and services are called factors of production.

A business is defined as a firm, a shop, a factory, etc., which produces or sells goods or provides a service. Businesses combine various inputs or factors of production to create goods and services, generating opportunities for people to engage in economic activities.

7.2 Factors of Production

In economics, the inputs used in a production process are classified into four types: land, labour, capital, and entrepreneurship. Technology acts as a facilitator that enables businesses to produce more goods with the same or fewer inputs.

7.2.1 Land (Natural Resources)

The word land in economics encompasses not only geographical land but also natural resources like soil, forests, water, air, sunlight, minerals, oil, and natural gas. Businesses either purchase the required land or pay rent to use it for a period of time.

7.2.2 Labour (Human Resources)

Labour is essential in production, involving physical and mental effort. Everyone contributes differently through their work, helping create goods and services for society's needs.

Human beings play a key role in economic activities as they apply their knowledge, skills, and decision-making abilities. A skill is the ability to do an activity or job well with practice and training.

While labour refers to the physical and mental effort used in production, human capital refers to the specialised skills, knowledge, abilities, and expertise required to perform that labour. It represents the quality and efficiency of labour.

7.2.3 Facilitators of Human Capital

Human capital is developed and enhanced through several key facilitators:

  • Education and Training: Education helps individuals gain knowledge, starting from basic literacy to expertise in specific fields. Training is the process of learning the required skills to do a particular job or activity. Together, they prepare individuals to excel in their careers.
  • Healthcare: Good health supports cognitive development (the process of learning, knowing, and understanding), allowing children to attend school regularly and workers to perform at their physical and mental best.
  • Social and Cultural Influences: A culture of hard work and continuous improvement helps nations progress. Examples include the Japanese concept of kaizen (continuous improvement) and the German work ethic, which values punctuality, attention to detail, and quality.

7.2.4 Challenges to Human Capital

Despite progress, developing human capital faces challenges. Literacy is crucial for enhancing skills and productivity. As of $2023$, the Adult Literacy Rate in India is estimated at $85\%$ for males and $70\%$ for females.

According to the Economic Survey of India $2024$, $65\%$ of people in India are below the age of $35$ years. This provides a potential demographic dividend, which is the benefit a country gets when it has a large number of young and working people. To reap this benefit, individuals must have access to quality education, healthcare, training, and skilling.

7.2.5 India's Ancient Skill Heritage

In ancient India, work was considered a form of devotion and striving for perfection. Creating products involved a unique blend of kalā (art) and vidyā (knowledge). Ancient texts called śhilpa śhāstras contained detailed design guidelines for sculptures, paintings, buildings, and jewellery.

An example of ancient skill is stitched shipbuilding, a unique technique dating back over $2000$ years where wooden planks were stitched together using cords instead of nails, making ships flexible enough to navigate the Indian Ocean with ease.

7.2.6 Capital

Businesses require capital, which in economics refers to any asset, whether physical or financial, used to produce goods and services. This includes monetary resources and durable assets like machinery, tools, equipment, vehicles, and buildings.

Sources of capital include:

  • Personal Savings, Family, and Friends: The primary source of funds for individuals starting a business.
  • Bank Loans: Borrowed funds for which the borrower pays interest (the amount of money paid by the borrower of a loan to the lender for using their money for a specific time).
  • Stock Market: A special type of market where shares of large companies are bought and sold. Companies raise financial capital from the public by offering them a share of the profits, called a dividend.

7.2.7 Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship means starting your own business or creating something new to solve a problem. An entrepreneur is a person who comes up with an idea, takes risks, gathers other factors of production, and works hard to make their startup (an entrepreneurial venture with limited resources aiming for rapid growth while leveraging technology) successful.

Case Study: J.R.D. Tata
Jehangir Ratanji Dadabhoy (J.R.D.) Tata ($1904$–$1993$) was one of India's greatest entrepreneurs and industrialists. He established India's first airline, Tata Airlines, in $1932$ (later Air India), and expanded the Tata Group into steel, cars, power, and chemicals. He believed businesses should help society and care for workers, and was awarded the Bharat Ratna in $1992$.

7.2.8 Technology: An Enabler of Production

Technology is the application of scientific knowledge to production. It makes it easier and cheaper for businesses to operate. Modern examples include UPI (Unified Payments Interface) for instant payments, GPS for transport routing, and drones for agricultural spraying.

Technology also facilitates learning and job access through government platforms like:

  • SWAYAM: Offers free online courses (MOOCs) from Grade 9 onwards in subjects like robotics and aquaculture.
  • National Career Service: An online portal helping people find job opportunities across various sectors.
7.3 How are the Factors Connected?

The factors of production—land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship, and technology—are combined to produce goods and services. The proportion of each factor depends on the product:

  • Labour-intensive: Sectors like agriculture, construction, and handicrafts rely more heavily on labour.
  • Capital-intensive: Sectors like semiconductor chips or satellite manufacturing require more capital and specialised machinery.

These factors are highly interconnected. A disruption in one part can halt the entire supply chain, which is the network of individuals, organisations, resources, activities, and technology involved in the production and sale of goods.

7.4 Responsibilities towards Factors of Production

Producers must use natural resources responsibly to avoid pollution and degradation (e.g., preventing leather factory waste from polluting rivers or recycling electronic waste containing lead and mercury). Sustainable practices are essential to replenish resources for future generations.

Businesses also have responsibilities towards their workers:

  • Fair compensation and working conditions: Ensuring fair pay and safe environments.
  • Skill development and training: Investing in workers' continuous learning.
  • Workplace rights and protections: Adhering to laws against discrimination and providing benefits like healthcare and paid leave.

Many businesses address these social and environmental concerns through Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR). In $2014$, India became the first nation to mandate by law that certain companies spend $2\%$ of their average net profits on CSR activities.
