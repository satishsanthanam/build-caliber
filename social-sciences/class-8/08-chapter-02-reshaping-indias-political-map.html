Class 8 — Chapter 2: Reshaping India’s Political Map

2.1 Introduction & Timeline

The period from the 11th to the 17th centuries in India is often referred to as the latter part of the medieval period. This era was marked by unprecedented invasions from beyond the Hindu Kush mountains, which reshaped India's political boundaries. Many of these invaders were of Turkic or Afghan origin, drawn by India's riches and territorial ambitions.

  • 1206: Establishment of the Delhi Sultanate
  • 1326: Re-establishment of the Mewar Kingdom
  • 1336: Establishment of the Vijayanagara Empire
  • 1347: Establishment of the Bahmani Sultanate
  • 1526: First Battle of Panipat (Mughal Empire established)
  • 1556: Second Battle of Panipat (Akbar's victory)
  • 1565: Battle of Talikota (destruction of Vijayanagara)
  • 1671: Battle of Saraighat (Ahoms defeat Mughals)
  • 1699: Formation of the Khalsa by Guru Gobind Singh
  • 1799: Establishment of the Sikh Empire
2.2 Rise and Fall of the Delhi Sultanate

The Delhi Sultanate was formed in 1192 after the defeat of King Prithviraj Chauhan. It saw the rule of five successive foreign dynasties of Turkic-Afghan origin: the Mamluks (Slave dynasty), the Khiljis, the Tughlaqs, the Sayyids, and the Lodis.

2.2.1 Key Rulers and Expansion

  • Ala-ud-din Khilji: Conducted extensive military campaigns across northern, central, and southern India (led by his slave-general Malik Kafur). He successfully repelled several Mongol invasions and styled himself as "Sikander Sani" (the second Alexander).
  • Muhammad bin Tughlaq: Expanded the Sultanate to its greatest extent, briefly bringing most of the subcontinent under one ruler. However, his ambitious schemes, such as shifting the capital from Delhi to Daulatabad (Devagiri) and introducing token currency (copper coins), failed and led to economic decline.

2.2.2 Impact of Sultanate Rule

  • Plunder and Iconoclasm: Military campaigns were often marked by the destruction of temples and seats of learning, motivated by plunder and iconoclasm (the rejection or destruction of religious images).
  • Discriminatory Taxation: Some sultans imposed the jizya, a tax on non-Muslim subjects (referred to as infidels), which served as an economic burden and social incentive for conversion.
  • Timur's Invasion: At the end of the 14th century, the Turkic-Mongol conqueror Timur sacked Delhi, leaving the city in ruins and the Sultanate severely weakened.
2.3 Resistance to the Delhi Sultanate

The expansion of the Delhi Sultanate faced continuous resistance from regional powers across the subcontinent.

  • Eastern Ganga Kingdom: Based in Kalinga (Odisha), King Narasimhadeva I repelled Sultanate inroads, defeated the governor of Bengal, and built the famed Surya Temple at Konark to commemorate his victories.
  • Musunuri Nayakas: Telugu chieftains who formed a confederacy of over 75 leaders to defeat the Sultanate forces and expel Muhammad bin Tughlaq's army from Warangal.
  • Hoysalas: Ruled parts of southern India (Karnataka) and fended off several attacks, remaining independent until they were eventually absorbed into the Vijayanagara Empire.
  • Rana Kumbha: Ruler of the Mewar kingdom in Rajasthan who successfully repelled invasions from regional sultanates and built the massive Kumbhalgarh Fort.
2.4 The Vijayanagara Empire

Established in the 14th century by two brothers, Harihara and Bukka, the Vijayanagara Empire became a dominant political and cultural force in southern India, serving as a major rival to the northern Bahmani Sultanate.

2.4.1 Peak under Krishnadevaraya

In the 16th century, the empire reached its zenith under Krishnadevaraya:

  • He secured dominance over the Deccan and patronized literature in Sanskrit, Telugu, and Kannada.
  • He composed the Telugu epic poem Amuktamalyada, which included a section on Rajaniti (royal policy/governance).
  • He provided grand patronage to temples, including the Vitthala temple in his capital, Vijayanagara (Hampi), famous for its "musical pillars."
  • Foreign travelers like Domingo Paes described the capital as large, beautiful, and "the best provided city in the world."

2.4.2 Decline and Fall

In 1565, a coalition of the Deccan Sultanates defeated the Vijayanagara forces at the Battle of Talikota. The capital was systematically sacked, destroyed, and left in ruins, leading to the fragmentation of the empire into smaller regions ruled by Nayakas.

2.5 The Mughal Empire

The decline of the Delhi Sultanate paved the way for the rise of the Mughal Empire, founded by Babur in 1526.

2.5.1 Foundation and Early Rulers

  • Babur: A Turkic-Mongol ruler who defeated Ibrahim Lodi at the First Battle of Panipat (1526) using advanced gunpowder, field artillery, and matchlock guns. He detailed his impressions of India in his autobiography, the Baburnama.
  • Humayun: Struggled to maintain the empire and was briefly ousted by the Afghan leader Sher Shah Suri, who established the short-lived Sur Empire before Humayun reclaimed his territory.

2.5.2 Akbar's Reign (1556–1605)

Akbar expanded and stabilized the empire through a combination of military conquest and strategic diplomacy:

  • Military Brutality: Early conquests, such as the siege of Chittorgarh, were brutal, resulting in massacres and the practice of jauhar (mass self-immolation by Rajput women to avoid capture).
  • Political Alliances & Tolerance: Akbar later adopted a policy of religious tolerance, promoting the doctrine of sulh-i kul ("peace with all"). He abolished the jizya, entered into marriage alliances with Rajput princesses, and appointed Hindu officials to high administrative posts.
  • Cultural Synthesis: He established a "house of translation" at Fatehpur Sikri to translate major Sanskrit texts (like the Mahabharata and Ramayana) into Persian.

2.5.3 Jahangir, Shah Jahan, and Aurangzeb

  • Jahangir & Shah Jahan: Continued the patronage of art and architecture. Shah Jahan is best remembered as the builder of the Taj Mahal at Agra and the Red Fort in Delhi.
  • Aurangzeb (1658–1707): Reached the greatest territorial expansion but faced constant rebellions. A deeply religious Sunni Muslim, he reversed Akbar's policies by banning music and dance at court, reimposing the jizya, and ordering the demolition of numerous temples, schools, and gurudwaras. His prolonged military campaigns in the Deccan depleted the treasury and led to the rapid decline of the empire.
2.6 Resistance to the Mughals

Aurangzeb's orthodox policies and heavy taxation triggered widespread rebellions across India.

2.6.1 Peasant, Tribal, and Rajput Resistance

  • Jat Peasantry: Rebelled against harsh exploitation in northern India, confronting the Mughal army in major battles.
  • Tribal Groups: Bhils, Gonds, Santhals, and Kochs resisted annexation and taxation. Rani Durgavati of the Gond kingdom of Garha fought valiantly against Akbar's forces in 1564, choosing suicide over capture.
  • The Rajputs: Mewar's ruler Maharana Pratap refused Mughal suzerainty and fought the Mughals at the Battle of Haldighati (1576), continuing guerrilla warfare from the Aravalli hills with the support of the Bhils.

2.6.2 The Ahoms

Migrating from Myanmar to the Brahmaputra Valley in the 13th century, the Ahoms resisted both Sultanate and Mughal expansions:

  • Paik System: A unique labor system where every able-bodied man rendered military or labor service to the state in exchange for land, eliminating the need for a permanent standing army.
  • Battle of Saraighat (1671): Led by commander Lachit Borphukan, the Ahoms used their knowledge of the riverine terrain to decisively defeat a much larger Mughal force.

2.6.3 The Rise of the Sikhs

  • Founded by Guru Nanak in the 15th century as a spiritual movement emphasizing equality and compassion.
  • Mughal persecution led to the militarization of the community. After Guru Arjan was tortured to death by Jahangir, Guru Hargobind introduced martial training.
  • In 1675, Guru Tegh Bahadur was publicly beheaded by Aurangzeb for refusing to convert to Islam.
  • His son, Guru Gobind Singh, established the Khalsa, a martial brotherhood committed to justice and equality.
  • In the early 19th century, Maharaja Ranjit Singh unified the Sikh confederacies to establish a strong, centralized Sikh Empire.
2.7 Administering India

2.7.1 Delhi Sultanate Administration

  • Centred on the absolute authority of the Sultan, assisted by a council of ministers.
  • Iqta System: Territories were assigned to nobles (iqtadars) to collect taxes. A portion of the revenue was sent to the Sultan's treasury to maintain the army. The posts were non-hereditary, and tax collection was often harsh on the peasantry.

2.7.2 Mughal Administrative Framework

  • Akbar reorganized the administration with key ministers: the Diwan (finances), Mir Bakhshi (military), Khan-i-Saman (public works/household), and Sadr (justice/religion). The empire was divided into twelve provinces (subahs).
  • Mansabdari System: Officers (mansabdars) were assigned ranks and expected to maintain a specific number of troops and animals. They were paid through land grants (jagirs).
  • Revenue Reforms: Akbar's finance minister, Todar Mal, introduced a systematic land survey and crop yield assessment to establish an efficient revenue system.
2.8 People's Lives and Economy
  • Economic Prosperity: Despite political instability, India remained one of the wealthiest regions in the world, supported by agriculture, artisanal industries, and trade guilds (shrenis).
  • Currency: The Mughals introduced a standardized currency system using the silver rupaya and the copper dam.
  • Hundi System: A written payment instruction (precursor to modern banking) that allowed merchants to transfer funds across political borders without carrying physical currency, facilitating safe trade.
  • Temples as Economic Hubs: Temples received land donations (dana), managed community infrastructure (irrigation, pilgrim shelters), and provided credit to fund local and maritime trade.
