Class 10 (Part 4) — Chapter 4: Political Parties

4.1 Overview

Political parties are among the most visible institutions in a democracy. For most ordinary citizens, democracy is virtually equal to political parties. This chapter takes a close look at the nature and working of political parties, especially in India, by addressing key questions: Why do we need parties? How many parties are good for a democracy? What are the national and regional parties in India? What challenges do they face, and how can they be reformed?

4.2 Why Do We Need Political Parties?

4.2.1 Meaning of a Political Party

A political party is a group of people who come together to contest elections and hold power in the government. They agree on some policies and programmes for the society with a view to promote the collective good.

Parties reflect fundamental political divisions in a society and involve partisanship (a tendency to take a side and inability to take a balanced view on an issue). A political party has three core components:

  • The leaders
  • The active members
  • The followers

4.2.2 Functions of Political Parties

Political parties perform a series of crucial functions in a democracy:

  • Contest Elections: In most democracies, elections are fought mainly among candidates put up by political parties.
  • Formulate Policies and Programmes: Parties reduce a vast multitude of opinions into a few basic positions, which the voters can choose from.
  • Making Laws: Parties play a decisive role in making laws for a country. Since most members belong to a party, they go by the direction of the party leadership.
  • Form and Run Governments: The big policy decisions are taken by the political executive that comes from political parties. Parties recruit, train, and appoint leaders as ministers.
  • Role of Opposition: Parties that lose elections play the role of opposition by voicing different views and criticizing the government for its failures or wrong policies.
  • Shape Public Opinion: They raise and highlight issues, often launching movements for the resolution of problems faced by people.
  • Provide Access to Government Machinery: For an ordinary citizen, it is easier to approach a local party leader than a government officer to access welfare schemes.

4.2.3 Necessity of Political Parties

We need political parties because they perform all these essential functions. Without political parties, every candidate in an election would be independent. No one would be able to make major policy promises to the people, the utility of the government would remain uncertain, and no single entity would be responsible for how the country is run as a whole. The rise of political parties is directly linked to the emergence of representative democracies in large and complex societies.

4.3 How Many Parties Should We Have?

4.3.1 Types of Party Systems

Different countries have evolved different party systems based on their historical and social contexts:

  • One-Party System: Only one party is allowed to control and run the government (e.g., the Communist Party in China). This is not considered a democratic option.
  • Two-Party System: Power seriously alternates between two main parties, though other smaller parties may exist (e.g., the United States of America and the United Kingdom).
  • Multi-Party System: Several parties compete for power, and more than two parties have a reasonable chance of coming to power, either on their own strength or in alliance with others (e.g., India). This often leads to coalition governments.

4.3.2 Alliances and Fronts

When several parties in a multi-party system join hands for the purpose of contesting elections and winning power, it is called an alliance or a front. In India, major alliances include:

  • National Democratic Alliance (NDA)
  • United Progressive Alliance (UPA)
  • Left Front
4.4 National Parties

4.4.1 Criteria for Recognition

Every party in India must register with the Election Commission. The Commission lays down detailed criteria for a party to be recognized as a State or National party:

  • State Party: A party that secures at least $6 ext{ ext{\%}}$ of the total votes in an election to the Legislative Assembly of a State and wins at least two seats is recognized as a State party.
  • National Party: A party that secures at least $6 ext{ ext{\%}}$ of the total votes in Lok Sabha elections or Assembly elections in four States and wins at least four seats in the Lok Sabha is recognized as a National party.

4.4.2 Major National Parties in India (as of 2023)

  • Aam Aadmi Party (AAP): Formed in 2012 following the 2011 anti-corruption movement. Focuses on clean administration, transparency, and good governance.
  • Bahujan Samaj Party (BSP): Formed in 1984 under the leadership of Kanshi Ram. Seeks to represent and secure power for the bahujan samaj (Dalits, Adivasis, OBCs, and religious minorities).
  • Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP): Founded in 1980 by reviving the erstwhile Bharatiya Jana Sangh. Wants to build a strong and modern India drawing inspiration from India's ancient culture and values, and the idea of integral humanism and Antyodaya. Cultural nationalism (Hindutva) is a core element.
  • Communist Party of India - Marxist (CPI-M): Founded in 1964. Believes in Marxism-Leninism, supports socialism, secularism, and democracy, and opposes imperialism and communalism.
  • Indian National Congress (INC): One of the oldest parties in the world, founded in 1885. A centrist party that espouses secularism and the welfare of weaker sections and minorities.
  • National People's Party (NPP): Formed in 2013 under the leadership of P.A. Sangma. It is the first political party from North East India to attain national party status.
4.5 State Parties

Most of the major parties of the country are classified by the Election Commission as State parties, commonly referred to as regional parties. However, they need not be regional in their ideology or outlook. Parties like the Samajwadi Party and Rashtriya Janata Dal have national-level political organizations with units in several states. Over the last three decades, the expansion of state parties has made the Parliament of India politically more diverse, making coalition governments at the Centre a necessity from 1996 to 2014.

4.6 Challenges to Political Parties

Popular dissatisfaction and criticism focus on four major problem areas in the working of political parties:

  • Lack of Internal Democracy: Concentration of power in the hands of one or a few leaders at the top. Parties do not keep membership registers, hold regular organizational meetings, or conduct internal elections.
  • Dynastic Succession: Since procedures are not transparent, leaders are in a position to favor people close to them or their family members, preventing ordinary workers from rising to the top.
  • Money and Muscle Power: Especially during elections, parties tend to nominate candidates who can raise lots of money or have criminal links to secure wins.
  • Lack of Meaningful Choice: Decline in ideological differences among parties in most parts of the world, leaving voters with limited distinct options.
4.7 How Can Parties Be Reformed?

4.7.1 Recent Efforts and Laws in India

  • Anti-Defection Law: The Constitution was amended to prevent elected MPs and MLAs from changing parties after elections. Defection is changing party allegiance from the party on which a person got elected to a different party.
  • Filing of Affidavits: The Supreme Court passed an order making it mandatory for every candidate contesting elections to file an affidavit giving details of their property and criminal cases pending against them.
  • Organizational Elections and IT Returns: The Election Commission passed an order making it necessary for political parties to hold organizational elections and file their income tax returns.

4.7.2 Suggestions for Further Reforms

  • A law should be made to regulate the internal affairs of political parties (maintaining registers, following constitutions, holding open elections).
  • It should be made mandatory for political parties to give a minimum number of tickets (about one-third) to women candidates.
  • There should be state funding of elections, where the government gives parties resources to support their election expenses.

4.7.3 Role of Ordinary Citizens

Parties can also be reformed through public pressure (petitions, publicity, and agitations by pressure groups, media, and citizens) and direct participation (more people joining political parties to improve the quality of democracy from within).
