Class 10 (Part 4) — Chapter 2: Federalism

Overview

In modern democracies, the vertical division of power among different levels of government is a major form of power-sharing, commonly referred to as federalism. This chapter explores the theory and practice of federalism, with a specific focus on India, its constitutional provisions, and the dynamics of local government.

1. What is Federalism?

Federalism is a system of government in which power is divided between a central authority and various constituent units of the country. Usually, a federation has two levels of government, both enjoying power independent of the other.

Key Features of Federalism

  • Two or more levels: There are two or more tiers of government.
  • Same citizens, different jurisdictions: Different tiers govern the same citizens, but each tier has its own jurisdiction in specific matters of legislation, taxation, and administration.
  • Constitutional guarantee: The existence and authority of each tier of government is constitutionally guaranteed.
  • Consent for constitutional changes: Fundamental provisions of the constitution cannot be unilaterally changed by one level of government; they require the consent of both levels.
  • Judicial arbitration: Courts have the power to interpret the constitution. The highest court acts as an umpire in case of disputes between different levels of government.
  • Financial autonomy: Sources of revenue for each level of government are clearly specified to ensure financial independence.
  • Dual objectives: To safeguard and promote the unity of the country, while accommodating regional diversity.

Two Routes of Federation Formation

  • Coming Together Federations: Independent States come together on their own to form a bigger unit, pooling sovereignty and retaining identity to increase security. Examples: USA, Switzerland, Australia.
  • Holding Together Federations: A large country decides to divide its power between the constituent States and the national government. Examples: India, Spain, Belgium. In these, the central government tends to be more powerful vis-à-vis the states.
2. What Makes India a Federal Country?

The Constitution declares India as a Union of States. Although it does not use the word 'federation', the Indian Union is based on federal principles, originally providing a two-tier system of government (Union Government and State Governments), later expanded to a three-tier system with Panchayats and Municipalities.

Three-Fold Distribution of Legislative Powers

  • Union List: Includes subjects of national importance like defence, foreign affairs, banking, communications, and currency. Only the Union Government can make laws on these.
  • State List: Contains subjects of State and local importance like police, trade, commerce, agriculture, and irrigation. Only State Governments can make laws on these.
  • Concurrent List: Includes subjects of common interest like education, forest, trade unions, marriage, adoption, and succession. Both Union and State Governments can make laws. In case of conflict, the law made by the Union Government prevails.
  • Residuary Subjects: Subjects that do not fall in any of the three lists (like computer software) are legislated upon solely by the Union Government.

Special Provisions and Union Territories

Not all constituent units of the Indian Union have equal powers:

  • Special Status: States like Assam, Nagaland, Arunachal Pradesh, and Mizoram enjoy special powers under Article 371 of the Constitution due to their peculiar social and historical circumstances.
  • Union Territories: Areas like Chandigarh, Lakshadweep, or Delhi are too small to become independent states and cannot be merged with existing states. They are run directly by the Central Government.

Constitutional Amendments and Judiciary

The power-sharing arrangement is basic to the structure of the Constitution. Any change requires a two-thirds majority in both Houses of Parliament, followed by ratification by the legislatures of at least half of the total States. The Judiciary (High Courts and Supreme Court) plays a crucial role in resolving disputes regarding the division of powers.

3. How is Federalism Practised?

The real success of federalism in India is attributed to the nature of democratic politics, which ensures respect for diversity and a shared desire for living together.

Linguistic States

The creation of Linguistic States was the first major test for democratic politics. Boundaries of several old states were changed to ensure people speaking the same language lived in the same state. Some states were created based on culture, ethnicity, or geography (e.g., Nagaland, Uttarakhand, Jharkhand).

Language Policy

The Indian Constitution does not give the status of national language to any single language. Hindi is identified as the official language, alongside 21 other Scheduled Languages. English continues to be used for official purposes to protect the interests of non-Hindi speaking states.

Centre-State Relations

Restructuring Centre-State relations has strengthened federalism. Before 1990, the same party often ruled both at the Centre and in most States, undermining state autonomy. Post-1990, the rise of regional parties led to the era of Coalition Governments at the Centre, supported by a major Supreme Court judgment making it difficult for the Central Government to arbitrarily dismiss State Governments.

4. Decentralisation in India

When power is taken away from Central and State governments and given to local government, it is called Decentralisation. The basic rationale is that local problems are best settled at the local level with direct public participation.

The 1992 Constitutional Amendment

A major step towards decentralisation was taken in 1992 to make the third-tier of democracy more powerful:

  • Mandatory regular elections to local government bodies.
  • Reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes (SC), Scheduled Tribes (ST), and Other Backward Classes (OBC).
  • At least one-third of all positions reserved for women.
  • Creation of an independent State Election Commission in each state.
  • State governments must share some powers and revenue with local bodies.

Rural Local Government (Panchayati Raj)

  • Gram Panchayat: A decision-making council for each village or group of villages, consisting of ward members (panch) and a president (sarpanch).
  • Gram Sabha: The body of all voters in the village that supervises the Gram Panchayat.
  • Panchayat Samiti: A block or mandal level body formed by grouping several Gram Panchayats.
  • Zilla Parishad: The district-level body formed by all Panchayat Samitis in a district, headed by a political chairperson.

Urban Local Government

  • Municipalities: Set up in towns, controlled by elected bodies.
  • Municipal Corporations: Set up in big cities, headed politically by a Mayor.
