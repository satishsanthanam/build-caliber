Chapter 4: The Age of Industrialisation

Introduction: Visualising Industrialisation

The chapter opens by analyzing historical illustrations that shaped the popular perception of industrial progress at the turn of the twentieth century.

  • Dawn of the Century (1900): A music book cover by E.T. Paull depicting an 'angel of progress' flying into the future on a winged wheel of time, surrounded by symbols of modernity like railways, cameras, printing presses, and factories.
  • Two Magicians (1901): An illustration contrasting Aladdin representing the traditional, mysterious East (building palaces with magic) and a modern mechanic representing the technological, progressive West (building bridges, ships, and high-rise structures).
  • The Modern Narrative: These images established a powerful association between industrialisation, rapid technological innovation, and societal development.
1. Before the Industrial Revolution

Industrial history is often mistakenly equated only with the rise of factories. However, a highly organized system of production existed long before the first mills appeared.

1.1 Proto-Industrialisation

The phase of large-scale industrial production for international markets that was not based on factories is known as proto-industrialisation.

  • Town-Countryside Relationship: In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, European merchants moved to the countryside to bypass powerful urban crafts and trade guilds that held monopolies.
  • Peasant Participation: Poor peasants and cottagers, facing shrinking incomes from enclosed common lands, eagerly accepted merchant advances to spin and weave at home, supplementing their agricultural livelihoods.
  • The Production Network: Production was decentralized. A merchant clothier bought wool from a stapler, passed it to spinners, then to weavers, fullers, and dyers. The final finishing was done in London before export.

1.2 The Coming Up of the Factory

The earliest factories in England emerged in the 1730s, but their numbers multiplied rapidly in the late eighteenth century, led by the cotton industry.

  • The Cotton Boom: Raw cotton imports to Britain soared from 2.5 million pounds in 1760 to 22 million pounds by 1787.
  • Richard Arkwright's Cotton Mill: Arkwright created the cotton mill, bringing expensive new machinery and all production processes (carding, twisting, spinning, and rolling) under a single roof and management, allowing for strict quality control and labor supervision.

1.3 The Pace of Industrial Change

The transition to a fully industrial economy was gradual and faced several constraints:

  • Dominant Sectors: Cotton was the leading sector up to the 1840s, after which the iron and steel industry took the lead, driven by railway expansion in England and its colonies.
  • Resilience of Traditional Sectors: New steam-powered industries could not easily displace traditional ones. Even by the late nineteenth century, less than 20 percent of the total workforce was employed in advanced industrial sectors.
  • Ordinary Innovations: Growth in non-mechanized sectors (food processing, building, pottery, glass, furniture) occurred through small, incremental improvements rather than steam power.
  • High Cost of Technology: Industrialists were cautious about adopting new machines because they were expensive, broke down frequently, and repairs were costly. For instance, James Watt's improved steam engine found very few buyers for decades.
2. Hand Labour and Steam Power

In Victorian Britain, the abundance of cheap human labor influenced the choice of production methods over mechanization.

  • Preference for Hand Labour: Because labor was cheap and abundant, industrialists had no incentive to invest in expensive machinery that would replace workers.
  • Seasonal Industries: Gas works, breweries, bookbinders, printers, and ship repairers experienced seasonal fluctuations in demand and preferred hiring temporary hand laborers for specific months.
  • Demand for Custom Goods: Many goods required intricate designs, specific shapes, and human skill (e.g., hundreds of varieties of hammers and axes), which machines producing standardized goods for a mass market could not replicate.
  • Class Preference: The aristocrats and bourgeoisie in Victorian Britain preferred handmade products as symbols of refinement, class, and superior finish.

2.1 Life of the Workers

The abundance of labor created severe hardships for the working class:

  • Job Hunting: Getting a job depended heavily on personal networks of friendship and kinship. Many without connections spent weeks waiting, sleeping under bridges or in night shelters.
  • Seasonality of Employment: Long periods of unemployment followed the end of busy seasons, forcing many workers back to the countryside or onto the streets to look for odd jobs.
  • Wages and Poverty: While nominal wages rose slightly in the early nineteenth century, inflation during the Napoleonic Wars reduced their real value. About 10 percent of the urban population lived in extreme poverty, rising sharply during economic slumps.
  • Resistance to Technology: Fear of unemployment made workers hostile to new machines. The introduction of the Spinning Jenny by James Hargreaves in 1764 led to widespread attacks on the machines by women spinners.
  • Infrastructure Boom: After the 1840s, intensive building activity in cities (widening roads, digging tunnels, laying sewers, extending railways) opened up new employment opportunities in transport and construction.
3. Industrialisation in the Colonies

The focus shifts to India, examining how a colony industrialised under the influence of British colonial rule, with a primary focus on the textile sector.

3.1 The Age of Indian Textiles

Before the age of machine industries, Indian silk and cotton goods dominated the international market.

  • Pre-Colonial Trade Networks: Finer varieties of Indian textiles were exported via land routes through mountain passes to Central Asia, and via sea routes from vibrant pre-colonial ports like Surat (Gujarat), Masulipatam (Coromandel coast), and Hoogly (Bengal).
  • Local Merchant Networks: A complex network of Indian merchants, bankers, and brokers financed production, advanced money to weavers, and carried goods to the ports.
  • Decline of Old Ports: By the 1750s, this network began breaking down as European companies gained power, secured trade concessions, and established monopolies. This led to the decay of Surat and Hoogly, and the rise of colonial ports like Bombay and Calcutta.

3.2 What Happened to Weavers?

After consolidating political power in the 1760s, the British East India Company sought to eliminate competition and secure a regular supply of textiles.

  • Eliminating Middlemen: The Company appointed paid servants called gomasthas to supervise weavers, collect supplies, and inspect the quality of cloth, bypassing traditional local merchants.
  • The System of Advances: Weavers were given loans to buy raw materials, which tied them to the Company. Those who took loans had to hand over their entire production to the gomastha and could not sell to other buyers.
  • Clashes and Resistance: Unlike traditional merchants who lived in the villages, gomasthas were outsiders who acted arrogantly and punished weavers for delays. In response, weavers revolted, deserted villages, migrated to set up looms elsewhere, or turned to agricultural labor.

3.3 Manchester Comes to India

By the turn of the nineteenth century, Indian weavers faced a double blow as British cotton industries expanded:

  • Loss of Export Markets: Industrial groups in Britain pressured their government to impose import duties on foreign textiles, causing Indian exports to collapse.
  • Glut of the Home Market: Cheap, machine-made British cotton goods flooded the Indian market, making it impossible for local weavers to compete.
  • Raw Cotton Crisis: During the American Civil War (1860s), cotton supplies from the US to Britain were cut off. Britain turned to India, causing raw cotton prices to skyrocket and starving Indian weavers of affordable raw materials.
  • Factory Competition: By the late nineteenth century, the setup of modern factories in India further threatened traditional handloom weavers.
4. Factories Come Up

The mid-nineteenth century marked the establishment of modern machine industries across India.

  • Timeline of Early Mills: The first cotton mill in Bombay was set up in 1854; jute mills emerged in Bengal (1855); the Elgin Mill started in Kanpur (1860s); and spinning and weaving mills opened in Ahmedabad and Madras.

4.1 The Early Entrepreneurs

Many early Indian industrialists accumulated their initial capital through international trade networks:

  • The China Trade: Businessmen like Dwarkanath Tagore in Bengal, Dinshaw Petit and Jamsetjee Nusserwanjee Tata in Bombay, and Seth Hukumchand and G.D. Birla's family in Calcutta made fortunes exporting opium to China and importing tea to England.
  • Other Trade Routes: Some merchants traded with Burma, the Middle East, and East Africa, while others operated as internal financiers and goods carriers.
  • Colonial Constraints: As British colonial control tightened, Indian merchants were barred from trading with Europe in manufactured goods and were forced to export only raw materials (cotton, opium, wheat, indigo). They were also excluded from European-dominated Managing Agencies.

4.2 Where Did the Workers Come From?

With the expansion of factories, the demand for industrial labor grew rapidly:

  • Local Migration: Most workers came from neighboring agricultural districts (e.g., Ratnagiri peasants migrating to Bombay cotton mills) where local artisans and peasants could no longer find work.
  • The Role of the Jobber: Industrialists employed a jobber (usually an old, trusted worker) to recruit new workers from his village, help them settle, and provide financial aid during crises. Over time, jobbers acquired immense power and began demanding money and favors.
5. The Peculiarities of Industrial Growth

Industrialisation in India followed a unique pattern shaped by colonial policies and nationalist resistance.

  • Non-Competitive Production: Early Indian cotton mills produced coarse cotton yarn rather than finished fabric to avoid competing with Manchester imports. This yarn was exported to China or used by local handloom weavers.
  • Impact of the Swadeshi Movement: In the first decade of the twentieth century, the Swadeshi movement encouraged the boycott of foreign cloth, prompting Indian industrialists to shift from yarn to cloth production.
  • The First World War Boom: The war created a dramatic shift. British mills were diverted to produce war supplies, causing Manchester imports to decline. Indian factories were mobilized to supply war needs (jute bags, army uniforms, leather boots, tents), leading to a massive boom in local production.

5.1 Small-Scale Industries Predominate

Despite the growth of large factories, small-scale and household industries continued to dominate the Indian economic landscape.

  • Survival of Handlooms: Handloom production actually expanded and trebled between 1900 and 1940.
  • Technological Adaptation: Weavers adopted new, cost-effective technologies like the fly shuttle, which increased productivity per worker and reduced labor demand.
  • Market Segmentation: Weavers producing fine varieties (like Banarasi or Baluchari saris) survived better than those producing coarse cloth, as the demand from the rich was stable and unaffected by famines, and specialized designs could not be easily replicated by machines.
6. Market for Goods

Manufacturers used various strategies to persuade consumers to buy their products and create a new consumer culture.

  • Manchester Labels: British manufacturers placed prominent labels on cloth bundles (e.g., 'MADE IN MANCHESTER') as a mark of quality and to build buyer confidence.
  • Use of Religious Imagery: Beautifully illustrated images of Indian gods and goddesses (like Krishna, Saraswati, and Lakshmi) were printed on labels to give divine approval to foreign goods and make them feel familiar.
  • Calendars as Advertisements: Calendars were printed and distributed widely to tea shops, offices, and homes. Because they were visual, they successfully advertised products to illiterate populations who saw them daily throughout the year.
  • Royal and Historic Figures: Images of emperors, nawabs, and historical figures (like Maharaja Ranjit Singh) were used to project a message of quality and prestige.
  • Nationalist Messages: Indian manufacturers used advertisements to spread the message of Swadeshi, urging citizens to buy Indian-made goods as a form of service to the nation.
