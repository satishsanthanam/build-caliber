Class 10 (Part 3) — Chapter 1: The Rise of Nationalism in Europe

Introduction: Sorrieu's Vision and the Concept of a Nation

In 1848, French artist Frédéric Sorrieu prepared a series of four prints visualising his dream of a world made up of democratic and social Republics.

  • Utopian Vision: A vision of a society so ideal that it is unlikely to actually exist. Sorrieu grouped peoples of the world as distinct nations, identified by flags and national costumes.
  • Absolutist Institutions: Monarchical governments that were centralised, militarised, and repressive, whose shattered remains are depicted on the earth.
  • What is a Nation? French philosopher Ernst Renan defined a nation as the culmination of a long past of endeavours, sacrifice, and devotion, characterized by a plebiscite (a direct vote by which all people of a region accept or reject a proposal).
1. The French Revolution and the Idea of the Nation

The French Revolution of 1789 marked the first clear expression of nationalism, transferring sovereignty from the absolute monarch to French citizens.

  • Collective Identity: Introduced concepts like la patrie (the fatherland) and le citoyen (the citizen) to emphasise a united community with equal rights under a constitution.
  • National Symbols: A new tricolour flag replaced the royal standard, the Estates General was renamed the National Assembly, and French became the common language.
  • The Napoleonic Code (Civil Code of 1804): Established equality before the law, secured the right to property, and abolished the feudal system, though it restricted women's rights and limited suffrage.
2. The Making of Nationalism in Europe

2.1 Aristocracy and the New Middle Class

Mid-eighteenth-century Europe had no nation-states; it was divided into kingdoms, duchies, and empires (like the diverse Habsburg Empire).

  • Aristocracy: A dominant, land-owning social class united by a common lifestyle, speaking French for diplomacy.
  • Middle Class: Emerged with industrialisation, consisting of industrialists, businessmen, and professionals who popularized ideas of national unity.

2.2 Liberal Nationalism

Derived from the Latin root liber (meaning free), liberalism stood for individual freedom and equality before the law.

  • Political Sphere: Emphasised government by consent, a constitution, representative government, and the inviolability of private property.
  • Economic Sphere: Demanded freedom of markets and the abolition of state-imposed restrictions. In 1834, a customs union called Zollverein was formed under Prussian initiative to abolish tariff barriers.

2.3 New Conservatism after 1815

Following Napoleon's defeat in 1815, European governments embraced conservatism, aiming to preserve traditional institutions like the monarchy, Church, and social hierarchies.

  • Treaty of Vienna (1815): Hosted by Austrian Chancellor Duke Metternich, representatives of Britain, Russia, Prussia, and Austria met to undo Napoleonic changes and restore deposed dynasties (like the Bourbons).

2.4 The Revolutionaries

Fear of repression drove liberal-nationalists underground to form secret societies to fight for liberty and establish nation-states.

  • Giuseppe Mazzini: An Italian revolutionary who founded Young Italy in Marseilles and Young Europe in Berne, advocating for a unified Italian republic.
3. The Age of Revolutions: 1830-1848

3.1 Romanticism and National Feeling

Romanticism was a cultural movement that criticized reason and science, focusing instead on emotions, intuition, and mystical feelings to shape nationalist sentiments.

  • Folk Culture: Philosophers like Johann Gottfried Herder popularized the idea that true national spirit (volksgeist) is found among the common people (das volk) through folk songs and dances.
  • Language as Resistance: In partitioned Poland, the Polish language became a weapon of national resistance against Russian dominance.

3.2 Hunger, Hardship and Popular Revolt

The 1830s brought severe economic hardship to Europe due to population growth, rural-to-urban migration, and competition from machine-made British imports.

  • 1848 Paris Revolt: Food shortages and unemployment led to a popular uprising, forcing King Louis Philippe to flee and leading to the proclamation of a Republic.

3.3 1848: The Revolution of the Liberals

Parallel to peasant revolts, the educated middle classes demanded constitutionalism and national unification.

  • Frankfurt Parliament: In May 1848, 831 elected representatives met at the Church of St Paul to draft a constitution for a German nation, but it was rejected by King Friedrich Wilhelm IV of Prussia and subsequently disbanded.
4. The Making of Germany and Italy

4.1 Unification of Germany

Nationalism was mobilized by conservatives to promote state power. Prussia took leadership of German unification.

  • Otto von Bismarck: The chief minister of Prussia and architect of unification, who achieved it through three wars over seven years with Austria, Denmark, and France.
  • Proclamation: In January 1871, Kaiser William I of Prussia was proclaimed German Emperor at Versailles.

4.2 Unification of Italy

Italy was politically fragmented into seven states, with only Sardinia-Piedmont ruled by an Italian princely house.

  • Key Figures: Count Camillo de Cavour engineered diplomatic alliances, while Giuseppe Garibaldi led armed volunteers (Red Shirts) to conquer southern regions.
  • Proclamation: In 1861, Victor Emmanuel II was proclaimed king of a united Italy.

4.3 The Strange Case of Britain

The formation of the British nation-state was a long-drawn-out process rather than a sudden revolution.

  • Act of Union (1707): United England and Scotland to form the United Kingdom of Great Britain, leading to the systematic suppression of Scottish culture.
  • Ireland: Forcibly incorporated into the United Kingdom in 1801 after a failed Catholic revolt.
5. Visualising the Nation

Artists in the 18th and 19th centuries personified nations using female allegories (expressing abstract ideas through a person or thing).

  • Marianne: Represented the French Republic, symbolizing liberty and the republic with the red cap, tricolour, and cockade.
  • Germania: Represented the German nation, wearing a crown of oak leaves symbolizing heroism.
6. Nationalism and Imperialism

By the late 19th century, nationalism transitioned from liberal-democratic idealism into a narrow creed linked with imperialist rivalries.

  • The Balkans: A highly explosive region of geographical and ethnic diversity under Ottoman control. Rivalries among major European powers (Russia, Germany, England, Austro-Hungary) over the Balkans led to a series of conflicts, culminating in the First World War.
