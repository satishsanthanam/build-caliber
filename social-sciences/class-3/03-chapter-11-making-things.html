Class 3 — Chapter 11: Making Things

11.1 Introduction to Making Things

We see and use many objects made of clay in our daily lives, such as:

  • Gullak (piggy bank) used for saving money.
  • Kulhad (clay cup) used for drinking.
  • Birdbaths and pots for storing water.

Have you ever wondered what these things are made of, and who makes them?

11.2 A Potter's Family & The Pottery Process

Pottery is a traditional craft of making vessels and objects from clay.

11.2.1 The Step-by-Step Process of Pot Making

  • Preparing the Clay: The potter takes clay, adds water to get the right consistency, pounds it well, and kneads it like dough.
  • Shaping: The kneaded clay is placed on a spinning stone wheel (potter's wheel) and shaped by hand.
  • Drying: The shaped pots are left to dry.
  • Baking: Once dry, the pots are baked in a very hot fire inside a big oven called a kiln. Baking makes the pots strong and ready for use.
  • Decorating: Potters and their family members paint beautiful patterns on the pots to make them look attractive.

11.2.2 Activity: Make Your Own Clay

You can make clay at home with the help of elders:

  1. Collect soil from wet or muddy places.
  2. Remove big pebbles, stones, sand, or leaves using a sieve.
  3. Put the soil in a flat vessel filled with water.
  4. After a few days, pour away the water that stays on top.
  5. Knead the remaining soil into a smooth dough to shape into objects.
11.3 Patterns in Nature and Pottery

Patterns are repeating shapes, lines, and colors. Artists often get inspiration for their pottery designs from nature.

11.3.1 Patterns in Nature

Patterns can be found everywhere in the natural world:

  • Animals: Spots on a leopard, stripes on a zebra or squirrel, and patterns on peacocks, tigers, butterflies, and fish.
  • Plants: The symmetrical arrangement of leaves on branches, such as on a neem tree.

11.3.2 Patterns in Indian Pottery

Different parts of India have unique and beautiful traditional forms of pottery, often decorated with distinct geometric or nature-inspired patterns.

11.4 Brick by Brick

Just like pots, bricks are essential building blocks made from mud and clay.

11.4.1 How Bricks are Made

  • Moulding: Wet mud is pressed into rectangular boxes called moulds to give them their shape.
  • Baking: The shaped mud bricks are baked in a kiln to make them hard and strong.
  • Use: Bricks are used to construct strong buildings like houses, schools, and hospitals.
11.5 Types of Houses

Houses are built using different materials depending on the climate, availability of resources, and location.

11.5.1 Traditional Mud Houses

In warm areas, people build traditional mud houses to keep the interiors cool.

  • Materials: Built using natural materials like mud, grass, and wood.
  • Cow Dung: Often used to coat walls and floors to make them smooth, keep the heat out, and prevent insects from entering.

11.5.2 Other Types of Houses

  • Tents: Temporary shelters made of canvas or plastic.
  • Skyscrapers / Apartments: Tall buildings in cities made of concrete, steel, and glass.
  • Stone Houses: Built in hilly or rocky areas using stones.
  • Houseboats: Floating houses found on water bodies (like in Kashmir or Kerala).
11.6 Safety First

Construction sites can be dangerous. Workers must follow strict safety rules to protect themselves.

  • Safety Gear: Wearing a helmet with a strap, a high-visibility safety vest, safety goggles, gloves, and sturdy footwear (boots).
