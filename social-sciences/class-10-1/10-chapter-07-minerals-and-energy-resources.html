Chapter 7: MINERALS AND ENERGY RESOURCES

5.1 Introduction to Minerals

We use various items made of metals in our daily lives. The Earth's crust is made up of different minerals embedded in rocks, from which metals are extracted after proper refinement. Minerals are an indispensable part of our lives, used in everything from a tiny pin to towering buildings, ships, railway lines, and machinery. Even the food we eat contains minerals, which are vital for life processes.

What is a Mineral?

Geologists define a mineral as a "homogenous, naturally occurring substance with a definable internal structure." Minerals are found in varied forms in nature, ranging from the hardest diamond to the softest talc. A particular mineral's characteristics (color, hardness, crystal form, lustre, and density) depend upon the physical and chemical conditions under which it forms.

All Living Things Need Minerals

Life processes cannot occur without minerals. Although our mineral intake represents only about $0.3\%$ of our total intake of nutrients, they are so potent and important that without them we would not be able to utilise the other $99.7\%$ of foodstuffs.

5.2 Mode of Occurrence of Minerals

Minerals are usually found in ores. The term ore is used to describe an accumulation of any mineral mixed with other elements. Minerals generally occur in the following forms:

  • Igneous and Metamorphic Rocks: Minerals may occur in cracks, crevices, faults, or joints. Smaller occurrences are called veins and larger ones are called lodes. Examples: tin, copper, zinc, and lead.
  • Sedimentary Rocks: Minerals occur in beds or layers, formed as a result of deposition, accumulation, and concentration in horizontal strata. Examples: coal, iron ore, gypsum, potash salt, and sodium salt.
  • Decomposition of Surface Rocks: Involves the removal of soluble constituents, leaving a residual mass of weathered material containing ores. Example: bauxite.
  • Alluvial Deposits: Found in sands of valley floors and the base of hills, also known as placer deposits. These contain minerals not corroded by water. Examples: gold, silver, tin, and platinum.
  • Ocean Waters: Contain vast quantities of minerals, though mostly widely diffused. Examples: common salt, magnesium, bromine, and manganese nodules on ocean beds.
5.3 Classification of Minerals

Minerals are broadly classified into three main categories:

  • Metallic Minerals:
    • Ferrous: Containing iron (e.g., iron ore, manganese, nickel, cobalt).
    • Non-Ferrous: Do not contain iron (e.g., copper, lead, tin, bauxite).
    • Precious: High economic value (e.g., gold, silver, platinum).
  • Non-Metallic Minerals: Examples include mica, salt, potash, sulphur, granite, limestone, marble, and sandstone.
  • Energy Minerals: Used to generate power (e.g., coal, petroleum, natural gas).
5.4 Metallic Minerals

Ferrous Minerals

Ferrous minerals account for about three-fourths of the total value of the production of metallic minerals in India.

Iron Ore

Iron ore is the basic mineral and the backbone of industrial development. India is rich in good quality iron ores:

  • Magnetite: The finest iron ore with a very high iron content up to $70\%$, possessing excellent magnetic qualities.
  • Hematite: The most important industrial iron ore in terms of quantity used, with an iron content of $50\text{--}60\%$.

Major iron ore belts in India include:

  • Odisha-Jharkhand Belt: High-grade hematite mined in Badampahar (Odisha) and Gua/Noamundi (Jharkhand).
  • Durg-Bastar-Chandrapur Belt: High-grade hematite found in the Bailadila range of Chhattisgarh, exported to Japan and South Korea.
  • Ballari-Chitradurga-Chikkamagaluru-Tumakuru Belt: Located in Karnataka; includes the Kudremukh mines, which are a $100\%$ export unit.
  • Maharashtra-Goa Belt: Includes Goa and Ratnagiri district of Maharashtra; ores are exported through Marmagao port.

Manganese

Mainly used in manufacturing steel and ferro-manganese alloy. Nearly $10\text{ kg}$ of manganese is required to manufacture one tonne of steel. It is also used in bleaching powder, insecticides, and paints. Madhya Pradesh and Maharashtra are major producers.

Non-Ferrous Minerals

Copper

India is critically deficient in the reserve and production of copper. Being malleable, ductile, and a good conductor, it is mainly used in electrical cables, electronics, and chemical industries. Leading producers include Balaghat mines (Madhya Pradesh), Khetri mines (Rajasthan), and Singhbhum (Jharkhand).

Bauxite

Bauxite is a clay-like substance from which alumina and later aluminium is obtained. Aluminium is important because it combines the strength of metals like iron with extreme lightness, good conductivity, and great malleability. Major deposits are in Amarkantak plateau, Maikal hills, and Bilaspur-Katni. Odisha is the largest producing state.

5.5 Non-Metallic and Rock Minerals

Mica

Mica is made up of a series of plates or leaves that split easily into thin sheets. Due to its excellent di-electric strength, low power loss factor, insulating properties, and resistance to high voltage, it is indispensable in electric and electronic industries. Major deposits are in Chota Nagpur plateau (Koderma-Gaya-Hazaribagh belt in Jharkhand), Ajmer (Rajasthan), and Nellore (Andhra Pradesh).

Rock Minerals: Limestone

Limestone is found in association with rocks composed of calcium carbonates or calcium and magnesium carbonates. It is the basic raw material for the cement industry and essential for smelting iron ore in blast furnaces.

5.6 Hazards of Mining and Conservation of Minerals

Hazards of Mining

Mining is often called a "killer industry" due to its severe impacts on health and the environment:

  • Miners are vulnerable to pulmonary diseases from dust and noxious fumes.
  • Constant threats of collapsing mine roofs, inundation, and fires in coalmines.
  • Contamination of local water sources and degradation of land and soil due to waste dumping.

Conservation of Minerals

Mineral resources are finite and non-renewable. The geological processes of mineral formation are extremely slow compared to current consumption rates. Conservation strategies include:

  • Using mineral resources in a planned and sustainable manner.
  • Improving technologies to allow the use of low-grade ores at low costs.
  • Recycling metals and using scrap metals or other substitutes.
5.7 Energy Resources

Energy is required for all activities and can be generated from conventional and non-conventional sources.

Conventional Sources of Energy

Coal

The most abundantly available fossil fuel in India. It is classified into different types based on compression and depth of burial:

  • Peat: Low carbon, high moisture, low heating capacity.
  • Lignite: Low-grade brown coal, soft with high moisture (e.g., Neyveli in Tamil Nadu).
  • Bituminous: Most popular coal in commercial use, buried deep and subjected to high temperatures.
  • Anthracite: Highest quality hard coal.

In India, coal occurs in Gondwana deposits (Damodar valley: Raniganj, Jharia, Bokaro) and Tertiary deposits (North-eastern states).

Petroleum

Provides fuel, lubricants, and raw materials for various industries. Petroleum refineries act as a "nodal industry" for synthetic textiles, fertilizers, and chemical industries. Major production areas include Mumbai High, Gujarat (Ankeleshwar), and Assam (Digboi, Naharkatiya).

Natural Gas

An environment-friendly fuel used as a domestic and industrial fuel, power generation, and transport fuel (CNG). Major reserves are in Mumbai High, Cambay basin, and Krishna-Godavari basin. The Hazira-Vijaipur-Jagdishpur (HVJ) pipeline is a key cross-country pipeline.

Electricity

Generated via running water (hydro electricity - renewable) or by burning fossil fuels (thermal electricity - non-renewable).

Non-Conventional Sources of Energy

  • Nuclear or Atomic Energy: Obtained by altering the structure of atoms. Uranium and Thorium (found in Jharkhand, Rajasthan, and Monazite sands of Kerala) are used.
  • Solar Energy: Photovoltaic technology converts sunlight directly into electricity. Highly popular in rural and remote areas.
  • Wind Power: India has great potential, with the largest wind farm cluster located in Tamil Nadu (Nagarcoil to Madurai).
  • Biogas: Produced from shrubs, farm waste, animal, and human waste in rural areas, commonly known as 'Gobar gas plants'.
  • Tidal Energy: Generated using oceanic tides. Ideal conditions exist in the Gulf of Khambhat, Gulf of Kuchchh, and Gangetic delta.
  • Geo Thermal Energy: Refers to heat and electricity produced by using heat from the interior of the Earth. Experimental projects exist in Parvati Valley (Himachal Pradesh) and Puga Valley (Ladakh).

Conservation of Energy Resources

Energy conservation and the increased use of renewable energy sources are the twin planks of sustainable energy. Citizens can contribute by using public transport, switching off electricity when not in use, and using power-saving devices.
