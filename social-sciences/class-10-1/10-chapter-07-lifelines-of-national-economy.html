Class 10 (Part 1) — Chapter 7: Lifelines of National Economy

7.1 Introduction to Transport, Communication and Trade

The pace of development of a country depends upon the production of goods and services as well as their movement over space. Therefore, efficient means of transport are prerequisites for fast development.

Movement of goods and services can be over three important domains of our earth: Land, Water, and Air. Based on these, transport is classified into different categories.

7.1.1 Classification of Means of Transport
  • Land: Roadways, Railways, and Pipelines.
  • Water: Inland and Overseas.
  • Air: Domestic Airways (Public Undertaking and Private Airlines) and International Airways.
7.2 Land Transport: Roadways

India has the second largest road network in the world, aggregating to about 62.16 lakh km (2020–21). Roadways have preceded railways and still hold an edge over them due to ease of construction and maintenance.

7.2.1 Advantages of Roadways over Railways
  • Construction cost of roads is much lower than that of railway lines.
  • Roads can traverse comparatively more dissected and undulating topography.
  • Roads can negotiate higher gradients of slopes and traverse mountainous terrains like the Himalayas.
  • Road transport is economical for transporting few persons and smaller amounts of goods over short distances.
  • It provides door-to-door service, reducing loading and unloading costs.
  • It acts as a feeder to other modes of transport, linking railway stations, air ports, and sea ports.

7.2.2 Classification of Roads in India
  • Golden Quadrilateral Super Highways: A major road development project linking Delhi-Kolkata-Chennai-Mumbai and Delhi by six-lane Super Highways. It includes the North-South corridor linking Srinagar to Kanniyakumari, and the East-West corridor connecting Silchar to Porbandar. Implemented by the National Highway Authority of India (NHAI).
  • National Highways: Primary road systems linking extreme parts of the country, maintained by the Central Public Works Department (CPWD).
  • State Highways: Roads linking a state capital with different district headquarters, constructed and maintained by the State Public Works Department (PWD).
  • District Roads: Connect district headquarters with other places of the district, maintained by the Zila Parishad.
  • Other Roads: Rural roads linking rural areas and villages with towns, which received impetus under the Pradhan Mantri Grameen Sadak Yojana.
  • Border Roads: Constructed and maintained by the Border Roads Organisation (BRO) (established in 1960) in the bordering areas of strategic importance.

7.2.3 Classification based on Material
  • Metalled Roads: Made of cement, concrete, or bitumen of coal. These are all-weather roads.
  • Unmetalled Roads: Go out of use during the rainy season.

7.2.4 Key Highlight: Atal Tunnel

The World's longest Highway tunnel, Atal Tunnel (9.02 Km), has been built by the Border Road Organisation. It connects Manali to Lahul-Spiti valley throughout the year, bypassing heavy snowfall blockages in the Pir Panjal range of the Himalayas at an altitude of $3000$ metres from Mean Sea Level (MSL).

7.3 Land Transport: Railways

Railways are the principal mode of transportation for freight and passengers in India. They bind the economic life of the country and accelerate the development of industry and agriculture.

7.3.1 Key Facts and Distribution
  • The Indian Railways is the largest public sector undertaking in the country.
  • The first train steamed off from Mumbai to Thane in 1853, covering a distance of $34\text{ km}$.
  • The Indian Railway is reorganised into 17 zones.
  • The distribution pattern of the railway network is influenced by physiographic, economic, and administrative factors. The northern plains provide the most favourable conditions, while hilly terrains, sandy deserts, and forested tracks present major obstacles.

7.3.2 Railway Track Gauges in India
  • Broad Gauge ($1.676\text{ m}$): Route length of $63,950\text{ km}$.
  • Metre Gauge ($1.000\text{ m}$): Route length of $2,402\text{ km}$.
  • Narrow Gauge ($0.762\text{ m}$ and $0.610\text{ m}$): Route length of $1,604\text{ km}$.
7.4 Land Transport: Pipelines

Pipeline transport network is a new arrival on the transportation map of India, used for transporting crude oil, petroleum products, natural gas, and solids converted into slurry.

7.4.1 Major Pipeline Networks
  • From oil fields in upper Assam to Kanpur (Uttar Pradesh) via Guwahati, Barauni, and Prayagraj.
  • From Salaya in Gujarat to Jalandhar in Punjab via Viramgam, Mathura, Delhi, and Sonipat.
  • The first cross-country gas pipeline: Hazira-Vijaipur-Jagdishpur (HVJ) ($1,700\text{ km}$ long), linking Mumbai High and Bassein gas fields with fertilizer, power, and industrial complexes in western and northern India.
7.5 Waterways and Major Sea Ports

Waterways are the cheapest, fuel-efficient, and environment-friendly mode of transport, highly suitable for carrying heavy and bulky goods.

7.5.1 National Waterways (NWs)

India has inland navigation waterways of $14,500\text{ km}$ in length. Under the National Waterways Act, 2016, 111 inland waterways have been declared as National Waterways, including:

  • NW No. 1: The Ganga river between Prayagraj and Haldia ($1620\text{ km}$).
  • NW No. 2: The Brahmaputra river between Sadiya and Dhubri ($891\text{ km}$).
  • NW No. 3: The West-Coast Canal in Kerala ($205\text{ km}$).
  • NW No. 4: Specified stretches of Godavari and Krishna rivers ($1078\text{ km}$).
  • NW No. 5: Specified stretches of river Brahmani ($588\text{ km}$).

7.5.2 Major Sea Ports

With a coastline of $11098.81\text{ km}$, India is dotted with 12 major and 200 notified non-major ports. Major ports handle $95\%$ of India's foreign trade:

  • Deendayal Port (Kandla): A tidal port developed soon after Independence to ease volume on Mumbai port.
  • Mumbai Port: The biggest port with a spacious natural and well-sheltered harbour.
  • Mormugao Port (Goa): Premier iron ore exporting port, accounting for about $50\%$ of India's iron ore export.
  • New Mangalore Port: Caters to the export of iron ore concentrates from Kudremukh mines.
  • Cochin Port: Located at the entrance of a lagoon with a natural harbour.
  • Tuticorin Port (V.O. Chidambaranar): Located in Tamil Nadu, has a natural harbour and rich hinterland.
  • Chennai Port: One of the oldest artificial ports, ranked next to Mumbai in cargo volume.
  • Vishakhapatnam Port: The deepest landlocked and well-protected port.
  • Paradwip Port: Located in Odisha, specializes in the export of iron ore.
  • Kolkata Port (Shyama Prasad Mookerjee): An inland riverine tidal port serving the vast Ganga-Brahmaputra basin.
  • Haldia Port: Developed as a subsidiary port to relieve pressure on Kolkata port.
7.6 Airways

Air travel is the fastest, most comfortable, and prestigious mode of transport. It can easily cover difficult terrains like high mountains, deserts, and dense forests.

7.6.1 Key Services and Schemes
  • Pawanhans Helicopters Ltd.: Provides helicopter services to ONGC in off-shore operations and to inaccessible areas in the north-eastern states, Jammu and Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh, and Uttarakhand.
  • UDAN (Ude Desh ka Aam Nagrik): A regional connectivity scheme designed to make flying affordable for the common citizen and jump-start the regional aviation market.
7.7 Communication

Long-distance communication has become rapid and easy without physical movement of the communicator or receiver.

7.7.1 Personal and Mass Communication
  • Indian Postal Network: The largest in the world. It classifies mail into:
    • First-class mail: Cards and envelopes, which are airlifted.
    • Second-class mail: Book packets, registered newspapers, and periodicals, carried by surface mail.
    • Six Mail Channels: Introduced for quick delivery: Rajdhani, Metro, Green, Business, Bulk Mail, and Periodical Channels.
  • Telecom Network: India has one of the largest telecom networks in Asia, with extensive Subscriber Trunk Dialling (STD) facilities integrated with space technology.
  • Mass Media: Includes Radio (All India Radio/Akashwani), Television (Doordarshan), Newspapers (published in about 100 languages, with Hindi being the largest), and Films (certified by the Central Board of Film Certification).
  • Digital India: An umbrella programme to prepare India for a knowledge-based transformation.
7.8 International Trade

The exchange of goods among people, states, and countries is referred to as trade. Trade between two countries is called international trade, considered the "economic barometer" of a country.

7.8.1 Balance of Trade
  • Favourable Balance of Trade: When the value of exports exceeds the value of imports.
  • Unfavourable Balance of Trade: When the value of imports exceeds the value of exports.

Major exports from India include gems and jewellery, chemicals, and agricultural products. Major imports include petroleum crude, chemicals, coal, and machinery. India has also emerged as a software giant at the international level.

7.9 Tourism as a Trade

Tourism in India has grown remarkably over the past two decades, promoting national integration and supporting local handicrafts and cultural pursuits.

  • Key Schemes: Swadesh Darshan 2.0, Vibrant Village Programme, PRASHAD, and Paryatan Mitra.
  • Types of Tourism: Heritage tourism, eco-tourism, adventure tourism, cultural tourism, medical tourism, and business tourism.
