Chapter 3: Landforms and Life

3.1 Introduction to Landforms

Humans live on land, which has many forms and features that change from one region to another. A landform is a physical feature on the surface of our planet Earth. Landforms take shape over millions of years and have a significant connection with the environment and life.

Landforms can broadly be divided into three categories: mountains, plateaus, and plains.

  • Altitude: The height of an object above sea level. Examples include the altitude of a mountain, a bird in flight, or a satellite.
3.2 Mountains

Mountains are landforms that are much higher than the surrounding landscape. They are recognized by a broad base, steep slopes, and a narrow summit. Depending on their height, some mountains are permanently snow-capped.

  • Hills: Highlands with a lower height, less steep slopes, and rounded tops.
  • Precipitation: Water from the atmosphere reaching the ground in any form, such as rain, snow, and hail.
  • Mountain Ranges: Groups of mountains stretching for thousands of kilometres, such as the Himalayas in Asia, the Alps in Europe, and the Andes in South America.

3.2.1 Major Mountain Peaks

  • Mount Everest: Located between Tibet (China) and Nepal, it is the highest mountain in the world at $8,848\text{ m}$.
  • Kanchenjunga: Located between Nepal and the Indian state of Sikkim.
  • Mount Aconcagua: Located in South America, the highest peak of the Andes.
  • Mount Kilimanjaro: An isolated mountain in eastern Africa.
  • Mont Blanc: The highest mountain of the Alps in Western Europe.
  • Anamudi: Located in Kerala (also known as 'Anai Peak'), the highest mountain in south India.

3.2.2 Mountain Environment

Mountain slopes are often covered with montane forest, where conifer trees such as pines, firs, spruce, and deodar are common. These trees are tall, cone-shaped, and have thin, pointed leaves. At higher altitudes, trees give way to grasses, mosses, and lichens.

Mountain Fauna: Mountains are home to diverse animals, including the golden eagle, peregrine falcon, Canadian lynx, snow leopard, ibex, Himalayan tahr, mountain hare, yak, grey fox, and black bear.

3.2.3 Life in the Mountains

The mountain terrain is rugged and steep, making regular farming difficult. Cultivation is practiced on slopes by cutting steps, known as terrace farming. Herding is often preferred over agriculture.

Tourism & Culture: Tourism is a major source of income (skiing, hiking, mountaineering, paragliding). Many traditional communities consider mountains sacred (e.g., Mount Everest is called 'Chomolungma' by Tibetans and 'Sagarmatha' by Nepalis; Mount Kailash is sacred to Hindus, Buddhists, Jainists, and Bon followers).

Challenges: Mountain life involves natural hazards such as flash floods, landslides, avalanches, cloudbursts, and extreme cold weather, alongside human-made challenges like uncontrolled tourism.

3.3 Plateaus

A plateau is a landform that rises up from the surrounding land and has a more or less flat surface, often with steep slopes on one or more sides.

  • Tibetan Plateau: The largest and highest plateau in the world, with an average altitude of $4,500\text{ m}$ (nicknamed the "Roof of the World"). It stretches nearly $2,500\text{ km}$ from east to west.
  • Deccan Plateau: Located in central and south India, it is one of the oldest plateaus in the world, formed through volcanic activity millions of years ago.

3.3.1 Resources and Features of Plateaus

  • Mineral Wealth: Plateaus are "storehouses of minerals." The East African Plateau is famous for gold and diamond mining, while India's Chhota Nagpur Plateau has huge reserves of iron, coal, and manganese.
  • Soil: Many plateaus have rocky, less fertile soil. However, lava plateaus formed by volcanic activity have rich black soil highly favorable for farming.
  • Waterfalls: Plateaus host spectacular waterfalls, such as Victoria Falls (Zambezi River), Hundru Falls (Subarnarekha River), Jog Falls (Sharavati River), and Nohkalikai Falls (dropping $340\text{ m}$ from the Cherrapunji Plateau).
3.4 Plains

Plains are landforms with an extensive flat or gently undulating surface, generally not more than $300\text{ m}$ above sea level.

  • Floodplains: Plains formed by rivers originating in mountains. Rivers collect and deposit sediments (rock, sand, silt), making the soil highly fertile and ideal for agriculture.

3.4.1 Life in the Plains

Fertile plains have historically been the cradles of the first civilizations. Today, a large portion of the world's population lives in plains.

  • The Ganga Plain (Gangetic Plain): Home to about 40 crore people (more than $\frac{1}{4}$ of India's population).
  • Occupations: Agriculture is the primary occupation. Food crops include rice, wheat, maize, barley, and millets. Fibre crops include cotton, jute, and hemp. River fishing is also common.
  • Challenges: Transition from rainfed agriculture to intensive canal/groundwater irrigation has led to the depletion of groundwater. Other issues include high population density and pollution.
  • Cultural Value: Rivers hold immense cultural and spiritual value. The meeting point of two or more rivers is called a confluence, which is often considered sacred.
3.5 Deserts

Deserts are large, dry expanses of land with very little precipitation. They have unique flora and fauna adapted to harsh conditions.

  • Hot Deserts: Characterized by high temperatures, such as the Sahara Desert in Africa and the Thar Desert in India.
  • Cold Deserts: Characterized by freezing temperatures, such as the Gobi Desert in Asia and the continent of Antarctica.
  • Resilience: The capacity of humans and ecosystems to meet challenges, adapt, and overcome difficult living conditions.

3.7 Summary & Exercises

Key Takeaways

  • Landforms are classified into three main types: mountains, plateaus, and plains, each with distinct physical environments.
  • Human lives, occupations, and cultures are deeply shaped by the landforms they inhabit.
  • Each landform offers unique opportunities (like fertile soil or mineral wealth) and challenges (like rugged terrain or water scarcity).
