Class 6 — Chapter 7: India’s Cultural Roots

7.1 Introduction to Indian Culture

Indian culture is several millenniums old, resembling an ancient tree with many roots and branches united by a common trunk. These branches manifest as art, literature, science, medicine, religion, governance, martial arts, and various schools of thought (groups of thinkers or spiritual seekers sharing similar ideas about life and the world).

Many of India's cultural roots trace back to the Indus, Harappan, or Sindhu-Sarasvatī civilisation, shaping the unique personality of 'India, that is Bharat'.

7.2 The Vedas and Vedic Culture

7.2.1 What are the Vedas?

The word Veda comes from the Sanskrit root vid, meaning 'knowledge'. There are four Vedas, which are among the most ancient texts in the world:

  • Ṛig Veda (the most ancient)
  • Yajur Veda
  • Sāma Veda
  • Atharva Veda

The Vedas consist of thousands of hymns (prayers in the form of poems and songs) composed in the Sapta Sindhava region. They were transmitted orally with rigorous training across generations. In 2008, UNESCO recognised Vedic chanting as 'a masterpiece of the oral and intangible heritage of humanity'.

The hymns were composed by rishis (male seers) and rishikas (female seers) in an early form of Sanskrit. They addressed deities like Indra, Agni, Varuṇa, Mitra, Sarasvatī, and Uṣhas, who sustained ṛitam (truth and order in the cosmos). The early seers viewed these deities as manifestations of a single supreme reality: "ekam sat viprā bahudhā vadanti" (The Existent is one, but sages give it many names).

7.2.2 Vedic Society

Early Vedic society was organised into larger groups of people called janas (clans). The Ṛig Veda lists over 30 janas, including the Bharatas, Purus, Kurus, Yadus, and Turvaśhas. Society was governed with the help of collective assemblies called sabhā and samiti, led by a rājā (king or ruler). Various professions existed, including agriculturists, weavers, potters, builders, carpenters, healers, dancers, barbers, and priests.

7.2.3 Vedic Schools of Thought

Vedic culture developed rituals (yajña) directed towards deities. Over time, a group of texts called the Upaniṣhads built upon these concepts, introducing:

  • Rebirth: Taking birth again and again.
  • Karma: Our actions and their results.
  • Brahman: The single divine essence of human life, nature, and the universe (central to the Vedanta school of thought).
  • Ātman: The divine essence or Self residing in every being, ultimately one with brahman.

These concepts fostered a worldview of interconnectedness, as expressed in the prayer "sarve bhavantu sukhinah" ("May all creatures be happy"). Another major school, Yoga, developed methods to achieve the realisation of brahman in one's consciousness.

7.2.4 Stories from the Upaniṣhads

  • Śhvetaketu and the seed of reality (Chhāndogya Upaniṣhad): Rishi Uddālaka teaches his proud son Śhvetaketu that the invisible essence (brahman) is everywhere and in everything, concluding with "You are That, Śhvetaketu."
  • Nachiketa and his quest (Katha Upaniṣhad): Nachiketa journeys to the world of Yama (god of death) and persists in asking what happens after death. Yama reveals that the ātman is immortal, neither born nor dying.
  • The debate of Gārgī and Yājñavalkya (Bṛihadāraṇyaka Upaniṣhad): The rishika Gārgī challenges the renowned rishi Yājñavalkya in a philosophical debate at King Janaka's court, questioning him on the nature of the world and brahman.
7.3 Buddhism

Emerging in the 1st millennium BCE, Buddhism did not accept the authority of the Vedas. It was founded by Siddhārtha Gautama, a prince born in Lumbini around 560 BCE. After witnessing an old man, a sick man, a dead body, and a peaceful ascetic, he renounced palace life at age 29. He attained enlightenment under a pipal tree at Bodh Gaya, becoming the 'Buddha' (the enlightened one).

Core Teachings:

  • The root causes of human suffering are avidyā (ignorance) and attachment.
  • Ahimsa: Non-violence, originally meaning 'non-hurting' or 'non-injuring'.
  • Sincere inner discipline and self-conquest over external conquest.
  • Establishment of the Sangha, a community of monks (bhikṣhus) and nuns (bhikṣhuṇīs).
  • Jātaka Tales: Stories of the Buddha's former births (e.g., the selfless Monkey King) that convey Buddhist values.
7.4 Jainism

Another major school of thought, Jainism, was popularised by Prince Vardhamāna, who became known as Mahāvīra ('great hero'). Born in the early 6th century BCE near Vaiśhālī, he left home at age 30, practiced rigorous asceticism for 12 years, and attained supreme wisdom. The word 'Jain' comes from jina, meaning 'conqueror' (conqueror of ignorance and attachments).

Core Teachings:

  • Ahimsa: Extreme non-violence towards all living, breathing, and sentient creatures.
  • Anekāntavāda: The belief that truth has many aspects and cannot be fully described by a single perspective.
  • Aparigraha: Non-possession and detachment from material wealth.
  • Interconnectedness and interdependence of all living organisms.
7.5 Other Schools of Thought

A wide diversity of belief systems coexisted. For example, the Chārvāka (or Lokāyata) school was a materialist philosophy that believed only the physical world exists and rejected the concept of life after death.

7.6 Folk and Tribal Roots

India's cultural heritage is also deeply rooted in rich oral, folk, and tribal traditions. A tribe (janjāti in Hindi, historically referred to as different janas) is a close-knit community sharing common descent, culture, language, and holding no private property.

Mutual Interaction and Enrichment:

  • Folk, tribal, and Hindu belief systems share similar concepts, such as regarding nature (mountains, rivers, trees, animals) as sacred and conscious.
  • Deities and legends have been freely exchanged. For example, Lord Jagannath of Puri was originally a tribal deity, and many tribes have their own versions of the Mahābhārata and Rāmāyaṇa.
  • Many tribes worship a supreme being alongside natural elements, such as Donyipolo (Sun and Moon) in Arunachal Pradesh, Khandoba in central India, and Singbonga by the Munda and Santhal tribals.
