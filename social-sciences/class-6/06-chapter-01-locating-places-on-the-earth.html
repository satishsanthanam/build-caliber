Introduction: Why Social Science?

Social Science is the systematic study of human society, helping us understand our world, address global challenges, and learn to live in peace and harmony.

Five Broad Themes of Social Science

  • Theme A – India and the World: Land and the People: Explores geographical features, maps, and how natural settings shape civilisations.
  • Theme B – Tapestry of the Past: Explores history to understand the present, our identities, and learn from past mistakes.
  • Theme C – Our Cultural Heritage and Knowledge Traditions: Explores India's rich cultural foundations and guiding principles.
  • Theme D – Governance and Democracy: Explores political systems, citizenship, rights, and duties (dharma).
  • Theme E – Economic Life Around Us: Explores economic activities, resources, and how economies function.
Chapter 1: Locating Places on the Earth

1.1 A Map and Its Components

A map is a representation or drawing of an area on a flat surface, viewed from the top. An atlas is a collection of maps.

Types of Maps:

  • Physical maps: Show natural features like mountains, rivers, and oceans.
  • Political maps: Show boundaries of countries, states, cities, and capitals.
  • Thematic maps: Focus on specific information (e.g., rainfall, vegetation, industries).

Three Key Components of Maps:

  • Scale (Distance): The ratio between the actual distance on the ground and the distance shown on the map (e.g., $1\text{ cm} = 500\text{ m}$).
  • Direction: Includes cardinal directions (North, East, South, West) and intermediate directions (Northeast, Southeast, Southwest, Northwest).
  • Symbols: Drawings, letters, or colors used to represent real-world features in limited space.

1.2 Mapping the Earth & The Globe

The Earth is nearly a sphere (slightly flattened at the poles). Representing a sphere accurately on a flat sheet of paper is impossible without distortion. A globe is a spherical model of the Earth that represents its geography accurately.

1.3 Understanding Coordinates

A system of coordinates uses two values to precisely determine any location on Earth.

Latitudes:

  • The Equator is the imaginary line halfway between the North Pole and South Pole, representing $0^\circ$ latitude.
  • Parallels of latitude are imaginary circles running parallel to the Equator, getting smaller towards the poles ($90^\circ\text{N}$ and $90^\circ\text{S}$).
  • Latitude and Climate: Near Equator is hot (torrid), mid-latitudes are moderate (temperate), and near poles are cold (frigid).

Longitudes:

  • Meridians of longitude are imaginary half-circles running from the North Pole to the South Pole.
  • The Prime Meridian (Greenwich Meridian) is the reference line ($0^\circ$ longitude) passing through Greenwich, London.
  • Longitudes increase from $0^\circ$ to $180^\circ$ East (E) or West (W).
  • Historical Note: Long before Europe, India used its own prime meridian called madhya rekhā passing through Ujjayinī (modern Ujjain), associated with astronomer Varāhamihira.

The Grid:

The intersection of parallels of latitude and meridians of longitude forms a grid, allowing precise location tracking (e.g., Delhi lies at approximately $29^\circ\text{N}, 77^\circ\text{E}$).

1.4 Understanding Time Zones

The Earth rotates from west to east, completing a full turn of $360^\circ$ in 24 hours ($15^\circ$ per hour or $1^\circ$ every 4 minutes).

  • Local Time: Calculated based on the sun's position at a specific meridian. Moving $15^\circ$ eastward adds 1 hour; moving $15^\circ$ westward subtracts 1 hour.
  • Standard Time: A single meridian chosen to represent the entire country's time. Indian Standard Time (IST) is based on the $82.5^\circ\text{E}$ meridian, which is $5.5$ hours ahead of Greenwich Mean Time (GMT).
  • Time Zones: The world is divided into time zones that generally follow international borders. Large countries have multiple time zones (e.g., USA has 6, Russia has 11).
  • International Date Line: Located approximately at $180^\circ$ longitude. Crossing it traveling eastward subtracts a day; traveling westward adds a day.
