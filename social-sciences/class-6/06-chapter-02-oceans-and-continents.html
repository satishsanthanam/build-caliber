Class 6 — Chapter 2: Oceans and Continents

2.1 Introduction to the Blue Planet

The Earth is often referred to as the blue planet because, when seen from outer space, it appears mostly blue due to the vast expanse of water covering its surface.

  • Water Cover: Approximately three-fourths ($\approx 75\%$) of the Earth's surface is covered with water.
  • Land Cover: A little over one-fourth ($\approx 25\%$) of the globe consists of land.
  • Oceans: The largest water bodies on the globe.
  • Landmass: A large body of land.
  • Continent: A large continuous expanse of land.

Cultural Significance

The emblem of the Indian Navy contains the motto Sam noh Varunah ("Be auspicious to us, O Varuna"), which is an invocation to Varuᅇa, a Vedic deity associated with the oceans, sky, and water.

2.2 The Distribution of Water and Land

Oceans and continents are not distributed equally between the Northern and Southern Hemispheres.

  • The Northern Hemisphere contains significantly more landmass than the Southern Hemisphere.
  • The Southern Hemisphere is dominated by water.
  • Smaller extensions of oceans are classified under various names such as sea, bay, and gulf.
2.3 Oceans of the World

Although conventionally divided into five distinct oceans, all oceans on Earth are interconnected, allowing seawater and marine life to flow constantly across boundaries.

The Five Oceans (by Size)
  1. Pacific Ocean: The largest ocean on Earth.
  2. Atlantic Ocean: The second-largest ocean.
  3. Indian Ocean: The third-largest ocean, bounded by Asia to the north, Africa to the west, and Australia to the east. Its two major parts bordering India are the Arabian Sea (west) and the Bay of Bengal (east).
  4. Southern (Antarctic) Ocean: The fourth-largest ocean, surrounding Antarctica.
  5. Arctic Ocean: The smallest ocean, located around the North Pole.

Marine Life
  • Marine Flora: Plant life of the ocean, including tiny algae and various seaweeds.
  • Marine Fauna: Animal life of the ocean, consisting of thousands of species of fish, dolphins, whales, and deep-sea creatures.
2.4 Oceans and Natural Disasters

Oceans play a dual role in sustaining life and generating destructive natural events.

  • Monsoons: Oceans are the source of clouds that bring essential monsoon rains to continents, sustaining agriculture.
  • Storms and Cyclones: Oceans give rise to violent weather events with extreme rainfall and strong winds, causing damage to coastal regions.
  • Tsunami: A huge and powerful ocean wave generally caused by a strong earthquake or volcanic eruption at the sea floor.
  • The 2004 Tsunami: On 26 December 2004, a massive tsunami triggered by an earthquake in Indonesia caused widespread devastation across 14 countries bordering the Indian Ocean, heavily affecting India's Andaman and Nicobar Islands, Tamil Nadu, and Kerala.
  • Disaster Management: Measures taken to protect lives and property. India utilizes the Indian Ocean Tsunami Warning System and coordinates relief through the National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA).
2.5 Continents of the World

Continents can be counted in multiple ways depending on whether certain landmasses are considered single or separate entities.

Different Models of Continent Counts
  • Four Continents: Africa-Eurasia, America, Antarctica, Australia.
  • Five Continents: Africa, America, Antarctica, Australia, Eurasia.
  • Six Continents: Africa, Antarctica, Australia, Eurasia, North America, South America.
  • Seven Continents (Most Widely Adopted): Asia, Africa, North America, South America, Antarctica, Europe, Australia.

Key Geographical Observations
  • Eurasia: Europe and Asia form a single continuous physical landmass, though they are historically and culturally treated as two separate continents.
  • The Olympic Rings: The five rings symbolise the gathering of athletes from the five inhabited continents: Africa, America, Asia, Australia, and Europe.
2.6 Islands and Polar Exploration

Islands

An island is a piece of land completely surrounded by water on all sides, which is smaller than a continent.

  • Greenland: The largest island in the world.
  • India's Islands: India has over 1,300 small islands, including two major groups: the Andaman and Nicobar Islands (Bay of Bengal) and the Lakshadweep Islands (Arabian Sea).

Antarctica Exploration

Antarctica is an extremely cold, ice-covered continent at the South Pole.

  • The Indian Antarctica Programme (initiated in 1981) conducts scientific research on climate and environmental evolution.
  • India established its first scientific base station, Dakshin Gangotri, in 1983 (followed by two more bases later).
2.7 Ecological Importance and Human Impact
  • The Planet's Lungs: More than half of the world's oxygen is produced by marine flora (algae and seaweeds).
  • Climate Regulation: Oceans regulate global temperatures and drive the Earth's water cycle.
  • Human Impact and Threats:
    • Pollution: Millions of tonnes of plastic waste are dumped into oceans annually, choking marine life.
    • Overfishing: Excessive fishing threatens the survival of marine species and ecosystems.
  • Conservation: The United Nations has designated June 8 as World Oceans Day to raise awareness about protecting marine environments.
