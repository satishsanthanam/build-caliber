Class 6 — Chapter 9: Family and Community

9.1 Introduction & The Big Questions

"Love and dharma are the flower and fruit of family life." — Tiruvalluvar

  • Why is the family unit important?
  • What is a community and what is its role?
9.2 Family

Almost all of us live in a family. The family is the fundamental and most ancient unit of any society.

9.2.1 Types of Families

  • Joint Family: Has several generations living together, including grandparents, parents, uncles, aunts, brothers, sisters, and cousins.
  • Nuclear Family: Limited to a couple and their children, and sometimes one parent and children.

9.2.2 Language and Family Bonds

Indian languages have many specific terms to describe family relationships (e.g., bua, tau, tai, chacha, mausi, nana, nani in Hindi). In most Indian languages, there is no direct word for 'cousin'; they are referred to as 'brothers' and 'sisters', emphasizing deep family bonds.

9.3 Roles and Responsibilities

Relationships among family members are based on love, care, cooperation, and interdependence.

9.3.1 Core Values in a Family

  • Cooperation: Working together to support each other in daily duties and tasks.
  • The family acts as a 'school' where children learn important values:
    • Ahimsa (non-violence)
    • Dāna (giving)
    • Sevā (service)
    • Tyāga (sacrifice)

9.3.2 Family Stories & Case Studies

  • Shalini's Family (Kerala): Illustrates how family members support each other during financial difficulties, sharing resources and sacrificing personal desires (like Shalini accepting a simple cotton dress instead of silk) for the collective well-being.
  • Tenzing's Family (Meghalaya): Highlights gender-equal participation in household chores (father helping grandmother cook and clean) and the active role of grandparents in teaching wisdom and helping with education.
9.4 Community

Families are connected with other families and people around them to form a community.

9.4.1 What is a Community?

A Community is a group of connected people who come together to celebrate festivals, organize events, support agricultural practices, and share natural resources like water, grazing lands, and forest produce.

9.4.2 Rural Communities & Traditions

  • Halma Tradition: A Bhil community tradition in Madhya Pradesh of coming together to support individuals or families in times of crisis. For example, planting thousands of trees and digging trenches to conserve rainwater.
  • Chennai Floods (2015): An example of community mobilization where spiritual, religious, and private groups cooked and distributed food to those in need.

9.4.3 Urban Communities & Modern Initiatives

  • Kamal Parmar's Initiative (Ahmedabad): Providing free education, mentorship, and dinner to underprivileged children after work hours.
  • Residents' Welfare Associations (RWAs): Modern urban communities that make rules for waste management, cleanliness, and security, showcasing local self-governance.

9.4.4 Interdependence & Diversity

Communities are highly interdependent (e.g., RWAs depend on trading communities and municipal workers). The term 'community' is flexible and can refer to a jāti, religious groups, regional groups, or school clubs.
