Chapter 11: Grassroots Democracy – Part 2: Local Government in Rural Areas

11.1 Introduction to Rural Local Government

India is a country of enormous size and diversity, with about 600,000 villages, 8,000 towns, and over 4,000 cities. Almost two-thirds of India's 1.4 billion population lives in rural areas.

"The real India lives in its villages." – M.K. Gandhi

The Big Questions

  • What are Panchayati Raj institutions?
  • What are their functions?
  • Why are they important in governance and democracy?
11.2 The Panchayati Raj System

The Panchayati Raj is a system of local self-government in rural India that brings governance closer to the people, enabling active participation in decision-making.

The Three-Tier System

The system is organized from the bottom up into three levels:

  • Village Level: Village Parishad / Gram Panchayat (closest to the rural population).
  • Block Level: Block Panchayat / Panchayat Samiti / Mandal Parishad (acts as a link between village and district levels).
  • District Level: District Panchayat / Zila Parishad (top tier at the district level).
11.3 Gram Panchayat and Gram Sabha

The Gram Panchayat is the base of the three-tier system.

  • Gram Sabha: A body consisting of all adults in a village (or group of villages) who are enrolled as voters. They discuss local matters and make decisions.
  • Sarpanch / Pradhan: The elected head or president of the Gram Panchayat.
  • Panchayat Secretary: An appointed official who performs administrative functions, calls meetings, and maintains records.
  • Patwari: An officer responsible for maintaining land records and maps of the village.

Exemplary Sarpanchs (Case Studies)

  • Dnyaneshwar Kamble: A transgender person elected as Sarpanch of Tarangfal village (Solapur, Maharashtra) in 2017, working under the motto "lok seva, gram seva" (Service to the village is service to the public).
  • Vandana Bahadur Maida: A member of the Bhil community from Khankhandvi (Madhya Pradesh) who defied patriarchal norms to become her village's first female Sarpanch, focusing on education and sanitation.
  • Popatrao Baguji Pawar: Sarpanch of Hiware Bazar (Ahmednagar, Maharashtra) who transformed a drought-prone village into a prosperous, green model village using rainwater harvesting and watershed conservation. He was awarded the Padma Shri in 2020.
11.4 Child-Friendly Panchayat Initiative

Panchayats are encouraged to listen to children's voices through the Child-Friendly Panchayat Initiative, creating platforms like Bal Sabhas and Bal Panchayats.

  • Maharashtra: Bal Panchayats have successfully worked to eliminate child labour and child marriage, bringing children back to school.
  • Sikkim (Sangkhu Radhu Khandu Gram Panchayat): Declared a child-friendly Gram Panchayat for building school compound walls and constructing kitchens for hygienic midday meals.
  • Rajasthan (Children's Parliament): An initiative of Bunker Roy's 'Barefoot College' empowering children aged 8 to 14 to participate in democratic processes, run night schools, and form a 'Cabinet' to advocate for community needs. It won the World's Children's Honorary Award in 2001.
11.5 Panchayat Samiti and Zila Parishad

These higher-level institutions coordinate development plans and resources across villages:

  • Panchayat Samiti: Coordinates development plans from all Gram Panchayats and presents them at the district level, facilitating funds for schemes like the Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana (all-weather rural roads).
  • Inclusivity and Representation: Special rules ensure representation for disadvantaged sections, including a mandatory reservation of one-third of all seats for women.
11.6 Historical Context: The Arthaśhāstra

The concept of structured local administration dates back thousands of years. In the Arthaśhāstra, written by Kauṭilya (Chāṇakya) about 2,300 years ago, a similar hierarchical administrative structure was described:

  • sangrahaṇa: A sub-district headquarters for every 10 villages.
  • kārvaṭika: A district headquarters for every 100 villages.
  • droṇamukha: An administrative center for every 400 villages.
  • sthānīya: A provincial headquarters for every 800 villages.
