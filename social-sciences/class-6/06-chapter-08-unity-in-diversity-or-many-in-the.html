Chapter 8: Unity in Diversity, or ‘Many in the One’

8.1 Introduction & The Big Questions

This chapter explores the foundational Indian concept of Unity in Diversity, or the 'Many in the One', examining how a vast array of distinct regional cultures, languages, foods, and traditions weave together into a single, cohesive national identity.

The Big Questions

  • What is meant by 'unity in diversity' in the Indian scenario?
  • What aspects of India's diversity are the most striking?
  • How do we make out the unity underlying the diversity?

"Oh, grant me my prayer, that I may never lose the bliss of the touch of the one in the play of the many."

— Rabindranath Tagore

"... The principle of unity in diversity which has always been normal to [India] and its fulfilment the fundamental course of her being and its very nature, the Many in the One, would place her on the sure foundation of her Swabhava and Swadharma."

— Sri Aurobindo
8.2 A Rich Diversity

India's immense diversity is the first characteristic that strikes visitors. With over 1.4 billion inhabitants (representing about 18 percent of the world's population), this variety spans landscapes, languages, dresses, food, customs, and traditions.

The People of India Project

In the late 20th century, the Anthropological Survey of India conducted a massive study called the People of India project, which surveyed 4,635 communities across all States and Union Territories. Key findings included:

  • Identification of 325 languages written using 25 distinct scripts.
  • A high rate of internal migration, meaning many Indians live away from their birthplaces or original communities, further mixing cultural practices.

Defining Unity in Diversity

Over a century ago, British historian Vincent Smith posed the question of how a unified history of India could be written given such bewildering diversity. He concluded that the answer lies in the fact that "India offers unity in diversity." This means that beneath the surface-level differences lies a shared cultural consciousness and mutual respect.

8.3 Food for All

While India boasts thousands of regional dishes and preparations, there is a deep underlying unity in the agricultural staples and ingredients used across the subcontinent.

  • Staple Grains: Cereals like rice, barley, and wheat, along with millets (such as pearl millet/bajra, sorghum/jowar, finger millet/ragi) and pulses (various types of dals, gram, and rajma) form the core diet of most Indians.
  • Common Spices: Spices like turmeric, cumin, cardamom, and ginger are used universally across different regional cuisines.
  • The Unity-Diversity Dynamic: The same basic ingredients (unity) are combined in endless regional variations (diversity) to create unique local dishes.
8.4 Textiles and Clothing

Clothing styles in India vary by region and community, yet they share common traditional forms and historical roots.

The Sari as a Symbol of Unity

The sari is a prime example of unity in diversity. It is a single, unstitched length of cloth worn across almost all parts of India, yet it exhibits immense variety:

  • Fabrics & Weaves: Made from cotton, silk, or synthetic fabrics. Famous silk varieties include Banarasi, Kanjivaram, Paithani, Patan Patola, Muga, and Mysore.
  • Draping Styles: There are dozens of regional ways to drape a sari, with new styles continuously being invented.
  • Historical Depth: The sari has a long history, as evidenced by archaeological findings like the stone relief of a woman in a sari from Vaiśhali (Bihar), dating back several centuries BCE.
  • Versatility: Beyond being a dress, women creatively use the sari as a baby cradle, a utility sack for grains, a canopy against rain, or a makeshift filter.

Indian Cotton and Chintz

For centuries, India produced the finest cotton textiles in the world. A highly popular printed cotton fabric called chintz was exported to Europe in the 17th century. It became so popular in England and France that local manufacturers lobbied their governments to ban its import to protect domestic industries.

8.5 Festivals Galore

India celebrates an immense variety of festivals, many of which occur at the same time to mark seasonal changes, though they are known by different regional names.

The Harvest Festival (Mid-January)

Celebrated around January 14th to mark the beginning of the harvest season, this festival is known by various names across India:

  • Lohri / Maghi: Punjab and Haryana
  • Makar Sankranti / Makar Sankraat: North and Central India
  • Uttarayan: Gujarat
  • Khichdi Parv: Uttar Pradesh
  • Shishur Saenkraat: Jammu and Kashmir
  • Magh Bihu: Assam
  • Poush Songkranti: West Bengal
  • Pedda Panduga: Andhra Pradesh and Telangana
  • Pongal: Tamil Nadu
  • Makara Sankramana: Karnataka
  • Makara Vilakku: Kerala
8.6 An Epic Spread

Literature provides a powerful illustration of how singular cultural narratives spread and adapt across diverse linguistic and regional landscapes.

The Pañchatantra

The Pañchatantra is an ancient Sanskrit collection of animal fables teaching essential life skills. Over 2,200 years old, it has been adapted into almost every Indian language and has traveled globally, with over 200 adaptations in more than 50 languages across Southeast Asia, the Arab world, and Europe.

The Two Great Epics: Rāmāyaṇa and Mahābhārata

The Rāmāyaṇa and the Mahābhārata are two monumental Sanskrit epics that narrate the struggles of heroes to establish dharma (righteousness).

  • Mahābhārata: Details the conflict between the Pāṇḍavas (supported by Kṛiṣhṇa) and their cousins, the Kauravas.
  • Rāmāyaṇa: Details Rāma's quest, aided by Lakṣhmaṇa and Hanuman, to rescue his wife Sītā from the demon king Rāvaṇa.
  • Regional and Folk Adaptations: These epics have been translated, adapted, and retold in countless regional languages and folk forms. A survey in Tamil Nadu alone identified around 100 folk versions of the Mahābhārata.
  • Tribal Connections: Tribal communities like the Bhils, Gonds, and Mundas have their own oral versions of the epics, often incorporating local geography and legends suggesting the epic heroes visited their specific regions.
