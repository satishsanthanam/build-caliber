Class 6 — Chapter 13: The Value of Work

13.1 Introduction to Work and Activities

Every day, people around us engage in various types of activities to run their households, serve the community, or earn a livelihood.

The Big Questions

  • What are the different types of activities that people engage in?
  • What is their contribution to our everyday lives?

A Story of Everyday Work

Through the story of Anu and Kabir, we observe different individuals performing diverse roles:

  • Geeta Aunty: A pilot in the Indian Air Force who serves the nation.
  • Kabir's Grandfather: A retired Border Security Force officer who voluntarily teaches free Geography classes to neighbourhood kids, tends to a vegetable garden, and runs household errands.
  • Anu's Parents: Run a shop selling uniforms and ready-made garments.
  • Anu's Maa: Helps in the shop and also volunteers to teach knitting to women in the community.
  • Rohan (Anu's Brother): A software engineer who also volunteers on weekends to teach computer skills at a nearby college.
13.2 Economic vs. Non-Economic Activities

Human activities are broadly classified into two major categories based on their purpose and monetary involvement.

Economic Activities

Economic activities are those that involve money or are performed in exchange for money or money's worth.

  • Examples: A business person selling school bags, a farmer selling produce, a lawyer arguing a case for a fee, a truck driver transporting goods, or factory workers.
  • Market: A place where people engage in the exchange of goods and services, usually for money.
  • Fee: A payment made to a person or organisation in exchange for professional advice or services (e.g., paid to a doctor or lawyer).
  • Money's worth: The monetary value that a person places on an object based on the benefit they derive from it.

Non-Economic Activities

Non-economic activities are those that do not generate income or wealth but are done out of feelings like gratitude, love, care, and respect.

  • Examples: Parents cooking food for the family, helping children with schoolwork, youth taking care of grandparents, or family members helping in home renovation.
13.3 Types of Economic Activities and Compensation

Economic activities differ in how individuals are compensated for their labor and skills.

Modes of Compensation

  • Salary: A fixed regular payment, generally paid monthly by an employer to an employee (e.g., pilots, software engineers, post office employees).
  • Wage: A payment made by the employer to the worker for a specific period of time, often on a daily basis (e.g., farm labourers).
  • Payment in kind: A non-cash payment received for the work performed (e.g., receiving a portion of wages in the form of agricultural produce like mangoes).

Value Addition

Value addition is the process of adding value at each stage of transforming a raw material into a finished product.

Example of Rajesh the Carpenter:

  • Cost of raw wood: $₹600$
  • Selling price of the finished chair: $₹1,000$
  • Value added by Rajesh's skill, time, and effort: $$₹1,000 - ₹600 = ₹400$$
13.4 The Importance of Non-Economic Activities

Even though non-economic activities do not involve monetary transactions, they generate immense social and personal value.

Sevā (Selfless Service)

Sevā fosters a sense of satisfaction, community bonding, and gratitude.

  • Example: Langars or community kitchens at Gurudwaras that serve free food to all visitors.

Community Participation

Collective efforts of citizens contribute to the welfare of society and the nation.

  • Swachh Bharat Abhiyan: A collective national effort to keep surroundings, streets, parks, and public places clean.
  • Van Mahotsav: The festival of forests in India, bringing communities together for tree plantation drives to promote environmental conservation.
