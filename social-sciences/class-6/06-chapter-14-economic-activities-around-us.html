Class 6 — Chapter 14: Economic Activities Around Us

14.1 Introduction to Economic Activities

Economic activities are those human actions that create monetary value (value of something that can be measured in terms of money). Understanding how these activities are classified and differentiated helps us comprehend how they function and connect with one another.

"The root of prosperity is economic activity, the lack of it brings material distress. The absence of fruitful economic activity endangers both current prosperity and future growth."
— Kauṭilya’s Arthaśhāstra

The Big Questions
  • How are economic activities classified?
  • What differentiates these activities to be grouped into sectors?
  • How are the three sectors interconnected?
14.2 Classification of Economic Activities into Sectors

Economic activities share similar characteristics and are grouped into broader categories called economic sectors. These sectors are essential for organizing and understanding a nation's economic prosperity.

A. Primary Activities (Primary Sector)

Those economic activities in which people are directly dependent on nature to produce goods are known as primary activities or primary sector economic activities.

  • Definition: The group of activities involving the extraction of raw materials directly from nature.
  • Key Examples:
    • Agriculture & Greenhouse farming
    • Mining & Quarrying
    • Fishing & Fish farming (fishery)
    • Raising livestock (poultry, dairy farming)
    • Forestry & Wood collection

B. Secondary Activities (Secondary Sector)

Economic activities in which people are dependent on outputs of the primary sector and transform them to produce goods are known as secondary activities or secondary sector economic activities.

  • Definition: The group of activities that involves processing of raw materials derived from the primary sector into products for sale or consumption.
  • Key Components: Manufacturing in factories, construction of buildings and roads, and providing utilities (water supply, electricity production, solar power, gas).
  • Key Examples:
    • Processing grains into flour in mills
    • Extracting oil from groundnuts
    • Converting wood into furniture and paper
    • Using cotton to make clothes in textile factories
    • Using steel from iron ore to manufacture automobiles

C. Tertiary Activities (Tertiary Sector / Service Sector)

All those economic activities that provide support to people involved in primary and secondary activities are called tertiary activities or tertiary sector economic activities.

  • Definition: The group of activities that involves the provision of services which complement both primary and secondary sectors.
  • Key Examples:
    • Transportation & Logistics: Truck drivers, railways, shipping, and airport services.
    • Trade & Retail: Fruit/vegetable vendors, retail stores, and warehouses (large buildings used for storing products before they are sold, used, or rented out).
    • Professional Services: Doctors, nurses, teachers, lawyers, and pilots.
    • Technical Support: Electricians, mechanics, and electronics repair technicians.
    • Modern Services: Banking, communication services (mobile/internet), and software development.
14.3 Interdependence Among Sectors

The three economic sectors do not work in isolation; they are deeply interconnected and support each other in converting natural raw materials into finished products for final consumption.

Case Study: Dairy Cooperative (AMUL)

The story of Anand Milk Union Limited (AMUL) in Gujarat illustrates how the three sectors work together:

  • Historical Context: In the 1940s, farmers in Anand district faced exploitation by middlemen (persons who buy goods from producers and sell them to consumers for a fee). Under the advice of Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, they formed a cooperative (a group of people who voluntarily come together to meet their economic and social needs in a formal way, owning the cooperative and making decisions collectively).
  • Establishment: Set up in 1946 under the leadership of Tribhuvandas Patel and Dr. Varghese Kurien.
  • Sector Interconnection in AMUL:
    • Primary Sector: Farmers milking their cows and buffaloes (livestock rearing).
    • Secondary Sector: Processing liquid milk into butter, ghee, cheese, and milk powder in a factory, including the process of pasteurisation (preserving milk by heating it to kill harmful bacteria).
    • Tertiary Sector: Transporting products via trucks, trains, and planes; storing them in warehouses; and selling them through retail shops or preparing them for export (selling goods to other countries).

Case Study: How Textbooks Are Made

The production of school textbooks relies on all three sectors working together:

  • Primary Sector: Extracting wood and pulp from trees in the forest.
  • Secondary Sector: Transforming wood pulp into paper in mills, and printing text and images onto the paper.
  • Tertiary Sector: Transporting paper rolls and finished books, and selling them in retail bookstores.
  • Environmental Note: Recycling paper saves resources. Recycling 1 tonne of paper saves 17 trees, 2.5 cubic metres of landfill space, and uses 70% less energy and water than making new paper from wood pulp.
