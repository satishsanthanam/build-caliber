Class 6 — Chapter 4: Timeline and Sources of History

Introduction & The Big Questions

History is "an unending dialogue between the present and the past... between the society of today and the society of yesterday." — E.H. Carr

The Big Questions

  • How do we measure historical time?
  • How can various sources help us understand history?
  • How did early humans live?
4.1 How Do We Learn About the Past?

The Earth has a very long history, of which humans occupy only a tiny, recent part. Various specialists help us uncover this past:

  • Geologists: Study the physical features of the Earth, such as soil, stones, hills, mountains, rivers, and oceans.
  • Palaeontologists: Study the remains of plants, animals, and humans from millions of years ago in the form of fossils (impressions of footprints or preserved parts within soil or rocks).
  • Anthropologists: Study human societies and cultures from the oldest times to the present.
  • Archaeologists: Study the past by digging up physical remains left behind, such as tools, pots, beads, figurines, toys, bones, teeth, burnt grains, and ruins of houses.
4.2 How Is Time Measured in History?

Different societies have unique ways of measuring time, often marking a new era (a distinct period of time) with major events like the birth of a leader or the start of a reign.

The Gregorian Calendar & Dating Systems

The Gregorian calendar is the most common calendar used worldwide today. It features 12 months (365 days, with a leap year every four years, except centenary years unless they are multiples of 400).

  • Common Era (CE): Formerly marked as AD (Anno Domini), counting forward from the conventional birth year of Jesus Christ.
  • Before Common Era (BCE): Formerly marked as BC (Before Christ), counting backward from the birth of Jesus Christ.

Calculating Time Intervals: Because there is no "year zero" in the Gregorian calendar (1 CE immediately follows 1 BCE), calculating the years between a BCE date and a CE date requires adding the two years and subtracting 1:

$$\text{Years elapsed} = \text{BCE year} + \text{CE year} - 1$$

For example, if Gautama Buddha was born in 560 BCE, his birth in relation to the year 2024 CE is calculated as: $560 + 2024 - 1 = 2583$ years ago.

Time Durations & Traditional Calendars

  • Century: A period of 100 years. Centuries are counted starting from 1 CE (e.g., the 21st century CE runs from 2001 to 2100).
  • Millennium: A period of 1,000 years (e.g., the 3rd millennium CE runs from 2001 to 3000).
  • Pañchānga: A traditional Indian calendar book of tables relying on the positions of the sun and moon to define months, predict astronomical events (eclipses, sunrise/sunset), weather, and festivals.
4.3 What Are the Sources of History?

A source of history is any place, person, text, or object from which we gather information about past events or periods.

Classification of Historical Sources

  • Archaeological Sources:
    • Structures: Monuments, mounds.
    • Excavations: Human, animal, and plant remains; tools and weapons; figurines and ornaments; pottery and toys; habitations and burials.
    • Inscriptions: Manuscripts, copper plates, coins.
  • Literary Sources:
    • Indian Literature: Vedas and Itihasas, poems and plays, historical texts, collections of stories, scientific and technological texts.
    • Foreign Accounts: Travelogues, historical chronicles.
  • Oral Sources: Genealogical records, folklore.
  • Artistic Sources: Paintings, sculptures, panels.

Reconstructing the Past

Historians study and write about the past by gathering and cross-verifying information from multiple sources. When sources contradict, they must evaluate which is more reliable. Modern historical reconstruction also relies on scientific fields like genetics (the study of inherited characteristics) and climate studies.

4.4 The Beginnings of Human History

Modern humans (Homo sapiens) have existed for about 300,000 years.

  • Early Lifestyle: Early humans lived in bands or groups, seeking shelter in caves and rock shelters. They were hunters and gatherers, relying on wild plants, fruits, and animals.
  • Tools & Technology: They used fire and crafted stone tools like axes, blades, and arrowheads. They also created rock paintings depicting animals, humans, and symbols.
  • Beliefs: They held beliefs about natural elements and developed early concepts of an afterlife (life beginning after death).
4.5 The First Crops and Settlements

The Transition to Agriculture

The last Ice Age (a cold period when much of the Earth was covered in ice) lasted from over 100,000 years ago to about 12,000 years ago. As the climate warmed, humans began settling down, cultivating cereals/grains, and domesticating animals (cattle, goats).

Social Complexity & Technology

  • Settlements: Early agricultural communities settled near fertile river valleys. Hamlets (small villages) grew into larger villages and eventually small towns.
  • Social Structure: Chieftains or leaders managed community welfare. Land was sowed and harvested collectively, with no initial concept of individual ownership.
  • New Technologies: Development of pottery for storage and the introduction of metallurgy (using copper first, and iron later) to create durable tools and ornaments.
