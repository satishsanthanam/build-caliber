Class 6 — Chapter 12: Grassroots Democracy – Part 3: Local Government in Urban Areas

Introduction to Urban Governance

In a democracy, good governance aims to empower citizens so they can actively participate in their country's functioning at all levels. This is known as participatory democracy. While rural governance is managed through Panchayati Raj Institutions, urban areas require a more complex system due to their size, diversity, and density.

The Structure of Indian Governance

The Indian system of governance is structured like a pyramid, with the local level at the base and the national level at the top:

  • National Level: Union Government
  • State Level: State Government
  • Local Level: Divided into Rural and Urban branches:
    • Rural (Panchayati Raj): Gram Sabha → Gram Panchayat → Panchayat Samiti → Zila Panchayat
    • Urban (Urban Local Bodies): People of the Ward → Ward Committee → Nagar Panchayat / Municipal Council / Municipal Corporation
Urban Local Bodies and Their Functions

Local government structures in urban areas are called urban local bodies. They operate on the principle of decentralisation, giving local communities a direct say in how their areas are managed.

Wards and Ward Committees

Cities and towns are divided into smaller territorial units called wards. Each ward has a ward committee that facilitates local activities and monitors issues such as:

  • Conducting health camps and awareness campaigns (e.g., against single-use plastics).
  • Monitoring local infrastructure issues like water leaks, blocked drains, and damaged roads.
  • Reporting local grievances to higher municipal authorities.

Key Functions of Urban Local Bodies

Urban local bodies are responsible for a wide range of civic and developmental functions:

  • Maintaining public infrastructure, roads, and streetlights.
  • Garbage collection, waste segregation, and disposal.
  • Maintaining burial grounds and crematoriums.
  • Implementing state and central government welfare schemes.
  • Levying and collecting local taxes (like property tax) and fines.
  • Planning for the economic and social development of the urban area.
Types of Urban Local Bodies

Urban local bodies are classified into three categories based on the population size of the city or town:

  • Municipal Corporation (also known as Mahanagar Nigam): Established in large cities with a population above 10 lakhs (e.g., Mumbai, Chennai, Indore).
  • Municipal Council (also known as Nagar Palika): Established in towns and cities with a population between 1 lakh and 10 lakhs.
  • Nagar Panchayat (or City Council): Established in transitional areas that are changing from rural to urban, with smaller populations.

Historical Context: The Oldest Municipal Corporation

The Madras Corporation (now Greater Chennai Corporation), established on 29 September 1688, is the oldest municipal institution in India. It was granted the power to levy municipal taxes under a Parliamentary Act of 1792, marking the formal beginning of municipal administration in India.

Municipal Services and Citizen Participation

For urban local bodies to function efficiently, active citizen participation is essential. Citizens have a duty to support municipal efforts by segregating waste, conserving water, and reporting civic issues promptly.

Case Study: Indore Municipal Corporation

Indore in Madhya Pradesh has been awarded the cleanest city in India under the government's Swachh Survekshan scheme for seven consecutive years. This achievement highlights the critical role of active citizen cooperation alongside efficient municipal services.

Services Offered by Municipal Corporations

Modern municipal corporations utilize Citizen Relationship Management (CRM) systems to deliver various services:

  • Revenue & Licensing: Property tax collection, water charges, business licenses, trade licenses, and marriage registration.
  • Emergency & Utility Services: Fire services, water tankers, debris clearance, and tree cutting.
  • Services on Request: Septic tankers, public auditoriums, funeral vans, mobile toilets, and ambulances.
  • Grievance Redressal: Portals for citizens to register complaints regarding civic amenities.
