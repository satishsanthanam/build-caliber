Class 10 (Words and Expressions) — Chapter 6: Mijbil the Otter

Unit Overview & Let's Begin

This unit focuses on the relationship between humans and animals, exploring themes of care, coexistence, animal rights, and conservation through various texts, vocabulary exercises, and grammatical concepts.

Let's Begin: Caring for Pets

Reflecting on the chapter 'Mijbil the Otter', this section highlights the commitment and arrangements made by the author for his pet otter, including:

  • Providing suitable food (getting fish).
  • Taking the pet for walks and exercise.
  • Providing toys, space, and water to play.
Reading Comprehension

Text I: Baby Bhakat (The Baby Civet Cat)

A true story from Assam written by conservation photographer Rommel Shunmugam about a baby Civet cat named Bhakat who was rescued and raised by a human family (Dharini and Anjali).

Key Vocabulary:

  • arboreal: Living in trees.
  • inculcate: To instill an attitude, habit, or belief in someone.
  • nocturnal: Active during the night.
  • omnivorous: Feeding on food of both plant and animal origin.
  • rehabilitates: Restores to health or normal life.

Key Facts about Civet Cats:

  • Also called toddy cats because they live on palm trees and drink sweet sap.
  • They are nocturnal, having large eyes to see in the dark, and long tails for balance on tree-tops.

Text II: Human-Wildlife Conflict

An article discussing the global issue of human-wildlife conflict arising from community expansion and reduction of natural habitats.

Key Vocabulary:

  • coexistence: Living together in peace.
  • conflict: A serious disagreement or argument.
  • endangered: Seriously at risk of extinction.
  • livestock: Farm animals regarded as an asset.
  • retaliation: The action of returning a military attack; counterattack.

Key Concepts:

  • Impacts: Loss of crops, livestock, property, and human lives; retaliatory killing of endangered animals.
  • Solutions: Simple and creative measures like planting barrier crops (e.g., chilli barriers to repel elephants) which also generate income.

Text III: Animal Rights

An article from PETA advocating for the inherent worth of animals, independent of their utility to humans.

Key Vocabulary:

  • consideration: Careful thought, typically over a period of time.
  • exploitation: The action of treating someone unfairly in order to benefit from their work.
  • familial: Relating to or occurring in a family.
  • inherent: Existing in something as a permanent, essential, or characteristic attribute.
  • arbitrary: Based on random choice or personal whim.
  • compassion: Sympathetic pity and concern for the sufferings or misfortunes of others.
  • empathy: The ability to understand and share the feelings of another.

Core Principles:

  • Animals have the capacity to feel pain, pleasure, fear, loneliness, and familial love.
  • Distinction between equal consideration (not causing suffering) and equal treatment (e.g., voting rights, which are irrelevant to animals).
  • Cruelty-free choices in food, fashion, and entertainment act as a form of non-violent protest.
Vocabulary & Word Study

Odd One Out (Synonyms)

  • (a) serious, grave, ridiculous (odd), stern, solemn
  • (b) suitable, unfitting (odd), appropriate, right, proper
  • (c) hostile, unfriendly, averse, agreeable (odd), unreceptive
  • (d) peaceful, harmonious, quiet, serene, violent (odd)
  • (e) appalling, atrocious, wonderful (odd), awful, dreadful

Words as Nouns and Verbs

Many English words can function as both nouns and verbs depending on context:

  • Cross: (Verb) By then it had crossed my mind... | (Noun) I've put a cross on the map.
  • Ring: (Verb) Camusfearna, ringed by water... | (Noun) She put the ring back in the box.
  • Other examples: Book, Mail, Cable, Sack, End, Head, Colour, Lead.
Grammar Rules

Noun Modifiers (Adjective Phrases)

Noun modifiers are adjectives or adjective phrases that provide more information about a noun.

Example: I went to a party. It was a Christmas party. The party was lovely. → I went to a lovely Christmas party.

Practice Exercises:

  • (a) I bought a Maruti car. The colour is blue. It is sporty. → I bought a sporty blue Maruti car.
  • (b) He made a speech. The speech was short. The speech was interesting. → He made a short, interesting speech.
  • (c) Suresh went to a house. It was decorated beautifully. → Suresh went to a beautifully decorated house.
  • (d) She bought a dress. It is a designer one but frightfully expensive. → She bought a frightfully expensive designer dress.
  • (e) The food was tasty. It was prepared quickly. → The quickly prepared food was tasty.

Participle Adjectives (-ing vs -ed)

Adjectives ending in -ing describe the characteristic of a person, thing, or situation. Adjectives ending in -ed describe a feeling or emotion.

  • His behaviour was disappointing. People present there were disappointed.
  • The death of 40 jawans was shocking. We were shocked when we heard the news.
  • I met an interesting person in the party. He was interested in tasting each and every dish.

Past Habits: 'Would' vs 'Used to'

Both would and used to can describe repeated past actions, but used to is preferred for past states or situations that are no longer true.

  • Every day she would come home from work with a rose.
  • When I was a child, I used to believe that fireflies were small fireballs.
Listening & Speaking

The Story of Gangaram (The Friendly Crocodile)

A heartwarming story of a 130-year-old crocodile named Gangaram who lived in a pond in Bawamohatra village, Chhattisgarh.

  • Friendly Nature: Children could swim around him; he never harmed anyone; he would eat rice and dal served by villagers.
  • Coexistence: The villagers worshipped him as a divine creature and friend. Upon his death, over 500 villagers attended his funeral, carrying him on a decorated tractor.
  • Village Name: The village was affectionately called Magarmachha vala gaaon (the Crocodile's village).
