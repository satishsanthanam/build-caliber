Class 9 — Chapter 6: Twin Melodies

Play Overview & Characters

Play Overview

"Twin Melodies" is a three-act play written by Mitra Phukan. It explores the conflict between traditional classical music and modern fusion music, highlighting the generational gap, parental expectations, and the ultimate reconciliation of artistic expressions.

Character Profiles

  • Shruti Sharma: A young, talented, and aspiring violinist who is torn between her respect for her father's classical traditions and her passion for exploring Western fusion music.
  • Guru Nabin Sharma: Shruti's father, a master violinist, and the Principal of Sangeetika Music School. He is highly traditional, strict, and initially views fusion music as a desecration of classical art.
  • Leela Devi: Shruti's mother, who acts as a mediator. She helps Nabin realize his own past struggles against tradition, leading to his acceptance of Shruti's choices.
  • Iqbal: The flutist of the fusion group, in whose room the rehearsals take place.
  • Avinash: The tabla player of the fusion group.
  • Peter: The keyboard player of the fusion group.
Act-by-Act Summary

Act I: The Dilemma

The play opens in Iqbal's room where the four friends (Shruti, Iqbal, Avinash, and Peter) are wrapping up a rehearsal of an Indo-Western fusion piece. While the boys are excited about a positive newspaper review of Shruti's classical performance with her father, Shruti is visibly distressed. She confesses that she has not told her father about the upcoming fusion concert. She fears his reaction, as he believes classical Hindustani music with its ragas and aalaaps is the only worthy form of music, and playing Western tunes on the violin is a desecration. Her friends encourage her to bite the bullet and speak to him at dinner.

Act II: The Confrontation

At the dinner table, Shruti is absent-minded and lost in thought. She gathers the courage to tell her father about the upcoming Indo-Western fusion concert. Nabin reacts with strong disapproval, firmly refusing permission. He argues that playing in an orchestra drowns individual style in a hubbub and reiterates his disdain for fusion music, mockingly calling it "phoo" music. Shruti's mother, Leela, scolds her for keeping secrets. Shruti is left disheartened, fearing her group won't be able to perform.

Act III: Reconciliation & Acceptance

The scene shifts back to Iqbal's house. Shruti decides to practice with her friends one last time before telling them she must withdraw. During their practice, Nabin and Leela quietly enter and listen. Nabin is deeply moved by the performance, noticing that Shruti beautifully maintained the notes of the raga. He praises the group, offers them his own music room for future practices, and supports their concert. Leela reveals to Shruti that Nabin himself had once gone against his family of traditional vocalists to play the violin—a Western instrument at the time—working his fingers to the bone to succeed. Realizing his own past, Nabin embraces his daughter's musical journey.

Key Themes & Literary Analysis

Tradition vs. Modernity

The play contrasts the rigid boundaries of classical Hindustani music with the creative freedom of Indo-Western fusion. It shows that modernity does not necessarily destroy tradition but can build upon it, creating a harmonious blend of "twin melodies."

Generational Gap & Parental Expectations

Nabin's initial refusal reflects the common generational conflict where parents impose their values and fears on their children. The resolution highlights the importance of open communication and parental empathy, as Nabin remembers his own youthful rebellion.

Vocabulary & Key Terms

Dramatic & Musical Vocabulary

  • crescendo: A gradual increase in the volume or intensity of sound.
  • desecration: Disrespect towards something sacred.
  • ragas and aalaaps: Core elements and melodic frameworks of Hindustani classical music.
  • contempt: A strong feeling of dislike and disrespect.
  • hubbub: A loud, confused noise or disturbance.
  • iterated: Repeated or stated repeatedly.
  • painstakingly: Done with great care and effort.

Idiomatic Expressions

  • bite the bullet: To face a difficult or unpleasant situation with courage and determination.
  • worked his fingers to the bone: Worked extremely hard.
  • root for: To show support or cheer for someone.
Poetry Appreciation

"A Friend Found in Music" by Bryanna T. Perkins

This poem explores the therapeutic and comforting nature of music in a person's life.

Stanza Breakdown & Key Lines:

  • Music as an Ocean: "Music is the ocean / That pulls me to the shore." (Metaphor representing music as a powerful, guiding force).
  • Music as Therapy: "Music is the therapy / I need when I feel blue." (Highlights music's healing power during sadness).
  • Music as a Friend: "Music is the needed friend / When no one seems to care." (Emphasizes companionship and emotional support).

"Music" by Walter de la Mare

A lyrical poem depicting the magical, transcendent power of music to transform reality and evoke mystical imagery.

Key Imagery:

  • Transformation of Nature: Flowers flame in vision, and forest trees lift their branches in ecstasy when music sounds.
  • Mythical Elements: Naiads (water nymphs) rise from the water, enchanted by the melody.
Grammar & Language Study

Dramatic Devices: The Aside

An aside is a theatrical technique where a character speaks directly to the audience or thinks aloud, unheard by the other characters on stage. It is used to reveal a character's inner thoughts, feelings, or secret plans.

Example from the play: SHRUTI (Aside): "Oh, how much I enjoy performing this piece. Perhaps I can practise one last time..."

Non-Lexical Fillers

Non-lexical fillers are sounds or words (like 'uh', 'um', 'err', 'ahem') used in speech to fill pauses while a speaker gathers their thoughts or transitions between ideas.

  • whew: Expresses relief.
  • err: Expresses hesitation or uncertainty.
  • arrgh: Expresses frustration or anger.
  • hmmm: Expresses thoughtfulness or contemplation.
  • ahem-ahem: Used to clear the throat or draw attention.

Phrasal Verbs with "Move"

  • move out: To leave a place where one has been living.
  • moved in: To begin living in a new home or place.
  • moved on: To start doing or discussing something new.
  • move over: To shift position to make space for someone or something.
  • move along: To start moving; to leave.
  • moved off: To go to a new position, especially to make room.
