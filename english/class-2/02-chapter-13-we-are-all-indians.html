Class 2 — Chapter 13: We are all Indians

Chapter Overview: Diversity of India

This chapter introduces children from different states of India, highlighting the rich cultural diversity, languages, festivals, and unique lifestyles that unite them as Indians.

Profiles of Children from Different States
  • Meenakshi: Comes from Tamil Nadu, speaks Tamil, and her favourite festival is Pongal.
  • Ramu: Comes from Gujarat, speaks Gujarati, is seven years old, and likes to dance the Garba.
  • Gulabo: Comes from Rajasthan, likes wearing colourful clothes, enjoys puppet shows, and travels by camel.
  • Badal: Comes from Bengal, speaks Bengali, likes painting and music, and studies at Santiniketan.
  • Surinder: Comes from Punjab, speaks Punjabi, and mentions his neighbour Mallika who likes to do Bhangra.
  • Vibha: Comes from Maharashtra, speaks Marathi, and has visited the famous Ellora and Ajanta caves.
  • Neerja: Comes from Uttar Pradesh and has seen the Taj Mahal.
  • Nonglik: Comes from Manipur and speaks Manipuri.
  • Kamala: Comes from Karnataka, speaks Kannada, and plays the Mridang (a musical instrument).
  • Bharti: Comes from Andhra Pradesh, speaks Telugu, and travels to Kashmir during summer holidays to stay with friends Salma and Suresh.
  • Two Sisters: Belong to Odisha, speak Odia, and love to decorate their walls and floors.
  • Unni: Comes from Kerala, speaks Malayalam, and his favourite tree is the coconut tree.

Core Message

Despite the differences in language, clothing, food, and festivals, the chapter emphasizes: "But we are all Indians. Each of us is unique."

New Vocabulary
  • festival: A day or period of celebration, typically for religious or cultural reasons.
  • puppet: A model of a person or animal moved by strings or hands, used in entertainment.
  • travel: To make a journey, typically of some length.
  • painting: The action or skill of using paint to create pictures.
  • neighbour: A person living next door to or very near to the speaker.
  • fingerprints: An impression or mark made on a surface by a person's fingertip.

Joining Sentences with 'but'

The conjunction but is used to join two sentences that show contrasting ideas.

  • Example:
    • Sentence 1: I like to eat mangoes.
    • Sentence 2: I don't like to eat apples.
    • Joined: I like to eat mangoes but I don't like to eat apples.
  • Practice Exercises:
    • Sentence 1: I like milk. / Sentence 2: I don't like tea.
      Answer: I like milk but I don't like tea.
    • Sentence 1: My grandfather is old. / Sentence 2: He is strong.
      Answer: My grandfather is old but he is strong.
    • Sentence 1: I wanted to go out. / Sentence 2: I was too tired.
      Answer: I wanted to go out but I was too tired.

Phonics & Pronunciation

Practice speaking and framing sentences with words starting with consonant blends:

  • 'br' blend: bread, bridge, brown
  • 'fr' blend: frock, frame, frog
  • Example Sentence: "It was a bright sunny day. My brother went to buy bread. He crossed the bridge to reach the shop."
Something New: The Charkha

A short classroom drama where students discover an unfamiliar object on the teacher's table.

The Discovery

Students notice a wooden object with one wheel, no handle, no pedals, and a thread on it. They learn from their teacher that it is a Charkha (or spinning wheel).

Significance of the Charkha
  • It was very dear to Mahatma Gandhi (the Father of the Nation).
  • Gandhiji used it to spin thread to make cloth (like khadi).
  • The Charkha was used as a symbol for self-reliance and independence during the Indian Freedom movement, promoting local handicrafts.
Cultural Insights: Festivals of India

Phool Dei Festival
  • A festival of flowers that marks the beginning of Spring.
  • Children collect different kinds of flowers and offer them to elders or place them on doorsteps.
  • Elders give children rice and jaggery in return.

Sharad Poornima
  • Celebrated on a full moon night in the month of Ashvin (September-October).
  • Marks the ending of the monsoon season.

Aadi Perukku
  • A Tamil monsoon festival celebrated in the Aadi month (July-August).
  • People worship the river Cauvery, and farmers pray for a good harvest.

Pang Lhabsol
  • A festival of Sikkim celebrated in August or September.
  • Mount Kanchenjunga is worshipped as a protector.

Let us speak: Classroom Phrases

What do you say when:

  1. You want to enter the classroom? → "May I come in, please?"
  2. You want to visit the washroom? → "May I go to the washroom, please?"
  3. You want to borrow a book from your friend? → "Can I please borrow your book?"
  4. You hurt someone by mistake? → "I am sorry."
  5. You need a pencil and your friend gives you a pencil? → "Thank you."
