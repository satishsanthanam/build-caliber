Class 8 — Chapter 5: Science and Curiosity

Unit Overview: Science and Curiosity

This unit explores the intersection of scientific exploration, curiosity, and the human spirit through three distinct literary pieces: a science fiction story, a reflective poem, and a biographical account of a pioneering Indian scientist.

Prose: Feathered Friend (Arthur C. Clarke)

Plot Summary

Set on a space station, the story follows the crew who adopts Claribel, a small yellow canary smuggled aboard by construction worker Sven Olsen. Claribel quickly adapts to zero gravity, performing effortless loops. One morning, the narrator wakes up with a sluggish headache and finds Claribel unconscious. Realizing that miners historically used canaries to detect toxic gas, the crew checks the air systems. They discover that a rare eclipse caused the air purifier to freeze, and the primary alarm circuit failed because the backup was not yet connected. Claribel's fainting saves the crew from asphyxiation, proving that simple nature can sometimes safeguard advanced technology.

Character Analysis

  • Sven Olsen: A wiry, highly skilled construction worker who excels in zero-gravity girder assembly. His curiosity and love for pets lead him to smuggle Claribel.
  • Claribel: The small yellow canary who becomes the space station's beloved pet. She is highly resourceful, learning to fly efficiently without gravity, and serves as a literal lifesaver.
  • The Narrator: An observant office worker on the station whose quick thinking and memory of mining history save the crew.

Key Vocabulary & Expressions

  • wiry: Thin but strong.
  • girders: Long, thick pieces of steel supporting a structure.
  • dovetailed: Fitted together precisely.
  • cubbyhole: A small enclosed space or room.
  • keeled: Collapsed or fainted.
  • sheepish: Embarrassed or self-conscious.
Poetry: Magnifying Glass (Walter de la Mare)

Stanza Breakdown

  • Stanza 1: The speaker describes the magical power of a simple magnifying glass, which reveals intricate details like myriad shells in a scrap of chalk, a forest in an inch of moss, and a drop of water resembling a busy hive of bees.
  • Stanza 2: The lens allows the speaker to watch a deft spider spinning web-silk from its spinnerets, highlighting its tigerish claws and the multi-eyed flies that stumble into its net.
  • Stanza 3: The speaker reflects on how the glass makes even the tiniest, most ordinary things look marvellous and surpass their natural appearance.
  • Stanza 4: The poem concludes with the idea that powerful lenses can bring celestial bodies like the moon so close that it feels as if one could walk there in an afternoon.

Literary Devices

  • Simile: "Like hive of bees" (comparing a drop of water full of microscopic life to a beehive).
  • Metaphor: "A forest—flowers and trees" (comparing an inch of moss to a vast forest).
  • Personification: "Magic talk" (giving the magnifying glass the human ability to speak).
  • Alliteration: "woven web-silk" (repetition of the 'w' sound).
  • Rhyme Scheme: ABCB pattern in quatrains.
Biography: Bibha Chowdhuri

Life and Achievements

Bibha Chowdhuri (1913–1991) was India's first woman physicist, pioneering research in high-energy particle physics and cosmic rays during an era when women faced immense barriers in STEM fields.

  • Academic Journey: Born in Kolkata, she later joined the University of Manchester in 1945, earning her Ph.D. under Nobel Laureate Patrick M.S. Blackett.
  • Scientific Contributions: She co-discovered the pi-meson (a subatomic particle) with D.M. Bose. Her remarkable work led to a Nobel Prize nomination in 1950 by Erwin Schrödinger.
  • Institutional Pioneer: She was the first woman faculty member at the Tata Institute of Fundamental Research (TIFR), handpicked by Homi J. Bhabha, and later worked at the Physical Research Laboratory (PRL) under Vikram Sarabhai.
  • Legacy: In 2019, the International Astronomical Union (IAU) honored her by renaming the star HD 86081 as Bibha (meaning "beam of light").

Key Vocabulary

  • trailblazers: Pioneers who pave the way for others.
  • persistence: The determination to keep doing something difficult.
  • sceptical: Doubtful or disbelieving.
  • tenacity: Gripping or holding fast; persistent determination.
Grammar & Language Study

Merged Words (Portmanteaus)

Words formed by combining parts of two distinct words:

  • intercom: internal + communication
  • brunch: breakfast + lunch
  • motel: motor + hotel
  • smog: smoke + fog
  • sitcom: situation + comedy
  • vlog: video + log
  • spork: spoon + fork
  • webinar: web + seminar

Future Time References

  • Will: Used for future occurrences, offers, or certainty (e.g., "I think our team will win.").
  • Simple Present: Used for regular schedules or planned future actions (e.g., "Our flight leaves at 10:00 a.m. tomorrow.").
  • Present Progressive: Used for personal arrangements and fixed plans (e.g., "We are moving into our new house next month.").
  • Going to: Used for intentions or predictions based on evidence (e.g., "The weather forecast says it is going to rain later today.").

Active vs Passive Voice

Passive voice is preferred when the action is more important than the doer, the doer is obvious, or the doer is unknown.

  • Active: "Early pioneers laid the foundation."
  • Passive: "The foundation was laid by early pioneers."

Word Clipping Methods

  • Back Clipping: Removing the end of a word (e.g., exam from examination, gym from gymnasium).
  • Front Clipping: Removing the front of a word (e.g., phone from telephone, net from internet).
  • Middle Clipping: Removing both front and end (e.g., flu from influenza, fridge from refrigerator).
