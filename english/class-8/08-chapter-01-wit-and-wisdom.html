Class 8 — Chapter 1: Unit 1: Wit and Wisdom

Prose: The Wit that Won Hearts

This story highlights the quick wit and intelligence of Tenali Ramakrishna, a legendary courtier and poet in the court of King Krishnadeva Raya of the Vijayanagara Empire.

Plot Summary

King Krishnadeva Raya and his queen, Thirumalambal, had a quarrel over a trivial matter. The king had written a poem filled with vague expressions and recited it to the queen late in the evening. Exhausted from a tiring day, the queen yawned several times during the recital. Taking this as a sign of disrespect, the king stormed off and stopped speaking to her for weeks, casting a gloomy shadow over the palace.

Distraught, the queen sought the help of the clever court poet, Tenali Rama. Rama devised a brilliant strategy to make the king realize his mistake without causing further offense.

Tenali Rama's Clever Strategy

The next morning in the bustling royal court, Tenali Rama presented a plateful of "revolutionary" paddy seeds. He claimed that if sown, they would yield three times more than regular seeds. However, he added a catch: the seeds would only grow if sown by someone who had never yawned in their life.

The king and courtiers found this condition absurd, noting that yawning is a natural bodily function that everyone does. Upon hearing this, Rama smiled and pointed out that if yawning is as natural as breathing, then the queen's yawning during the poem recital was also natural and not an act of disrespect. Realizing his foolishness and pride, the king regretted his harsh behavior, apologized to the queen, and rewarded Tenali Rama handsomely.

Poem: A Concrete Example

A humorous poem by Reginald Arkell that explores the eccentric gardening habits of a neighbor and ends with a witty, ironic twist.

Poem Analysis & Summary

The speaker describes his next-door neighbor, Mrs. Jones, who has a garden filled almost entirely with stones, featuring a crazy path, a lily pond, a rockery, and a strange sundial. Instead of typical large plants, she meticulously plants tiny, delicate flowers in the small crevices between the stones, so small that the speaker jokes she must plant them with a pin.

One day, Mrs. Jones invites the speaker to see her stones. They stand and discuss a specific, highly treasured flower for fifteen minutes. When the speaker finally asks where this lovely flower is, Mrs. Jones dryly replies, "You're standing on it."

Literary Devices

  • Irony (Situational Irony): The speaker spends fifteen minutes admiring and talking about a delicate flower, only to discover he has been crushing it under his feet the entire time.
  • Pun: The title "A Concrete Example" is a pun. Literally, it refers to the concrete, stones, and hard materials making up Mrs. Jones's garden. Symbolically, it represents a solid, clear example of her unique personality and gardening style.
  • Refrain: The repetition of the line "My next-door neighbour, Mrs. Jones" at the beginning of each stanza emphasizes her central role in the poem.
  • Alliteration: The repetition of consonant sounds in close proximity, such as "plants them with a pin" and "quite a quarter".
Drama: Wisdom Paves the Way

A play demonstrating how keen observation, logical reasoning, and wisdom can solve mysteries and earn high respect.

Scene I: The Road to Ujjain

Four young men—Ram Datt, Shiv Datt, Har Datt, and Dev Datt—are traveling to the city of Ujjain in search of employment. Along the dusty road, they notice fresh tracks of a camel and begin to closely scrutinize them. Shortly after, a distressed merchant approaches them, searching for his lost camel.

Without having seen the camel, the four men ask the merchant highly specific questions that describe the animal perfectly, leading the merchant to believe they have stolen it.

The Four Deductions

  • Ram Datt's Observation (Lame Leg): He noticed that only three footprints left deep impressions on the road, while the fourth was faint, indicating the camel had a limp.
  • Shiv Datt's Observation (Blind in Right Eye): He observed that the foliage on the left side of the road was nibbled, while the right side remained completely untouched, showing the camel could not see to its right.
  • Har Datt's Observation (Short Tail): He found small droplets of blood along the track, likely from mosquito bites. A camel with a long tail would have easily fended off these pests; hence, the camel had a short tail.
  • Dev Datt's Observation (Stomach Pain): He noticed the prints of the forefeet were deep and clear, while the hind feet impressions were faint. This indicated the camel was drawing up its hind legs to spare itself from pain in its belly.

Scene II: The King's Court

The indignant merchant drags the four men to the court of the young King of Ujjain, accusing them of theft. The King listens to the merchant's grievances and then asks the young men to explain how they knew these details. Upon hearing their logical, step-by-step deductions, the King is deeply impressed by their extraordinary intelligence. He dismisses the merchant's false accusations and appoints the four young men as his royal advisers, declaring their wisdom a beacon for his kingdom.

Grammar & Language Study

Compound Words

A compound word is formed when two or more words combine to create a new word with a distinct meaning. There are three types:

  • Closed Compounds: Written as a single word (e.g., sunlight, courtroom, bookstore).
  • Hyphenated Compounds: Connected by a hyphen (e.g., well-planned, quick-witted, soft-spoken, ill-tempered).
  • Open Compounds: Written as separate words (e.g., paddy seeds, dance hall, common sense).

Conditionals (If-Clauses)

Conditional sentences explore a condition and its consequence. They consist of a subordinate clause (if-clause) and a main clause.

  • Type 1: Real / Possible Future
    • Structure: Simple Present + Present Modal (will/can/may)
    • Example: If you like, I will try my best to resolve this.
  • Type 2: Hypothetical / Imaginary Situation
    • Structure: Simple Past + Past Modal (would/could/might)
    • Example: If something were to go wrong, our farmers would suffer.
  • Type 3: Unreal Past (Impossible to change now)
    • Structure: Past Perfect + would have + Past Participle
    • Example: If I had known this would happen, I would have chosen another day.

Modal Verbs and Functions

Modal verbs express the speaker's attitude or the function of the action:

  • will: Willingness or prediction (e.g., "I will gladly pay your fee.")
  • must: Logical conclusion or necessity (e.g., "You must have stolen it.")
  • shall: Command or determination (e.g., "You shall come with me!")
  • could: Ability or expression of disbelief (e.g., "How could you possibly know?")
  • would: Imagined possibility or prediction (e.g., "A long tail would have fended off pests.")
  • may / might: Possibility or wish (e.g., "May your wisdom shine upon Ujjain.")
  • ought to / should: Moral obligation or advice (e.g., "We ought to be united.")
  • need to: Necessity (e.g., "We need to devise a strategy.")
  • dare to: Challenge or boldness (e.g., "Yet you dare to claim not to have seen it!")

Intonation & Stress

Intonation is the rise and fall of pitch in spoken language, which helps convey meaning and emotion:

  • Rising Tone: Used at the end of 'Yes' or 'No' questions (e.g., Are you coming home? ↗).
  • Falling Tone: Used at the end of '-Wh' questions, statements, and exclamations (e.g., Where do you live? ↘, Yes, yes, it is! ↘).

Sentence Stress: Changing the stressed word in a sentence alters its implied meaning:

  • I take my dog for a walk. (Emphasizes that I do it, not someone else.)
  • I take my dog for a walk. (Emphasizes that I take my dog, not another pet.)
  • I take my dog for a walk. (Emphasizes the activity is a walk, not a run.)
