Class 4 — Chapter 9: Hekko

Chapter Overview & Origin

The chapter introduces Hekko, a traditional game and folk story from Nagaland, India.

Etymology and Legend

  • Hek: Means to challenge.
  • Ko: Means tiger.
  • Hekko: Translates to Challenging the tiger.
  • The Folk Story: Originates from Nagaland, depicting a disagreement over superiority between Aami (The Man) and Aakho (The Tiger). Humans challenged the tiger, and this challenge became known as Hekko.
The Game of Hekko

Today, Hekko is a well-known team sport played in the Mokokchung district of Nagaland to identify the strongest people in the village.

Game Setup & Players

  • Teams: Two teams named Aami (Humans) and Aakho (Tigers).
  • Players: 11 players in each team.
  • The Toss: Decides which team plays as Aami and which plays as Aakho.

Rules of Play

  • A circle is drawn on the ground.
  • The Aami team must stay inside the circle, forming a human chain.
  • The Aakho team starts outside the circle.
  • Two players from the Aakho team enter the circle to push Aami players out. Pushed-out players are eliminated.
  • The Aami team tries to surround and catch the Aakho players. A caught Aakho player is only released when they say Joko (surrender).
  • Points are gained whenever a team gets an opponent out.

End of the Game & Titles

The game ends when one of the following occurs:

  • All members of the Aakho team are caught.
  • All members of the Aami team are pushed out of the circle.
  • Time runs out (the team with the maximum points wins).

Special titles are awarded to outstanding players:

  • Khozuba: Title given to the fastest player of the Aami team.
  • Khotiba: Title given to the strongest player of the Aakho team.

Traditional Games of India

The chapter highlights several traditional Indian games:

  • Lagori / Seven Stones: Players knock down a stack of seven stones with a ball and try to rebuild it before being hit by the opponent's ball.
  • Kabaddi: A team game where a raider crosses into the opponent's half chanting "kabaddi" without taking a breath, tagging opponents and returning safely.
  • Gilli-danda: Played with two wooden sticks (a short one and a long one) where the short stick is struck into the air and hit far away.
  • London Bridge / Arch Game: Two children form an arch with their arms while others walk under it singing a song; the caught player joins the arch.
  • Kancha / Marbles: Children use a marble to hit and knock other marbles out of a circle.
