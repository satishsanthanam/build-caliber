Class 4 — Chapter 2: The Tinkling Bells

Story Summary

The Tinkling Bells is a heartwarming story about a young boy named Chinna who lives in a village and studies in grade 4. He loves playing with his five-month-old pet kid (baby goat) named Tara.

The Missing Money

On his way home from school, Chinna sees a pair of beautiful, shiny tinkling bells at Chacha's shop. They cost ten rupees. When he reaches into his pocket, he realizes the money his grandfather (Dadaji) gave him is missing. Feeling sad, he returns the bells and goes home empty-handed.

A Mother's Comfort

Chinna's mother, Kamala, comforts him and promises to give him money for the bells, advising him to be more careful in the future. She then takes him to the market to buy fruits and vegetables.

The Test of Honesty

At a pineapple stall, Kamala buys two pineapples for 45 rupees each (totaling 90 rupees) and hands the seller a 100-rupee note. She asks Chinna to collect the change. By mistake, the fruit seller gives Chinna two ten-rupee notes (20 rupees change instead of 10). Chinna is tempted to keep the extra money to buy the bells and chocolates. However, his mother explains that keeping it is stealing and asks him to reflect on how he felt when he lost his own money. Realizing his mistake, Chinna returns the extra money, earning praise from the fruit seller for his honesty.

A Happy Ending

Pleased with his honesty, Kamala takes Chinna to Chacha's shop to buy the tinkling bells. Chinna happily ties the bells around his pet goat Tara's neck.

Key Themes & Moral Lessons
  • Honesty: The story highlights that honesty is always rewarded with peace of mind and respect from others.
  • Empathy: Chinna learns to put himself in the fruit seller's shoes, understanding how his actions affect others.
  • Responsibility: Learning to take care of money and personal belongings.
Grammar & Vocabulary

Degrees of Comparison (Adjectives)

Adjectives change form to compare qualities of people or things:

  • Comparative Degree (-er): Used to compare two people or objects. Always followed by 'than'.
    Example: Charu is taller than Chinna.
  • Superlative Degree (-est): Used when comparing a person or object within a whole group.
    Example: Jasmine is the tallest person in the group.

Opposites (Antonyms)

  • Beautiful × Ugly
  • Reward × Punishment
  • Buy × Sell
  • Whispered × Shouted
Activities & Exercises

Let us Think (Comprehension)

  • Why was Chinna upset? He lost the ten rupees given by his grandfather and couldn't buy the bells.
  • How did Kamala help Chinna feel better? She comforted him, wiped his tears, and promised to give him money for the bells.
  • What did the fruit seller do by mistake? He gave Chinna ten rupees extra in change.
  • How did Chinna manage to buy the bells in the end? After returning the extra money, his mother happily bought the bells for him.

Let us Do (Bell Craft)

Students can create their own bell craft using recyclable items like metal bottle caps or tin cans, string, cardboard, pebbles/beads, aluminum foil, and glue.
