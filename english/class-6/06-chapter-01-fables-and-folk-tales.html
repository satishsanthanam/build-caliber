Class 6 — Chapter 1: Fables and Folk Tales

Unit Overview: Fables and Folk Tales

Unit 1 introduces students to the rich tradition of fables and folk tales. Through three distinct narratives—a prose story, a classic poem, and a comic strip—students explore themes of hard work, the dangers of flattery, and clever problem-solving, alongside foundational grammar and vocabulary exercises.

Story 1: A Bottle of Dew

Written by Sudha Murty, this story highlights the value of hard work over the pursuit of quick wealth through magic.

Character Analysis

  • Rama Natha: The lazy son of a rich landlord who wastes his time and wealth searching for a magic potion to turn objects into gold.
  • Madhumati: Rama Natha's hardworking and wise wife who worries about their dwindling finances.
  • Sage Mahipati: A wise sage who cleverly channels Rama Natha's obsession into productive agricultural labor.

Plot Summary

Rama Natha is obsessed with finding a magic potion. Sage Mahipati tells him that to create it, he must plant banana trees and collect five litres of winter dew with his own hands. Rama Natha spends six years working tirelessly on his fields to collect the dew. Meanwhile, Madhumati harvests and sells the bananas, accumulating a fortune. When the dew fails to turn copper to gold, the sage reveals that Rama Natha's hard work, which created the plantation and the wealth, is the true magic potion.

Vocabulary & Key Terms

  • large tracts of land: Large areas of land.
  • potion: A liquid with magical properties.
  • give up: To stop trying.
  • dew: Small drops of water that form on leaves overnight.
  • chant: To repeat or sing a word or phrase.
  • tended: Looked after or cared for.
  • muttered: Spoke in a low, quiet voice.
  • precious: Highly valuable.
  • wisdom: Deep understanding and good judgment.
Poem: The Raven and the Fox

A classic poem by Jean de La Fontaine that warns against the dangers of vanity and flattery.

Stanza Breakdown & Plot

  • Stanza 1: Mr. Raven sits on a tree branch holding a piece of food (morsel) in his beak. Reynard the Fox spots him and wants the food.
  • Stanza 2: The Fox flatters the Raven, praising his beauty and feathers, and suggests that if his voice is as beautiful as his looks, he would be the King of the woods.
  • Stanza 3: Overcome with foolish pride, the Raven opens his beak to sing, forgetting his voice is just a croak. The food falls down.
  • Stanza 4: The Fox laughs, eats the food, and advises the Raven to ignore sweet words of flattery, noting that pride is unwise.

Literary Devices

  • Alliteration: The repetition of consonant sounds at the beginning of words (e.g., "big beak", "song of sweet").
  • Rhyme Scheme & Rhyming Pairs: limb-him, beak-seek, word-bird, sing-King, joke-croak, pride-eyed, know-glow, unwise-surprise.

Vocabulary

  • perched: Sat on a branch.
  • morsel: A small piece of food.
  • seek: To look for something.
  • pride: A feeling of being better than others.
  • eyed: Looked at with interest.
  • limb: A branch of a tree.
  • woods: A small forest area.
Story 2: Rama to the Rescue

An entertaining folktale from Tamil Nadu presented in a comic strip format by Amar Chitra Katha.

Plot & Clever Strategy

Rama, the village kotwal (policeman), and his wife realize a thief is trying to break into their house. Instead of panicking, they play a clever game. They talk loudly about naming their future child "Rama" and how they will call out his name softly, loudly, or very loudly depending on where he is (house, yard, or street). The thief waits outside, listening to their chatter. When they scream "Rama! Rama!" very loudly, the actual village kotwal (who is also named Rama) hears his name, runs to the house, and arrests the thief.

Vocabulary

  • kotwal: A village policeman (also called thaanedar).
  • rescue: To save someone from danger.
  • wonder: A feeling of surprise.
  • yard: An area outside a building.
  • chatter: Talking for a long time about unimportant things.
  • instead: In place of someone or something.
  • closely: Carefully.
  • perhaps: Maybe.
Grammar & Language Study

Homophones

Homophones are words that sound the same but have different spellings and meanings.

  • I / eye
  • your / you're (short form of 'you are')
  • dew / due (happening in the future)
  • son / sun
  • one / won (past tense of win)
  • ate / eight
  • see / sea

Present Progressive Tense

Used to describe actions happening at the exact moment of speaking. Structure: [am/is/are + verb + -ing].

  • Example: "I am feeling so sleepy."
  • Example: "Somebody is trying to get in."
  • Example: "They are saying something."

Adverbs

Adverbs provide more information about a verb, such as how, when, or where an action is performed.

  • How does he listen? He listens closely.
  • How does she call out? She calls out softly / loudly.
  • Other examples: warmly, gracefully, smoothly, beautifully, funnily, quickly, hurriedly, tirelessly, slowly, peacefully.

Contractions & Apostrophes

An apostrophe (’) is used to show missing letters in contracted words, which are common in spoken English.

  • I'm (I am)
  • It's (It is)
  • They're (They are)
  • What's (What is)
  • I'll (I will)
  • Don't (Do not)
  • Must've (Must have)
