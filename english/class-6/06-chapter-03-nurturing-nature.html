Class 6 — Chapter 3: Nurturing Nature

Unit Overview: Nurturing Nature

This unit explores the deep connection between humans and nature, highlighting the medicinal, ecological, and nurturing aspects of trees, birds, and spices through three distinct sections: Neem Baba, What a Bird Thought, and Spices that Heal Us.

Section 1: Neem Baba

Story Summary & Dialogue

A young girl named Amber talks to an ancient neem tree in her courtyard, whom she addresses as Neem Baba. Through their conversation, Amber learns about the history, global presence, and immense medicinal value of the neem tree.

  • Origin: Born millions of years ago in North India or Myanmar.
  • Global Presence: Found in many Asian and African countries, and parts of America.
  • Sanskrit Names: Arishta (meaning "the one who cures diseases"), Nimba, and Nimbaca.
  • Other Names: Given the name 'Neem' by Iranians. In India, it is also known as Nim, Leemba, Nimori, Nimbamond, Nimbe, and Nimo.
  • Scientific Names/Titles: Scientists call it the bitter grace of God, nature's gift to man, cleanliness-parting tree, magic tree, and the tree of the twentieth century. In Latin, its scientific name is Azadirachta indica (derived from the Persian name Azad Darakhte Hindi).

Medicinal & Practical Uses of Neem

  • Leaves: Have chemical properties that destroy germs. Used to treat measles, soothe itching, and prevent germs from breeding. Also used to protect stored clothes.
  • Twigs: Used as natural toothbrushes to clean teeth.
  • Bark: Used to make medicines for coughs and breathing difficulties.
  • Flowers: Used to prepare kajal to cure eye itching.
  • Seeds: Powdered and mixed with water to spray on plants to repel locusts, stop mosquito breeding in stagnant water, and protect crops from termites.
  • Wood: Termite-resistant, making it excellent for furniture.
  • Oil: Used in making soaps and toothpaste.
  • Air Purification: Neem trees naturally purify the surrounding air.

Vocabulary & Key Terms

  • thought-provoking: interesting, making you think seriously.
  • grace: blessing.
  • twigs: small, thin branches of a tree.
  • measles: an infectious disease causing fever and red spots on the skin.
  • itching: an uncomfortable feeling on the skin that makes you want to scratch.
  • breed: to produce offspring or grow in number (e.g., germs or insects).
  • locusts: large, flying insects that travel in swarms and destroy crops.
  • termites: small insects that eat and damage wood and buildings.
  • stagnant: water that is not flowing or moving, often becoming dirty.
  • cost-effective: producing good results without spending a lot of money or resources.
  • purifies: removes harmful substances or contaminants.
  • personification: a figure of speech where non-human things are given human qualities (e.g., "The leaves whispered in the wind").
Section 2: What a Bird Thought (Poem)

Poem Text

I lived first in a little house,
And lived there very well,
I thought the world was small and round,
And made of pale, blue shell.

I lived next in a little nest,
Nor needed any other,
I thought the world was made of straw,
And nestled by my mother.

One day, I fluttered from my nest,
To see what I could find,
I said the world is made of leaves,
I have been very blind.

At length I flew beyond the tree,
Quite fit for grown-up labours,
I don’t know how the world is made,
And neither do my neighbours.

— Anonymous

Stanza-by-Stanza Analysis

  • Stanza 1 (The Egg): The bird's first home is a small, round, pale blue eggshell. To the unborn baby bird, this shell is the entire world.
  • Stanza 2 (The Nest): After hatching, the bird lives in a cozy nest made of straw, cared for by its mother. It believes the world is made entirely of straw.
  • Stanza 3 (The Tree): As it grows, the bird flutters out of the nest onto the branches of the tree. Seeing the green canopy, it concludes that the world is made of leaves.
  • Stanza 4 (The Open Sky): Finally, the fully grown bird flies beyond the tree into the vast sky. It realizes the world is far larger and more mysterious than it ever imagined, and admits that neither it nor its neighbours truly know how the world is made.

Vocabulary & Literary Devices

  • pale: light in color.
  • nestled: settled comfortably and warmly close to someone.
  • fluttered: flew unsteadily or moved wings quickly and lightly.
  • at length: after a long time; finally.
  • labours: hard work or tasks.
  • Rhyme Scheme: The poem follows an AABB rhyme scheme in each stanza (e.g., well/shell, other/mother, find/blind, labours/neighbours).
Section 3: Spices that Heal Us

Summary of the Letter

A letter written by Daadi to her grandchildren, Vikram and Vaibhavi, explaining the healing and medicinal properties of common kitchen spices used in traditional home remedies.

Healing Properties of Spices

  • Haldi (Turmeric): Improves energy levels, aids digestion, and reduces body pain.
  • Methi (Fenugreek): Helps control blood sugar levels and body weight when soaked overnight and consumed with water in the morning.
  • Jeera (Cumin): Soaked overnight to improve digestion and cure sleeplessness.
  • Heeng (Asafoetida): Applied with water on the tummy to relieve gas in babies; also helps control coughs and colds.
  • Dalchini (Cinnamon) & Laung (Clove): Provide temporary relief from toothaches.
  • Adrak (Ginger): Used for over 4000 years; cures coughs, colds, and provides pain relief.
  • Kali Mirch (Black Pepper): Aids digestion and relieves body pain.
  • Saunf (Fennel seeds) & Ajwain (Carom seeds): Served at the end of meals to assist in digestion.
  • Elaichi (Cardamom): Helps with digestion, respiratory issues, and cures bad breath.

Grammar Focus: Modal Verbs

The text introduces modal verbs (helping verbs) and their specific functions:

  • May: Used for permission or possibility (e.g., "You may share them with your friends.").
  • Should: Used for advice or suggestion (e.g., "You should try to remember these cures.").
  • Can: Used to express ability or possibility (e.g., "It can help control cough and cold.").
  • Must: Used to express strong obligation or compulsion (e.g., "You must consult an elder before you use them.").
  • Need to: Expresses necessity (e.g., "I need to soak the methi seeds overnight.").
  • Used to: Expresses a past habit (e.g., "I used to put heeng water on your tummy.").
