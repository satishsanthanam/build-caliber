Class 103 — Chapter 3: Two Stories about Flying

Overview & Let's Begin

This unit explores themes of flight, courage, and the interpretation of rules through literary texts and language exercises.

His First Flight

A story about a young seagull overcoming his fear of flying. Key learning points include:

  • The seagull's initial fear that his wings would not support him.
  • The family's attempts to encourage him through taunting and leaving him hungry.
  • How hunger ultimately compelled him to dive for food and discover his ability to fly.

The Black Aeroplane

A mystery story about a pilot lost in a storm who is guided to safety by a mysterious black aeroplane, which disappears without a trace once he lands safely.

Reading Comprehension: All about a Dog

A narrative by A.G. Gardiner highlighting the conflict between the strict letter of the law and its humane spirit.

Plot Summary

On a bitterly cold night, a bus conductor objects to a passenger carrying a small Pekinese dog inside the bus. Despite protests from other passengers and the freezing weather, the conductor insists on the rule that dogs are not allowed inside. The bus is halted, causing immense discomfort to everyone. Eventually, the lady passenger complies and goes to the top of the bus. When the engine breaks down, she temporarily returns inside, restarting the conflict once the engine is fixed. At the end of the journey, the narrator discusses with the conductor the difference between hard-and-fast rules (like rules of the road) and rules of guidance meant for passenger comfort.

Key Vocabulary

  • bitterly: extremely cold or harsh.
  • embittered: expressing anger or resentment.
  • evidently: plainly or obviously.
  • malice: the intention or desire to do evil; ill will.
  • imperturbably: calmly, without getting excited or upset.
  • indignant: feeling or showing anger at what is perceived as unfair treatment.
  • imperiously: arrogantly or domineeringly.
Vocabulary & Word Study

Adverbs

Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Examples of converting adjectives to adverbs:

  • Angry → Angrily
  • Vague → Vaguely
  • Indignant → Indignantly
  • Threatening → Threateningly
  • Hurry → Hurriedly

Prefixes for Antonyms

Prefixes like im-, il-, and in- are added to base words to create antonyms:

  • im-: mobile → immobile, movable → immovable, patient → impatient, perfect → imperfect
  • il-: logical → illogical, legal → illegal, literate → illiterate
  • in-: secure → insecure, active → inactive, direct → indirect
Grammar

Phrasal Verbs

A phrasal verb is a combination of a verb and a preposition or adverb that creates a new meaning.

  • walk in: to enter a place.
  • walk out: to leave suddenly, often in protest.
  • take away: to remove something.
  • take up: to begin a new activity or occupy space/time.
  • take in: to comprehend, or to deceive/cheat.
  • take out: to remove or extract.
  • give away: to donate or reveal a secret.
  • give in: to yield or surrender.
  • give up: to stop trying or abandon a habit.
  • put in: to insert or spend time/effort.
  • put away: to store or discard.
  • put out: to extinguish (e.g., fire) or cause inconvenience.

The Passive Voice

The passive voice is used when the focus is on the action or the recipient of the action rather than the doer.

  • Active: Ramesh painted the walls.
  • Passive: The walls were painted by Ramesh.

Practice Examples:

  • Taj Mahal / build / Shah Jahan (Simple Past): The Taj Mahal was built by Shah Jahan.
  • Malaria virus / transmit / female Anopheles mosquito (Simple Present): Malaria virus is transmitted by the female Anopheles mosquito.
  • The case / solve / police and two persons arrest (Present Perfect): The case has been solved by the police and two persons have been arrested.

Sentence Unscrambling (Amelia Earhart)

Reconstructing scrambled sentences to form a coherent biographical passage about the famous aviator:

  • Amelia Earhart was an American aviator who set many flying records.
  • She became the first woman to fly solo across the Atlantic Ocean.
  • During a flight in July 1937 to circumnavigate the globe, Earhart disappeared somewhere over the Pacific.
  • Her plane wreckage was never found and she was officially declared lost at sea.
  • Her disappearance remains one of the greatest unsolved mysteries of the twentieth century.
Language Skills & Writing

Editing & Punctuation

Focuses on correcting capitalization, commas, and full stops in a paragraph about Unidentified Flying Objects (UFOs) to improve readability and grammatical accuracy.

Listening & Lateral Thinking Riddles

  • Riddle 1 (River Crossing): A farmer must transport a goat, a cabbage, and a wolf across a river in a small boat that can only hold him and one item at a time, ensuring the goat is never left alone with the cabbage, and the wolf is never left alone with the goat.
  • Riddle 2 (Humans and Monkeys): A complex logic puzzle involving three humans and three monkeys crossing a river under strict safety constraints.

Writing Tasks

  • Article Writing: Discussing the statement "Rules are meant to be observed in spirit not only in letter" across various contexts such as gender rights, environmental protection, public discipline, and traffic rules.
  • Story Writing: Developing a narrative based on personal experiences of overcoming difficulties or learning new skills (e.g., learning to ride a bicycle).
