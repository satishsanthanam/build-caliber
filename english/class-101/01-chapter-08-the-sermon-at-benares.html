Class 101 — Chapter 8: The Sermon at Benares

Introduction to Gautama Buddha

Early Life and Renunciation

Born as Prince Siddhartha Gautama (563 B.C. – 483 B.C.) in northern India, he was shielded from the world's sufferings. At twelve, he was sent for schooling in Hindu sacred scriptures, returning four years later to marry a princess. They lived in royal comfort for ten years and had a son.

The Four Sights

At age twenty-five, while out hunting, the Prince chanced upon four sights that changed his life: a sick man, an aged man, a funeral procession, and a monk begging for alms. Deeply moved by these sights of suffering, he renounced his royal life to seek enlightenment concerning the world's sorrows.

Attaining Enlightenment

He wandered for seven years and finally sat under a peepal tree, vowing to stay until he found truth. After seven days, he attained enlightenment and renamed the tree the Bodhi Tree (Tree of Wisdom). He became known as the Buddha (the Awakened or the Enlightened) and preached his first sermon at Benares, a holy city on the River Ganges.

The Story of Kisa Gotami

The Loss of her Son

Kisa Gotami's only son died. In her extreme grief, she carried the dead child to her neighbors, begging for medicine to bring him back to life. The neighbors pitied her but realized she had lost her senses, as the boy was dead.

The Mustard Seed Trial

A man directed her to the Buddha. She went to him crying, "Lord and Master, give me the medicine that will cure my boy." The Buddha agreed, asking for a handful of mustard seed, but with one condition: it must be procured from a house where no one has lost a child, husband, parent, or friend.

The Realization

Kisa Gotami went from house to house. People were willing to give her mustard seeds, but whenever she asked if anyone had died in their family, they replied, "Alas! the living are few, but the dead are many." Unable to find a single household untouched by death, she sat weary by the wayside, watching the city lights flicker and extinguish. She realized that death is common to all, and she had been selfish in her grief.

The Buddha's Sermon on Mortality

The Nature of Mortal Life

The Buddha taught that the life of mortals in this world is troubled, brief, and combined with pain. There is no way to avoid death. He used two powerful analogies:

  • Ripe Fruits: Just as ripe fruits are in early danger of falling, mortals are always in danger of death.
  • Earthen Vessels: Just as all earthen vessels made by a potter end in being broken, so is the life of mortals.

Overcoming Grief

The Buddha explained that neither weeping nor grieving can bring peace of mind; instead, it increases pain and damages the body. The dead cannot be saved by lamentation. To obtain peace of mind, one must draw out the arrow of lamentation, complaint, and grief, and become composed to overcome all sorrow.

Supplementary Readings on Grief

Feelings of Grief

Grief is a natural response to loss, characterized by various emotions:

  • Sadness: The most common emotion, not always shown through crying.
  • Anger: Frustration over being unable to prevent death.
  • Guilt and Self-reproach: Regret over past actions or perceived shortcomings.
  • Anxiety: Fear of the future and self-care.
  • Loneliness: Constant reminders of the missing loved one.

Good Grief by Amitai Etzioni

The author shares his personal experience of losing his wife and son. He emphasizes that there is no "right" or systematic way to grieve, and that focusing on what survivors can do for one another is the ultimate source of consolation.

Poem: For Anne Gregory

Poem Text

Stanza 1:
"Never shall a young man,
Thrown into despair
By those great honey-coloured
Ramparts at your ear,
Love you for yourself alone
And not your yellow hair."

Stanza 2:
"But I can get a hair-dye
And set such colour there,
Brown, or black, or carrot,
That young men in despair
May love me for myself alone
And not my yellow hair."

Stanza 3:
"I heard an old religious man
But yesternight declare
That he had found a text to prove
That only God, my dear,
Could love you for yourself alone
And not your yellow hair."

Themes and Analysis

  • Physical vs. Inner Beauty: The poem explores the idea that humans are easily attracted to external physical traits (like Anne's beautiful yellow hair, compared to ramparts), making it difficult to love someone purely for who they are inside.
  • Divine Love: The final stanza suggests that only God is capable of loving a person unconditionally for their true self, free from physical biases.
Glossary & Key Terms
  • Chanced upon: Came across by chance.
  • Enlightenment: A state of high spiritual knowledge.
  • Sermon: A religious or moral talk.
  • Dipping places: Bathing places (specifically on the River Ganges).
  • Inscrutable: Something which cannot be understood.
  • Repaired: Went to (stylistic/archaic use).
  • Valley of desolation: An area filled with deep sorrow.
  • Mortals: Those bound to die.
  • Afflicted with: Affected by suffering, disease, or pain.
  • Lamentation: Expression of sorrow.
  • Ramparts: High, wide walls around a castle or fort (used metaphorically for hair framing the ear).
