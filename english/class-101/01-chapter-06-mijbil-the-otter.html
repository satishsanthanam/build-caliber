Class 101 — Chapter 6: Mijbil the Otter

Introduction & Background

Gavin Maxwell, living in Camusfearna, Scotland, decides to keep an otter as a pet instead of a dog after his dog Jonnie passes away. The chapter explores the joys, challenges, and deep bond formed with this unusual pet.

Pet Ownership & Responsibility

  • SPCA Guidelines: Owning a pet is a lifetime commitment requiring family consensus, proper care, vaccinations, toilet training, and socialization.
  • Environmental Comparison: Contrast between the marshes of Basra (wet, hot/cool, flooded) and London (temperate, busy, multi-storeyed with parks).
Narrative Breakdown

Part I: The Arrival of Mijbil

In 1956, Maxwell travels to Southern Iraq. While waiting for his mail in Basra, he receives an otter as a gift from a friend, brought by two Arabs in a squirming sack.

Part II: Characteristics & Behavior

  • Appearance: Resembled a small, medievally-conceived dragon with symmetrical mud-armour scales, later classified as Lutrogale perspicillata maxwelli (Maxwell's otter).
  • Water Obsession: Mijbil goes wild with joy in water, splashing in the bathtub, and learns to turn on the chromium taps with his paws.
  • Playfulness: He invents games with a rubber ball, juggles marbles on his belly, and plays with a terrapin shell.

Part III: The Journey to London

  • The Box Incident: To fly, Mijbil must be kept in an 18-inch square box. He tears the inner lining, injuring himself and causing blood to trickle out.
  • Flight Chaos: Released in the aircraft, Mijbil escapes, causing panic among passengers who mistake him for a rat. A kind air hostess helps calm the situation, and Mijbil eventually returns to Maxwell's lap.

Part IV: Life in London

  • Compulsive Habits: On walks, Mijbil insists on stepping in the center of paving blocks, touching every seventh railing, and galloping along a school's low wall.
  • Public Curiosity: Londoners, unfamiliar with otters, make wild guesses, calling him a baby seal, squirrel, walrus, hippo, beaver, bear cub, leopard, or even a brontosaur.
Vocabulary & Glossary
  • Thraldom: Being under the control of (an otter fixation).
  • Apathy: Absence of interest or enthusiasm.
  • Fumbling: Trying to do something in a clumsy manner.
  • Appalling spectacle: A shocking or horrific scene.
  • Nuzzle: To rub gently with the nose.
  • Compulsive habits: Habits that are impossible to control.
Grammar & Language Study

Describing Repeated Actions in the Past

We use would or used to to describe repeated past actions. However, for past states or situations, only used to is correct.

  • Action: "Mij would follow me without a lead." / "He used to follow me."
  • State/Situation: "We used to believe there were ghosts." (Incorrect: "We would believe...")

Noun Modifiers

Nouns can act as modifiers to describe other nouns (e.g., the London streets, the Tigris marshes, iron railings).

Quantifying Phrases (Collocations)

  • A pool of blood
  • A cloud of dust
  • Flakes of snow
  • A gust of wind
Poem Study: "Fog" by Carl Sandburg

Poem Text

The fog comes
on little cat feet.

It sits looking
over harbour and city
on silent haunches
and then moves on.

Analysis & Metaphor

  • Extended Metaphor: The poet compares the fog to a cat.
  • Points of Comparison:
    • Arrives silently ("on little cat feet").
    • Sits quietly observing ("on silent haunches").
    • Departs stealthily ("and then moves on").
  • Form: Written in free verse (no regular rhyme scheme or rhythm).
