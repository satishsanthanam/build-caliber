Class 5 — Chapter 2: Gone with the Scooter

Story Summary

Gone with the Scooter is a cheerful story about a young boy named Gopi and his friends. On a hot summer afternoon, Gopi hears a rustling sound and a thud in his garden. Upon searching, he finds a white hockey ball hidden under a heap of dry leaves. Determined to find its rightful owner, Gopi takes the ball to the local playground in the evening where his friends gather to play hockey.

Key Events

  • The Discovery: Gopi finds a white hockey ball in his garden and decides not to keep it for himself.
  • The Inquiry: At the playground, Gopi asks his friends if anyone lost a ball. Manoj mentions losing one four months ago, and Ramani tries to claim it without knowing its color.
  • The Game: The children begin playing hockey. Gopi passes to Deepak, who drives it to Ramani. Ramani dribbles and passes to Jay.
  • The Climax: Jay hits the ball so hard that it flies over the gate and lands directly into the basket of a passing scooter. The unaware scooterist drives away with the ball, leaving the children laughing at the unexpected turn of events.

Characters

  • Gopi: An honest and active boy who finds the ball and seeks its owner.
  • Manoj: Gopi's friend who plays as the goalkeeper.
  • Ramani: A playful friend who tries to claim the ball.
  • Deepak: A friend who boasts about his new football.
  • Jay: The powerful hitter who accidentally sends the ball into the scooter's basket.
Vocabulary & Word Meanings

Learn the key vocabulary words introduced in this chapter:

  • Dribble: Moving a ball with small taps while playing hockey or football.
  • Rustling: A soft crackling sound, like leaves moving in the wind.
  • Grab: To take something with a sudden, quick movement.
  • Boast: To speak too proudly about oneself or one's possessions.
  • Unaware: Not knowing or realizing something.
  • Thud: A dull, heavy sound made by a heavy object falling.
Grammar & Language Study

Conjunctions

Conjunctions are linking words used to connect sentences or clauses. Use the following words to complete stories or sentences:

  • since
  • however
  • because
  • when

Adverbs of Frequency vs. Adverbs of Time

Adverbs modify verbs by telling us how often or when an action happens.

  • Adverbs of Frequency (How many times): Words like usually, often, regularly, always, and never.
  • Adverbs of Time (When): Words like today, immediately, yesterday, and now.

Compound Words

Compound words are formed by joining two distinct words together to create a new meaning:

  • sun + rise = sunrise
  • sun + set = sunset
  • sun + shine = sunshine
  • sun + flower = sunflower
  • foot + ball = football
  • base + ball = baseball
  • volley + ball = volleyball
  • basket + ball = basketball

Phonics: Long vs. Short 'oo' Sounds

Though words may contain the double 'o' spelling, their pronunciation can differ:

  • Short 'oo' Sound: Hood, Foot, Shook, Look, Book, Took.
  • Long 'oo' Sound: School, Maroon, Scooter, Bloom, Food, Moon.
Activities & Exercises

Let us Think (Comprehension)

  1. What was Gopi doing on the veranda? (Reading a book)
  2. What sound did Gopi hear before going to the garden? (A rustling sound and a thud)
  3. Describe Gopi's search: He searched the thick grass near the fence, looked among the bushes, and finally found the ball on a heap of dry leaves.

Let us Do: Tigers and Goats Board Game

A traditional strategy board game played with pebbles on a drawn grid:

  • Requires at least two players: one representing 'Tigers' (3 big pebbles) and one representing 'Goats' (13 smaller pebbles).
  • The Tiger's objective is to 'capture' (jump over) 5 goats.
  • The Goat's objective is to surround and trap all 3 tigers so they cannot move.
