Class 10 (First Flight) — Chapter 4: From the Diary of Anne Frank

Chapter Overview & Historical Context

Anneliese Marie Anne Frank (12 June 1929 – February/March 1945) was a German-born Jewish girl who wrote her famous diary while hiding from the Nazis in Amsterdam during World War II. After her family was betrayed and sent to concentration camps, only her father, Otto Frank, survived. He published her diary as The Diary of a Young Girl.

Key Historical Facts

  • Hiding Period: July 1942 to August 1944 in hidden rooms of Otto Frank's office building.
  • Betrayal: After two years, the group was betrayed and sent to concentration camps. Anne died of typhus in Bergen-Belsen.
  • The Diary: Given to Anne for her 13th birthday, it chronicles her life from 12 June 1942 to 1 August 1944.
Prose Summary: From the Diary of Anne Frank

Why Anne Keeps a Diary

Anne feels that paper has more patience than people. Despite having loving parents, a sister, and about thirty friends, she lacks a true friend to whom she can confide her innermost feelings. She names her diary Kitty to treat it as this long-awaited friend.

Anne's Life Sketch

Anne provides a brief background: her parents married when her father was 36 and mother 25. Her sister Margot was born in 1926, and Anne in 1929. They emigrated to Holland in 1933. Anne attended the Montessori nursery school. Her grandmother fell ill in 1941 and died in January 1942, leaving a deep impact on Anne.

The Classroom Anecdote

On 20 June 1942, Anne's class is anxious about who will be promoted. Anne is confident except about maths. Her maths teacher, Mr. Keesing, is annoyed by her constant talking and assigns her extra homework as punishment: essays on chatterboxes.

  • First Essay: "A Chatterbox" - Anne argues that talking is a student's trait and inherited from her mother.
  • Second Essay: "An Incorrigible Chatterbox" - Assigned after she continues talking.
  • Third Essay: "Quack, Quack, Quack, Said Mistress Chatterbox" - Anne writes a beautiful poem about a mother duck and father swan who bites his ducklings to death for quacking too much. Mr. Keesing enjoys the joke and stops punishing her.
Poem Analysis: Amanda! by Robin Klein

The poem depicts the tension between a nagging parent and a young girl, Amanda, who escapes into her own vivid imagination to find freedom.

Stanza-by-Stanza Breakdown

  • Stanza 1 & 2: The parent commands Amanda not to bite her nails or slouch. Amanda imagines herself as a sole mermaid drifting blissfully in a languid, emerald sea.
  • Stanza 3 & 4: The parent asks if Amanda finished her homework, tidied her room, and cleaned her shoes. Amanda imagines herself as an orphan roaming the street, enjoying golden silence and sweet freedom.
  • Stanza 5 & 6: The parent warns Amanda not to eat chocolate due to her acne. Amanda imagines herself as Rapunzel, living a tranquil life in a tower, promising never to let down her hair.
  • Stanza 7: The parent demands Amanda stop sulking and being moody, fearing others will think she is being nagged.
