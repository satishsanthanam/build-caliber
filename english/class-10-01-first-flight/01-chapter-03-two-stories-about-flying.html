Class 10 (First Flight) — Chapter 3: Two Stories about Flying

Part I: His First Flight

Plot Summary

The story is about a young seagull who is afraid to fly. While his siblings have already mastered flight, he remains terrified on his ledge, fearing his wings won't support him. Left alone without food for twenty-four hours, he watches his family fly and dive. Driven by hunger, he is tempted by his mother holding a piece of fish. As he dives for the food, he falls into space, but instinctively spreads his wings and begins to soar, overcoming his fear and completing his first flight.

Key Themes

  • Overcoming Fear: The necessity of courage and self-belief to overcome psychological barriers.
  • Independence: The transition from parental dependence to self-reliance.
  • Parental Guidance: The tough love used by parents to push their offspring towards growth.

Vocabulary & Meanings

  • ledge: A narrow horizontal shelf projecting from a wall or cliff.
  • upbraiding: Scolding or rebuking.
  • skim: To move lightly just above a surface (like the sea).
  • herring: A soft-finned sea fish.
  • preening: Making an effort to clean and maintain feathers.
  • whet: To sharpen.
  • derisively: In a manner showing ridicule or that someone is stupid.
  • curveting: Leaping like a horse.
  • banking: Flying with one wing higher than the other.
Part II: The Black Aeroplane

Plot Summary

The narrator is flying his old Dakota aeroplane from France to England, dreaming of a holiday with his family. Suddenly, he encounters massive storm clouds resembling black mountains. Driven by the desire to reach home, he takes the risk and flies directly into the storm. Inside, his compass, radio, and other instruments fail completely. Lost and helpless, he spots a mysterious black aeroplane with no lights. The pilot of this phantom plane guides him safely through the storm to a runway. When the narrator tries to thank his savior, he discovers from the control tower lady that no other planes were in the sky that night.

Key Themes

  • Mystery & Faith: The unexplained presence of the black aeroplane represents hope, inner strength, or a supernatural guardian in times of crisis.
  • Risk-Taking: The consequences of making dangerous decisions driven by personal desires.

Vocabulary & Key Terms

  • Dakota DS 088: The old model aeroplane flown by the narrator.
  • compass: An instrument used for finding direction.
  • radar: A system for detecting the presence, direction, distance, and speed of aircraft.
Poem: How to Tell Wild Animals

Overview & Tone

A humorous poem by Carolyn Wells that suggests dangerous and highly impractical ways to identify wild beasts. The tone is light-hearted, playful, and ironic.

Animal Identification Guide

  • Asian Lion: Large, tawny beast in eastern jungles whose roar makes you feel like you are dying.
  • Bengal Tiger: Noble beast with black stripes on a yellow ground who greets you by eating you.
  • Leopard: Beast with a peppered hide who leaps on you repeatedly (lep and lep again).
  • Bear: Hugs you very, very hard in your yard.
  • Crocodile vs. Hyena: Hyenas come with merry smiles, while crocodiles weep tears as they swallow victims.
  • Chameleon: Small, lizard-like creature with no ears or wings; if the tree is empty, it is there.

Vocabulary & Poetic Devices

  • tawny: Yellowish-brown color.
  • discern: To make out or identify.
  • hide: Animal skin.
  • peppered: Covered with spots.
  • novice: Someone new to a job.
  • nonplus: Puzzled, confused, or surprised.
  • Poetic License: The poet uses non-standard spellings like 'dyin', 'lept', and 'lep' to maintain rhyme and rhythm.
Poem: The Ball Poem

Overview & Theme

Written by John Berryman, this poem explores the grief of a young boy who loses his ball in the water. It serves as a metaphor for the inevitable losses we experience in life and the necessity of learning to stand up and move on.

Key Concepts

  • The Epistemology of Loss: Understanding the nature of loss—what it truly means to lose something valuable and accept it.
  • Responsibility: Realizing that we live in a "world of possessions" where things will be lost, and money cannot buy back memories or lost time.
  • Resilience: Learning how to stand up and survive after experiencing grief.

Vocabulary & Meanings

  • intrude on: To enter a situation where one is not welcome.
  • dime: Ten cents (U.S. currency).
  • desperate: Hopeless.
  • rigid: Stiff and unmoving.
  • shaking grief: Sadness that deeply affects and destabilizes a person.
