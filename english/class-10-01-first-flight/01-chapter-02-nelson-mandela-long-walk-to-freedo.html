Class 10 (First Flight) — Chapter 2: Nelson Mandela: Long Walk to Freedom

Chapter Overview & Historical Context

This chapter is an extract from the autobiography of Nelson Rolihlahla Mandela, titled "Long Walk to Freedom". It describes the historic inauguration of South Africa's first democratic, non-racial government and Mandela's reflections on the struggle against apartheid.

What is Apartheid?

Apartheid is a political system that separates people according to their race. Under this system, the black majority in South Africa was subjected to extreme discrimination, oppression, and deprivation of basic human rights by the white-skinned minority rule for over three centuries.

The Historic Election of 1994

  • The first democratic, non-racial elections were held in South Africa in 1994.
  • Nelson Mandela's party, the African National Congress (ANC), won 252 out of 400 seats.
  • Mandela became the first black President of South Africa, marking the end of more than three centuries of white minority rule.
The Inauguration Ceremony (10th May 1994)

The historic inauguration took place on a bright and clear autumn day in Pretoria, symbolizing a new dawn of freedom and equality.

The Venue & Attendees

  • Venue: The lovely sandstone amphitheatre formed by the Union Buildings in Pretoria.
  • Dignitaries: Politicians and leaders from more than 140 countries gathered, making it the largest assembly of international leaders on South African soil.
  • Significance: The site, which had been the seat of white supremacy for decades, transformed into a "rainbow gathering" of different colors and nations.

The Swearing-In Ceremony

  • Second Deputy President: Mr. de Klerk was sworn in first.
  • First Deputy President: Thabo Mbeki was sworn in next.
  • President: Nelson Mandela pledged to obey and uphold the Constitution and devote himself to the well-being of the Republic and its people.

Mandela's Inaugural Address Key Points

  • He referred to the era of apartheid as an "extraordinary human disaster" that lasted too long.
  • He celebrated the birth of a new society of which all humanity would be proud—a "glorious human achievement".
  • He pledged to liberate all South Africans from the continuing bondage of poverty, deprivation, suffering, gender, and other forms of discrimination.
  • He declared: "Never, never, and never again shall it be that this beautiful land will again experience the oppression of one by another."
Symbols of the New Democratic Nation

Military Display & Loyalty

A spectacular array of South African jets, helicopters, and troop carriers roared in perfect formation over the Union Buildings. This was not just a display of military precision, but a demonstration of the military's loyalty to democracy and the newly elected government. High military and police generals, whose chests were bedecked with ribbons, saluted Mandela—generals who, not many years before, would have arrested him.

The New Flag & National Anthems

  • The Flag: Impala jets left a smoke trail of black, red, green, blue, and gold—the colors of the new South African flag.
  • The Anthems: The day was symbolized by the playing of two national anthems: 'Nkosi Sikelel’ iAfrika' (sung by whites) and 'Die Stem' (the old anthem, sung by blacks). Although neither group initially knew the exact lyrics, Mandela noted they would soon know them by heart.
Reflections on History, Courage, & Humanity

The Genesis of Apartheid

Mandela reflected that in the first decade of the twentieth century (after the bitter Anglo-Boer War and before his birth), the white-skinned peoples of South Africa erected a system of racial domination against the dark-skinned peoples. This structure formed the basis of one of the harshest, most inhumane societies the world had ever known.

The Unimaginable Sacrifices

The freedom achieved was the result of the unimaginable sacrifices of thousands of South African patriots. Mandela paid tribute to freedom fighters who went before him, such as Oliver Tambo, Walter Sisulu, Chief Luthuli, Yusuf Dadoo, Bram Fischer, and Robert Sobukwe. He described them as men of extraordinary courage, wisdom, and generosity, noting that "it requires such depths of oppression to create such heights of character."

The Definition of Courage

Mandela learned the meaning of courage from his comrades in the struggle: "Courage was not the absence of fear, but the triumph over it." The brave man is not he who does not feel afraid, but he who conquers that fear.

Love vs. Hate

Mandela asserted that no one is born hating another person because of their skin color, background, or religion. "People must learn to hate, and if they can learn to hate, they can be taught to love, for love comes more naturally to the human heart than its opposite." Even in the grimmest times in prison, a glimmer of humanity in a guard was enough to reassure him that man's goodness is a flame that can be hidden but never extinguished.

The Concept of Freedom & Twin Obligations

The Twin Obligations

Mandela explained that every man has twin obligations in life:

  1. First Obligation: To his family, parents, wife, and children.
  2. Second Obligation: To his people, his community, and his country.

In an apartheid-dominated South Africa, it was virtually impossible for a dark-skinned man to fulfill both obligations. Anyone who attempted to live as a human being was punished, isolated, and ripped away from their family.

The Evolution of Mandela's Understanding of Freedom

  • As a Boy: He believed he was born free—free to run in the fields, swim in the stream, and roast mealies. As long as he obeyed his father and tribe, he felt free.
  • As a Student: He wanted transitory freedoms only for himself—the freedom to stay out at night, read what he pleased, and go where he chose.
  • As a Young Man in Johannesburg: He yearned for basic, honorable freedoms—achieving his potential, earning his keep, marrying, and having a family.
  • The Transition: He realized that not only was his freedom curtailed, but the freedom of everyone who looked like him was also restricted. This realization led him to join the African National Congress (ANC), transforming his personal hunger for freedom into a greater hunger for the collective freedom of his people.

The Oppressor and the Oppressed

Mandela concluded that the oppressor must be liberated just as surely as the oppressed. "A man who takes away another man’s freedom is a prisoner of hatred; he is locked behind the bars of prejudice and narrow-mindedness." Both the oppressed and the oppressor are equally robbed of their humanity.

Language & Grammar Study

Noun-Verb Formations

Many nouns in the text are formed by adding suffixes like -tion, -ation, or -ment to verbs:

  • Rebel (Verb) → Rebellion (Noun)
  • Constitute (Verb) → Constitution (Noun)
  • Contribute (Verb) → Contribution (Noun)
  • Assist (Verb) → Assistance (Noun)
  • Subjugate (Verb) → Subjugation (Noun)
  • Humiliate (Verb) → Humiliation (Noun)
  • Imprison (Verb) → Imprisonment (Noun)
  • Resist (Verb) → Resistance (Noun)

Using the Definite Article 'The' with Proper Names

Generally, the definite article 'the' is not used before proper nouns. However, when used in the plural or with 'the' (e.g., "the Oliver Tambos of our time"), it carries a special meaning: "many other men like" or "men of their type or kind" whose names represent a standard of excellence or character.

Idiomatic Expressions

  • Not unmindful of: Conscious of; aware of.
  • Pushed to our limits: Pushed to the last point of one's ability to bear pain or suffering.
  • To earn my keep: To earn enough money to live on.
Poetry: "A Tiger in the Zoo" by Leslie Norris

This poem contrasts a majestic tiger confined within the artificial cage of a zoo with its natural, free life in the wild jungle.

Stanza-by-Stanza Breakdown

Stanza 1: Captivity
He stalks in his vivid stripes
The few steps of his cage,
On pads of velvet quiet,
In his quiet rage.
Analysis: The tiger is confined to a small cage. His movements are restricted ("few steps"). His paws are soft like velvet, and he suppresses his anger ("quiet rage").

Stanza 2: The Wild (Imagined)
He should be lurking in shadow,
Sliding through long grass
Near the water hole
Where plump deer pass.
Analysis: In his natural habitat, the tiger would hide in shadows and slide through tall grass near water sources to hunt deer.

Stanza 3: Natural Instincts
He should be snarling around houses
At the jungle’s edge,
Baring his white fangs, his claws,
Terrorising the village!
Analysis: Free, he would show his wild nature at the forest boundaries, growling and showing his fangs to terrorize nearby villages as a natural predator.

Stanza 4: Helplessness in Captivity
But he’s locked in a concrete cell,
His strength behind bars,
Stalking the length of his cage,
Ignoring visitors.
Analysis: Instead, he is locked in a strong concrete cell. His immense power is useless behind bars. He ignores the visitors, finding them uninteresting and irrelevant to his plight.

Stanza 5: Constant Confinement
He hears the last voice at night,
The patrolling cars,
And stares with his brilliant eyes
At the brilliant stars.
Analysis: At night, the only sounds he hears are the patrolling cars of the zoo keepers. He stares longingly at the free, brilliant stars, reflecting his own trapped brilliance.

Key Themes & Poetic Devices

  • Theme: The cruelty of captivity versus the beauty and necessity of natural freedom.
  • Contrast: The poet constantly shifts the setting between the restricted cage and the vast, free jungle.
  • Repetition: The repetition of words like "quiet" (velvet quiet / quiet rage) and "brilliant" (brilliant eyes / brilliant stars) intensifies the contrast between the tiger's helpless state and his natural splendor.
  • Glossary: snarls (makes an angry, warning sound).
