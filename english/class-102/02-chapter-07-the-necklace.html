Class 102 — Chapter 7: The Necklace

Chapter Overview

"The Necklace" is a classic short story by the French writer Guy de Maupassant. It tells the story of Matilda Loisel, a beautiful but dissatisfied woman born into a family of clerks. Her relentless desire for wealth, luxury, and social status leads her to borrow a diamond necklace for a high-society ball. When she loses the necklace, she and her husband spend ten years in ruinous debt to replace it, only to discover a devastating truth at the end.

Detailed Plot Summary

The Invitation and the Dress

Matilda Loisel is pretty and charming but deeply unhappy with her modest lifestyle. She feels she was born for all delicacies and luxuries, constantly suffering from the poverty of her apartment. One evening, her husband, a petty clerk in the Board of Education, returns home elated with an invitation to a grand party hosted by the Minister of Public Instruction. Instead of being delighted, Matilda weeps because she has nothing suitable to wear. To make her happy, her husband gives her 400 francs—money he had saved to buy a hunting gun—to purchase a pretty dress.

Borrowing the Necklace

As the day of the ball approaches, Matilda is still anxious because she has no jewels to wear. Her husband suggests she visit her rich schoolmate, Mme Forestier, to borrow some jewelry. Mme Forestier graciously offers her entire collection. Matilda is captivated by a superb diamond necklace in a black satin box and borrows it eagerly.

The Ball and the Loss

At the ball, Matilda is a grand success. She is the prettiest, most elegant, and happiest woman in the room, dancing with enthusiasm and basking in admiration. They leave at four o'clock in the morning. Upon returning home, Matilda looks in the glass for a final view of herself in her glory, only to discover that the necklace is gone. They search the folds of her dress, her cloak, and their pockets, but find nothing.

The Replacement and the Debt

After a week of fruitless searching by her husband, they realize they must replace the lost jewel. In a shop at the Palais-Royal, they find a matching diamond necklace valued at forty thousand francs, which they negotiate down to thirty-six thousand. To pay for it, Monsieur Loisel uses his inheritance of eighteen thousand francs and borrows the rest from usurers and lenders at ruinous interest rates. Matilda returns the replacement necklace to Mme Forestier, who does not notice the substitution.

A Decade of Hardship

To pay off the massive debt, the Loisels enter a life of extreme poverty. They dismiss their maid, change their lodgings to an attic room, and Matilda learns the heavy, odious work of a kitchen. Her husband works evenings and nights doing bookkeeping and copying. This grueling life lasts for ten years. By the end of this decade, Matilda has aged drastically, losing her beauty and becoming a rough, hard woman of a poor household.

The Final Twist

One Sunday, while walking in the Champs-Elysees, Matilda encounters Mme Forestier, who is still young and beautiful. Matilda decides to tell her the truth about the lost necklace and the ten years of misery spent replacing it. Deeply moved, Mme Forestier takes Matilda's hands and reveals the tragic irony: the original necklace was false, made of imitation diamonds, and was worth at most five hundred francs.

Character Analysis

Matilda Loisel (Mme Loisel)

  • Vain and Materialistic: She is obsessed with wealth, social status, and luxury, constantly daydreaming of elegant dinners and shining silver.
  • Resilient and Heroic: Despite her vanity, when faced with ruin, she accepts her fate and works heroically for ten years to pay off the debt without complaint.
  • Tragic Figure: Her downfall is caused by her pride and her inability to be content with her life.

Monsieur Loisel (M. Loisel)

  • Selfless and Loving: He is a devoted husband who sacrifices his own desires (like saving for a gun) to make his wife happy.
  • Practical and Hardworking: He immediately takes charge of searching for the necklace and works tirelessly for ten years, taking on extra jobs to clear the debt.

Madame Forestier (Mme Forestier)

  • Wealthy and Generous: She is a good friend who readily trusts Matilda with her jewelry.
  • Symbol of High Society: Her possession of imitation jewelry that looks real highlights the theme of appearance versus reality in upper-class society.
Themes & Key Messages

Appearance vs. Reality

The central theme of the story is the contrast between appearance and reality. The fake necklace looked genuine, symbolizing how the high-society lifestyle Matilda craved was built on superficiality and illusion.

The Danger of Vanity and Pride

Matilda's pride prevents her from admitting to Mme Forestier that she lost the necklace. Had she been honest, she would have avoided ten years of unnecessary suffering and poverty.

Contentment vs. Greed

The story teaches that one should be content with what life offers. Matilda's constant dissatisfaction with her modest but comfortable life ultimately leads to her complete ruin.

Glossary
  • incessantly: continuously; without interruption.
  • tureen: a deep, covered dish from which soup is served.
  • M.: abbreviation for 'Monsieur' (Mr. in French).
  • Mme: abbreviation for 'Madame' (Mrs. in French).
  • vexation: the state of being annoyed, frustrated, or worried.
  • ruinous: disastrous or destructive; bringing ruin.
  • usurers: money-lenders who charge extremely high rates of interest.
  • sou: a French coin of low value.
  • awry: out of the correct position; crooked or twisted.
Think & Discuss Questions
  • How did the course of the Loisels' life change due to the necklace? (The loss of the necklace forced them into ten years of extreme poverty, hard labor, and debt, which stripped Matilda of her youth and beauty.)
  • What was the cause of Matilda's ruin? How could she have avoided it? (Her ruin was caused by her vanity, materialism, and pride. She could have avoided it by being honest with Mme Forestier about losing the necklace.)
  • What would have happened if Matilda had confessed to her friend? (She would have learned immediately that the necklace was fake, and they would only have had to pay a small sum of five hundred francs to replace it.)
